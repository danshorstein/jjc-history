BUILDING AND EXPANSION PROGRAM
There is no point in identifying this picture. The Center’s Activity Building has been taken
into the hearts of young and old. Ever since it was dedicated not quite 3 years ago, it has been
acclaimed one of the most magnificent synagogue structures in the country. Years of planning «md
of zealous work by the Building Fund Committee, combined with the generosity of our member-
ship, made it a reality.
The same sense of joy and pride, with God’s help, will be ours when we complete the next
phase of our Building and Expansion program — The rebuilding of the Synagogue and the remod-
eling of classrooms.
Plans invisage for this part of the construction effects commensurate with the magnificence
of the Activity Building. Their realization will depend on the response from the Center membership.
The exterior and interior of the synagogue building will take on new appearances. Sorry we
cannot print pictures.

MRS. SAMUEL J. THIER
What does the Sisterhood accomplish? The answer may
be obvious to those of us who have been close to the work
and participate in the many activities and worthwhile pro-
jects. Large numbers within our membership take the
organization for granted. This one time a year when we
take a quick glance at the past year, it is not amiss to
point out a number of highlights in our program.
The range of Sisterhood interests is wide. Sisterhood’s
influence within the community is profound.
This very Year Book, a project of long standing, and for
a number of years directed by that indefatigible worker,
Mr. Hyman Selber, was in recent years made a Sisterhood
project and benefited from the zealous cooperation of Sister-
hood members whose names are given on the Year Book
Committee page. Through their solicitation of advertising
they have raised the larger portion of the funds which the
Sisterhood has been able to put to the disposal of the
Building Fund Committee. From the present effort the re-
building of the Synagogue will be advanced substantially.
Through our affiliation with the National Women’s
League of America we promulgate a variety of activities
that aim to revitalize the religious faith of our people and
instill the Jewish way of life among the American Jewish

A natural association with our national ties is the Torah
Fund Project. Most of our members contribute annually
towards the Seminary campaign through this special
Women’s League solicitation. The training of Rabbis and
teachers so badly needed throughout the country is thus
supported.
Our Book and Gift Shop is featured below. We

project to cultural and financial success. The beautifully
stocked show-case in the lobby of the Activities Building
acquaints our membership with articles Jewishly and
artistically significant. It serves as a constant reminder of
the place that Jewish ceremonial objects have in our lives.
In the past year the profits were used to provide much

The youth of the congregation has been assisted by the

A large porportion of the Sisterhood’s income was ac-

porportion were initiated and carried out with the single
over-all purpose — to make the religious life of our com-

salaries. Our children are our constant delight. Whether
by means of offering prizes at the end of the year or by

hoods feels that influencing our children in the Jewish
tradition is one of our most sacred tasks.

educational agency and as a focal point for social activities.

IRWIN GENDZIER
The Center Men’s Club is one arm of the Center, a
part of the whole, a part strong in its endeavors, con-
tinually expanding and striving in every way to aid and
assist the Center in attaining its educational, social, cul-
tural and religious goals.
We feel that to base our future actions on past activity
is using a standard that is continually too low. We are
forever striving for greater projects, and to enhance those
values which the Center is constituted to provide. Close
affiliation with the Men’s Club aids in directing the
energies, skills and talents of its membership towards a
richer self, at the same time strengthening and developing
Jewish life in our community.
The Men’s Club annual Labor Day Dance and Spring
Frolics Dance are two affairs always enjoyed by many
people each year. This past year we brought to Jack-
sonville a Yiddish theatre group. Everyone present at
this show enjoyed themselves thoroughly.
The sponsorship of the Minyan Club is the religious
activity from which we derive the greatest satisfaction.
This group is composed of boys about to be or who have
already been Bar Mitzvah. The Men’s Club arranges
transportation for the boys every Sunday morning and
after the services acts as hosts at a breakfast. The Men’s
Club also assists, in cooperation with the Center Sister-
hood, in the celebration of various Holidays and Festivals
and Sunday School projects.
For the coming year we hope to begin an enlarged and
more active program of youth work. Awards will be
given to the outstanding students in the Center Hebrew
school. Also outstanding members of all Center affiliated
youth organizations will be recipients of Men’s Club
awards. We are hoping to start a fund to send deserving
children to Camp Ramah. In this way we hope to
develop future leaders for the Jacksonville Jewish com-
munity.
The men who give of their time and effort in carrying
out the program of the Men’s Club are too numerous to
mention. However they are rewarded by the knowledge
of a job well done. They have helped themselves by
helping others. In a service organization such as ours,
there is no better goal. There is a place in the Men’s
Club for every Center member who desires to avail him-
self of the joys and opportunities inherent therein.

BYRON SELBER
During its fourth year of organization the Center Youth
League has continued to hold its position as one of the
most dynamic organizations of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. It has made great strides towards fulfilling its
aim; to offer opportunities to Jewish Youth to continue
and strengthen their identification with Judaism and the
Synagogue.
The program during this past year was broad in scope,
based on self-development, interests, and needs of the
Jewish teen-ager. The bi-weekly business meetings on
Sunday evenings, which have become a must on the
calendar of Jacksonville youth, are followed by an inter-
esting cultural program and social.
One of the high points during the year was the organ-
ization of a Post-Confirmation study group which held
one series on Sunday nights and one on Friday evenings
after services. The participants held very thought-provok-
ing discussions on some of the basic philosophies of our
religion.
Other features of this year’s program included par-
ticipation in Sabbath services, athletics, projects, and
Saturday evening canteens in the Youth Activities Build-
ing, climaxed by the annual Spring Dance.
With the financial assistance of the Sisterhood and the
Men’s Club, C.Y.L. participated in several conventions—
the Leadership Training Institute for Regional United Syn-
agogue Youth, the Second Annual Convention of the
United Synagogue Youth, and the Southeastern Regional
Conventions. These conventions are of great assistance
in invigorating local membership and leadership, and also
in strengthening the Regional and the National organ-
izations.
The Center Youth League looks with pride on the
achievements of the previous administration and considers
them a challenge for the coming year. With the wonder-
ful inspiration and guidance of Rabbi Tofield, the con-
tinued support of the Center family, and with the assist-
anee of our advisor, Miss Myra Thier, C. Y. L. eagerly
looks forward to a year of successful development.

THE CANTOR AND CHOIR
The Jewish Center Choral Society has completed what
has been the outstanding year of its history, not only ex-
panding its program of presenting concerts in neighboring
communities, but by presenting locally one of its finest
concerts.
The local concert, which was held March 8th under the
sponsorship of the local B’nai B’rith lodge, featured as
soloist the renowned American Cantor, David Kusevitsky,
in a concert which will be long remembered as the finest
evening of Jewish music in the history of Jacksonville.
In addition to their annual concert, the Choral Group
also appeared in radio and television programs, as well as
in many other local programs. The group also appeared
in concert in Savannah on March 16th and in Augusta on
April 12, being very well received in both communities.
Cantor Abraham Marton not only conducted the Society
in both communities, but appeared as guest soloist.
Cantor Marton personally appeared as soloist in St.
Petersburg on February 1st in a memorial ceremony for
the late Chaim Weizman, and in Tampa on April 29th in
a fifth anniversary celebration of Israel’s independence.
An unusual distinction came to the Choral Group when
they were invited from the state at large to be the repre-
sentative of typical Jewish music at the “All Florida Folk
Festival” sponsored by the Stephen Foster Memorial Com-
mission and the Florida Federation of Music Clubs on
Sunday afternoon, May 10th at the Stephen Foster Me-
morial Grounds at White Springs, Florida.
CANTOR ABRAHAM MARTON
The Choral Group presents a varied repertoire of liturgi-
cal and Jewish folk music. The work of this group has
contributed in large measure to the meaningful and in-
spirational development of the synagogue music now a
part of the Center’s religious services. From the larger
group of the Choral Society are selected the volunteer
choir for Friday evening services and augmented choir
for the High Holy Day Services.
Cantor Marton not only acts in the official role of Can-
tor at all Center services, but also directs the Choral Group
and the program of training students of the Hebrew School
in the traditional Hazanut chants.

ATHLETICS (Continued)
The third year of an enlarged athletic program, made
possible by the completion of the Activities Building,
witnessed an up-surge of interest and vastly expanded
schedule of activities. William Goldman took over the chair-
manship from Frank Diamond and Sam Silver became act-
ing Athletic Director upon the resignation of Sidney X.
Cohen. Under their leadership and with the assistance
of an active committee, many new features were added.
The department was especially activated during the sum-
mer months.
The outstanding addition in the total field of athletics
was “Ladies Night” on Wednesdays. Under the super-
vision of Patricia Stevens Studio the ladies enjoyed a
most beneficial program of supervised exercises, ballet,
free activity and healthful, relaxing steam baths.
For the most part, this department carried on with well
established features of previous years for men, women and
youth of all ages.
During basketball and baseball seasons the Center
entered teams in the respective Church Leagues. Sunday
afternoon will again be devoted, whenever the gym is
available, to basketball games and practice.
An active season is anticipated for 1953-54. Detailed
schedules will be announced for all programs through the
Center Bulletin and through direct mailings to those who
are signed up in this department. They will be centered
around the Equipment that the Center possesses with
Activities listed and others that may be inaugurated as
the demand arises and in keeping with the space avail-
able.

when he makes a pilgrimage to the headquarters of Con-
servative Judaism in America. He may call at one, or both
of its two locations.
The Jewish Theological Seminary of America, located
at 122nd Street and Broadway, houses the offices of the
various Seminary departments, the United Synagogue of
America and the Rabbinical Assembly of America. The
visitor will primarily be attracted by the exhibits in the
lobby and the reading room of its world famous library,
(see photograph on this page.)
We mention only the graphic portrayals in the treasure
house of Judaism that has been planted on this continent.
Anyone who has special interests in the American Jewish
life will of course seek out the individual or office devoted
to the cause. A telephone call to Riverside, 9-8000, or
a stop at the information desk, will bring ready guidance.
The grandeur and spiritual strength that is housed in
the Jewish Museum, sponsored by the Jewish Theological
Seminary, is impossible to convey in words. An hour or two
spent at its building on 92nd Street and 5th Avenue will
convince anyone that he walks in the midst of beauty on
a road winding through Jewish creativeness of the past
and of the present. Special exhibits are featured in modern
Jewish art, ceremonial objects of inestimable value and
the handwork by children or of special child interest, (see
the sample show case on this page.)

EXPLANATORY NOTES
Tebet
The year 5714 in the Jewish Religious Calendar is a leap year. It will
be noted in the list below that this year has an Adar I and an Adar II,
giving the year 13 months. In the ancient Hebrew Calendar the months
are determined by the phases of the moon, rendering it a lunar calen-
dar, but the seasonal festivals are observed in keeping with the solar
system. Since the lunar month has only 29 V 2 days, an extra month is
added in seven of a 1 9 year cycle, so that Jewish festivals which always
occur on the same date of the lunar month still come within the proper
solar season.

GENERAL NOTES
Opposite each Friday and the eve of festivals, also on the first day of
a festival, there appears the symbol L. C. “Light Candles’’. This is a re-
minder that 20 minutes before sunset of that evening the traditional
Sabbath or festival lights are to be kindled in the home.
Rosh Hodesh, Beginning of the Month, is observed with special prayers
in the service for that day and with the Blessing of the New Month on
the Sabbath previous. When the new moon appears in the middle of the
day the outgoing month consists of 30 days and the last day is con-
sidered the first day Rosh Hodesh for the coming month.
The Sidrah, the portion of the Torah read on Saturday morning, is in-
dicated. Special Sabbaths will be explained below under the month in
which they occur.
Occasions in Tishri
Rosh Hashanah, Jewish Religious New Year.
Fast of Gedalia, commemorates the assassination of the governor of
Judea who attempted to restore Jewish independence after the destruc-
tion of the First Temple by the Babylonians.
Shabbat Shuva, Sabbath of Repentance, derives its name from the
Prophetic reading, “Shuva Yisrael," “Return, O Israel."
Yom Kippur, Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and prayer, a day
for confession of sin and supplication for forgiveness. “For transgressions
between man and God repentance on Yom Kippur brings atonement. For
transgressions between man and man, Yom Kippur brings no atonement
until amends are made."
Sukkot, Tabernacles, commemorates the period when our forefathers
dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings through the wilderness
of Sinai.
Hoi Hamoed, Intermediate Days of the Holiday, are observed as semi-
festivals.
Hashanah Rabba, the day on which prayers with the refrain Hosanah
(“O Save Us") is recited.
Shemini Atzeret, Eighth Day of Assembly, marks the conclusion of the
fall festivals.
Simhat Torah, Torah Festival, is the second day of Shemini Atzeret and
celebrates the completion of the annual reading cycle from the Five
Books of Moses and the beginning of readings for the New Year.
Kislev
Hanukah, Feast of Dedication, commemorates the rededication of the
Temple by the Maccabees in 165 B.C.E. The Maccabean triumph over
Syrian forces was the first victory for a people's right to the freedom of
worship. Lights are kindled in the synagogue and in the home on every
one of the eight evenings.
Asarah BeTebet, Fast of the 10th of Tebet, commemorates the begin-
ning of the siege of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in 586 B.C.E. which
led to the destruction of the First Temple.
Shebat
Hamisha Asar BeShebat, also known as Tu BeShebat, 15th of Shebat,
is Holy Land Arbor Day or New Year of the Trees.
Adar II
Shabbat Shekalim, the Sabbath when every adult Jew in the days of
the Temple was reminded to pay his poll tax of a half a shekel on the
first day of Adar.
Shabbat Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of Purim a spe-
cial portion of the Torah is read beginning with the word “Zakhor”—
“Remember Amaelk.” Haman is presumed to have been an Amalekite.
Purim commemorates the day when, through the intervention of Mor-
decai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly escaped being
victims of Haman's plot to exterminate them.
Shabbat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning the ashes of
the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual purification by our
ancestors in preparing for the Passover Festival.
Shabbat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read, emphasiz-
ing the significance of the month of Nisan in which Israel’s great de-
liverance occurred.
Nisan
Shabbat Hagadol, the Sabbath which precedes the great Festival of
Passover, derives its name from the Haftorah which contains a reference
to the “Great Day of the Lord."
Pesah, Passover, celebrates Israel's deliverance from Egyptian bond-
age. The festival lasts eight days during which matzot are eaten in the
place of leavened bread. The celebration of the Passover with the beau-
tiful ceremony of the Seder on the first and second evenings has fos-
tered by Jewish people the ideals of liberty and freedom.
lyar
Yom Ha’atzma’ut. The new State of Israel declared its independence
on May 14, 1948, which was the 5th year of lyar according to the
Hebrew calendar.
Lag B’Omer, the date of a victory by Bar Kokhba in the final Jewish
struggle against the Romans. He was supported by the sage Rabbi Akiba
and his disciples. It is known as “The Scholars Festival” and marked by
field day outings.
Sivan
Shabout, Feast of Weeks, completes 7 weeks from the second day of
Passover on which the Omer (a measure) of the new barley was brought
to the Temple. It was also the day when Bikkurim (first fruits) were
presented. Tradition assigned to Shabout commemoration of the Covenant
established at Sinai between God and Israel and the giving of the Ten
Commandments.
Tammuz
Shivah Asar Be’Tamuz, Fast of the 17th of Tammuz, that commemo-
rates the breach in the walls of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in the
year 586 B.C.E.
Ab
Shabbat Hazon, precedes Tisha B’Ab, derives its name from the first
words of the Haftorah “Hazon”—the vision of Isaiah, which foretells
the destruction of Jerusalem.
Tisha B’Ab, the Ninth Day of Ab, is a fast day which marks the de-
struction of the First Temple in Jerusalem by the Babylonians (586
B.C.E.) and also of the Second Temple by the Romans (70 C.E.).
Shabbat Nahamu, Sabbath of Consolation; the first word of the
Haftorah is “Nahamu, nahamu, Comfort ye, My People."
Elul
Selihot, Penitential Prayers, recited at midnight in preparation for the
holiday season, “The Great Days of Awe."

P. N. COLEMAN
The Jewish Community Council represents and acts as
the spokesman for Jacksonville Jewry. As the most repre-
sentative Jewish organization in the city, it has on its
Governing Boards, which make policies and set the pro-
gram of the Council, the representatives of some 25 dif-
ferent organizations.
The Council was formally organized about 20 years ago
and received its charter from the State of Florida. Among
its presidents have been such outstanding community
workers as Alexander Brest, L. A. Stein, Morton Hirsch-
berg, David Harris, David Lasarow, Sam Wolfson and
Leonard Moss. The Council has developed from an organi-
zation which raised some $3,000 in one year to a high of
over $346,000. Within the past five years, it has raised
about one and a half million dollars, distributed annually
to over 40 different beneficiaries. Its actual fund-raising
costs have approximated about 1%.
The Council operates through committees, selected by
the President and the Governing Boards. Its major standing
committees are: Campaign, Budget, Refugee, Public Re-
lations, Armed Services and Nomination. Other committees
function for specific purposes, such as the present Census
Committee, responsible for the on-going census study of
our Jewish community.
The “Commentator,” published by the Community
Council, is the official voice of the Jewish Community.
It is mailed, at no cost to all Jewish families in Jackson-
ville. Its columns contain news and articles of local and
national importance. This community newspaper has been
twice awarded national recognition as an outstanding
publication by the National Council of Jewish Federations
and Welfare Funds.
The Community Calendar, listing all organizational
events taking place each month in the city, is maintained
by the Council for the benefit of all. These and many
other functions make the Jewish Community Council the
representative of the entire community. It is, as once
described—the Voice, Arm and Heart of the Jewish Com-
munity of Jacksonville.

SIDNEY ENTMAN
River Garden is now in its eighth year of serving the
aged and infirm people of Florida. The Home makes
every effort to meet all of the many needs of the aged.
As the life span is extended due to the miracle of modern
medicine, it is accompanied by many social, mental and
emotional problems which many elderly people find diffi-
to cope with.
Many look to the Home for a solution to their prob-
lems and those who make their home with us generally
resolve their difficulties. Unfortunately, the lack of bed-
rooms necessitate others to wait many months before they
are admitted. In order to help those on our waiting
list, the Home has inaugurated an Extension Program.
The Home takes the same responsibility of the resident
on the Extension Program as they do for the residents
of the Home. Sleeping accommodations are provided in
a nearby furnished rooming house, and the resident utilizes
all the services and facilities of the Home during the day.
This program is supervised jointly with the Jewish Wei-
fare Society. Each room is carefully selected to meet the
highest standard. The owner of the lodging house is
made aware of our program and cooperates with the
Home in making our residents comfortable.
As a result of the Extension Program, River Garden
is now serving 50 residents, as compared to its rated bed
capacity of 43. The demand for the services of River
Garden is increasing, and the Home is now planning a
new Building Program. The most urgent need is in the
infirmary area, which houses the chronically ill and
mentally confused. Our present facilities are inadequate
and insufficient to give proper care to the ailing and
helpless aged. A modern infirmary wing will enable the
Home to give excellent service to the most helpless res-
idents, who must have individual care and attention.
To extend life without providing a good life is no
blessing. River Garden is providing a good life for its
many residents and is looking forward to open new doors
for those eager and waiting to enter.

Hopes, cherished ideals, dreams, these were the things
that inspired and brought together a small group of women
who combined their energies to give this community what
is known today as River Garden Home for the Hebrew
Aged. Theirs was the foresight, knowledge of the need for
such a home, and the wisdom to instill love for the aged
as commanded by the sages of our faith.
The women of the Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society have
been endowed with this wonderful legacy. Ours is a precious
gift from the elders who founded this organization. To
them we owe a debt of gratitude which can only be repaid
with continued efforts to expand and increase the measure
of services we offer to River Garden. As the auxiliary of
this home we are ever mindful of the needs which are
constantly arising and ever ready to fulfill these needs;
whether they be for linens, a Summer pavilion for outdoor
picnics or a most urgently needed new infirmary.
To carry on the aims and purposes of our organization
we have three major functions: The Linen Shower, Tom-
bola Project and Donors Social Affair.
The Women’s Auxiliary of River Garden Home have
been inspired and have inspired others to continue this
work to which the founders dedicated themselves and for
which we in turn further dedicate ourselves and pray that
we may ever carry on this blessed legacy.

LAWRENCE RACKOW
For thirty-six years, since 1917, the Jewish Welfare
Society, a Community Chest agency, served individuals
and families in Jacksonville with financial and counseling
services. Originally organized to help the needy, it soon
became evident, as it did in communities all over the
country, that financial, inter-personal and some psycho-
logical problems, with which a case-work agency could
be of assistance, occurred to people of all economic levels
and stations of life. Alert to the needs of the community,
the services broadened and contracted to fit the needs and
time. The name was changed twice to reflect the changed
concept and services. A point was reached where a
professional staff was secured.
Now, the Jewish Welfare Society is a multi-functional
casework agency—that is, besides helping with finances,
problems in marital situations, individual adjustment,
mental and physical illness, etc., it also provides child
care, foster homes and adoption services. It is still the
only Jewish child placing agency in the State of Florida.
The great majority of cases are requests for services
that do not involve financial assistance. This is true,
even though Jacksonville, because of its geographical posi-
tion, seasonally has many requests from transients. The
transients are counseled and helped whenever possible
with food, lodging, employment or contact with their
families.
The Welfare Society works in close relationship with
the River Garden Home in processing applications and
providing casework services to the residents of the Home
and their families when needed. Last year we helped
initiate and supervise the “extension program”, which
provides for people who can live outside of the Home
and use its facilities.
Through funds allocated by the Jewish Community
Council and with the help of the Council of Jewish
Women, the agency helped resettle a number of families in
the city. The families are now financially self-sufficient
and as a group, they are making a wholesome adjustment.
These and others, seek and secure our help with prob-
lems of immigration and naturalization.
The Society offers a limited social service program with
Jewish inmates at the State Prison at Raiford. For this
purpose, Community Chest funds are not used.
Last year, the agency concluded the demonstration
project of the Family Consultation Center that was run
on a non-sectarian b^sis, with Levy Memorial Funds. Its
services to families with marital discord, and problems in
parent-child relationship, was highly successful and its
need for the community, we believe, amply demonstrated.
The Jewish Welfare Society is continuing this work with
the Jewish Community. The Community Chest is work-
ing on plans to establish a permanent non-sectarian agency
in this field.

Clubs from ages of 8 to 18, and at present there
are 7 Clubs under the sponsorship of the Zionist

The basic aims of Judaea are:
1. Developing the individual in the American
Jewish Community
2. Inspiring the youth to be of service to
Jewish people everywhere
3. Creating the desire in our Youth to aid
Israel
4. Exchanging of modern Jewish values with
Israel

and meetings are planned around Jewish Holidays, also
studies of current events, personalities, both modern and
Biblical, and Choral Groups, Israeli Dances, Arts and

the raising of funds for the Tzofim of Israel (Scout
Movement). These are personal and group projects.

MRS. MORTON TURBOW
The National Council of Jewish Women, organized in
1893, is one of the oldest women’s organizations in
America. It now has 250 sections throughout the country
with an enrollment of over 100,000 members. Council’s
purpose is to bring about a closer fellowship among
Jewish women, to foster a program devoted to social
welfare, community affairs, education, and international
understanding for peace. Its program is one of service and
education.
Council’s overseas projects are threefold: the support
of the School of Education of Hebrew University in
Jerusalem; maintenance of graduate scholarships awarded
to students from Israel and some European countries for
study in American universities; and shipments of clothing,
play and study material to orphanages in the Middle East.
Local services include Council’s Medical Loan Shelf,
Golden Age Club, sewing for the less fortunate, Service
to Foreign Born, and parties for hospitalized servicemen
at the Naval Air Station. Through education, Council
gives an opportunity to all its members to work toward
the betterment of the individual, the community, the
nation, and the world.

country. Its value to the American Jewish community
rests on its special nature as a veterans organization. Mem-
bers of the J. W. V. participate actively, as members, in
such veterans organizations as the American Legion, Vet-
erans of Foreign Wars, Disabled American Veterans. Be-
cause of its special relationship with both past and present
leadership in veterans circles, J. W. V. is able to effectively
serve Jewish interests in important areas.
The Jewish War Veterans of the U. S. A. with its
more than 650 posts, spread throughout all sections of the
country, with an active membership of close to one hun-
dred thousand, act as a powerful bulwark in the battle
against the spread of doctrines of bigotry and hatred. Its
395 ladies auxiliaries with thirty thousand active members,
is keeping faith with the unfortunate victims of the past
wars who are still hospitalized.

the national program by its regular visits to the Veterans
Hospital at Lake City, and the Naval Air Station; by

The Progressive Credit Union, sponsored by the Jewish
Progressive Society, has continued to grow in its 13 years
of operation. It is one of more than 10,000 Credit
Unions in the United States, having almost 5 million
members and more than 1 billion dollars in assets.
From a very modest beginning of 20 members and
$500.00 in assets, we have grown to over 350 members,
and more than $185,000.00 in assets. During our exist-
ence we have made over 1,300 loans to members, amount-
ing to approximately $1,600,000.00 without a single loss.
We are organized to encourage thrift and provide a safe,
convenient medium whereby members can invest and
accumulate savings. We are chartered by the State of
Florida and regularly examined by the Comptroller of the
said State, and the savings of the members are safeguarded
in several ways. The Credit Union being a cooperative,
assists its members when circumstances require and permit
such assistance. The purchasing and business power of
the members is increased by their ability to borrow and
repay loans under convenient arrangements such as are
afforded them by our Credit Union. We sponsor the
Blue Cross Hospitalization Plan under group rates for
those members who desire it. Life Insurance equal to a
member’s savings up to $1,000.00 and Loan Protection
Insurance is paid by the Progressive Credit Union without
any cost to the individual member.
The climax of each year’s activities comes with the
annual meeting and banquet held in the Jacksonville
Jewish Center Auditorium. Last year the board declared
a 2%% dividend on savings and a 25% rebate of interest
to borrowing members.
