Communications with candidates should serve to help formulate their views of the special
U.S./Israel relationship and of Israel's significance as an ally to the United States. Our voices need
to be heard on several issues.
ISSUE ONE: FOREIGN AID IN GENERAL:
* There is a rising anti-foreign aid sentiment in our nation. This is caused primarily by a
misunderstanding of what foreign aid is, and just how many Americans benefit from it.
* The small amount we invest in foreign aid — only 1.2% of our budget — is more than made up
for by its benefits to the U. S. economy. For example:
* 70% of all foreign aid is spent in the U.S. Military and humanitarian aid pumps
hundreds of millions of dollars into local economies every year.
ISSUE TWO: FOREIGN AID TO ISRAEL:
* Foreign aid to Israel is one of our most cost effective foreign aid investments, protecting our
democratic ally, Israel, as well as defending U.S. interest at a fraction of the $170 billion we spend
to defend NATO or the $19 billion we spend to defend Korea.
* A strong Israel — strengthened by foreign aid -- is even more important as the U.S. addresses
the dangers of the post Cold War era: acquisition of nuclear weapons by unstable radical regimes
and rising anti-Western Islamic fundamentalism in an area that contains 70% of the world's oil
supply.
* 85% of all U.S. aid to Israel is spent in the Unted States including $35 million in Florida in
1990-91.
ISSUE THREE: ABSORPTION LOAN GUARANTEES:
* The passage of loan guarantee legislation remains the highest priority for the American
pro-Israel community.
* The loan guarantees do not cost the taxpayer anything. However, the U.S. stands to benefit by
providing them. If Israel's absorption effort is successful, Israel's imports of U.S. goods are
expected to swell to $30 billion over the next five years as a result of the population influx.
ISSUE FOUR: ARMS TO SAUDI ARABIA:
* It is ironic that the U.S. is considering the sale of 72 sophisticated F-15 fighter bombers to
Saudi Arabia at the same time it is promoting Middle East peace talks and arms proliferation as a
central concern of U.S. foreign policy.
ISSUE FIVE: ISRAEL'S DEFENSE AND SECURITY: Trading land for peace. . . .
* The Golan Heights and many specific areas in the administered territories, Judea and Samaria,
are of indispensable value strategically to the survival of the Jewish state as long as it is surrounded
by totalitarian regimes.

• Simhat Torah
Three Members Honored on Simhat Torah, Tuesday, October 1, 1991
Nathan Shmunes
Hatan Torah
In the eighteen years that Nathan
Shmunes was captain of Sunday even¬
ing minyan, there was always a mi-
nyan. Nathan never left anything to
chance. He reserved every Sunday for
calling his prospects and was even
happy to drive them to shul.
A regular Shabbat and holiday wor¬
shipper, our honoree is proud to be a
gabbai at services. On his own he
takes full responsibility for repairing
our Torahs and has even designed
ways to hang them securely in the
Aron Kodesh and on the bima. He
regularly checks our ceremonial ob¬
jects to make sure everything is in top
condition.
His dedication is further demon¬
strated by his volunteer involvement in
the Hevrah Kadisha, the local Jewish
Burial Society. He and his wife Anne
coordinate the volunteers and make
sure the deceased is given the proper
ritual care and respect required by
Jewish law.
Nathan is always there when called
upon. He takes his volunteer com¬
mitments seriously and is grateful that
he has the opportunities to perform
many mitzvot.
A loving husband, father and grand¬
father and caring Center member, our
Hatan Torah is truly worthy of his
honor. Mazel tov to his wife Anne; his
daughter, Ruth Bernstein; and his son,
Dr. Edward Shmunes and other
members of his family.
George Strumlauf
Hatan Bereshit
Our “Bridgegroom of the Beginning”
begins every Tuesday morning by at¬
tending minyan at the Center. . .a
commitment he is very proud of.
Although most of our honoree’s
volunteer work is behind the scenes,
that suits George just fine. His joy
comes from being able to share his
talents and being part of the Center’s
success.
He was instrumental in establishing
the Sunday-Funday athletic program.
He served as co-chairman of the
Youth Commission, and was a
member of the Board of Directors,
later being appointed Vice President-
Youth. Currently George serves as co-
chairman of the Interior Design Com¬
mittee where he devotes much of his
free time. Through the years, George
has designed most of the fund raising
material for the Center, as well as
many of the year book covers.
George attended the University of
Florida and the Atlanta Art Institute.
An advertising and graphics designer
for more than thirty years, George has
received over 100 design and adver¬
tising awards. He is a member of the
Jacksonville Advertising Federation
having held office and served on its
Board of Directors. He is a founding
member of the A.I.G.A. (American In¬
stitute of Graphic Arts).
George has been married to the
former Phyllis Katz for 30 years. His
children Ross, Lane and Beth are all
Solomon Schechter alumni. As
teenagers they were very active
members of the Center youth pro¬
grams. His mother, Pauline Strumlauf
is proud of George’s involvement at
the Center.

Cub Scout Pack 14 had a great year. We have fond memories of a
busy time of weekly den meetings and monthly pack meetings to show
the other Cubs and their families what was learned during the month.
The Cubs ran a recycling box during the Sunday Basketball League,
had an indoor campfire, Knights of the Round Table Tournament and
Pinewood Derby car race. The boys loved the field trips to the air show

to express their appreciation to the volunteers who keep our pack going.

to attract. Cub Scout 14 has been blessed over the past years with two
of the best Jacksonville has to offer. Carole Goldberg and Lanny Zim¬
merman have served the pack with all their hearts and in return have
won the love and respect of all those who have had the honor of working
side by side with them. To help a boy succeed in Cub Scouts, you don’t
have to be an expert in Cub Scouting. He does need guidance, enthusiasm
and participation—Lanny and Carol have done just that and so much
more. We love you.

• Message from Rabbi David Gaffney
The synagogue is the oldest existing institution in Jewish life. It dates back to
the first exile to Babylonia, in 587 B.C.E. Some scholars even suggest that the
synagogue is older than this. We have ample archeological evidence of synagogues
in the ancient world from Israel to Egypt, Syria, Turkey and Italy. The role of the
synagogue was always changing and in some respects the various names given
to designate the Jewish House of Worship reflected those changes.
The synagogue was called a Bet-ha-knesset, which means a gathering place; a
place of coming together. The word synagogue, from the Greek meaning a place
of assembly, essentially, means the same thing. It emphasizes the communal aspect
of Jewish prayer which is not only a reaching up to God, but a reaching out to
other Jews. You can turn to God alone, but for a Jewish service you need a Minyan.
That’s why we have appointed times for services—not because God keeps office
hours, but because we come to synagogue to find other Jews to pray with. That
is why we read someone else’s words at services instead of trying to come up with
our own—so that by offering the same words, separate individuals can become
one in prayer.
Another designation for the synagogue is—Bet Tefillah, a house of prayer. . . the
means by which a people worships God. Calling the synagogue a temple was a
bold political act. The word “temple” had traditionally been reserved for the Temple in Jerusalem, the Bet Mikdash,
the holy site where God was served with animal offerings. The synagogue, or the Bet Tefillah—the house of prayer
was where we offered words while waiting for the Temple in Jerusalem to be rebuilt. The early Reform Jews called
their houses of prayers temples as a way of declaring they no longer looked forward to a restored Temple. Berlin and
Paris, New York and Cincinnati were their new Jerusalems. Like so many political gestures which were once shocking¬
ly bold, using the word temple to designate a house of worship has become commonplace and even some Orthodox
congregations call their buildings temples.
Another word that was used was Bet Midrash—a house for the study of Torah. This reflects the fact that in various
periods of Jewish history the study aspect predominated in the designation of what a synagogue was all about. An expres¬
sion of this is the way our parents would call a synagogue—a shul which was the simple Yiddish word for school. The
synagogue was a place, not only for children but for adults. One learned from the Torah reading that a commentary is
to be consulted, from the books that lined the bookcases along the wall, from the many gatherings which would take place
where Jews taught other Jews the Torah, Mishnah, Talmud and other classic Jewish texts. It emphasized the important
component of Jewish education and the study of Torah as essential to Jewish life.
In the twentieth century the synagogue center was born so that social, recreational, communal and education elements
might be enjoyed by members of die congregation in one setting.
Today the synagogue fulfills all of the above functions (as it probably always did)—a gathering place, a holy site and
a place of learning. Certainly our own Jacksonville Jewish Center fulfills these functions to the benefit of our congregation
and community. That is one of the reasons that I think our Center exists; so that families and individuals could come
together and do together those things that no Jew and Jewish family should ever be left to have to do alone. At our services
and in our classes; in our lifecycle celebrations and in our gatherings we recognize that Judaism was not meant to be
observed by individuals or by individual families. It was fashioned for communities of people. Shabbatot and holy days
celebrated by yourself are inevitably empty days. Shabbot and Yomtov celebrated with others magically transforms us
from individual Jews to a Jewish community.
I think that the synagogue represents something in addition to the above. Whatever the impulse, experience has shown
that without the synagogue, the touchstone of Jewish life, all other activities in which the Jewish community engages become
trivial. Yes, there are institutions that serve the fundamental values of the Jewish people... concern for one another, reverence
for the elderly, etc. But it is the synagogue which directs us to these values and urges us to further those institutions that
make Jewish life and Jewish values possible.
We also come to the synagogue to probe our weakness and strength, to consider the gap between what we practice
and what we profess. We come to the synagogue to quiet the turbulence of our heart; to engage in self-renewal and regenera¬
tion, to make sure that we remain sensitive and conscioius of the source of all being which we call God. We are enriched
by our connections to the core of our collective life... the synagogue and we become impoverished in its neglect. What
unites us in our common history, in our common culture and in our common faith and hope is the synagogue. Without
holiness, without prayer, without study and without the great moment of Sabbath and holidays we would become a faint
image of the community and people we were intended to be.
As we begin a new year may we be inspired to find ourselves doing those things the synagogue represents—in our gathering
and in our study, in our worship and in our celebration. May we never forget the synagogue’s purpose so that we become,
as is described in the book of Exodus,—“a kingdom of priests and a holy nation.”
From our family to yours a healthy, fulfilling Shanah Tovah—a year of blessing, health, joy and peace. Amen.
Rabbi David and
Inge Gaffney

Junior Congregation is modeled after the main con¬
gregation. It is an organization of students grades 3-7
who attend our Afternoon Hebrew School and Day
School. These services allow our youth to participate,
leads, and apply knowledge and skills gained through
Hebrew training. Students earn points for attendance
and are rewarded with Sunday field trips.
Relaxing in the sun
The Sunday School of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
is planned expressly for children in kindergarten, first
and second grades. A two-hour program gives each child
the opportunity to experience the richness of his/her
Jewish heritage through song, dance, role playing, cook¬
ing, short stories, games, arts and crafts. Emphasis is
placed on the holidays and festivals; Jewish values and
mitzvot are encouraged.

students, and administrators to provide enrichment for the

magazine sale, candy sale, and book fairs), along with your

• Message from Education and Youth Director
Dear Friends:
The Education and Youth Departments of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center look forward with great anticipation to the year 1992/93. Our goals
are not only to bring Jewish education to our children but to totally in¬
volve our children and their families in Jewish life.
Our children gain a feeling of security from their Jewish education,
because it fills an important psychological need—the need of community
and belonging to a people. The need for security and activity confirms
the importance of Jewish schools with rich and varied programs. Pro¬
grams administered by caring, creative teachers and administrators who
are thoroughly aware of making Jewish education realistic and enjoyable.
Our children have the opportunity to participate in a wide variety of
activities such as dramatization, arts and crafts, singing, dancing, discus¬
sion, debating, journalism, writing and the reading of meaningful material.
Education can be enjoyable and worthy of respect.
Our commitment is to bridge the gap between the school and the home.
By involving our families in the early stages, we can have a positive ef¬
fect on Jewish home practices. The programs we have planned will help
to strengthen our commitment to our synagogue and to Jewish life as a whole.
I pray that all the children who graduate from our schools and programs will look back one day and be proud to
say: “I was a part of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and it made a difference in my Jewish upbringing.”
I wish you and yours a Happy and Healthy New Year.
Sincerely,
Rabbi Dov Kentof

• Summer Youth Programs
CAMP RAMAH
Camp Ramah provides the opportunity of a total Jewish living
experience for children and teenagers in the relaxed atmosphere of
a summer camp. Values built on a shared experience and emotion
reach our sons and daughters in a manner impossible in a formal
school program. Over the years, RAMAH has helped instill a deeper
commitment to Judaism in many of its participants.
The Center believes that benefits accrue to the congregation, not
just to the individual. We have therefore made monies available to
reduce the financial cost of qualified students regardless of need.
These monies are not part of the regular Center budget, but rather are raised by donations to the Myra Thier Lazarus
Ramah Fund, and Sisterhood, and matching funds from Jewish Federation.
Two families have helped us greatly. The Gordon Family has established a scholarship fund in memory of the beloved
Robert J. Gordon. A long list of deserving boys and girls have been able to go to Ramah because of this scholar¬
ship. Abe Selber established in his will a perpetual trust for specific Jewish education purposes. With the full support
of his children, Iris and Franklin, who have never lost interest in the Center, the proceeds of the Abe Selber Trust
have been used to send campers to Ramah.
Our appreciation to Horty Gendzier, who has worked so diligently to coordinate this very worthwhile program.

During the course of the festive meal on Rosh Hashanah evening, pieces of apple that have
been dipped in honey are distributed to the participants and the following is read, either
in unison or responsively:
For centuries, Jews have celebrated Rosh Hashanah by eating apples and honey. We hope that
this new year ahead may be especially sweet for ourselves, our loved ones and all the Jewish
people around the world.
Seated at the festive table, we remember our precious heritage. We understand that the new year
is a sacred time for reflection, as we pray for guidance to meet the challenges which are ours.
As the year 5753 begins, extraordinary events are unfolding all around us.
A measure of peace may soon be on the horizon for the State of Israel. May it bring both security
and vitality for Israel and its people.
We are witnessing the ongoing emigration of thousands of Jews from the former Soviet Union
who have been isolated from the mainstream of the Jewish world for decades. May they find
empathy and caring, as they establish new homes in Israel.
We are witnessing the continuing redemption of Ethiopian Jews in Israel. May their memories
of famine and danger fade, as they experience the joy of reunification with their families.
We are witnessing the possibilities for an easier future for Jews long trapped in Syria and other
lands of oppression.
We are witnessing, through Operation Exodus, an outpouring of compassion and generosity for
Jews in need. May we be thankful for our religious freedom and the ability and opportunity
to extend help to others.
Now, on this Rosh Hashanah, as we once again dip our apples into honey, we recite the age-
old words of our tradition. May we pledge to ourselves that this year we will do even more
to reach out to Jews in distress, wherever they may be.

The cover of the yearbook
comes from an illuminated
manuscript of the “Rothschild
Mahzor” from Florence, Italy. It
is dated 1492 and it is found in
the rare book room of the
library in the Jewish Theological
Seminary of America. The
prayer, found on this highly
decorated page, begins with the
words - “Shomea Tefila.” The
first words of the prayer read -
And Thou who hearest prayers,
unto Thee all flesh shall come.
They shall come and worship
before Thee Oh Lord and do
honor to Thy name.
O Come let us worship and let
us bend a knee before the Lord
our Maker.
Let us enter into His gates with
thanksgiving and into His
courts with praise.
We have selected this cover
not only to indicate the sense
of reverence that Jews of an¬
cient times had for their liturgy
and for their holiday; not only
to illustrate the passion and
talent with which a Jew ad¬
dressed his or her tradition, but
also to indicate that we are
reciting this very prayer at ser¬
vices at the Jewish Center ex¬
actly five hundred years after it
was recited from the Rothschild
Mahzor.
This should bring us a sense
of our history; a feeling of the
landscape of many com¬
munities from which we are
derived but also a sense of the
eternity of the Jewish tradition.

depends on the commitment of our youth today.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center sponsors three
outstanding youth groups for youngsters in grades
5-12. Each group meets regularly and enjoys a pro¬

sense of pride and an identification with Judaism
are instilled. Jewish values, attitude and behavior
patterns are discussed and carried out through
various Mitzvah programs and study.

Willard Kennedy, Principal
Evelyn Borstein

We of the Solomon Schechter Day School of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center welcome a new year with the largest enrollment in our thirty-one
year history. The school now has double classes for three Primary Grades,
and is utilizing every square foot of educational space in the building.
To be a Schechter student is to be a responsible, contributing member
of both the Jewish community and the community at large. By becoming
envolved in community projects students learn the true meaning of tzedakah.
Our school put this concept into practice this year by raising $1,200 for
the Dreams Come True Program. Two children with life-threatening illness
were given the chance to see their “dream come true” by the efforts of our
students. We are the only school large or small to sponsor two children
at the same time.
There is the freedom at Schechter to explore new ideas in a secure set¬
ting. At every level, we strive to challenge students to reason objectively
as they expand their view of the world. Our students read more books than
any other school, public or private, in Jacksonville during a Read-A-Thon.
We are dedicated to providing the best possible education for each child.
Small classes and experienced teachers ensure that students receive per¬
sonal attention. Specialists in music, art, computer studies, physical educa¬
tion and Hebrew complete our professional staff. Teachers and administrator
meet to discuss individual needs, write and evaluate curriculum, and deter¬
mine the best use of resources.
Parents and families, too, make important contributions to the Schechter
School community — as active members of the PTA and by participating
in Jewish celebrations with their children. This year over; 600 new titles
were added to our library by our parents.
For all these possibilities made real, we thank the members of the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center for their continued support and encouragement.

• Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14 had another banner year in 1991-92. The Troop,
founded in 1919, is nearing in its diamond anniversary of serv¬
ing the youth of our community.
The Troop activities featured camping and outdoor activities,
interesting weekly programs, service to the congregation and
community, and dedication to achievement and the ideals of
scouting.
A highlight of the year was the presentation of the coveted
Eagle Scout award to our Senior Patrol Leader, Adam Dean.
The rank of Eagle signifies excellence in character and achieve¬
ment. The Eagle presentation to Adam Dean was made by the
Honorable Henry L. Adams, Jr., Circuit Judge at the annual
Blue and Gold Banquet before a few hundred proud onlookers,
including his parents Nelson and Linda. Troop 14 has the
distinguished honor of producing seven Eagle Scouts in the past
several years.
Troop 14 explored new arenas of excitement and revisited
others this year. The Troop returned to the Boy Scout High
Adventure Sea Base in the Florida Keys after a two-year hiatus.
There, along with scouts from throughout the world, our scouts
snorkeled in the crystal clear ocean on coral reefs abounding
with colorful sea life and ominous sharks, barracuda and rays.
The scouts also enjoyed boating, small boat sailing, and fishing,
and much more at this getaway paradise.
The Troop inaugurated the Fort Matanzas Historic Trail Hike
following in the path of Florida’s Spanish and French settlers
and studying this area’s colorful history and breathtaking land¬
scape. The hike was a departure from more rugged and lengthy
historic trail hikes which the Troop has bravely encountered in
recent years. The “hike” featured a boat ride across the Matan¬
za River to a 16th century coquino fortress. The scouts, along
with the Webloes, qualified by their efforts for local and national
Historic Trail Awards sponsored by the Florida Historic Trails
Society and the Boy Scouts of America.
Other outdoor adventures this year included: camping at the
Osceola National Forest where the Troop joined thousands of
onlooker for the annual reenactment of the Battle of Olustee,
the largest Civil War conflict in Florida; a visit to Gainesville,
creative display and the Admiral’s Camping Award, and a year
end campout at Fort White puncturated by a tubing trip down
the crystal-clear Itchetucknee River and an evening in a rustic
“game room” where the boys played pool and video games to
near exhaustion.
Program highlights this year included a CPR course taught
by fireman, Steve Gerbert; a creative meet court in which scouts
served as attorneys, witnesses, judge and jurors; a cleverly con¬
trived and well produced “The Last Bunny Scout” skit at the
Blue and Gold Banquet which bore no resemblance to the re¬
cent movie bearing a similar name; and another memorable
Scout Sabbath in which the boys joined Cubs, Girl Scouts, and
Brownie’s affiliated with the Center in leading services.
The scouts also followed their slogan, “do a good turn daily.”
Troop 14 served lunch to the residents of Mount Carmel
Gardens on Thanksgiving Day as has become an annual tradi¬
tion. The Scouts also constructed picnic tables to be used in
the Sukkah at the Center, under the planning and direction of
Adam Dean.
The Troop 14 scouts who attended summer camp at Camp
La-Noche in the Ocala National Forest included Bernie Echt,
Philip Echt, Greg Daks, Adam Dean, Larry Fiske, Ian Fiske,
Shai Smargon, Brian Usoff, Nick Morcom, Jeff Glassman, Eli
Fleet and Randy Echalico. Also this summer, Kenny Solomon
became Troop 14’s first ever attendee at the highly acclaimed
Brownsea leadership training encampment.
Troop 14 is for boys grades 6 (or age 11) through 12. Those
interested may contact Randy Silverberg at 398-0038 (w) or
733-6649 (h) or Harry Peltz at 737-0309.

• Hevra Kaddisha
For almost two thousand years, Judaism has
cared to treat the deceased with as much digni¬
ty as one would have in life. Burial societies ex¬
ist in communities to ensure that standards of
loving-kindness and dignity are maintained for all.
While Jacksonville has a Hevra Kaddisha,
if s membership is smaller than is desired. Addi¬
tional members would enable us to enhance
dignity after death even more than has been
available in the past.
Some of the ways a member may serve are:
(1) Tahara, the traditional washing, shrouding,
and placing of the body in the coffin; (2) making
arrangements for the Tahara; (3) being a
Shomer, who watches the body until the
funeral; (4) transportation for the Shomer.
Being part of the Chevra Kaddisha means
performing a Mitzvah for another Jew who can
never thank you. This is the greatest Mitzvah
of all. Please contact your Rabbi, or Nathan
Sandler, President of the “Chevra” if you wish
to take part.
• Center Cemetery
The Center owns and operates two cemeteries, both
of which are adjacent to the Evergreen Cemetery in
North Jacksonville. Both are beautiful pieces of proper¬
ty with large shade trees and mature landscaping. The
original cemetery is referred to simply as the “Old
Cemetery." The new cemetery is known as the Center
Memorial Park. The Center provides complete main-
tainence of these grounds. Both cemeteries are open
daylight hours every day except Shabbat and Jewish
Holidays. A memorial service is conducted each year
on the Sunday between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kip-
pur at each of the cemeteries.
Just as with other life cycle events at the Center, burial
and mourning are also reserved for Center members.
Therefore, only members may purchase a plot in one
of the Center cemeteries. Associate members are not
eligible to purchase a plot. For more information regard¬
ing the purchase of cemetery plots, please call the Center
administrative office.

President’s Message
Some people say that the difference between
a positive person and a negative person is
epitomized by the old story of two thirsty men
who see an eight ounce glass with four ounces
of water in it. The man with the positive outlook
will say that the cup is half full, and be thankful
for what’s available. The other man will
acknowledge that there is water in the glass but
he cannot be happy without a full glass because
he sees the glass as half empty. While their
opinions and attitudes might differ, both men
are smart enough, however, to appreciate the
value of water itself, and realize that the glass holds, or is capable of holding, a precious
Commodity.
It is, fortunately, a common occurrence for me to receive praise about our con¬
gregations. It could be about the friendliness of our congregation, our schools, our
professional leadership, our facility, or about any number of Center activities. On
the other hand, I sometimes receive negative comments about one thing or another,
too. But whatever the comments, I welcome them. Concern about the Synagogue,
whether expressed positively or negatively, reflects an appreciation for the value and
quality of our institution. Some will be thankful for what is available. Others will want
something more or different. But we can all agree that what we have, and what we
have the potential for doing, is a critical commodity that is crucial to the survival
of Judaism, as we know it, in our community.
Jeffrey B. Morris

• Brownie Troop 882
Brownie Troop 882 had a terrific year in 1991-92. The troop is part of
the Lakewood-Mandarin-San Jose neighborhood of Gateway Girl Scout
Council. There are over 1000 girls in scouting in this neighborhood alone.
Troop 882 was privileged to have 20 girls in grades 1-3.
The Brownies had many memorable experiences. The troop visited a farm
in the fall where they saw turkeys, cows, a bull, a new baby calf and chickens.
The girls learned to collect eggs from the chicken coop, select the best pecans,
and try their best at old-fashioned sack races. A bumpy hayride completed
our Farm Day!
For many of us, camping at Chowenwaw in Green Cove Springs was our
first camping experience. Services were held at campsite as the Sabbath
was observed in a beautiful woodland setting. The troop won first place for
camp decorations with a Japanese theme. They learned a song in Japanese
and sang it in front of other troops at a sing-along. Tiny felt flags were shared
with new Brownie friends.
Troop 882 also visited Marineland and made every effort to get wet.
Regular weekly meetings resulted in earning “try-its,” official recognitions for
indoor and outdoor activities.
The third graders in the troop visited Savannah and the birthplace of
Juliette Gordon Low who founded Girl Scouting in 1912. They learned that
Juliette painted and sculpted and saw evidence of her work throughout the
house. Juliette began Girl Scouting for her niece. Her nickname was Daisy
which became the name used for kindergarten level Girl Scouts.
Troop 882 will continue in the fall. New leaders for 1992-93 are Beth
Shorstein and Linda Schuster. Third graders will now have a Junior troop
to bridge to with Jan Lipsky as leader.
Troop 882 thanks the Jacksonville Jewish Center and Mrs. Goldie Port¬
noy for a marvelous facility and grounds for their activities.
If interested in Girl Scouting from kindergarten to 12th grade, please con¬
tact the Gateway Girl Scout Center at 388-4653. For Center Brownies -
Beth Shorstein and Center Juniors - Jan Lipsky.

Some people who are reading this were beaten yesterday, or terrorized, or kept in isolation.
Some who tormented them are reading this now.
And they are not strangers to each other; they are family. Intimates. People like us. Us.
Home should be a haven, the place where you can count on being valued and protected.
If instead it is a place where the people closest to you beat you up, or keep you on edge with threats,
or isolate and demean you-then what is safe?
Violence in the family is not love; it is not discipline; it is not deserved.
It is an abuse of power, and it is wrong-
because decent people don’t behave that way;
because it is against the law, and for one more reason:
we are all made in the image of God.
To lash out in violence-
especially against someone whose life is linked with yours-
is to violate a likeness of God, and to degrade that likeness in yourself.
Are you being hurt or humiliated by the person you are closest to?
Believe that you do not deserve the abuse.
No one has the right to tell you that you are worthless: your worth comes from God.
Have you been taking out your anger and frustration against the people who depend on you?
Know that you are better than that; you are made in the image of God. You have the power
to stop hurting and belittling them. God gives it to you.
To all who read this, we ask:
I Look at yourself, at your partner, at your elderly parents, at your children, as images of God. Treat each of them with
the respect which that demands.
H Make your homes a haven. Instead of raising your hand or your voice, raise your own dignity and the self-esteem of
the people who turn to you for love. You may not be able to perfect the world, but this much you can do.
H Help your religious community to face the fact of domestic violence and to offer active support to those who have
been enduring abuse, threats, and humiliation. A house of God should be a place for teaching restraint, decency,
and reverence; make yours that place.
■ Behave as though God made you worthy; it is true. Behave as though the world depends on your humanity
and your decency. It does.

The greatest challenge of directing our agency this year was grappling with the ongoing problem of powerful
contradictions. Out there, in the community we are committed to serving, problems are mounting at in¬
credibly fast rates. Both the long and short term impact of divorce is affecting nearly half of all married
families. Alcohol and drug abuse are being recognized as serious problems in the Jewish community. AIDS
is affecting everyone. Child abuse and wife battering are finally, if reluctantly, being recognized as pro¬
blems in the Jewish community. The cry for quality day care is loud and clear. The urgent need for more
programs to enable our frail elderly population to live independently is growing as fast as that segment
of our community is increasing. Recent changes in the politics of the Soviet Union have made Russian
Resettlement a top priority, as hundreds of refugee families land at Jacksonville International Airport with
high hopes and great expectations. We were told that 20% of Jewish couples are infertile, yet the number
of birth mothers considering releasing their babies for adoption has dwindled.
My staff, a remarkable group, has shown commendable strength and much-appreciated professionalism
during these extraordinary times. As someone recently quipped, “This is surely a growth industry.”
The hard part is that unlike other “growth industries,” as we do more “business,” we do not necessarily
have more income, which would enable us to hire more staff to continue to “do more business.” In fact,
often the opposite is true. Increased demands on our system more often contribute to our deficit than
to our surplus. While we happily can report increases in our fee income over the last three years, families
that face multiple problems, need long-term care and require many hours of professional attention, are
often people who must also be subsidized. And we won’t ever turn them away.
On the brighter side, I believe the Jewish community is very fortunate. We have had, for many decades,
a solid structure in place that is committed to taking care of our own. As families are less able to care
for themselves and support in both emotional and financial realms must come from outside, we are there
ready and able to help. This is our mission. We are committed, whatever the obstacles, to relieving suffer¬
ing and help people achieve meaningful personal lives. Despite the disappointing cuts in government fund¬
ing, the strain on available local resources, and the continuing increase in calls for new and innovative
programs, we persevere because it is the right thing to do. With appreciation to our President and our
Board of Directors, my staff and Friends of the JFCS, I look forward to the challenging tasks before us
in the years ahead. Thank you for your continuing confidence and support.
Iris T. Young
