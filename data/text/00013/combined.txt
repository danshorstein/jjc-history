Girl Scout Troop 29 and Brownie Troops 25, 197 and 212
completed another most successful year under the capable leadership
of Mrs. Irving Kramer, Chairman of the Center Sisterhood Girl
Scout Committee. These troops participated in many civic, religious
and outdoor activities. Trips to various nearby places of interest were
taken. Outdoor cooking, hiking and overnight camping were made
possible through the proceeds of the annual Cookie Sale.
Favors and napkins were made for the trays at the County
Hospital. Clothes were collected and sent to Israel and also to
Brewster and County Hospitals. The Girl Scouts took part in a
city-wide art exhibit. All troops participated in Girl Scout Sab¬
bath and Boy Scout Sabbath Services, Thinking Day and Court of
Awards. This year’s arts and crafts program included the making
of aprons, bun warmers, pot holders, wire-craft, chalk drawing and
flower planters. Corsages for Mother’s Day were also made.
The annual luncheon climaxed the most successful year. Mrs.
Charles Norton was the guest speaker and Miss B. Wimpee, Mrs. B.
Y. Abel and Rabbi Tofield were among the guests present. Mothers
of Girl Scouts and Brownies, under the direction of Mrs. Kramer
and Mrs. Robert L. Block were in charge of the luncheon.

WHAT IS THE JEWISH COMMUNITY COUN¬
CIL? The Jewish Community Council is a federation of
all Jewish organizations in Jacksonville. As such, it is the
spokesman for the Jewish community and has the respon¬
sibility for carrying on activities which benefit the com¬
munity as a whole.
HOW DOES IT REPRESENT THE JEWS OF
]ACKSONVILLE? Every Jewish organization elects or
appoints its own representative to the Governing Boards,
one for each one hundred members or major fraction
thereof. These representatives then elect the officers for
their two-year term of office.
WHAT DOES THE COUNCIL DO?
1. The Council conducts the annual United Cam¬
paign. From the funds raised, some 40 agencies—local,
such as River Garden; National, including hospitals, re¬
ligious institutions, civic protective groups, etc.; and Over¬
seas, including United Jewish Appeal, etc.—receive money
and each is helped to carry on its work. Over one and a
half million dollars have been raised in the past six years
and distributed to these causes.
2. Refugee Committee. The Council brought to Jack¬
sonville some 60 individuals, representing 24 refugee fam¬
ilies and helped them to the point where all are now eco¬
nomically self-sustaining. Jobs, housing, clothing, medical
supplies, even furniture and other needs were met. Dur¬
ing the period, these families were becoming part of our
American community. A. H. Rothstein served as chair¬
man of this committee.

ish community. Richard J. Lewinson is chairman of this

THE JEWISH RELIGIOUS CALENDAR
EXPLANATORY NOTES
The Jewish Religious Calendar is the result of many stages of
development. In ancient times the unit of time was the month,
determined by the phases of the moon. The Hebrew Calendar was
thus lunar. However, since religious Festivals are observed annual¬
ly, the calendar soon became related also to the solar system. The
lunar month consists of 291/2 days. In seven of a 19 year cycle an
extra month is added so that the religious occasions that are ob¬
served on the same date of the lunar month will occur in proper
season, which is determined by the solar calendar. Listed below are
the months in the Jewish Religious Calendar with their corres¬
ponding dates in the general calendar.
Tishri September-October
Heshvan October-November
Kislev November-December
Tebet December-January
Shebat January-February
Adar February-March
Nisan March-April
Iyar April-May
Sivan May-June
Tammuz June-July-
Ab July-August
Elul August-September
GENERAL NOTES
In this year’s edition of the Year Book the Calendar is printed
in a new format. The month’s run according to the Civil Calendar
with the Hebrew date appearing in the lower left corner.
On Fridays and on the eve of Festivals, as well as on the
first day of Festivals, there appears the symbol, L.C. "Light
Candles.”
Rosh Hodesh, Beginning of the Month, is observed with
special prayers in the service for that day and with the Blessing
of the New Month on the Sabbath previous. When the new
moon appears in the middle of the day the outgoing month con¬
sists of 30 days and the last day is considered the first day Rosh
Hodesh for the coming month.
The Sidrah, the portion of the Torah read on Saturday,
morning, is indicated. Special Sabbaths will be explained below
under the month in which they occur.
TISHRI
Rosh Hashanah, Jewish Religious New Year.
Fast of Gedalia, commemoratets the assassination of the gov¬
ernor of Judea who attempted to restore Jewish independence
after the destruction of the First Temple by the Babylonians.
Shahbat Shuva, Sabbath of Repentance, derives its name from
the Prophetic reading, "Shuva Yisrael,” "Return, O Israel.”
Yom Kippur, Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and
prayer, a day for confession of sin and supplication for forgive¬
ness. "For transgressions between man and God, repentance on
Yom Kippur brings atonement. For transgressions between man
and man, Yom Kippur brings no atonement until amends are
made.”
Sukkot, Tabernacles, commemoratets the period when our
forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings
through the wilderness of Sinai.
Hoi Hamoed, Intermediate Days of the Holiday, are observed
as semi-festivals.
Hashannah Rahha, the day on which prayers with the refrain
Hosanah ("O Save Us”) is recited.
Shemini Atzeret, Eighth Day of Assembly, marks the conclu¬
sion of the fall fetstivals.
Simhat Torah, Torah Festival, is the second day of Shemini
Atzeret and celebrates the completion of the annual reading cycle
from the Five Books of Moses and the beginning of readings for
the New Year.
KISLEV
Hanukah, Feast of Dedication, commemorates the rededica¬
tion of the Temple by the Maccabees in 163 B.C.E. The Mac-
cabean triumph over Syrian forces was the first victory for a
people’s right to the freedom of worship. Lights are kindled in
the synagogue and in the home on every one of the eight evenings.
144
TEBET
Asar ah BeTebet, Fast of the 10th of Tebet, commemorates the
betginning of the siege of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in 586
B.C.E. which led to the destruction of the first Temple.
SHEBAT
Hamisha Asar BeShebat, also known as Tu BeShebat, 15th
of Shebat, is Holy Land Arbor Day or New Year of the Trees.
Shabbat Shekalim, the Sabbath when every adult Jew in the
days of the Temple was reminded to pay his poll tax of a half
a shekel on the first day of Adar.
ADAR
Shabbat Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of
Purim a special portion of the Torah is read beginning with the
word "Zakhor”—"Remember Amaelk.” Haman is presumed to
have been an Amalekite.
Purim, commemorates the day w7hen, through the intervention
of Mordecai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly
escaped being victims of Haman’s plot to exterminate them.
Shabbat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning the
ashes of the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual purifica¬
tion by our ancestors in preparing for the Passover Festival.
Shabbat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read,
emphasizing the significance of the month of Nisan in which
Israel’s great deliverance occured.
NISAN
Shabbat Hagadol, the Sabbath which precedes the great Festi¬
val of Passover, derives its name from the Haftorah which con¬
tains a reference to the "Great Day of the Lord.”
Pesah, Passover, celebrates Israel’s deliverance from Egyptian
bondage. The festival lasts eight days during which matzot are
eaten in the place of leavened bread. The celebration of the
Passover with the beautiful ceremony of the Seder on the first
and second evenings has fostered by Jewish people the ideals of
liberty and freedom.
IYAR
Yom Ha’atzma’ut. The new State of Israel declared its in¬
dependence on May 14, 1948, which was the 5th of Iyar accord¬
ing to the Hebrew calendar.
Lag B’ Omer, the date of a victory by Bar Kokhba in the final
Jewish struggle against the Romans. He was supported by the
sage Rabbi Akiba and his disciples. It is known as "The Scholars
Festival” and marked by field day outings.
SIVAN
Shavuot, Feast of Weeks, completes 7 weeks from the second
day of Passover on which the Omer (a measure) of the new
barley was brought to the Temple. It was also the day when
Bikkurim (first fruits) were presented. Tradition assigned to
Shavuot commemoration of the Covenant established at Sinai be¬
tween God and Israel and the giving of the Ten Commandments.
TAMMUZ
Shivah Asar Be’Tamuz, Fast of the 17th of Tammuz, that
commemorates the breach in the walls of Jerutsalem by the Baby¬
lonians in the year 586 B.C.E.
Ab
Shabbat Hazon, precedes Tisha B’Ab derives its name from
the first words of the Haftorah "Hazon”—the vision of Isaiah,
which foretells the destruction of Jerusalem.
Tisha B’Ab, the Ninth Day of Ab, is a fast day which marks
the destruction of the First Temple in Jerusalem by the Babylon¬
ians (585 B.C.E.) and also of the Second Temple by the Romans
(70 C.E.)
Shabbat Nahamu, Sabbath of Consolation; the first word of the
Haftorah is "Nahamu, nahamu, Comfort ye, My People.”
ELUL
Selihot, Penitential Prayers, recited at midnight in preparation
for the holiday season, "The Great Days of Awe.”

Young Judaea has in Jacksonville 7 Clubs for boys and girls from
the ages of 9 through 16. The Senior Clubs meet weekly on Wednesday
evenings at 7:30 o’clock, either in the Center or at homes of members;
Junior Clubs meet in the Center every other Sunday afternoon at 2 P.M.
All activities center around the four-fold program, which consists
of: Jewish living, group activities, American affairs and Israel and Zion¬
ism.
Outstanding affairs during the past year consisted of: Sabbath Serv¬
ices sponsored by Judaea at the Jewish Center during December; Senior
Conclave in Daytona Beach in December; Program for joint Zionist and
Hadassah meeting at the Temple in January; Israeli Fair to raise funds
for the Tsofim held at River Garden Home in April; and concluded with
the Bikkurim Festival in the Jewish Center, which climaxed the year of
working on the Point System with a banquet honoring Judaean mem¬
bers.
Camp Judaea for 10-12 year olds, was sponsored for the first time
this year at Blue Star in Hendersonville, N.C. Also held there in June
was Camp and Convention for Senior Judaeans and a Leaders’ Institute.
Jacksonville sent a delegation to the National Judaean Camp, Tel Yehudah
in Barryville, N.Y. during August and also one delegate to Israel on the
Summer-In-Israel program.
Young Judaea is sponsored jointly by Hadassah and the Zionist Or¬
ganization of America, under the direction of the local Youth Commis¬
sion, headed by Dorothy Marx, Chairman, and Mrs. Irving Brukner, Di¬
rector. Young Judaea is a member of the Jacksonville Jewish Community
Council.

The National Council of Jewish Women, organized
in 1893, is one of the oldest women’s organizations in
America. It now has 250 Sections throughout the country
with an enrollment of over 100,000 members. Council, in
the spirit of Judaism, is dedicated to furthering human
welfare in the Jewish and general communities, locally,
nationally, and internationally. Through an integrated
program of education, service, and social action, it edu¬
cates the individual and the community toward the demo¬
cratic way of life.
Council’s overseas projects are threefold: the support
of the School of Education of Hebrew University in Jeru¬
salem; maintenance of graduate scholarships to students
from Israel and European countries for study in American
universities; shipments of clothing, play and study mater¬
ial to Jewish orphanages in the Middle East.
Local services include Council’s Medical Loan Shelf,
Golden Age Club, sewing for the less fortunate, Service
to Foreign Born, and parties for hospitalized servicemen
at the Naval Air Station.

The Lt. Meyer Leibovitz Post 199, Jewish War Veter¬
ans, was organized in Jacksonville, in 1939 and was
known at that time as Jacksonville Post 199. Its name
was changed to Lt. Meyer Leibovitz Post in 1946, after
Lt. Meyer Leibovitz, of this city, who gave his all for his
country.
The Post has always followed a course of giving serv¬
ice to our fellow-men, regardless of race, creed, or color,
service to our country and all its citizens and service to
the cause of peace, of brotherhood, and of humanity.
Many people ask why join the Jewish War Veterans:
One answer amongst many is, that JWV’s speak out
against bigotry, prejudice and totalitarianism; it devotes
its principal energies to positive, creative efforts for the
preservation and advancement of the freedoms of oppor¬
tunities that are vital to the security and well being of
America, and the Jewish people. It has demonstrated its
capacity for useful service to the Country and to Jewry
since its formation by Jewish Veterans of the Civil War,
fifty-eight years ago; Its members understand the needs
of service men, and veterans and are best situated to de¬
tect minority inequalties therein.
Jews have fought, bled and died in every war of our
republic. We seek no special pleading for ourselves as
veterans; However, when needed by our fellow Jews, we
are available, for we have earned the right to fight on
their behalf to preserve our constitutional privileges as
Americans, and JWV Posts have so done; Nor must we
ever forget our obligation to fight for these same privil¬
eges for non-Jews as well. JWV gives its members an
opportunity for all, young or old, to train in its ranks in
the ever rising echelons for useful citizenship and leader¬
ship on local and national levels, to watch and help where
needed in man’s fight against bigotry.
The above principles are the aims and principles of
your JWV Post, here, and our course of action is directed
towards those ends.

President And Chairman Of The Board
ROBERT J. GORDON, CHAIRMAN OF THE BOARD
In the name of the officers and members of the Board, I
extend greetings to all of our members with the traditional bles¬
sing for a Happy New Year, Le Shariah Tovah Tiketevu.
"May we be privileged to achieve in 5714 the goals for a
greater Synagogue that will ever bring us profound inspiration."
The above is an excerpt from last year’s greetings to our
congregation from your president, Philip Selber.
The response from our membership during the year was very
inspiring and far exceeded the fondest hopes of our president.
We invite you to take a "Cooks Tour" of the Synagogue at
your earliest convenience. You will be agreeably surprised and
delighted, its quite possible you might also be profoundly in¬
spired.
The progress made thus far represents the expenditure of all
funds contributed to date. When additional funds are available
we can proceed to finish the job in very short order.
We want each and every member of our congregation to
share in the personal joy and pride of having contributed to¬
ward the beautification of your Synagogue. During the coming
year you will be given an opportunity to participate. Should
we inadvertently overlook you, please be advised we will accept
unsolicited, voluntary contributions at any time. There are ab¬
solutely no restrictions to this liberal and unprecedented offer.
To those of our members who have not as yet paid their
pledges, I respectfully submit the following New Year’s resolu¬
tion for their careful consideration.
"I hereby pledge to pay my pledge promptly."
Our Synagogue has had many occasions on the beginning of
a New Year to picture the year ahead as "a critical one" or "a
year of destiny," but never has it had the occasion to stand on
the threshold of a year with greater opportunity or greater
promise than now.
The year 5715 should witness the crowning climax to a
building program which has now extended over some eleven
years. In the year ahead the congregation should dedicate its
new Ark and Pulpit, complete the Synagogue lobbies and install
the elevator, leaving only the renovation of the class rooms and
the first floor of the original building for future attention. With
the dedication of our new Synagogue the opportunity for the
spiritual rededication of our membership presents a real and
tremendous challenge.
In the field of Jewish education too, we find ourselves on
the threshold of a year to which we have long pointed. We
begin the school year in our Hebrew School, Daily Kindergarten,
and Sunday Religious School with the finest staff of teachers in
the entire history of this community. The ceremony of Bas
Mitzvah has come of age to the extent that the number of girls
participating this year will almost approximate the number of
boys who will become Bar Mitzvah.
We have now reached the vantage point in Jewish education
in Jacksonville, where for the first time we are given an even
chance for success in meeting the challenge to devise an educa¬
tional system capable of insuring a meaningful Jewish survival.
We cannot fail this opportunity.
May our congregation meet these challenges with the inspira¬
tion and the determination to acquit themselves well.
PHILIP SELBER, PRESIDENT

Aleph Zadik Aleph, the junior order of B’nai B’rith, in 1953-
54 had another banner year. It has fully carried out its Eive-Fold-
and-Full Program of religious, community service, cultural, social
and athletic activities.
A.Z.A. sponsored its annual Sabbath and held many inter¬
chapter programs on famous Jewish men and on the various re¬
ligious holidays. The main community service project was a Pesah
party at the Hebrew Home for the Aged. Refreshments were
served followed by a community sing and a few piano selections
by Mrs. Milton Dobrin. Debates, discussions, and oratory con¬
tests highlighted the chapter cultural program. Morris Rosen¬
zweig represented the local chapter at the regional convention in
oratory and discussion. Winning the discussion contest, he repre¬
sented the Florida Region at the District 5 convention in Hender¬
sonville, N. C.
Miss Linda Lovitz, outgoing chapter Sweetheart and reigning
Florida Regional Sweetheart, maintained the chapter motto of a
social-a-month. Included in the socials were a successful hayride,
beach party, and semi-formal dance. Inter-chapter bowling and
ping-pong tournaments as well as a 45-39 basketball victory over
the arch-rival Esquire Club highlighted a successful athletic pro¬
gram.
Last December A..ZA. played host to the annual Florida Re¬
gional Convention. Over 200 B’nai B’rith Youth attended. This
convention was acclaimed the greatest ever and was a credit to the
chairman, Frank Resnick. The local chapter won the state basket¬
ball championship and journeyed to Baltimore, Md., for the Dis¬
trict 5 playoffs only to lose in the semi-finals.
The 13th annual minstrel closed one of the most successful
years in A.Z.A.’s history. A portion of the proceeds was given to
the Leo N. Levi Arthritic and Rheumatic Hospital in Hot Springs,
Arkansas.
A.Z.A.’s success the past year can be credited to our wonder¬
ful advisor, David Weinstein, whose guidance inspired all of our
members. The entire chapter also wishes to thank the Jewish
Center, its staff, and the Jewish Community for its splendid co¬
operation and extends to everyone a Happy New Year.

B.B.G. is an organization for girls between the ages of 13
and 18. The new members become "Mits” (member in training)
until they are well enough acquainted with B’nai B’rith to become
members.
B.B.G.’s program is seven fold: cultural, religious, inter-faith,
community service, social activities, athletics, B’nai B’rith coopera¬
tion. Each member must take part in all phases of the program.
Community service will be one of our major projects this year.
B.B.G. is planning a toy lending library for under privileged
children, which will be operated from the Public Library on
Adams Street. Another phase of our program in which we plan
to be very active is inter-faith, inwhich we will work very closely
with the Anti-Defamation League. We hope this will help make
our community a better one in which to live.
B.B.G. is the sister organization of A.Z.A. and we work
closely together to help carry out our respective programs.
Girls entering our chapter will find friendship and good fel¬
lowship and have the assurance that wherever she goes she will
always find a B.B.G.

BERNARD GLOSSMAN
Scoutmaster
1954 has been a year of intensified activity in Scouting lore, prac¬
tices and skills, with the result that at mid-summer the membership of
Troop 14 is divided into the following categories: 2 Life Scouts, 9 Star
Scouts, 5 First Class Scouts, 1 Second Class Scout and 5 Tenderfoot
Scouts. Scoutmaster Glossman ventures the prediction that this year the
Troop will add one or more Eagles to their roster.
This year’s annual Scout Sabbath saw a Ner Tamid Award for
Reverence presented to Gene Shafton and Aleph Awards presented to
Herbert Fishier and Stephen Freedman. George Glossman received the
Ner Tamid Award during his Bar Mitzvah celebration on April 9th.
Troop 14 was represented by three patrols in the District Camporee
on St. Marys’ River and not only won three A ratings, but was adjudged
the best Troop at the Camporee.
The Troop’s part in the Scout Exposition consisted of a display of
the Merit Badge for Citizenship in the Community, for which it received
top honors. In order to assist the North Florida Council in publicizing
the Exposition, Scouts of Troop 14 made appearances at various civic
and social organizations and gave several radio and television broadcasts.
This year’s Charter Presentation was held jointly with the Cub Pack
and featured a reunion of all graduates of Troop 14. Award winners for
the Troop were as follows: Gene Shafton, Best Scout of the year; George
Glossman, Best Recruiter; and Eddie Robin, Highest personal advance¬
ment.
This year Troop 14 again enjoyed the use of the indoor swimming
pool at the Naval Air Station, under the expert tutelage of Mr. Frank
Woloszynowski. The Troop is greatly indebted to Mr. Woloszynowski,
not only for his excellent swimming instruction, but for character build¬
ing as well.
This year’s sojourn at Camp Eckokotee saw Ronnie Levin inducted
in the Order of the Arrow, National Society of Scouts and Explorers
Honor Campers.
For the first time in its history, Troop 14 had three representatives
on this year’s Philmont Expedition. If the enthusiastic and glowing re¬
ports that Gene Shafton, Mark Greenwald and Hebert Gordon brought
back are any criterion, this may well become an annual event.
In recounting the story of Troop 14 in this year 1954, we would be
very derelict in our duty to honesty and justice should we fail to recog¬
nize the services of the Troop Committee, under the resourceful and in¬
spiring leadership of the Chairman, Manny Goldstein, and the outstanding
services rendered the Troop by its Scoutmaster, Bernard Glossman.
The soil has been tilled, the seed has been planted and God willing,
before long, we shall have fruit.

Message delivered at the United Synagogue Convention,
Miami Beach, Fla., November 17, 1953
Friends:
I bring you felicitations from the Synagogue Council
of America, in behalf of its president, my friend and es¬
teemed colleague, Dr. Norman Salit. He is on on shelihut
shel mitzvah, on a sacred mission to European Jewry, a
mission in which, the United Synagogue of America shares
by its membership in the Council.
With these greetings the other two wings of Ameri¬
can Jewry pay their respects to Conservative Judaism for
the notable contribution it is making towards Jewishly in¬
spired living and to religious motivation on the American
scene generally.
The commendations I bring you are prompted by an
even profounder sense of appreciation. Gratified over its
own accomplishments, dedicated to its lofty purposes,
loyal to its distinctive principles, the United Synagogue
of America yet adds zeal and fervor to the Synagogue,
through cooperation with the other Synagogue federations
in many worthy and vital undertakings.
Timely insight comes to us in the imaginative light
cast by the Midrash on the Sidrah we read this past Sab¬
bath. The Rabbis credit Jacob with rounding out the pat¬
tern of Jewish daily devotion by offering a prayer "when
the sun was set." Moreover, it occured at the site where
the sanctuary at Jerusalem was destined to rise.
■ The Patriarch’s spiritual experience, his foresight and
intense aspiration for a thriving Judaism in the future,
actually molded the program of the Synagogue for all
time to come. Stones he arranged for his protection —
three in number, according to one opinion — he treated
symbolically. Should they remain intact, it would be a
sign that his mission in history is secure. But the stones
contended with one another: "Upon me shall the righte¬
ous one rest his head." Miraculously they were fused to¬
gether, and Jacob was able to consecrate one stone to
mark the spot of his vision and devotion.
What a parable for American Jewry of today!
Divergent views are inevitable; to a degree even de¬
sirable. In this connection it is fitting that we recall the
words of our recently lamented beloved teacher, Professor
Louis Ginzberg, Zekher Zadik Livrakha, with which he
summarized a lecture before the Harvard Divinity School:
"As each man’s emotions, needs and longings must differ,
so must each man’s religion differ. Each man lives his
own religion in his own way."
DR. SANDERS A. TOFIELD
Realities of American Jewish life, however, make it
imperative that the voice of the Synagogue be heard clear¬
ly and unmistakably — with the opinion, interests and
conviction of all directions in American Judaism. If it is
true that "two simultaneous voice cannot be heard," three
voices attempting to speak for Jewish religion at the same
place at the same time, will surely go unheeded.
The dignity and earnestness of the Jewish religious
message demands opportunity for unified expression. The
Synagogue Council of America offers at least a partial
answer to frequent contentions of individual motar and
stones, "Within me shall the righteous and pious find rest
and tranquility." In the halls of government, at the diplo¬
matic conference table, in the councils of other religious
bodies, the very ideals and objectives to which the United
Synagogue stands committed are given deserved emphasis
and illucidation by the Synagogue Council.
May each congregation within the United Synagogue
of America realize the spiritual goals of the Patriarch
Jacob; unitedly within its own fold and in the company
of the other religious fellowships, help humanity attain
the glorious vision:

Friends Of The Seminary
A Tribute To Professors Ginsberg And Marx
The late Professors Louis Ginzberg (left) and
Alexander Marx (right) examining a manuscript.
"One comes to the Seminary to associate with great men. In
the Library one finds great books but in the classroom one finds
great men." This statement made by Doctor Solomon Schecter in
1911 has been one of the underlying concepts of the Jewish
Theological Seminary through the years. As the citadel of Con¬
servative Judaism it has attracted scholars and students alike from
all parts of the world and has become one of the great intellect-/
ual centers of the Western Hemisphere. The Seminary has grad¬
uated rabbis and teachers who are learned in Judaism, its rich
heritage and traditions and concerned with the present-day appli¬
cations of Talmudic Law.
Like all other great institutions, the Seminary reflects the
creative genius of its leaders. Among these, in our time, Pro¬
fessor Louis Ginzberg and Professor Alexander Marx stood pre¬
eminent. Their passing during the course of this year, 5714,
brought grief into the lifes of all those who love Jewish tradi¬
tion and revere its great builders. In the fifty years that they
taught at the Jewish Theological Seminary they won world-wide
distinction as two of the greatest Judaica scholars of their time.
They were a rare union of T al mid Hakham, traditional sage and
scholar.
As one of the foremost authorities on the Talmud Doctor
Ginzberg was consulted by great scholars everywhere. It was in
a large measure due to his scholarship that a new dimension was
added to American Jewish life.
To his hundreds of students, colleagues and friends, Doctor
Marx was the epitome of the saintly scholar who combined wis¬
dom with righteousness. World renowned as the Director of
Libraries and Professor of History at the Seminary he succeeded
in building the largest and most valuable collection of books and
manuscripts on Jewish subjects in all of history. When Doctor
Marx was appointed Librarian in 1903, the entire Seminary Li¬
brary consisted of some 5,000 books and three manuscripts. To¬
day, the colection exceeds 155,000 volumes and 9,000 manu¬
scripts.
Professors Ginzberg and Marx truly obeyed the command:
"Rise up many disciples and make a fence around the Torah.’’

The beautiful River Garden Hebrew Home on the
banks of the St. Johns River has been a source of pleasure
and inspiration to the entire community. It is a source of
pride to all of us that all the functions and the care of the
residents have continued to be accomplished in a very
satisfactory and homelike atmosphere.
In evaluating the services rendered by us we are ever
mindful of the aims of our organization to meet the needs
of the residents as well as those of the Home. The needs
vary from sponsorship of a hobby program—the provide
ing of linens by our linen shower—a new movie projec¬
tor—refurnishing the sun parlor—as well as the main¬
tenance of the new summer pavillion, where picnics and
outdoor suppers are held throughout the summer.
The Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society has always ac¬
cepted the responsibility of providing the Home with
these special needs which could not be met through its
operating budget.
The financing of our many projects has been made
possible through the wonderful cooperation of our fund¬
raising committees. They consist of our napkin and sta¬
tionary project—the annual donors fund—and the yearly
Tombola bazaar, conducted both in and out of the city,
as well as our annual dues collection. This year all mon¬
ices raised from the various funds will give us a good start
towards the furnishing of the proposed new infirmary.
We extend our deepest appreciations and heartfelt
thanks to all the many friends of the River Garden Home
and Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society for their loyal and
devoted efforts to our dedicated cause.

HARRY GENDZIER
The admonition of the Fifth Commandment to hon¬
or thy father and mother has been translated through the
long years of Jewish history and tradition to honor all
Jewish fathers and mothers. In obeying this command¬
ment, we, the Jewish people of Florida, have interpreted
this concept most liberally in terms of a decent Home,
and a concern for the dignity and worth of every aged
Jewish person in need of care.
River Garden has continued during the past year its
same high standard of service, and particularly concen¬
trated its efforts on the medical program of the Home.
The many deteriorative and crippling diseases, common
in old age, tend to limit the activities of the residents, and
discourage their enjoyment of a rich and full life. The
Medical Board was challenged by these problems, and
reaffirmed our basic assumption that age itself is not a
barrier to good health or comfortable living. With this
principle as a guide, they met with enthusiasm the prob¬
lems of the deteriorative and chronic diseases. Their ef¬
forts resulted in a more comfortable life for the majority
of our residents.
The provision of a good life for our residents has
pointed up the needs of the many waiting desperately to
find such a life in the Home. Although our extension
services have provided security for some of our able resi¬
dents, the needs are definitely for infirmary facilities. Riv¬
er Garden has noted the changing character and needs of
its incoming residents and those on the waiting list. They
are older, feebler people, and require a great deal of nurs¬
ing care and attention. In addition, many are now pre¬
senting problems of personality disturbances, mental con¬
fusion as well as senility. The challenge to the Home to¬
day is to continue to work out services for these problem
aged so that they can live comfortably in a community
setting rather than to commit them to the State Hospital
for mental illness or private sanitariums.
The Executive Board of River Garden is encouraged
by the excellent results the Home has had in serving the
infirm and bedridden aged. They are also enthusiastic
about the fact that no aged Jewish person in Florida has
been committed to the State Hospital in the past 5 years.
As a result, they are planning in the new building pro¬
gram, specialized infirmary facilities to care for the senile
and mentally disturbed aged.
River Garden’s role in the community is that of a so¬
cial agency providing care and comfort to those aged
people who cannot find any other comfortable solution in
the community. By discharging its dedicated responsibil¬
ity to the Jewish communities of Florida, River Garden in¬
deed obeys the Fifth Commandment by honoring all Jew¬
ish mothers and fathers.

"As The Tree Shall Grow, So Shall Its Limbs.” The
Center Men’s Club has grown too, and now stands as a
strong limb on the "Center Family Tree.” The Men’s
Club, ever striving to aid the Center in attaining its edu¬
cational, cultural, social and religious goals, witnessed
one of its most successful years in 1953.
During the past year, we sponsored a scholarship for
one boy at Camp Tel Yehudah. We presented awards to
outstanding pupils in the Center’s Hebrew School and
Sunday Religious School, as well as to members of other
Center affiliated youth groups. We feel, that through
these efforts, we are furthering the development of future
leaders for the Jacksonville Jewish Community.
The sponsorship of the Minyan Club has long been a
religious activity of which we are indeed proud. This
group is composed of boys who are to be or who have
already been Bar Mitzvah. The Men’s Club arranges
transportation for the boys and act as hosts for the break¬
fast served each Sunday morning. The Men’s Club also
assists, in cooperation with the Center Sisterhood, in the
celebration of Holidays and Sunday School events.
Two of the most enjoyable events sponsored by the
Men’s Club last year were the Boat Ride and Valentine
Dance. All proceeds of these affairs were devoted to the
Men’s Club Youth Projects.
As a further service to the community, the Men’s Club
also maintains a blood bank which is contributed to by its
membership.
Looking into the future, we of the Mens’ Club pledge
ourselves to continue our endeavors to enrichen the Center
Youth Movement. We will always be ready and willing
to assist the Youth of the Center and of our community.
We will continually strive to further the strength and

During its fifth year of organized activity in Jacksonville, the Center Youth
League has continued along the important lines of cultural, social, and religious
development. We have not only doubled our membership, but have instilled
into our members the definite aims and objectives of National U.S.Y. (United
Synagogue Youth).
Many important projects were completed, of which the Southeast Regional
Convention was outstanding. We continued our interesting by-weekly post-
Confirmation Study Group which proved very informative.
Other projects included a Mid-Winter Dance in December and our Second
Annual Spring Dance.
The financial assistance of the Center Sisterhood, Center Men’s Club and
the Palot Memorial Fund enabled us to send delegates to the Leadership Train¬
ing Institute in Hendersonville, North Carolina, held during the Spring of
1953, to the National U.S.Y. Convention in Atlantic City last December, and
to our Regional Board Meeting in Atlanta, Georgia. We of the Center Youth
League are indeed grateful to these organizations and to the Center Youth Ac¬
tivities Committee for their wonderful cooperation.
The Center Youth League looks back upon the past year as a very success¬
ful one and considers it a challenge for the future. With the help and guidance
of Rabbi Tofield and our Advisor, Miss Myra Thier, we of C.Y.L. look for¬
ward to the coming year eagerly.

IRVING KLEPPER
The Jewish Welfare Society, now a multi-functional
case work agency and affiliated with the Community Chest
has been serving individuals and families in Jacksonville
with financial and counseling services for thirty-seven
years. Organized in 1917 to help the needy financially, it
became apparent after some time, as it did in communities
all over the country, that financial, inter-personal and
some psychological problems with which a case work
agency could be of assistance, occurred to people of all
economic levels and stations of life.
During the past few years, there has been a slow,
steady rise in the number of people served. This occurred
in spite of the changes in staff and fairly good economic
conditions. Part of the increase — at least for the past
year — can be attributed to the growing child welfare
orogram. The Jewish Welfare Society is still the only
icensed Jewish child placing agency in Florida. Last year
the agency placed four children in adoptive homes, had
three other children in temporary foster homes, and stud¬
ied many adoptive applications. Part, too, can be attrib¬
uted to the increased use of our counseling services for
marital problems, both of husband and wife and parent
and child.
The great majority of cases served are requests for
services that do not involve financial assistance. This is
true even though Jacksonville, because of its unique geo¬
graphical position, seasonally has a good number of re¬
quests from transients. The transients usually have short-
time requests. They are helped whenever possible with
food, lodging and, when indicated, with counseling, em¬
ployment or contact with their families.
The problems for which the local clients request assist¬
ance, like all needs, have all gradations. They vary from
minor, even trivial, to very serious. Some are simple and
clean cut, while others are complicated and enmeshed. The
same holds true, of course, for the solution, total or par¬
tial, of the problems. Arrangements may be worked out
by merely a phone call to a hospital, the filling out of a
form or it may involve hours of interviews, letter writing,
calls, and months of time. Long seated family problems
seem to respond best to regularly scheduled weekly inter¬
views. They may be held with the member of the family
who feels the need for doing something about the prob¬
lem, or with all members involved.
With all the developments of case work, there has not
yet been discovered a means that fills an empty stomach
or assures a roof over one’s head as well as money. We
have been fortunate in having a comparatively small num¬
ber of cases that need financial help. We have therefore,
been able to meet emergency needs promptly and help
families get back on their feet in a short time, to care,
over a period of years, for a number of people who are
not eligible for state assistance and to supplement the very
small income that the state provides for other families in¬
volving widows and elderly persons. It is hoped that the
state law permitting assistance for the totally incapaci¬
tated under 65 years of age, may successfully go through
the legal entanglements it is involved in, this year.
The Welfare Society works in close relationship with
the River Garden Home in processing applications and
providing case work services to the residents and their
families. The extension program, which we helped initi¬
ate and supervise, provides for people who can live out¬
side the Home and use its facilities. There is a waiting
list. With the anticipated building program for the Home,
it is believed that the number of applications will increase
even more this year.
Through funds allocated by the Jewish Community
Council and with the help of the Council of Jewish Wo¬
men, the Society helped resettle a number of families in
the city. The families are now financially self sufficient
and as a group, they are making a wholesome adjustment.
These and others, seek and secure our help with problems
of immigration, naturalization and the location of lost
relatives. This year it is hoped that five more families will
be brought over and helped to resettle.
The Society offers a limited social service program to
the prisoners at the State Prison at Raiford.

MRS. SAMUEL J. THIER
Sisterhood looks back with pride to its accomplish¬
ments of the past year. Our membership, due to the val¬
iant efforts of our Membership Committee, now stands
at the highest mark in our history. However, we are not
only striving to improve our position, but to consolidate
all the gains made in the past.
It has always been our firm belief that in order to
get the full cooperation of our membership, they should
be brought to realize what Sisterhood really stands for
and that fund-raising is only incidental to the fulfillment
of our over-all plan.
Why all these projects? Donor Affair, Funds for
Youth Dance, Year Book, Rummage Sale, etc. For in¬
stance, the funds realized from our solicitation of adver¬
tisements for the Center Year Book, our largest source
of revenue, are used primarily for the rebuilding of the
Synagogue. Our youth projects include financial assist¬
ance for the Center Youth League, such as defraying ex¬
penses for delegates to national conventions, leadership
training institutes and regional conventions. What this
has done for our teen agers is amply illustrated by the
favorable comments received after our local C.Y.L. were
hosts to the Southeast Regional Convention of United
Synagogue Youth in February. It was surely a thrilling
sight to witness hundreds of youngsters participating in
a Jewish activity in our Center.

viding the funds for the salaries of teachers in our Sun¬
day Religious School. Considering the rapid growth of

BUDDY SHORSTEIN
The Esquire Club is happy to announce that 1954 was
a most successful year for the organization, under the
capable leadership of its president, Buddy Shorstein.
However, much credit is due to Richard Hollins for de¬
voting so much of his time and effort to the affairs of
the group.
Without the cooperation of all the officers and mem¬
bers, the "Esquires” could not have grown to the out¬
standing organization that it is today. Credit is due also
to Miss Carol Chepenik, Mascot, who assisted so gracious¬
ly in all of the Club’s functions.
This year the Esquires contributed to the Charles H.
Palot Memorial Fund and to the Center Building Fund.
One of the highlights of the season was the Esquire Sab¬
bath held at the Jewish Center.
Our entire membership was saddened by the tragic
death of Allan Bernstein, who was an active member and
served as treasurer of Esquire. We mourn his passing
and will always cherish his memory.
