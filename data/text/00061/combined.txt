For These, We Give Thanks
O God, to whom we come so often with needs to be satisfied,
we come to You now in gratitude for what we already have and are.
For gifts beyond deserving or counting, we give thanks.
You have given us the ability to become more than we have been, the urge to be more than we are,
and a gnawing hunger to attain heights only dimly imagined.
For the power to grow, we give thanks.
You have endowed us with the capacity to discern the difference between right and wrong:
and You have enabled us to follow the right, to avoid the wrong.
For the power to choose, we give thanks.
You have blessed us with the ability to fashion things of beauty, to sing new songs, to spin new tales,
to add to the treasure-house of human civilization.
For the power to create, we give thanks.
You have equipped us with the yearning to commune with You, to bring You our fears and our dreams,
our hurts and our joys, our guilt and our gratitude; to share hopes and concerns with You and with others.
For the power to pray, we give thanks.
You have fortified us with the ability to rise above disappointment and failure,
to go on after we have been bruised and bereaved, to refuse to submit to defeat and despair.
For the power to hope, we give thanks.
You have enlarged us with the ability to cherish others, to make their lives as dear to us as our very own,
to share their hopes, to feel their hurts, to know their hearts.
For the power to live, we give thanks.
You have ennobled us with the strength to overcome our faults, to mend our ways,
and to answer the summons “to turn to You with all our heart and soul.”
For the possibilities of renewal, we give thanks!
To all our friends, a year of peace and good health, and to children everywhere, we pledge our efforts to improve the world.

“A Caring Community”
“Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long . . It was this
commandment which inspired the founding of River Garden Hebrew Home in
1946. It was this commandment which nourished the love and care that for more
than fifty years has made River Garden a nationally recognized model of excellence
in its field. It was this commandment that made it necessary to create a Home that
is welcoming to families and encourages its use as an extension of family space.
River Garden residents are a part of the community, not apart from it!
The River Garden Hebrew Home has also become the anchor of a growing
system of elder services including the Home, The Coves, Family Care Support
program and Home Health Agency. The success of River Garden is based on the
tradition of meeting 100 percent of local Jewish community needs and of serving
rich and poor alike with excellence. The River Garden Foundation accepts
donations to help River Garden continue to meet its mission. This would not be
possible without substantial charitable support from the community and the work
of The River Garden Foundation to stimulate planned gift annuities, trusts and
bequests.
River Garden’s programs of short-term rehabilitative/respite care, long¬
term residential care, Alzheimers’ and Adult Day Care, serve over 180 people on
any given day. These programs are guided by a devoted, professional full time
staff of over 200. Staff includes physicians and beauticians, nurses, social workers,
pharmacists, activities specialists, physical occupational and speech therapists,
dieticians, etc. In addition, scores of dedicated volunteers help create a special
caring community.
Located on a 35 acre Mandarin campus containing lush Florida oak and
pine trees, a private lake, meandering walking paths, River Garden is the perfect
setting for residents and visitors. River Garden is a community where people
live, love and care. It is a community of many lifestyles and of rich Jewish content,
and a community which every day strives to live up to the mandate of the Fifth
Commandment.

Scouting
Cadette/Senior Girl Scout Troop 969
The world of Cadette Girl Scouting is an exciting
and challenging one, that offers new opportunities for
teenage girls to learn about themselves and their
environment. Senior Girl Scouts explore personal
interests, develop a healthy way of life, develop leadership
skills and provide services to the community.
Girl Scouting is lacking without camping, so we
began the year with a campout at Girl Scout Camp Kateri.
Everyone had a wonderful time.
The girls learned several new skills this year. They
sewed their own Cadette vests, as part of the “Fashion
Design” interest patch requirements. They also learned
about making cosmetics for the “Fitness to Fashion”
interest badge. Earning interest patches, offered the girls
opportunities to have fun while learning about themselves.
Several of the Scouts earned the “From Dreams to
Reality” interest patch, which explores aspects of many
different careers. Several moms told the girls how they
handle the difficult task of balancing jobs and families.
They also talked about how they got their current position.
Then, the girls did research on the Internet to find out
about salary ranges and training requirements for five
different careers, as well as learning about two fields in
which women are in the minority.
Several girls also earned “Cadette Girl Scout
Challenge”, which involves understanding themselves,
relating to others, developing values, contributing to the
community and knowing about Girl Scouting.
We are proud of the tenth grader of the Troop,
who was selected to train and assist the Gateway
Council’s 6th grade event, based on historic St. Augustine
in the 1740s. She helped the younger girls camp and
served as a tour guide for visitors to the historic
recreation. This “wider experience” prepares teens for
adventures in Girl Scouting at the national and
international levels.
The year ended in a day of service to the
community. The girls presented crocheted baby covers
to the Emergency Pregnancy Service Center, toured and
served lunch at the I.M. Sulzbacher Center for the
Homeless, and delivered cookies they had baked to the
Ronald McDonald House.
Thanks to Janis Fleet for serving as Cookie Mom.
Thanks also to all the parents for the wonderful support
of their daughters and Troop 969.
— Helen Shacter
Members of Cadette Girls Scout Troup 969, in the kitchen of
Ronald McDonald House, where they helped to serve meals to guests.

Burial and Mourning
Center Cemetery
The Center owns and maintains two cemeteries adjacent to the Evergreen
Cemetery in North Jacksonville. Both are open daylight hours every day except
Shabbat and Jewish holidays. To reach the Old Cemetery, travel north on North
Main Street to Winona. Make a right on Winona and go until you reach Gate 5.
The Old Cemetery is on the left. To reach
the New Cemetery, travel north on North
Main Street to 43rd Street. Make a right
on 43rd Street and the entrance is at the
next right, which is Liberty Street.
This year, middle school students of
the Solomon Schechter Day School learned,
through the Mitzvah Program, about the
respectful care of the dead, including tending
of burial sites. On a visit to the Old
Cemetery, they discovered that several family plots no longer had loved ones
to maintain them. The students rolled up their sleeves and, with great care and
respect, cleaned the neglected headstones of a family that been laid to rest
more than 50 years ago. We are proud and grateful to these children for
performing an important mitzvah.
Future plans for the cemetery include improvements to the entrances, as
well as additional landscaping. Contributions can be made to the Cemetery
Fund in honor or in memory of loved ones. All donations will be used to enhance
and maintain our cemeteries.

Hevra Kaddisha performs the greatest mitzvah of all - caring for the
dead. Jewish tradition demands that all Jews, regardless of their circumstances
in life, be buried in a simple, respectful ceremony.
It is thought that Jewish Burial Societies have been in existence for over
1,600 years. Indeed when the Jewish community formed here many years ago,
one of the first organizations to be founded was the burial society.
Membership in the Hevra Kaddisha has always been considered a great
honor. It’s not unusual to find members from successive generations and they
are all guided by the principle that the deceased must be treated with reverence,
sensitivity and kindness. This is an injunction that has its foundation in the Torah.
Jacksonville can be assured and proud that we have this organization
with dedicated members, willing to serve our Jewish community 24 hours a
day, 7 days a week.
I would like to take this opportunity to reach out to members of our
synagogue and the Jewish community who are seeking to perform a great
mitzvah and suggest that they consider joining the Hevra Kaddisha.
—Nathan C. Sandler, president

Hadassah National Council of Jewish Women
“Hadassah has always been an important bridge to
peace. By treating all people without regard to nationality
or religion, Hadassah has shown the world what peace in
the Middle East will look like.”
— Yitzchak Rabin, 1994.
The National Council of Jewish Women (NCJW) is
a volunteer organization, inspired by Jewish values, that
works through a program of research, education, advocacy,
and community service to improve the quality of life for
women, children and families, and strives to ensure
individual rights and freedoms for all.
Judaism teaches that it is not enough to avoid iniquity,
we must actively pursue righteousness and justice at home,
in the community, and in the world. In this tradition,
Hadassah was founded in 1912 by Henrietta Szold to bring
public health services to pre-state Israel. Today, more than
300,000 American women support our network of Israel
projects in addition to our volunteer work at home. Across
the US, Hadassah promotes health education, community
service, and Jewish education and research. Hadassah
advocates on issues ranging from Israel-US relations to
First Amendment protections to women’s rights and health
concerns.
One by one, we must each take responsibility for
transforming hopes, promises, and echoes into deeds.
Jacksonville Hadassah invites your participation in one of
our five groups: Hannah Senesch, Shir Li, Tikva, Nurse’s
& HCP Council and Yamit.

We honor the Memory of
Philip N. Selber
“His passion for Jewish life, his passion for the Jacksonville Jewish Center, his hope for
the future - everything Jewish animated his life. ” — Rabbi Emeritus David Gaffney.
Through three terms as president of the
congregation, Philip Selber campaigned to make
the synagogue a center for Jewish life rather
than simply a place to pray. Philip was a
visionary with a grand plan that created a
facility designed to accommodate every
lifecycle event — from the birth of a child, to
bar and bat mitzvah, to marriage — for
generations to follow. He was the instrument
that made it all happen.
In December we celebrated the 100th
Anniversary of our congregation. For more than
sixty of those years, Philip was involved, to
obsession, in making the Jacksonville Jewish
Center one of the country’s premier synagogue
centers. He was the driving force in selecting
this wonderful piece of property, at a time when
it seemed to be at the end of the world. Philip
could see with utmost clarity the rare
possibilities in this bare land. He knew that
there was only one place for the Southside of
town to grow, and that soon it would be in the
center of all of this development surrounded by
a large population of Jews who would take
advantage of all the Jacksonville Jewish Center
had to offer. Through his determined, and
sometimes stubborn leadership, he cajoled, he
prodded, he demanded that we believe his
impossible dream. That dream for our
congregation would blossom into the all purpose
Jewish center we take for granted today.
“He had the indignation of a prophet when
it was warranted, ” said Rabbi Gaffney. “He had
the ability to inspire teenagers and young people
by his authenticity. ” If you were fortunate enough
to be in his Confirmation class, you came away
with a far better understanding of our heritage,
the history of our people, and the importance of
making sure you carried on that heritage and
added to its history into the future.
The sum of Philip Selber’s contributions to
growth of the Jacksonville Jewish Center cannot
be measured in a simple arithmetic formula. He
was an inspiration to the leadership of the Center
in his time, and guiding light for the leadership
of the future. He was a man of boundless energy,
and consummate attention to every single detail
no matter how small or how large. The completion
of his work, as he saw it, is a living endeavor
that will continue to grow and improve.
We’ll miss you Philip Selber, but we’ll see
your mark in every nook and cranny of our
Jacksonville Jewish Center. We know, as the 23rd
psalm says ... “You will live in the house of the
Lord forever ”
This tribute, written by Raymond Cohen, first
appeared in the April 2002 Center Bulletin.

Simhat Torah Honorees
The High Holiday season reaches its joyous climax on Simhat Torah, the last day of the festival
of Sukkot. Because of our perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to as Israel’s “bride”. On
Simhat Torah, we bring to a close the annual cycle of the reading of the Torah and immediately begin
anew. An ancient tradition evolved of honoring worthy men by calling them to the Torah for the last
reading, Hatan Torah, and the beginning portion, Hatan Bereshit. In our synagogue tradition, we
also honor the contributions made by a “Woman of Valor”.

The Religious School of the Jacksonville Jewish Center prides itself on
producing young Jewish adults with a well-rounded education in the basics of
Judaism.
In the Sunday School program, students in Kindergarten through 2nd grade
enjoy holidays and Bible studies through a variety of media, including music,
arts and crafts, stories, dancing, and games.
The Hebrew Alef-Bet is introduced, and reading is begun, in the 2nd
grade. The Mechinah (preparation) class meets on Sunday and Wednesday,
as a bridge to the afternoon Hebrew School program. Third through sixth graders
meet on Mondays, Wednesdays and Saturdays. They concentrate on the basics:
Hebrew skills in preparation for Bar and Bat Mitzvah, as well as history, holidays,
mitzvot, and current events.
All students participate in special youth services on Shabbat and holidays, where they can practice
these skills with their peers. The 7th grade Etgar program is designed to tie together all of this previously-
learned knowledge, while presenting opportunities and hands-on activities especially intended to prepare
these students to graduate as active, knowledgeable young leaders in our Jewish community.
This year, Serene Victor, a specialist in Jewish education, completed a two-year evaluation program
that began in the spring of 2000, courtesy of United Synagogue of Conservative Judaism. The purpose
was to strengthen our Religious School program. Mrs. Victor
held teacher workshops, discussed curriculum ideas and

A Message from Rabbi Grundfast
Talmud Torah K’neged Kulam
‘Jerusalem was destroyed only because
the people neglected the education of
school children.’” (Shabbat 119a)
Rabbi Hammuna teaches that no
community can sustain itself without
a strong and vibrant will to educate its
young to the best of its ability.
The Talmud, in Massechet
Shabbat (127a), boldly asserts that a
number of mitzvot are so important and
vital, that when a person fulfills one of
them, two rewards are realized. Among
these essential mitzvot are honoring
one’s parents, visiting the sick and
making peace between one person and
another. The first reward received is
immediate, while the second will
continue to yield its sweet fruit even in
the world to come.
While these mitzvot are of the utmost importance,
the Talmud comes to the conclusion that “Talmud Torah
K’neged Kulam-The study of Torah-surpasses them all.”
The rabbis of old considered this Talmudic section of such
importance that they incorporated it into the beginning of
the Shacharit (morning) service every day of the year, in
order to act as a constant reminder of the essential
message that Jewish education and learning must be
woven into the very fabric of our beings. Jews do not
give lip service to learning and knowledge. We have always
understood quite well that they have sustained us as a people
of faith for over three thousand years.
For one hundred of those years, the Jacksonville
Jewish Center has dedicated itself to becoming a focal
point for Jewish education. Our Preschool, Religious
School and Solomon Schechter Day School have educated
generations of Center members, and given them the
necessary tools to become serious and dedicated Jews
for their entire lives. Offering a high quality Jewish
educational experience to all our children has been, and
must continue to be, viewed as being of the highest priority.
Once again the Talmud teaches, “Rabbi Hammuna said,
I congratulate all those who have
dedicated themselves each and every
day, to the holy task of educating
our precious youngsters. From
administrators and teachers to lay leaders and dedicated
parents, I say todah rabbah for helping to insure the
continued strength and future of our congregation and
of Jewish life by making our educational programs the
best they can possible be. In the years ahead, we must
continue to strive for excellence within the classrooms
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
The word in Hebrew for education is hinuch. This
word also can mean to “dedicate” or “consecrate,” as
in the word Hanukkah, when the ancient Temple in
Jerusalem was dedicated anew by Judah Maccabee.
When we successfully educate our children at the
Center, we not only pass on knowledge, but also help
them dedicate their lives for living a meaningful and
serious Jewish life.
In Sefer Hasidim we are taught, “They who
train their children in good deeds from childhood on, train
their children to be good navigators who know how to
steer their ships to port.” I hope and pray that in the
years to come, we will continue to help fashion and
create future generations of Jews, who will be ready
and able to steer their ships in the proper direction with
righteousness, respect and reverence as their guides to
a bright and strong Jewish future. This holy task is
incumbent upon all of us.
Rabbi Stephen Grundfast
On behalf of my wife, Judy, and children, Rachel
and Joe, I wish each of you a Shanah Tovah
U’Metukah — A Happy and Good New Year. May it
be one of Shalom for you and yours, Israel and for the
entire world.
— Rabbi Stephen Grundfast

Religious Committee
David Schmerer, Chairman
We are entering a new era at the Jacksonville Jewish Center and
the Religious Committee will play a central role in our congregation’s religious
development. This is no small undertaking, for we must blend the traditions
of the past century with the challenges of a new professionals and the current
trends in Conservative Judaism.
Our Committee is about to become one of the hardest working groups
at the Center. We oversee all areas of religious practice, ceremony and
decorum and have a substantial interest in the areas of education and youth.
The Religious Committee is dedicated to ensuring that all facets of the religious
life of our congregation are spiritual, meaningful and central to each and
every congregant.
It is equally important that all of us demonstrate our visible
commitment to Conservative Judaism and to our congregation by attending
Shabbat and Holiday Services and attending at least one daily Shacharit or
Mincha/Maariv Minyan each week. We should also make every attempt to
participate in adult education programs and to join our Sisterhood and Men’s
Club to show our support for those valuable groups. It is critically important,
especially if you have young children, to practice Jewish ritual in your home
like lighting Shabbat candles, doing Havdalah and keeping Kashrut. Wherever
you currently are on the observance scale, try moving it up a notch.
Any member of the Religious Committee stands ready to help you
achieve these goals. All you need to do is ask.

Shabbat Torah Readers
Scott Zimmerman, Coordinator
Torah study has been vital to
Jews for centuries precisely because
it lends itself to search and discovery
by each new generation. The text is
accessible on some level to all
individuals. The teacher is, therefore,
wise to act as a patient guide,
allowing students to discover for
themselves the meanings and
messages of the Torah.
Our liturgy calls Torah a “tree
of life.” Trees sway to and fro in the
wind, but they are firmly rooted in
the ground. Trees can shelter us from
rain, satisfy our senses with sweet
fruit, delightful fragrance, rustling
leaves, and ever-changing beauty.
Similarly, a knowledge of Torah can
root us firmly in Jewish tradition and
keep us from being blown about by
the changing currents of modern life.
Torah study can satisfy our need for
beauty and poetry, a sense of identity,
relevance and meaning.
I would like to thank all the
many members of our congregation
for their dedication and devotion to
maintaining the high level of
participation in reading Torah that our
synagogue has achieved. The
commitment and encouragement of
our synagogue organizations and their
leaders will ensure that this high
quality will be maintained for future
generations to come. I would like to
thank Ted Gross for his guidance and
encouragement as well as all of our
teachers for their continued support
and cooperation and wish you all a
heartfelt Mazel Tov.
— Scott

You can help support Kosher Nutrition, and the elderly in our community who rely

Now the community can rent the JFCS buses to shuttle guests to and from life cycle
events and family gatherings. Enjoy bar and bat mitzvahs, weddings and other special
occasions without the headache of coordinating rides for out-of-town guests.

CKNP continues to provide hot, nutritious, kosher meals to the elderly at Mt. Carmel,
in a safe and friendly environment.
Counseling
JFCS provides quality mental health care by licensed professionals, at reasonable
rates. Confidentiality is always guaranteed. Services include counseling for grief,
depression, family issues, parent-child conflict, life transitions and much more.
Feed A Needy Neighbor
With donations of non-perishable food items from a concerned community, JFCS
provides emergency groceries to families and individuals experiencing difficult times.
Jewish Healing Network
The Jewish Healing Network reaches out to those who are struggling, ill or dying
by sharing the rich resources within the Jewish tradition.
Jewish Hospice Connection
JFCS is now collaborating with Community Hospice Northeast to offer services to
Jewish individuals. When a member of the Jewish community enters the hospice
program, a social worker from JFCS will be assigned. If requested, a volunteer
from the Jewish Healing Network will be provided, as well.
Senior Transportation
JFCS transports seniors to religious services, doctors appointments, shopping centers
and recreational activities for an affordable fee.
There’s So Much More . ..
These are just a few of the many services offered at JFCS. For those needs the
agency cannot address, staff is always available to refer you to the appropriate
resource.

Message from the Principal
The Solomon Schechter Day School of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center exists today as the outstanding educational institution that it is,
because of the persistence and vision of the congregation and its
leadership, both lay and professional, throughout the years.
Beginning in 1961, families were attracted to the school because
it stood for intellectual excellence and Jewish tradition. The embryonic
school grew with the support of the community, rabbis and a growing
number of gifted and dedicated parents.
Upon this base, the school is entering its forty- first year of
serving the youth of the Jewish community.
Our overall structure now embodies a Primary Department
(kindergarten through third grade), an Intermediate Department (fourth
and fifth grades), and now a Middle School (grades sixth through eighth). This marks another milestone
for our school.
Willard M. Kennedy

now employ the Tal Sela Program through the elementary program. This year the Ma Tok Program will
be introduced for the study of Bible. Thus, our curriculum will be on a national level with all other
Schechter schools.
Our instructional departments are being wired to bring the Internet to our classrooms, extending
our computer services to every student. This year, an after-school sports program will begin for our
Intermediate and Middle School students, to give them the opportunity to develop their physical skills
beyond our Physical Education Program. In addition, a state-of-the-art science lab was completed this
year, expanding our ability to improve our Science curriculum.
Some ask why we continue to develop our services. The answer, simply
put, is that Jewish day school education has been identified as one of the strongest
forces in preserving Jewish continuity in America. Students who attend our school
establish a firm foundation based on knowledge of source material, Hebrew
language, peer involvement and values clarified through the prism of Judaism.
Schechter students that enter other schools, both public and private, after
leaving us are confident and proud of themselves, both as Jews and as academic
achievers. Our students have been eminently successful
not only as scholars, but as citizens of the schools they go
on to attend.

A Toast to an Historic Day at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center
Saturday, December 9, 2001, was a
Shabbat like no other in the 100-year history
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Our
congregation rejoiced from morning ‘til night
on its Centennial Anniversary.
Michael Shorstein, Center president, offered a toast.
Beginning at 9 a.m., the main sanctuary overflowed
with congregants who gathered to worship and
commemorate the people, events and synagogue buildings
that made such an incredible impact on our lives. Young
and old, recent and longtime members participated in a
memorable Shabbat service. It was like Rosh Hashanah,
Simhat Torah and a huge Bar Mitzvah all rolled into one.
Representatives of families who boast as many as six
generations of Center members led the Torah processions.
Each Torah portion was dedicated to a significant individual
or group. Everyone received an aliyah in categories
symbolizing a member’s proud association — officers,
valued volunteers, graduates of the schools, youth group
alumni, club and committee members, etc. All participants
represented some facet of congregational life. Rabbi
Emeritus David Gaffney delivered a stirring account of his
30 years at the Center and new spiritual leader, Rabbi
Stephen Grundfast offered words of congratulations and
hope for the future. The sanctuary resounded with joyful
singing as the children of the Pre-Junior and Junior
Congregations lined the bima to lead the closing hymn. The
festive Kiddush following the Services provided
opportunities for further reminiscing. Gratitude is extended
to Meryl Rittenberg and Scott Zimmerman for
organizing an inspiring experience.

Center Youth Department
A Message from the Director
It has been my honor over the past 15 years to work with the youth of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center. In my newest position as Youth Director, with help
from the Youth Commission and the youth group advisors, I strive to create warm,
caring and responsible young adults.
Together, we are instilling in our children a sense of pride in their heritage,
and a commitment to the ideals of Jewish life. We are helping our young people to
develop an appreciation for our synagogue, and a willingness to work for its
betterment. They are learning to fulfill the social needs of our community, and to
participate in many civic activities. Together, we have experienced the gifts of
mitzvot.
In an article titled “What Parents ‘Owe’ Their Children,” it is stated that,
“Goodness is not an innate or natural disposition. People are not born good. Rather, they become good human
beings by learning from role models who are good.”
In this, our children certainly have much help from our wonderful youth group advisors. Our younger children
learn from the older ones, and we have so many volunteer adults who serve as excellent role models.
I’d like to thank everyone who gave me their help and support. I
have enjoyed this past year, and look forward to many social,
educational and fun times with our youngsters in the future.
— Gayle Bailys
Center Youth Commission
The Youth Commission oversees all youth organizations and programs
sponsored by the Jacksonville Jewish Center including Chalutzim, Kadima,
USY, Scouts and the Center Day Camp. The committee is made up of youth
group advisors, presidents of our clubs and caring Center members who
believe that the future of Conservative Judaism can be secured through
participation in innovative programs designed to develop upcoming Jewish
leaders.
In addition to membership in Center Youth Groups, the Youth
Commission encourages our youngsters to attend United Synagogue summer
programs, such as Camp Ramah, USY - on - Wheels, Ramah Seminar in
Israel and USY Poland/Israel Pilgrimage. These provide excellent
opportunities for our children to share wholesome, fun and uniquely Jewish
experiences with their peers from all over the United States. The Youth
Commission assists applicants with a variety of scholarships.
Through our College Outreach program, our college undergraduates
stay connected to the synagogue and community. Four times a year, at Rosh
Hashanah, Chanukah, Purim and Passover, care packages are sent that bring
warm wishes and promote holiday observance. Help us to stay in touch with
your children by keeping us update on their school mailing and email
addresses. Thank you to Stella Finck for her long-standing devotion to this
project.

Hazak - pm
for adults 55 and up
The United Synagogue of Conservative Judaism
now sponsors an organization for congregants age 55
and up called Hazak.
The name HAZAK is an acronym. The het stands
for Hokhmah (wisdom), the zayan for Ziknah (maturity)
and the kuph for Kadima (looking ahead). Hazak is
designed to compliment congregational programs with
additional social, spiritual and educational components
geared to and for the senior population. Hazak members
will not only have the opportunity to meet on a regular
basis with peers from their own congregations, but with
fellow Jews from other affiliated Conservative
congregations in their community, region and nation.
Hazak offers it members the opportunity to travel
together, participate in retreats, Shabbatonim and
seminars utilizing the skills and knowledge available within
the group as well as outside resources. Hazak promotes
Judaism to seniors through projects and meaningful
experiences both in the synagogue environment and on
a community level.

that will answer the educational and social needs of our
empty-nesters and mature members. If you would like
to join a group for older adults, call Estelle Kritz at

Founded in 1930 as a First Aid Society, ARMDI evolved
into Israel’s emergency medical, disaster, ambulance and
healthcare network - 365 days a year, 24 hours a day. Every
person in every corner of Israel looks to MDA to perform the
usual tasks carried out by Red Cross Societies throughout the
world, and then some. Within minutes, MDA’s rescue units
arrive on the scene - for medical emergencies, accidents and,
unfortunately, terrorist bombings. Trained paramedics
administer immediate help and then transport the patient to
the hospital if necessary.

programs that reflect the ideals of social justice and academic

occasion.

Centennial Celebration. An elegant atmosphere was
created in the Setzer Social Hall and Fletcher-Selber
Auditorium to form a fitting backdrop for the magnificent
event that included cocktails, a sumptuous dinner, a warm
multifaceted program, a sentimental retrospective film,
entertainment and dancing. Nationally known comedian,
Robert Klein, was the featured performer and The Big
Apple Band from Orlando provided music until midnight.

Pozin for arranging the superb entertainment, Ellen and

About the Cover:
Once again, artist Raymond
Cohen has designed a dynamic and
significant logo in tribute to the
excellence in education that has been
a central part of the mission of our
congregation for 100 years. Here is
his concept:
‘The torch is a universal Jewish symbol for
education. We are commanded to be a “light unto
the nations,” bringing the truth of the Torah and the
beauty of Judaism to each generation. ‘ Torah orah -
Torah is light.’ The three-pronged flame and the icons
of children boldly represent the Jacksonville Jewish
Center’s Religious Schools, Preschool and Solomon
Schechter Day School. It illustrates the evolution of
Jewish education at the Center from a chedar, to a
modern 20th century Hebrew School, to today’s all-
encompassing educational experience that not only
imparts knowledge but immerses our children in the
richness of Jewish life. Moreover, the tri-flamed
torch exemplifies our reverence for the traditions of
the past, our efforts to keep Judaism vibrant in our
own time and our understanding that the future of
our congregation rests on bringing our children into
the fold through meaningful Jewish education.”

Scouting
Brownie Troop 882
Brownie Troop 882 had a wonderful first year. Many of the girls were members of our Daisy Troop last year
and stayed together as they moved up.
Our year was filled with exciting trips and activities,
as well as community projects. We started with a
wonderful birthday party for Juliet Gordon Low, founder
of Girl Scouting. It took place at the I.M. Sulzbacher
Center for the Homeless, where we left a variety of gifts
that the girls staying there could enjoy throughout the
year.
Our very first cookie sale was a great success. We
sold 2,100 hundred boxes of cookies for a total profit of
$945. Many boxes were donated and we took these “Gifts
of Caring” to the Park Place Retirement Community. We
enjoyed a tea and cookie party with the residents.
We also enjoyed fun field trips to the Karpeles
Manuscript Library Museum, and to the Imax Theatre
to see the movie “Beauty and the Beast.”
Thank you to all who helped make this
year special. I look forward to more great
Brownie experiences next year. For more
information on Brownie Scouts, call Sonya
Cohen at 268-5685.

Our Jewish identity is important. The JCA seeks to create a positive and affirming
environment of Jewish culture and heritage. We strive to welcome all and share our
Jewish traditions.
The mission of the JCA is to (1) contribute to the continuation of Jewish life,
culture, and community (2) provide a common meeting ground for the Jewish community
(3) enhance the quality of life for all segments of the community and (4) strengthen
family life. The JCA fulfills its mission through an integrated range of high quality
programs for people of all ages including: childcare and family programs, fitness and
recreational facilities and activities, Jewish education, culture and arts programs, and by
providing a place for family, “neighborhood,” and community gatherings.
The JCA takes pride in working with the Jacksonville Jewish Center and other
local synagogues and agencies in conducting special educational programs and
celebrations.

our members. We serve the Jacksonville Jewish community with programs like the
NAEYC accredited Gan Yeladim preschool, after school care for elementary age youth,
the Challenge Program, Yiddish for Fun, adult Torah study group, classical music
appreciation, pottery classes, children’s birthday parties, computer classes, yoga, aerobics,

Beyond Hebrew School
On September 28,1942, the Jacksonville Jewish Center
entered a new phase of Jewish education when it opened a
Nursery-Kindergarten program for 15 three-year-olds. The
children met weekdays from 9 to noon with Mrs. S.H.
Rosenberg and Mrs. M.J. Rieser. Everyone who grew up at
the Center over the next 24 years remembers Tillie Rieser’s no-
nonsense approach to child etiquette and love for youth
theater.
After WWII, Jewish education at the Center began to
focus on motivation not just skills. Inculcating our young
with a love of our
traditions and culture,
a loyalty to our people
and community, the
harmony between
Americanism and
Judaism and adding
extra activities for
enjoyment became
curriculum objects.
In 1947, Ted
Gross was hired to be
the Educational
Director of all the
Center’s schools. He
ushered in a modern
era of instructional
Jewish education and
began a devoted
career that touched
our lives for over 50
years. Through his efforts, “Bas Mitzvah” for girls became
the norm. A Consecration ceremony for first year Hebrew
students was instituted. A group of skilled Torah readers was
trained and is now training our children and grandchildren.
That year, Mr. Gross invited Dr. Azriel Eizenberg, Director of
the Philadelphia Council of Jewish Education, to evaluate our
program. He reported we were “well on the road to making the
Jacksonville Jewish Center one of the outstanding schools in
the country.” Under Ted’s guidance, nearly every child of
Center members received a comprehensive Jewish education.
In 1958, the Confirmation Class had 42 students. Other
programs were seeing large class sizes as well: 638 in Sunday
School, 95 in Kindergarten, 304 in Hebrew School. In 1959, the
school building was enlarged to make room for expanding
school programs.
The Ultimate In Jewish Education
In 1961, Rabbi Moshe and Evelyn Goldblum introduced
the concept of Hebrew Day Schools to an enthusiastic, if
small, group of parents. Among its strongest supporters were
Sylvia Shorstein and Horty and Sheldon Gendzier. Through
their efforts, the Jacksonville Jewish Center opened a Solomon
Schechter Foundation School with 13 first-grade students. It
combined secular and Hebraic studies for a total educational
experience. The inclusion of Israeli-born teachers to the staff
added an authenticity to spoken Hebrew and a new spirit of
Zionism. Ten years later, under the guidance of Principal
Marilyn Sandler, the Day School program had an enrollment
of 131 in grades 1-6 and 80 in the Nursery-Kindergarten.
1976 was a monumental year as the Jacksonville Jewish
Center moved to the present Mandarin campus. The
magnificent synagogue-center envisioned 50 years earlier was
now a reality. School wings for the Day School and Preschool
allowed the program to grow at an amazing rate. Soon it
would be necessary to make two separate departments, each
with its own executives. Marilyn Sandler became Director of
the Preschool, which now included before & after school Day
Care. Willard Kennedy, a former private school headmaster,
was hired as Principal of Solomon Schechter Day School, as
it neared its 25th anniversary.
In 1977, Rabbi Dov Kentof became the Director of
Education & Youth,
and gave the
traditional Hebrew
School program a
much-needed shot in
the arm. To him,
education was not just
acquiring skills for life,
but life itself. Rabbi
Kentof’s innovative
approach included
now famous projects
of epic proportion.
Beginning with a
Jerusalem Fair in 1979,
the congregation and
Jewish community
experienced Noah’s
Ark, A Fantasy Trip to
Israel and The World
of Our Grandfathers
along with lavish Purim
celebrations and Holocaust Commemorations. Classroom
education gave students Hebrew skills and a love for Jewish
living. To Rabbi Kentof, even camping is a Jewish experience.
With youth education well in hand, Rabbi David Gaffney,
spiritual leader, turned his attention to adult education. During
his 29 years at the Jacksonville Jewish Center, he developed
the Safer Shabbaton, Setzer-Levit Memorial Lectures, Sunday
Minyanaire, Sisterhood Seminars, Adult B’nai Mitzvah
programs and a full curriculum of Jewish studies for adults.
By the 1980’s the Center was again bursting at the seams.
A massive enlargement gave much needed classroom space,
a modem computer center, play areas and an expanded library,
as well as meeting rooms and office spaces. The 1990’s saw
record enrollments in all Center school programs, and we opened
our classrooms to the community Beit Midrash, Hebrew High.
Today
Looking back on the past 100 years of education at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, we can say with pride that we
affirm the Talmud’s teaching, “The whole world rests on the
breath of children in the classroom.”
Join us on November 17th, when we celebrate
Yom HaMorim, a special day honoring the
teachers of the Center Preschool, Solomon
Schechter Day School and Center Religious School
for 100 years of Excellence in Education.

Message from the Director
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Preschool was first
established in 1942, making this our 60th year of providing
outstanding secular and Judaic education for Jacksonville’s youngest
students. Many changes have occurred over these years including
staff, programs, classrooms and even the location of the school.
But one thing that remains the same is the Center’s desire to provide
our precious young children with the best educational foundation
possible.
Our Early Childhood Department plays an important role in
the overall development of our young children. Studies show us
that a great deal of a young child’s critical physical, social, and
cognitive growth takes place in the first five years. Our high-
quality program addresses all these areas of development, by providing an age-appropriate, developmental
program in a nurturing and safe Jewish environment.
This coming year we look forward to many physical changes in our facility. Plans for the renovation
of both our indoor and outdoor playground have begun and our new “baby blue” paint has brightened
our halls and classrooms.
Dale Schemer
Director

our weekly Shabbat program will add an exciting new dimension. Judy Grundfast will continue her
involvement by planning a monthly Shabbat celebration with special visits by Rabbi Grundfast. We also
look forward to adding a very unique “Mommy and Me” program.
Suzanne Cooper, our capable and dedicated Extended Day Coordinator and her terrific staff, will
be returning this year. They have worked all summer making plans to expand and invigorate the activities
available for our day care students.
With these and many other new programs planned, we are looking forward
to another exciting and successful school year.

Committees
Adult Education
Etta Drashin, chair
This year, the Adult Education Committee, chaired
by Etta Drashin, coordinated a wide variety of classes
to answer the diverse needs of our congregation. Hebrew
literacy, Talmud, workshops, speakers and discussion
groups formed the basis of an ongoing curriculum.
In his first year at the Center, Rabbi Stephen
Grundfast taught a variety of classes. Talmud classes
met on Sunday mornings. On Monday evenings, he
taught an Introduction to Judaism class, which was open
to Jews who wanted to learn more about their heritage,
as well as to non-Jews contemplating conversion. In the
spring, Rabbi Grundfast led three, Thursday “Lunch and
Learn” sessions at the offices of Shorstein and Shorstein.
In addition, he met with expectant couples to teach them
the Jewish traditions that deal with birth and babies. Rabbi
Grundfast’s program for members who live at the Beach
was well received. He plans more outreach of this kind
in the coming year.
Judy Grundfast held a “kumsitz,” a Hebrew sing-
a-long, in the Goldman Sukkah in early October.
Hebrew Literacy classes for those that either never
learned to read Hebrew, or needed a refresher course
was offered. Arlene Yegelwel and Etta Drashin taught
on a weekly basis. Lor the first time in several years, a
conversational Hebrew class was offered. The teacher,
Marc Leventhal, was wonderful and there was a lot of
learning and a lot of fun to be had in his class.
Israeli Dancing met, and continues to meet, every
Monday night in the Levin Indoor Playground at seven-
thirty. The dancing, which is choreographed to music
from the Shabbat liturgy as well as popular Israeli music,
has been great exercise and great fun.
On January 10, the Center hosted the 4th JTS
Llorida Institute sponsored by the Jewish Theological
Seminary, along with the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, Beth
Shalom and Beth-El Beaches
synagogues. Two senior
rabbinical students from the
Seminary, Michelle Lisher and
Menachim Creditor, led study
sessions on issues of
contemporary Jewish
observance. They
were a joy to listen to
and to learn from.
The annual Safer Shabbaton Weekend featured Dr.
Jack Wertheimer, provost at the Jewish Theological
Seminary, As scholar-in-residence his topics included:
The Jewish Lamily, Tensions
Between Jewish and Western
Values, the New Anti-
Semitism, and American Jewry
after September 11.

Administration
Executive Director’s Message
It is indeed fitting that this
Yearbook, the first of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center’s new Centennial,
celebrates 100 years of education.
Education has been the cornerstone
of the Center’s existence since its earliest
days. Our commitment to education is the
very reason the Center has produced
such outstanding leadership in the
community and in our congregation over
the past century.
Bruce Horovitz
Executive Director
Our schools benefit from state
of the art computer and science labs,
as well as the latest advances in
Judaic and secular curriculum. As in
the past, our educational programs
are led by dedicated and committed
administrators, teachers and
volunteers.
As we enter our second century
as a congregation, we can be assured
that the strong educational foundation
forged during our first one hundred
years, will carry us well into the future.
Every principal, teacher and
volunteer who has ever taken part in the educational life
of our congregation has our gratitude and admiration.
Your influence continues to inspire us and move us
forward.
A final note about our Centennial year which has
come to a close - it was a remarkable year of
celebration, transition and progress.
The Center’s commitment to education has never
been greater than it is today. On any given week, more
than 400 students participate in our many Galinsky
Educational Programs including the Solomon Schechter
Day School, Preschool, Religious School and Beit
Midrash.
The Centennial celebration, held in December 2001,
was perhaps the most memorable night in the history of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center. We laughed and cried,
as we celebrated a hundred years of congregational life.
Last year, we also welcomed our first new Rabbi
in thirty years, made extensive renovations to our facility
and happily received a large influx of new members.
My hope and prayer is that we continue this
progress in the years to come and that our congregation
be blessed with happiness, health and peace.
L’ Shana Tovah from Edith, Sam, Max and me.
— Bruce Horovitz

President’s Message
What an exciting time to be part of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center Family.
We have just completed celebrating the
congregation’s first 100 years. Our
mortgage is completely paid, and we have
been blessed with many new members.
Yet, challenges and opportunities remain
in the years to come.
Philip Bloom
President
Education has always been a pillar
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and will continue to
be an important focus of my administration. The Center
is one of only a handful of Conservative synagogues
around the country with a Solomon Schechter Day School
on site. We also have a strong Preschool and Religious
School. While our programming for these schools is
excellent, the need exists to continually improve the quality
and the number of programs offered to our young people,
as well as our adults. Our greatest challenge over the
next two years is to increase our membership. With a
large congregation, the demands for increased funding
may be met.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is, however, much
more than an educational facility. It is a gathering place
where the needs of each congregant, at each of life’s
milestones, is met. Like a large extended family, every
birth is cherished, every death is mourned, and every
marriage and Bar or Bat Mitzvah is celebrated as a family
event. As an extended family, the next greatest challenge
is to increase the involvement of every member.
Volunteerism must remain an important aspect of
achieving whatever goals we set. Future leaders are
needed, and our existing and past leaders must become
mentors for those coming up.
The situation in Israel over the last
year, and the events of September 11 here
at home, have created a heightened
awareness of security issues. Fortunately,
we began working on the security needs
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center several
years ago. A master security plan was
already in place so our time-frame for
implementation was simply accelerated.
This past summer, security cameras were
placed throughout the property and
around the building. Additional security lighting was
installed. The security contingencies of our synagogue
will continue to be reviewed and modified to keep the
Jacksonville Jewish Center safe.
I am very excited to lead the new Officers, Board
of Directors, and Committee Chairs that have accepted
the challenge of leadership. Under their guidance and
with their enthusiasm and energy, there is so much that
can be accomplished. With the support and active
participation of every congregant, our membership will
increase, our schools will grow stronger, our after-school
education programs will flourish and the Jacksonville
Jewish Center will continue to be a leading force in the
community as well as in each of our lives.
On behalf of my wife, Linda, and our children,
Lauren and Sasha, and from all of us at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center, L’Shana Tova.
— Philip Bloom

Scouting
Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14 continued the
scouting tradition begun in
1919 at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center, with another
year of outdoor fun,
adventure and activities in
2001 -2002. B ob Fleet served
as Scoutmaster, and was
assisted by Assistant
Scoutmasters Louis Schwartz
and Billy Wilensky.
This year, the troop went on campouts to
Anastasia State Park, Crooked River State (Georgia)
Park, Cumberland Island National Seashore, and Walt
Disney World Fort Wilderness. At Anastasia, they
completed the St. Augustine Historic Trail hike. At
Cumberland Island, the scouts witnessed racoons
unzipping the tents and taking crackers out of their
backpacks. At Walt Disney World, the troop enjoyed
Blizzard Beach Water Park. Other activities included
miniature golf, laser tag, and go-cart racing.
The scouts built a Sukkah out of bamboo, using
lashings they learned to craft. Troop 14 participated
in Saturday morning Shabbat services at River Garden
and at the Center on Scout Shabbat. The scouts
collected and delivered hats for the Wolfson Cancer
Ward.
A lot of scouts made strides in advancement
this year. Max Schwartz and Jared Fleet became the
most recent members of Troop 14
to earn Boy Scouting’s highest
honor, the rank of Eagle. For his
Eagle project, Max collected
toiletries, which were donated to
the Sulzbacher Center for the
Homeless. Jared rebuilt the
campfire pit at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center’s campsite, for his
project. A double Eagle Court of
Honor was held at the annual Blue and Gold Banquet.
Also at the Blue and Gold Banquet, Bob Fleet, after
six years as Scoutmaster, turned over the reins to Louis
Schwartz.
Fourteen scouts attended summer camp with
Troop 14, at Camp Daniel Boone in North Carolina.
They were led by Scoutmaster Louis Schwartz, Steve
Meisel, and Scott Vining. Max Schwartz and Shaun
Jackrel went on a 2 week trek at Philmont Scout
Reservation in New Mexico.
Troop 14 looks forward to continuing its proud
tradition of providing Jewish youth with a Boy Scouting
experience. If you enjoy camping, sports, a variety of
outdoor activities, and are between the ages of 11 and
18, we invite you to participate. The troop also
welcomes participation from adults interested in
providing volunteer assistance.
For more information call Louis Schwartz
at (H) 268-8717.
Bruce Horovitz spoke to Eagle badge recipients
(r to l) Jared Fleet and Max Schwartz at the annual
Blue & Gold banquet.
Troop 14

Shabbat/Holiday Ushers
Fred Blum & Tom Spriggs, Chairs
During the High Holidays and on Shabbat and holidays,
friendly volunteer members serve as ambassadors to Center
congregants by helping with seating, guiding and acknowledging
newcomers, and welcoming regular worshippers. Ushers help to
maintain synagogue decorum which preserves the beauty of the
service for all of us. We thank all of our ushers for their efforts
throughout the year.
Traditionally, public worship requires a minyan, a
quorum of ten men. Only in the presence of a minyan may the
Torah be read or the Kaddish prayer be recited.
Our dedicated minyan captains ensure that there is a
minyan twice every single day of the year. It is often a hard
and thankless job. It requires hours of phoning to coordinate
attendance.
We thank those who served as minyan captains during
the 2001-2002 season: Jack Bettman, Raymond Cohen, Jack
Mizrahi, Fred Kupperman, Joseph Shacter, David Schmerer,
Jack Silverman, Edward Tannen, Billy Wilensky, Evan
Yegelwel, Leon Yergin, Seeman Zimmerman and Bill Zoske.
It is because of them that you can recite kaddish
when you mourn or observe yarhzeit. We owe the captains
not only our thanks, but also our participation, in order to
continue providing a minyan for each service. Please become
a “regular” for at least one minyan a week. Call Dave
Schmerer, Religious Committee Chair, at 268-9168.
It is with deep sadness that we mark the passing of one
of our most devoted minyan captains, Philip Selber, who
served longer than any other captain. He will be especially
missed by those attending services on Friday afternoons and
Sunday mornings.
Henry Kramer Library & Resource Center
Minnie Schreiber and May Goldie Stillman, Chairs
This maginificent library is the result of 50 years of
devotion and assistance from countless synagogue members
to whom we are eternally grateful. Those who served in 2001-
2002 were: Chairs - May Goldie Stillman and Minnie Schreiber.
Committee - Svetlana Goldiner, Roselyn Selber and Blanche
Slott. Assistants - Debbie & Stephanie Schacter.
The Center Library began in 1959. Philip Selber had
attended a United Synagogue Convention, where the
importance of a Judaic library was emphasized. He felt that the
Jacksonville Jewish Center needed such a library, and
expressed his desire to his wife Roselyn. This became her
project.
Books were gathered from everywhere in the building at
3rd & Silver Streets. Funds were obtained from the Center
Sisterhood as well as from individual synagogue members.
Roselyn Selber was assisted in her endeavors by Minnie
Boatwright, Charlotte Glossman and many young people, who
are adult members of our congregation, today.
From this modest beginning, we now have the Henry
Kramer Library and Resource Center which houses the
Solomon Schechter Day School Library, Tofield Reading Room
and the Hebrew/Yiddish Collection.
We are proud that our library belongs to the prestigious
fraternity of libraries and gatherings of Judaica and Hebraica.

A Brief History of Education at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
1902-2002
V’shinantam L’vanecha
True to the principals of our faith, the founders of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center instituted a Hebrew School
simultaneously with the 1901 charter of Congregation B’nai
Israel, as our synagogue was then named. At first, it was in
the form of a typical European cheder, familiar to
the largely recent-immigrant membership of its day.
Over the next 100 years, education would evolve
and expand keeping pace with, and often driving,
the growth of the congregation itself. Today, we
boast an award winning system of schools that
reflects our unique southern Jewish culture, while
promoting the highest standards of education in
the Conservative tradition.
The Early Days
In 1907, when our fledgling congregation had
increased to 75 families, the first synagogue
building was erected at the corner of Jefferson and
Duval, in what was then a fashionable residential
section of Jacksonville. It was designed not only
with public worship in mind, but with much
emphasis placed on the educational activities of
the youth. To that end, provision was made in the lower level
of the building for three classrooms and a larger assembly
hall. There, the shochet and acting cantor, Rev. Benjamin
Safer, along with other melameds, taught daily lessons in

From the beginning, the Daughters of Israel, the
forerunner of our Center Sisterhood, were the strong promoters
of Jewish education. By 1922, they had assumed the full
responsibility of maintaining the Talmud Torah and succeeded
in instituting modern educational methods.
In 1926, Rabbi Samuel Benjamin was elected rabbi. A
graduate of the Jewish Theological Seminary of America,
Rabbi Benjamin introduced Conservative Judaism to our
Orthodox congregation and revolutionized our thinking of
the purpose of synagogues. He saw in the younger
generation a desire to adapt Jewish life to American
conditions. He promoted the idea of the “synagogue center”
as a way to reenergize and crystallize modern religious life in
our community. Rabbi Benjamin envisioned a Jacksonville
Jewish Center that would be a “magnificent structure, worthy
of the large, wealthy community” we were becoming. It would
have facilities for religious, social, cultural and educational
activities. It would attract and hold the interest of young
Jews. Rabbi Benjamin wanted to literally bring the school out
of the basement, out of the now deteriorating neighborhood,
and into beautiful classrooms that would inspire them. He
began by reorganizing B’nai Israel’s Talmud Torah.
Rabbi Benjamin had a profound affect on the
congregation. Within a short time of his arrival, the
membership increased to 200 families. He hired a professional
educator to be the principal of the Hebrew School. The
curriculum adopted by “the best Hebrew schools in the
country” was instituted. Classes for four levels of students
plus Bar Mitzvah were now taught upstairs in the vestry rooms
of B’nai Israel. A bus was engaged to bring children from all
over the city. Enrollment in May 1926, was 125 with a projection
of 200 students for that fall. Sessions continued
during the summer.
A Board of Education formed with Joseph
Witten as its chairman. Adult education classes
and programs were developed. All of Rabbi Samuel
Benjamin’s innovations have become familiar
features of Jewish education in our congregation,
and all generations since have benefited by them.
The Beginning of a New Era
On January 8,1928, the long cherished dream
of Rabbi Samuel Benjamin was realized with the
dedication of the Jacksonville Jewish Center on
the corner of 3rd and Silver Street, in Springfield.
The first payment on the Center’s site had been
made entirely from pledges of members of
Congregation B’nai Israel, and now they were its
charter members.
The building was a triumph. It housed a beautiful
sanctuary that would accommodate almost a thousand people,
a spacious auditorium with a stage for plays, lectures, meetings
and banquets, as well as game and meeting rooms. Although
most of the congregation now embraced Conservative Judaism,
the Center wished to attract all members of the Jewish
community, so it provided a chapel where Orthodox services
were held.
With education always at the forefront, a wing devoted
to the Hebrew School was attached. It had eight spacious
classrooms that could also be used for club activities. Ornate
architecture above the entrance proclaimed it “Talmud Torah
- Education and Social Service.” Though it was remodeled in
later years, most of us remember passing through these doors
on our way to Hebrew and Sunday Schools.
The Path of Progress
In 1932, Rabbi Morris Margolis, also a JTS graduate,
began a long and productive tenure. Despite the hardships of
the Depression, the Center maintained its educational
programs and even added new opportunities. A Junior
Congregation was instituted in 1934. In 1936, the Center
maintained the only Hebrew School in the vicinity with a
school building and permanent classrooms. Buses now
brought students in from the growing community of Riverside.
By 1938, education for youth was a 9-year program that
included the study of Jewish History, customs and ceremonies.
