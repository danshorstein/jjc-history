For the past 2500 years the synagogue has served as the
focal point of all Jewiish community life. Even when the
Temple in Jerusalem still stood, it was the synagogue that
served the community in each local area.
To this very day the synagogue serves the three-fold pur¬
pose of Jewish life, as a place of worship, of study and social
gathering. The pictures and drawings of the many synagogue
edifices of the American Jewish community show the wonder¬
ful facilities that congregations everywhere are providing for
their membership.
As the synagogue has preserved Jewish life down through
the generations, so must it continue to preserve Judaism today.
It can only do so if we permit it to be a part of our personal
and communal lives.
The cover of our Year Book portrays some of the beautiful
synagogues of our land that are serving as centers of their
communities. In moving counter clockwise, we see the beau¬
tiful edifices of Milburn, N. J., the Temple in Cleveland, Ohio,
the New York City Central Synagogue, B’nai Amoona in St.
Louis, Mo. and Chizuk Amuno in Baltimore, Md.
The Synagogue is the very "tree of life" of American Jewry.
It was there to deeply imbed its roots in the rock soil of Ameri¬
can culture and it is here today to help in the flowering of
Jewish life in our own midst.
