A human life is like a single letter in
the alphabet.
It can be meaningless. Or it can be part
of a great meaning.
And we do not want to be meaningless.
I low, then, do we find meaning?
We look, for example, at such things as
power, at money, at the great and special
talents of the artist, the scientist, the
statesman.
And always (no matter who we are) we
see someone who has more power, more
money, more talent than we have.
Do we then say: “His life has more
meaning than mine.”
If we do, we have made a familiar mistake.
We have forgotten the profound democ¬
racy of Creation: that the deepest de¬
lights and the most enduring meanings
of life come from the things all of us are
given, not from what just a few are given.
For all of us are given these things:
1.We have been given ourselves...
we’ve been given life: the edifice of our
body and mind, senses and spirit. No two
of us are alike, yet each of us has the
power to raise himself in the image of God.
2. We have been given power to love...
the gift of sharing ourselves with others,
of belonging to them, of living on in them,
the ability to love and to receive love.
3. We have been given power to serve...
the strength to be great enough to seek
out the needs of others without seeking
reward, to experience the delight which
comes from the act of service itself.
Let us be more specific.
The gift of living . . . Have you ever
discovered the wonder of the sky or the
rhythm of your heart? Have you evqr seen
night pass into day; a smile change a face?
The gift of love . . . Has a child ever
slipped his hand into yours just because
he wanted to walk beside you?
The gift of service . . . Have you ever
been fortunate enough to find tears in your
eyes because someone said “Thank you”
for something you wanted to give him?
w„ do these things delight us so?
They are endless . . . These delights are
creative, each generating further delight
for your days and years. Material pleasures
consume themselves. Gifts of the spirit
renew themselves. This is the paradox of
i
the quest for meaning in life: it is a vessel
which, the more you fill it with beauty,
love and service, the larger it becomes.
They enlarge us ... In the very process
of giving ourselves to these delights, we
grow, we change, become more sensitive,
more alive. To enjoy beauty or to under¬
stand a wise word makes you only more
aware of beauty and wisdom still to come.
They unite us . . . Power, money or even
our talents, when used as ends in them¬
selves, separate us from others. And what
divides us ultimately fails us. But beauty,
love and service bring us closer to others.
They unite us with ourselves and with
the world.

Rabbi’s Message
New Year’s Confession of Faith
I was asked by the Yearbook Committee to prepare a message before I
departed for my summer vacation. But since I am an occasional
procrastinator, I failed to do so before and find myself turning to this task,
only now, as Inge and I sit in an airplane which is winging its way northward
so that we can see the family and spend some time in their surroundings.
At thirty thousand feet the phone does not ring (a luxury we may soon be
losing) and the only other distraction is a crossword puzzle I am working -
and working with arrogant self-confidence - I am using a pen. The first
word definition that challenges me is “confession of faith” which I have
correctly interpreted as “credo” and I find that the question of my own
credo, with the High Holidays looming in the future, is obtruding itself into
my consciousness.
My own situation at the moment, sitting in an airplane flying into the night, carries with it a certain
illumination. Down below, the lights of cities and towns flicker up at me at fitful intervals and I derive a
great deal of comfort from them. There is, after all, a fear of uncertainty deep within me in this situation
and it mounts with the patches of darkness. I would be happier if my path were lit all the way. Even so, I
think, with my life and any life -to live is to be afraid. It would be good to enjoy light all along the path of
life. But even as I must sustain myself here with light interspersed with darkness, so in my own day to day
life - so in my religious life, I must settle for the occasional illumination, for the rare experience, for the
warmth of the moment. But even to get there, I must, as I do in the plane, keep travelling.
Meanwhile, I find that even when the black darkness surrounds the plane, there is a steady light within
the ship. The plane is capable of creating a world of its own within the universe. So, I suppose, the human
soul is able to create its own inscape of light and courage no matter what the surrounding landscape is
like.
I find, too, that I gain comfort from the sound of voices in my ears and from the steady hum of the
engines. So I suppose, we all draw strength from being part of a congregation of people. Perhaps one can
be religious alone; but it certainly helps to have other people who share your life and your situation and
your needs and it is sustaining to hear their voices together with your own.
Most of all, I am impressed with the capacities for faith that I and my fellow passengers enjoy. Our
airplane is guided through the night sky by invisible patterns: the stars, the compass, the radar. I have
faith in them, in the pilot, in the inspectors of the plane, in the man who is guiding our landing. In short, I
have faith in orderly natural processes and in invisible guiding hands. I find it easy to make the transition
to an Invisible Guiding Hand which sustains our universe.
As I consider these musings and the context in which they are taking place I realize that this is what life
is all about...and a fitting message for each of us to consider during these High Holidays as we pass
another milestone in our individual journey -life. We need these days for the moments of illuminating
wisdom and comfort they bring. We need the opportunity to enable the soul to speak to us with its
message of renewal. We need the voices of loved ones and of a congregation to assure us that we do not
travel alone. We need a sense of deeper meaning and purpose to what we know is all of reality - the
“Ground of our Existence” - one theologian called God.
I have been stating a religious credo. I find it easy to make the transition to the final end of any religious
system. It must be translated into acts of affirmation. We are about to land, and I note that without any
premeditation I am engaged in prayer, and so I have come the full circle. Starting with the “confession of
faith” which is “credo” I have naturally arrived at the affirmation of faith which is prayer.
It is a prayer that I also offer on my behalf and on Inge’s behalf that the coming year of 5746 be a sweet
and good, healthy and fulfilling year for you and your loved ones. Amen
Rabbi David Gaffney

Message from the Director
It is an honor for me to greet our Center members and friends through this annual Yearbook
which contains the history of our Congregation for the past year.
I have recently found time to browse through the many volumes of yearbooks that are available in
the Tofield Library. To read through these books, to see pictures of the many dedicated leaders of
blessed memory and those who are still active today, can only fill one with much pride.
It was easy to see that the step by step growth each year has evolved into the outstanding Synagogue Center that we have
today. A Center that offers inspiring services, excellent religious and educational programs, a Solomon Schechter Day
School, Pre-school, Day Care and Afternoon Hebrew and Hebrew High Schools. Also included are an all around youth
programs, a Day Camp, United Synagogue Youth Groups and a complete Scouting Program.
In looking back, it is most revealing to realize how much of our lives are connected with the Center. May we continue to set
our goals high and to back them up with our loyalty and continuous support.
My sincerest thanks to the entire office staff who cooperate and serve our membership and join me with warm wishes for a
good and peaceful year for all.
Blanche Slott

President’s Message
You Did It! And I’m Glad!
¡ü
mmm
In Red Skelton’s comic portrayal of the Mean Little Kid, he was often heard to say, “I
did it, and I’m glad.” Well, this past year, it might be better phrased, YOU DID IT! and I’m
glad!
Last year, at High Holiday services, I shared with you the difficult financial posture of
your congregation. I asked that you heed your synagogue’s cry - “SHMA KOLENU” - and
you did just that. m
Your contributions to the SHMA KOLENU APPEAL enabled us to repay our bank loan for the operating fund deficit in its
entirety and to retire outstanding debts to creditors.
Your adoption of my request to promptly meet financial obligations to the Center for membership dues and other charges is
responsible for our ability to maintain the necessary cash flow to stay current with our expenses and remain within the
balanced budget that your Board of Directors provided.
Our healthy financial posture has enabled us to maintain the many and varied programs and services that make us a great
synagogue. And we look forward, please GOD, to the capability of expanding our Center’s horizons in the coming months
without getting into debt.
I want to thank you once again for all your support, ... for your participation in the religious, social and cultural aspects of
the synagogue, for financial support, for voluntarism in so many areas, and for just being the sort of congregants that are
there when we need you. Together we can look forward to a vibrant and exciting New Year for our congregation.
May we go, together from strength to strength!
YOU DID IT! And I’m glad!
Joseph Honigman, M.D.

Simhat Torah Honorees
The Festival of Sukkot reaches its joyous climax on Simhat Torah, the last day of the Holiday. Simhat Torah is the happiest
holiday on the Jewish calendar because we celebrate our joy and pleasure with the Torah.
Because of Israel’s perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to in Jewish tradition as Israel’s “bride.” A tradition evolved in
ancient times to honor the individual called for the last reading of the Torah—the Hatan Torah or the groom of the Torah. The
person honored to begin the Torah reading from its beginning is called Hatan Bereshit the groom of Genesis. We also honor a
woman who has made a distinctive contribution to our Synagogue and to Jewish life in general — the Woman of Valor honor. We
honored these individuals at Services on Simhat Torah morning, Friday, October 19.
Abe Fages, Hatan Torah
Abe Fages performs a mitzvah everyday.
Ever since his retirement 6 years ago, he
has attended morning minyan everyday at
r
the Center. He not only goes to pray he very
often leads the services and has acquired the
reputation of “Baal Tefillah” or “master of
prayer.”
A very knowledgeable Jew, Abe can
always relate a conversation to a rabbinic
saying or teaching. He has a very positive
attitude and a friendly and outgoing nature.
Abe has been a respected member of the
religious committee for many years; is a
gabbai at Shabbat services; and is an active
member of Z.O.A. After serving the
Canadian Armed Forces, he was recognized
by the Canadian Jewish Congress for his
distinguished service and leadership.
Our “Hatan Torah” is a man who truly
deserves this honor. His wife, Diane, children
and grandchildren share in this joy.
Jeffery Morris, Hatan Bereshit
In 1977, fresh out of the University of
Florida, Law School, Jeffery Morris and
wife, Robin, decided to return to
Jacksonville...How lucky for us that they did.
Almost immediately they both became
active members of the Center; Robin, as a
member of the Hebrew teaching staff, and
Jeff, on the Finance committee. In a short
time, Jeffery assumed the position of
Treasurer of the congregation, a position he
has held for the last three years.
A Phi Beta Kappa and an editor of the
Univ. of Fla. Law Review, Jeff has always
been one to give each job his very best. He is
continually “on call,” when the Center needs
him, always working diligently to balance the
budget and decrease the deficit.
Although Jeff is modest about his
accomplishments, his family is openly very
proud of his achievements in school, his law
career and the quality of his family life. The
Center, too, is especially proud of this “home
grown” creation of its many educational and
religious programs.
We honor Jeffery Morris as “Bridegroom
of the Beginning,” vibrant, young and
groomed to be a leader.
Rhoda Goldstein, Woman of Valor
If Rhoda Goldstein is not at the Center
attending a meeting, she’s probably helping
her parents; showing a newcomer around
the city; shopping with her children or busy
with some other volunteer activity.
As young marrieds, Rhoda and her
husband, Arthur, became popular youth
advisors of the Charles Palot Youth Group.
As charter members of Ha'Rayim, the Center’s
young couples club, they both were always
quick to share their creative talents. But, it is
in the area of youth and education that
Rhoda “gives her all.” After serving many
years on the Education committee, Rhoda
became its co-chairman, working daily on
perfecting the Center’s educational
programs. Strong-willed and perservering,
Rhoda has firmly represented the high ideals
and goals of the congregation.
Our “Woman of Valor” is a devoted
daughter, helpful mother, and caring wife,
sister and friend. She has been a P.T.A.
president, a Sisterhood Vice-President and
member of many Center committees.
Although her daily life is very hectic, she
manages to always stay calm and collected,
and still finds the time for “everyone.”

The Torah says “Rise up before the hoary head.” This was interpreted
by R. Judah B. Tema (circa 105-200 CE) as “The Age of Sixty.” Our
SISTERHOOD has been honored by the Florida Branch for being
SIXTY. We’d like to acknowledge and thank all those who have
. «r worked so diligently to enable us to reach this milestone. How lucky
]y 07 we are to have lived to see this happy occasion.
This past year we gave a tea at Mt. Carmel to encourage our
members there to participate in all our activities. A Savannah Bus
Safari provided a chance to explore a nearby community. Four of us
represented SISTERHOOD, in November, at the Biennial
Convention of Women’s League at The Concord. We came back
with new ideas, new merchandise for our excellent Judaica Shop and the determination to get new
people involved in every project. At the Jewish Family Living Seminar at Daytona’s Temple Israel we
discussed inter-faith challenges to the nuclear family. Joint Sisterhood at The Temple allowed us time
to learn about the law and visit with friends from other congregations. After long hours of rehearsals,
eight of us headed for Orlando and the Florida Branch Spring Conference where we not only
performed the D-N-A skit but were recognized for 60 years of affiliation and visual techniques.
What did we do at home? Well... we started the year learning about Rosh Hashonah and an
entertaining and informative program on Mikvah; quickly followed by another successful Art Auction.
In October, Rabbis Gaffney and Kentof spoke about the holiday of Sukkot while we busily strung fruit
and vegetables to be hung in the Goldman Sukkah. Marilyn Henry, of the Times Union staff, told of her
experiences as a volunteer in Israel at Paid Up Membership. New members were introduced and
presented with a rose.
This year we tried something new. In cooperation with PTA, SISTERHOOD participated in their
Book Fair while learning about reading disabilities and hearing short reviews of new children’s books.
Some of us got to serve food at Grandparents’ Chanukah.
January means TORAH FUND: 5 guardians and 22 benefactors plus many supporters who buy
Torah Fund cards to support the Jewish Theological Seminary. At Women’s League Shabbat we
honored the memory of our founder Mathilde Schechter and participated in services as well as
sponsoring the Kiddush.
Gene Kornblum did the book review at SISTERHOOD/LIBRARY SHABBAT after the Kiddush we
sponsored. Yum Center made hamentashen to serve at Purim and sell at the Joint Sisterhood
Purim carnival. Music-in-the-Round was a successful attempt to combine Jewish music with a social
evening. The Card Party funds bought new chaffing dishes for the kitchen.
Our Rosh Chodesh service and program paid tribute to Emma Lazarus in this year of restoring the
Statue of Liberty. There were 13 B’nai Mitzvah who participated in their special weekend. Linda
Bressler and Alina Kentof presented a very moving program on the role of the Chevrah Kaddisha in
our community. At DONOR, the ladies who support our pledge to building fund were treated to the
professional production of “Murder at Howard Johnson’s.”
During the year we also engaged in many fund-raising projects: floral arrangements, New Year’s
cards, personalized books, family portraits, calendar. There were special reports on Israel affairs,
Soviet Jewry, and social actions that needed our attention.
Have I forgotten anything? I hope not. I have tried to show you what a busy and varied program your SISTERHOOD has to offer.
SISTERHOOD is people. Women like you and me. Women who have needs, talents and ideas. There really is room for each of us.
Won’t you carve out your little niche? Make a place where you can contribute your unique skills. Y’all come.
May we all be inscribed for a good year - a year of peace ... Shalom
Wendy

assistants, travelled to Goldhead State Park for an overnight Shabbat campout. We began our activities with the Friday night
Shabbat services followed by a family discussion of pertinent issues of Jewish life. Afterwards, Dov arranged a night stalking game
in which parents and their children were left, (not far) in the wilds, to work together and get back to camp before Dov could notice.
Once back in camp, we sat by a warm campfire sitting, talking, and just being together. We all, then, bedded down for the night,
each family in their own tent; father and son, mother and daughter sometimes laying close together to stay warm. Arising early
Saturday morning, we awoke to the smell and warmth of a campfire on a crisp morning. Breakfast was followed by Shabbat
services and a day of hiking through beautiful woods, viewing a 30,000 year old lake, wading through a cold stream with mini¬
waterfalls and deep spots suitable for falling in (which some of the parents and children did.) At the end of the day we went to the
beach and played an Israeli stick game and just enjoyed the fun of being together. We returned to camp for dinner, and as night
began to fall, we had Havdalah services before driving back to civilization.
This experience was unique for me as it was my first time camping with my children. It was a treasured time as we, as a father and
son, worked together, played together, and sang together in a most enjoyable religious family experience. This was truly quality
family time.
I want to take this opportunity to thank Dov and Alina for a most enjoyable weekend; one filled with learning, Jewish life
enrichment, and togetherness with family and friends. I also want to recommend this experience to all of you. I, personally, look
forward to other such outings; to other such valuable family life experiences.
Sheldon J. Kaplan, an appreciative parent”

Holocaust Commemoration
April 14, 1985
We survived . . .
And must remember . . .
To build a better and more secure future
It should never have happened but it did.
So we tell the story to every generation. The story of how six million Jews were annihilated by fire, torture, starvation and other
abominable means. Between the years 1939-1945 Jewish homes, Jewish businesses, Jewish neighborhoods -- and Jewish people
were destroyed by a systematic program of genocide directed by Adolf Hitler.
To memorialize these victims of the Holocaust, a special Day of Rememberance has been proclaimed. To observe this day, the
Jacksonville Jewish Federation and the area synagogues held a Commemoration at the Center.
The Center was transformed into a Holocaust museum. The walls were filled with pictures, artwork, and creative writings by
children of our community. Jews and non-Jews toured our building...viewing a time in history that will never be forgotten. The tour
ended, however, on a happy note...the creation of the State of Israel, our hope for the future.
We are grateful to Rabbi Dov Kentof who created and directed the museum, and to Beverly Barnhard, mural artist.

Early Childhood Department
School is many things:
it is plunging fingers into sticky clay ... it is the wonderful gooey world
of finger paints ... it is playing games in the big gymnasium and
enjoying time out of doors on the playground ... it is learning to share
... and most of all, it is developing self-confidence, the attitude that “I
can do.”
Your children are carefully provided with many experienced that assure them success. They are guided
by skilled teachers who show warmth and affection to our little ones as their horizons expand beyond home.
Our Pre-School offers a variety of programs for young children. The educational program is based on the
developmental needs of individual children. The basic goals of early childhood education includes
structuring the environment to provide opportunities for children which aid in their physical, emotional and
intellectual growth. The program includes reading readiness, math concepts, science, social studies, motor
development, creative arts and music. Our Pre-School emphasizes fun and meaningful Jewish experiences,
which includes weekly Shabbat programs and holiday celebrations. Our Day Care is designed to nurture the
inquisitive, outgoing spirit in our children in a safe and happy environment. It has proven to be a very
successful part of our program.
Each child in our school is an individual. We also recognize that every family has different needs and
interests. I want to meet every family and child so that together we can find a program that meets the needs
of your child. 1 have an open door policy and you are encouraged to visit at any time.
Marilyn Sandler

determine our future progression.

Center Employees Retire
Kenneth Bowden
In 1958, through the efforts of Philip Selber and Jack
Shortstein, Kenneth Bowden assumed the position of
comptroller of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Many of his responsibilities included: Building Fund,
supervision of the cemetery, accounts payable,
transportation and payroll. During his tenure he has seen
a ten fold increase in the budget. Bowden’s greatest
challenge, however, was the sucessful sale of the building
on Third and Silver Streets.
Upon his retirement, this sports enthusiast will now be
able to devote more time to his other avocations - travel
and reading.
“I have always tried to do a good job,” Bowden says,
“which was made easier by the contagious enthusiasm
exhibited by the Center employees and volunteers who
encouraged me to do my very best.”
Deeply devoted to the Center, Kenneth Bowden
leaves with fond memories and sincere wishes for the
Center’s continued well-being.
We wish you, —Kenneth Bowden— only the very
best.

Message from the
Youth and Education Director
Rabbi Dov Kcntof
The purpose of the Educational and Youth program of the Jacksonville Jewish Center is to bridge the invisible gap between the
home, the school and the synagogue. Our schools and youth activities offer a unique opportunity to stimulate educational, spiritual
and social growth and development within a Jewish environment.
The programming is designed to offer the individual the chance to promote self-identity within an ever-changing world, and to
express one-self in a fun and relaxed atmosphere. The expressed view of the school and youth program is seen as the informal
phase of our total educational endeavor. The youth program is the laboratory in which the values taught in the school are
implemented in real life situations. The purpose of the total educational program, formal and informal are: camping programs,
conventions and projects. All this is to convert our young people who are Jews by accident of birth into Jews by choice.
This year we are offering an ambitious total program in and out of the classroom. I invite you, the parent, and your children
to participate in this endeavor and to enjoy these rich experiences that will last a lifetime.
I would like to add my wishes with Alina, Gil and Tali for a happy new year and a fulfillment of all your desires for the coming
year.

Experts agree that the survival of Judaism depends on the commitment of our youth today.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center sponsors three outstanding youth groups for youngsters in grades 5-12. Each group meets

Summer Programs
United Synagogue of America offers varied summer programs for youth of all ages. Many of our Center youth participated in
these programs.
U.S.Y. Pilgrimage
USY ISRAEL PILGRIMAGE is a six week program
serving over five hundred USYers and staff members.
Included in this trip are touring, classes, and the chance to
spend time with one of the Mesorati (Conservative)
Synagogues in Israel. The staff consists of outstanding
Rabbis, Jewish educators, and youth workers from the
United States, Canada, and Israel. Participants: Cindy
Goldstein, Michael Lublin, Karen Manasse, Laura Zel.
Manya and David Lazarus prepare to board plane for “Wheels”
program.
U.S.Y. On Wheels
USY ON WHEELS is a six and one half week bus tour
across the United States and Canada, with nearly two
hundred USYers participating in a program of intensive
Jewish living and learning. An integral aspect of the
WHEELS program is “Home Hospitality” in which
Conservative Congregations host the Wheelniks in their
homes. About 25% of the summer is spent in “Home
Hospitality”. The balance of the nights are spent in hotel and
motels. Each group of forty-five USYers travels with four
staff members who are experienced both as educators and
youth leaders. A luxurious, air-conditioned, chartered bus,
driven by a professional bus driver, is the “second home”
for WHEELniks during this great summer experience.
Among the places visited by WHEELS from coast to coast
are Disneyland, the Grand Canyon, Mount Rushmore, and
major cities, such as Los Angeles, San Francisco, Dallas and
Washington, D.C.
Participants: Manya and Davis Lazarus and Alyse Slaff.

CAMP RAMAH
Adds a New Dimension
to Your Child’s Growth as a Jew
Camp Ramah provides the opportunity of a total Jewish living experience for children and teenagers in the
relaxed atmosphere of a summer camp. Values built on a shared experience and emotion reach our sons and
daughters in a manner impossible in a formal school program. Over the years, RAMAH has helped instill a deeper

White Water Rafting
Chattooga . . . “Eggie in the Hole II”
Once again a group of 15 adventurous pioneers, (14 males, 1 female), followed our fearless leader Rabbi Dov “Rambo” Kentof
into the wilds of north Georgia and North Carolina. The purpose of this outing was to experience the challenge of white water
rafting down the mighty Chattooga River.
Our trip started off on an historical note with a grand tour of Athens, Ga, conducted by our number one Bulldog, Ronnie Pollock.
It consisted of a van ride around Sanford Stadium and a brief history of Georgia football. His intention was to convert a few Gators
to “Dawgs.”
During the camping trip many of us shared experiences that will never be forgotten. Just imagine, services in the van while driving
on narrow mountain roads, sleeping in tents, discussions around the camp fire late at night, and spending an afternoon in Cherokee
in a launderette teaching Dov, Ronnie and Alan how to wash and dry clothes for sixteen people. However, the most important
experience was watching twelve young people work together to become a totally self-sufficient and independent group. They were
completely responsible for setting up camp and cooking our meals.
Once again I would like to express my thanks to all the campers for making this such a memorable trip. I know I can speak for my
fellow advisors, Ronnie Pollock and Alan Cohen, that it was a privilege to spend five days with such a terrific group.
A special thank you goes to Rabbi Kentof; without his love, direction, and supervision a trip such as this would not be possible. I
only wish that everyone could have the opportunity to experience this kind of campout.

Robert Cantor

The Jacksonville Community Council was founded in 1935 and
became the Jacksonville Jewish Federation in 1979. This year we
are celebrating our 50th anniversary and are continuing to serve
the Jewish community in a variety of ways.
On June 5, 1983 we moved into our new building at 10829 Old
St. Augustine Road. The Jacksonville Jewish Federation is the
central fundraising, planning, budgeting and coordinating agency
for the Jewish community. Annually the Federation coordinates
and funds the annual Yom Hashoah Observance (Holocaust
Remembrance Program) and Yom Ha’atzmaut Celebration (Israel
Independence Day) programs in cooperation with our synagogues.
The major function of the Federation is to conduct the Annual
U.J.A./Federation Campaign which raises funds for local, national
and overseas Jewish obligations. The major beneficiary of this
fundraising effort is the United Jewish Appeal. Our local
beneficiaries are River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged, Jewish
Family & Community Services and the Kosher Nutrition Program.
The Federation also has a very active Women’s Division and is in
the process of implementing a Leadership Development Program.
The Federation also publishes the Commentator, our monthly
newspaper, which covers local, national and international issues of
Jewish interest.

The National Council of Jewish Women is the nation’s oldest women’s volunteer organization. Since its birth in 1893 NCJW has

community action and advocacy, focusing their attention on the priority areas of AGING, CHILDREN AND YOUTH, WOMEN’S

kosher Meals-on-wheels. PACE program, “practical & cultural education for dependent girls.”

communities locally, nationally, and internationally.

Jacksonville Hevrah Kaddisha
The Jewish way of death is one part of a philosophy of life in which all people are endowed with dignity and given respect. Even
after death, the body which once held a human soul retains its sanctity. Our sages have compared a corpse to an impaired Torah
scroll which, although no longer usuable, retains its holiness.
Tradition dictates that from the moment of death until burial, the body of the deceased must be attended by ashomer, a guard.
The importance of this mitzvah, is emphasized by the fact that the shomer is relieved of all other religious obligations during his
watch.
The body should be buried as soon as possible, usually within one day, except on the Sabbath; recent traditional practice allows
slight delays if a close relative or friend must travel long distances to attend the funeral.
Before burial, the body is to be cleansed by tahara, ritual washing.
The body is then dressed in tachrichim; clean, simple, white, handmade shrouds. Close to two thousand years ago, Rabban
Gamliel noted that the rich were buried in ostentatious clothing, which embarrassed the poor who could not afford them; he
insisted that he be buried in plan linen shrouds, and the custom became a central symbol of the egalitarianism of Jewish burial
practice.
The use of the plainest wooden coffin is required—to maintain the sense of equality in death and to follow the principle: “Dust
you are and unto dust you shall return.” The coffin must be made entirely of wood and must not retard the natural decomposition
of the body.
Since burial must be prompt, there should be no public viewing of the body, nor should there be embalming or cosmetic work
which are regarded as mutiliation and desecration. Today, embalming is only permitted by Jewish law in those instances when it is
required by civil law. Cremation, regarded as a total desecration is not permitted.
These traditions have been supervised in Jewish communities by a chevra kadisha—sacred society—which consists of
volunteers who aid the bereaved and ensure that law and custom are followed. Assisting in the preparation and burial of the body
is a highly valued mitzva and is considered a chesed shel emet, an act of kindness performed without ulterior motive, because the
dead cannot repay this act of kindness. Membership in a chevra kadisha has always been considered an honor, bestowed upon
the pious of the community. The Jacksonville community is fortunate to have a functioning Chevra Kaddisha active since 1974.
The members perform the necessary tasks on a rotating basis in committees of two men or two women. New members are
welcome and needed to continue these services important to our fellow Jews.
For further information call Joel Bressler at 733-4400.

The United Synagogue of America, founded in 1913 by Solomon Schechter, is the association of
more than 830 Conservative congregations in the United States and Canada. It functions through
commissions, departments and constituent organizations, and maintains regional offices in many cities
throughout the United States and Canada. The United Synagogue co-ordinates its program with The
Jewish Theological Seminary of America and the Rabbinical Assembly and is a constituent member of
the World Council of Synagogues.
