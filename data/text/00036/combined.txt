THIS IS MY SYNAGOGUE. It is composed of people like me. We make it what it is.
It will be warm and friendly if I am.
Its pews will be filled, if I help to fill them.
The sounds of joyous prayer and worship,
Of expressions of gratitude and hope
will be heard from within its walls,
If I join in its chorus.
It will do great work, if I work.
It will be a meeting point for the ages
If I can learn to revere what was worthy in the past,
and work to extend these values
through my life to the future.
It will be a home for an eternal people
If I am sensitive enough to
Catch a glimpse of that eternity.
Therefore, with the help of God, and
with my dedicated efforts,
I consecrate myself to the task of being all these things
which I want my synagogue to be.
It will reach out with concern to Israel,
to our dispersed in every land, to all of mankind,
If I can learn to extend my hand.
It will be a synagogue of loyalty and
Love, of fearlessness and faith,
a synagogue with a noble spirit
If I, who make it what it is, am filled with these.

Rabbinic Ordination of Martin Berman
RABBI MARTIN BERMAN
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is proud to announce
the Rabbinic Ordination of Martin Berman at the Jewish
Theological Seminary on Sunday, May 15, 1977. The
religious schools of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
provided him with much of his early Jewish background. He
attended Jewish Center schools from 3y2 years of age
through Confirmation.
According to Martin, “two of the men who provided me
with a fine example of the study of Torah and with a devoted
pattern of Jewish living were my teachers at the Center,
Ted Gross and Philip Selber.” During his pre-teens and
early teenage years, he was a member of the Center’s
Troop 14 Boy Scouts of America. He was also an active
member of U.S.Y. In his senior year he was elected
President of the chapter. From age 14 until recently he
spent his summers at Camp Ramah both as a camper and
later as a staff member. “Probably more than anything else
my experiences at Camp Ramah influenced me into taking
the course of life upon which I am presently embarking,” he
said.
Martin attended Yeshiva University receiving a B.A. in
Psychology in 1972. That same year he received an A.A.
from the James Striar School of Jewish Studies. He entered
the Jewish Theological Seminary of America and received
an M.A. in Rabbinics in 1975. On May 15, 1977 his long
awaited dream was fulfilled when he was granted Rabbinic
Ordination. He is married to the former Marilyn Gottlieb of
St. Louis Park, Minnesota, and they have a young son,
Etan.
The entire Center Family extends congratulations to
his parents, Mr. and Mrs. Harry Berman; grandparents,
Mrs. Louis Wolfson and Mr. Sam Berman, as well as the
other members of the family.
On August 1, 1977 Rabbi Martin Berman became the
spiritual leader of Shaare Zion Synagogue in Sioux City,
Iowa.

TORAH READERS
“This Book of the Law Shall Not
Depart Out of Thy Mouth”
This commandment to Joshua became the basis for
the continuing centrality of the Torah in Jewish life. The
institution of the regular readings of the Torah, which has
continued in force to the present day, is one of our oldest
forms of worship and public instruction. Readers not only
render an important service to the congregation, they also
place emphasis on the fact that in Judaism education is
paramount. It is no wonder then that this skill has been
highlighted in our educational procedures. The most
dedicated and gifted of our students are capable of chanting
the weekly and holiday portions during all of their teen
years.
Included in these pictures are the current group of
readers. However, many have continued to participate well
beyond their Confirmation year. Not included is the
sizeable number of parents, grandparents and relatives who
have been trained to chant special portions at Bar Mitzvah
occasions.
Reviewing the portion of the week with Mr. Gross
are Mark Deutsch, Jeff Presser and Bill Meisel.

SIMHAT TORAH CELEBRATION — SUNDAY, OCTOBER 17. 1976
CONGREGATION TO HONOR THREE LEADERS FOR OUTSTANDING SERVICE
Simhat Torah, the happiest holiday in the Jewish calendar is celebrated to display our joy and plQiiiire
of the Torah.
Each week on the Sabbath a portion from the Torah is read at Services. In that way it takes an en¬
tire year to complete the readings from the five books in the Torah. On Simhat Torah the last chapter is
completed and we start again from the beginning to repeat the cycle for another year of Torah reading and
study. This procedure is in keeping with the idea that Torah study is endless as well as continually enlightening.
A special ceremony marks this event on Simhat Torah. Two persons are called to the Torah. One is
called Hatan Torah (Bridegroom of the Torah) for the concluding section and the other is called Hatan
Bereshit (Bridegroom of the Beginning) to start the cycle over again.
Four years ago a custom was begun at the Jacksonville Jewish Center to honor individuals, for their
special service to our congregation and Jewish life. Simhat Torah has been designated as the official oc¬
casion for this recognition.

Why does a Conservative synagogue support and
promote Camp Ramah?
Camp Ramah provides the opportunity to offer a total
Jewish living experience for children and teenagers in the
relaxed and enjoyable atmosphere of a summer camp.
Values built on shared experience and emotion reach our
sons and daughters in a manner impossible in a formal
school program. Over the years, Ramah has helped instill a
deeper commitment to Judaism in many of its participants.
The Center believes that benefits accrue to the
congregation, not just to the individual. We have therefore
made monies available to reduce the financial cost for
qualified students regardless of need. These monies are not
part of the regular Center budget, but rather are raised by
donations to the Myra Thier Lazarus Ramah Fund, through
Sisterhood, and from other sources.
Two families have helped us greatly. The Gordon
family has established a scholarship fund in memory of the
beloved Robert J. Gordon. A long list of deserving boys and
girls have been able to go to Ramah because of this
scholarship.
Abe Selber established in his will a perpetual trust for
specific Jewish educational purposes. With the full support
of his children, Iris and Franklin, who have never lost their
interest in the Center, the proceeds of this trust have also
been used to send campers to Ramah.
It is our hope that the Camp Ramah experience will
continue to be a valuable complement to a formal Jewish
education.

MESSAGE FROM THE RABBI
Dear Friends:
Physical fitness has become something of a cult in
American life. Nowadays almost everyone we know is
walking, jogging or exercising in one way or another, either
before work or after dinner. Yoga, swimming and tennis are
more popular than ever before. Diet books sell as well as
cookbooks and most of our friends watch what as well as
how much they eat. I know I am trying. The sports crowd is
huge and the arena and stadium is as much an "in place” to
be as is (or was) the concert or lecture hall. We, in the
Jewish community, share these environs with all.
It isn’t an altogether new phenomenom for Jews. While
we have never been distinguished as a community of
"jocks” — saychel was more an achievement than muscle,
the Jewish world of pre-Maccabean and Maccabean times
was divided between the Hellenists and the Hassidim or
committed Hebraists. The former, which probably
outnumbered the latter, were exponents and devotees of
Greek culture with its heavy emphasis on the "body
beautiful”, phyical fitness and athletics. If complaints found
in Jewish literature of that period are to be believed, many
seemed to flock to the ancient equivalents of the Dolphin or Gator games as went to Shul. And undoubtedly, many in the
community clucked their tongues disapprovingly and said “What will become of the next generation?”
The French have a phrase for it: “The more things change, the more they remain the same.”
What would we have done if we lived in those ancient days? A fast chariot race in the stadium or probing the wisdom of an
ancient Jewish text? Who knows? But this we do know, we would not be here as Jews, nor would our children if the Jewish
community of 168 B.C.E. did not number in its ranks some zealous Hebraists who thought the “Shema” more precious than the
“shvitz”. Certainly history informs us that a goodly number were prepared to lay down their lives for Torah, prayer, Jewish life
and all it represents.
And this is where the synagogue comes in. If the Jews are a healthy, “passionate people” as one author recently suggested, it
is because these passions were directed towards something eternal, something lasting, something more than the ephemeral
interest in something passive. We are a people that shows a genius for surviving, a readiness to score points for God, for Torah,
for love of life, for moral purposes and for a tradition which edifies and makes for Menshlicheit out of the raw, unformed clay
which can, when activated, become human. The synagogue is the scene of this passion. It is the yearning of consciousness
against the marketplace of adulterated values. It is where the great stages of life from birth to death are witnessed. It is the setting
in which the fellowship of our people becomes a family and where Judaism can become the joyful experience it can be for
ourselves and our children.
This is our synagogue. It represents the future promise only if we are committed, as we, who are here as Jews today, give
meaning to the lives of those ancient Hebraists. Let us if we must continue to exercise muscle, watching weight and waistline. Let
us not forget, however, that the soul and spirit need their exercise too.
Inge, the family and I wish you and yours the blessing of a happy and healthy New Year —
and peace.
a life of contentment, fulfillment
Rabbi David Gaffney

Message From The President
Sheldon Gendzier
Once again, the Jacksonville Jewish Center faces a
critical year.
The move to our new home, the climax of years of
hoping and striving, presented new problems of
unprecedented magnitude. These problems must be
solved so that we can go on with the proper goals of our
institution.
Last year we faced absolute crisis. The debt for the
building appeared overwhelming, the accumulated
operating deficit was at an all time and virtually
unmanageable level, and the operating budget was still
unbalanced.
A dedicated group took the story of the needs of the
Building Fund to the congregation first as a whole and then
to the individual members. A sufficient number responded
to avert the crisis. We have not solved the problem, but we
now have orderly plans by which with continued
congregational support our obligations can be met. The
crisis was very real and required the efforts of a large part of
the leadership to the exclusion of other worthwhile goals.
We asked for substantial increases in dues. Enough
responded so that by adopting an attitude of extreme
frugality in regard to expenditures we could defer the
problem of the operating budget until this year. Now this
problem must be resolved.
During this year we must show an appreciable
reduction in the accumulated deficit. We must increase
income to cover the expenditures necessary to accomplish
our program.
We have deferred salary increases for one year. We
are going to have another year of frugality toward
expenditures. We must convince those members who did
not do so last year to accept their fair share of our budget
burden through an increase in dues.
We cannot allow ourselves to continue to operate in
this manner. We expect commitment from our staff and
they are entitled to equitable compensation. We cannot
forever defer essential expenses. We cannot allow
ourselves to starve vital programs.
Proper financing is an absolutely necessary means to
an end. But, it is the means - not the end. We must place
the leadership of this institution to give time and attention to
those ends instead of concentrating on financing.
We, as an institution, have many obligations. We must
meet the religious needs of our members. We must provide
Jewish education for our children. We must provide
activities that will strengthen the ties of our youth toward
Judaism. We must provide social, cultural and educational
opportunities for our adult congregants as well as for our
children and youth. Operating within the philosophy of the
Conservative Movement, we must be a force for Judaism
for our congregants, among all Jews, and within the general
community.
We should be setting goals and working towards them.
It is clear that our success depends on participation by
members of the congregation. Staff can guide, staff can
assist, but staff cannot accomplish the goals of the Center.
An involved participating membership is mandatory.
It is our hope and it can be a realistic hope that the next
President of the Center can write his message next year
concentrating on the programs and the valid goals of this
institution.
Sheldon Gendzier

“Hazak Ve’Ematz Me’od, Be Strong and Very
Courageous” (from Joshua 1.7) is our theme for a second
year. As Joshua leads the nation into the Promised Land, he
is charged with a tremendous task. He is faced with a
strenuous military campaign and must also remain faithful
to God’s moral law, which requires greater strength of
character and steadfastness of purpose than any military
conquest.
If there is to be a future for our descendants, greater
strength of character and steadfastness of purpose is also
required of us. Active participation in Sisterhood can be the
first step in securing our Jewish future. Through our
programs and projects we offer a variety of religious,
educational, social, cultural, and recreational activities.
Members are kept informed through a monthly newsletter,
Sisterhood Chai-Lites.
This year the Center Sisterhood sponsored Art 77 as
well as the sale of calendars, cookbooks with favorite
recipes of our own members, key chains, New Year cards,
and silver polish. For donor we held a “kitchen shower” to
raise funds for our Building Fund pledge to pay for the
kitchen. We completed our third Sisterhood tablecloth with
the names of members and their families beautifully
designed and embroidered. We sponsored holiday
observances for Simhat Torah, Hanukkah, Purim, and
Pesach. We provided leadership for Brownies and Girl
Scouts, and we welcomed the Seventeenth Annual Spring
Conference of the Florida Branch of Women’s League for
Conservative Judaism to our community April 24 — 27. It
was the first time the conference had been held in the
northern part of the state in eleven years!
The last feather in our cap was oversubscribing our
Torah Fund quota by $1,000. This enables our Sisterhood
to be listed on the Patron Founders Plaque of the newly
opened Mathilde Schechter Residence Hall. The opening of
this dormitory last fall marked the realization of an eighteen
year dream and campaign to provide a suitable place for
women to live while they attend classes at the Jewish
Theological Seminary in New York. Our own Karen
Mizrahi is one of the first year residents.
As we begin our fifty-third year of Sisterhood, we boast
a membership of more than four hundred women. Join us
this year in Sisterhood activities. There’s a place waiting for
you! Together we will work to achieve the continuity for
Jewish survival. It depends on each of us.

GIRL SCOUT TROOP 162
BROWNIES
Brownie Troop #147 had a marvelous year with 15
enthusiastic second and third graders. Although the
majority of the girls attended the Solomon Schechter Day
School, we also had a representative group from Beauclerc
and Hendricks Avenue schools.
The Troop was under the leadership of Ellen Schnipper
and Marcia Selmanoff who kept the Brownies very busy
with varied activities including crafts, painting and sewing.
Monthly birthdays were celebrated with special
“goodies” a la Dunkin’ Donuts. Trips included a visit to the
Schnipper home where the girls picked oranges and
participated in International Scout Day at which time the
girls gave a performance of Israeli dancing.
GIRL SCOUTS
The Girl Scouts of Troop 162 met in the white house on
the Center Campsite every Tuesday from 3:00 to 5:00 p.m.
We did a lot of interesting activities including a visit to a
ranch owned by a Cadet Troop Leader. We learned all
about horses - the parts of their bodies, the different gaits
and how to groom them. After learning about the horses,
we all had a turn at riding.
Other activities included two trips to a bowling alley
where we divided the T roop into two teams, each trying for
the best score. A real fun time was a trip to Noble Roman’s
Pizza Place where we could make our own pizzas, putting
on anything but meat.
All the girls in the troop worked on the sports merit
badge. The requirements were not too difficult. We had to
be able to play sting-ray, kickball and bowling, showing that
we could play fair and be a good sport by not quitting in the
middle of a game.
We had someone come to the meeting to teach us how
to do liquid embroidery using tubes of paint to make designs
on a special kind of cloth. We also learned how to do
macrame and to make earrings.
We took orders and sold Girl Scout cookies, baked
cakes and brownies for Girl Scout Sabbath, taught the
Brownies Israelii dances which we all did for International
Day with other troops in the district.
The year ended with a swim party and cookout at the
Campsite along with the Fly-up ceremony when Brownies
move up to Scouting.
The year culminated with the Brownie-Girl Scout Fly-
up Ceremony which was held at the Center Day Camp and
Picnic pool party at the Selmanoff s home.

Ha’Rayim is the young couples club of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. It is one of the most vibrant groups of the
Center. The group consists of couples mostly in the 20 - 40
age group who come together periodically to socialize and
share Jewish experiences.
Our year began with a Selichot Social. We combined a
very social atmosphere with a stimulating Jewish Identity
Program. The evening was very thought provoking and
enjoyable.
The major event of the year for Ha’Rayim was the Poor
Man’s Raffle. It was a big undertaking but worth while. Its
success can best be shown on the pictures on the preceding
pages. Lois and Alan Chepenik were our grand prize
winners.
In the spring we had our annual Hav-a-Nosh. Since
Ha’Rayim consists of couples from all over the United
States and the world, we decided to have an evening
devoted to hometown foods. Everything was served from
New York Egg Cream to Australian Pavlova. For
entertainment we placed Backgammon, cards and Twister.
It has always been our policy to invite newcomers to the
community to all our functions. Our get-togethers are
always informal, the atmosphere is always warm and
hospitable and the people are the friendliest.
Very Special plans have been made for this coming
year; the New Year’s Eve celebration is one of them.

CENTER DAY CAMP
The Center Day Camp had it all for the summer of 1977! Ask any camper.
The varied acitivities, programmed for all ages, from the half-day campers (3 and
4 years) to Pre-CITs (8th — 9th grade), kept everyone on their toes to keep it all
together.
Morning assembly gave each group the opportunity to participate and show
their originality by presenting songs and skits.
A typical Camp day consisted of instructional swim, free swim, boating,
skiing, arts n’ crafts, camp crafts, competitive sports including softball, tennis (on
the new courts donated by Mr. Alexander Brest), ping pong, archery and
paddleball.
As the only Jewish Camp in the area, the Center Day Camp is available to all
Jewish children regardless of affiliation. Each day includes Judaica in the form of
singing, dancing, games, prayer before and after lunch and a Shabbat program
each Friday. We also have stories, playground, hikes, field trips and optional
horseback riding lessons. Exciting trips and events included an all camp
scavenger hunt, the annual Carnival, a visit from the Story Lady who came
dressed in costume, guided tours of the nature trails at Tree Hill and the North Florida University, roller skating and
bowling.
Weekly visits to the Haydon Burns Library were always a treat as well as the overnights at the Camp for Kindergarten,
1st, 2nd and third grades and overnights at state parks for older campers — Goldhead, Fort Clinch and Hannah Park. All
camp-outs were preceded by instruction, teaching the Camper how to select a site, pitch a tent, gather wood, make a fire
and cook.
The arts n’ crafts room was a constant hub of activity giving campers the opportunity to create, design and just enjoy
the satisfaction that comes from seeing the finished craft of their own making. The projects were many including all types of
necklaces, ecology boxes, mobiles, candleholders, sand art, leathercraft and silk screening.
The Center Day Camp is open each summer for a nine
week period including four 2-week sessions, one special week
and weekly half day sessions for 3 and 4 year olds. We are
proud that we do not just offer activities, but instruct and teach
the camper how to participate and get the most enjoyment out
of each camp day by learning how to get along with others and
the practice of good sportsmanship.
When making summer plans for your children, be sure to
include all or part of the nine-week program at the Center Day
Camp.

By far the largest collection of Judaica in northern Florida, the Library has
serviced students from local universities doing research on Judaic topics, as well as
children and adults from the Center and the Jewish community. Its distinguished
collection of books contains Jewish literature, both ancient and modern, Jewish
history, Bible commentaries, Rabbinic literature, Prayer and Theology.
Beginning September 1959 and with the personal library of Rabbi Tofield added
in 1961, the Library has continued to expand during the past 17 years. It has
benefited from the expert guidance and devoted attention of Roselyn Selber and
Charlotte Glossman. Updated constantly by the librarians, dozens of new
publications have been purchased annually.
In addition to the more than 3800 volumes which constitute the collection, the
Library receives nearly two dozen Jewish periodicals. These include Commentary,
Judaism, National Jewish Monthly, Jewish Digest, Israel Digest, Moment, Sh’ma, Conservative Judaism, Present Tense,
Jerusalem Post, Jewish Week - and nearly a dozen more monthly or quarterly publications of Jewish interest.
Two problems are presently confronting the Library at this point in its development. Over the past year or two, various
individuals have borrowed books without having checked them out. Others have borrowed books and have failed to return
them after months of use. During the next month the Library will be pleased to receive back all books currently checked out - no
overdue fees being charged - in order to complete its inventory.
A second problem is more in the nature of a challenge. Greatly expanding its children’s section, the Library is now actively
serving the Solomon Schechter Day School. Mrs. Clara Mann is serving as Librarian of this department. As such, the children’s
section is in urgent need of general books, not only Judaica, in order to meet the needs of the children and also to enable the
Library to be accredited by the Florida Association of Independent Schools.
Used volumes of children’s books are most welcome. Center members are urged to check their shelves for juvenile books
no longer needed by grown children. If they are in good shape, they will surely be put to good use by the next generation of young
people.
In addition, any used copies of the Time-Life series would be most welcome contributions to the Library. Families who have
either complete or incomplete sets of any of these valuable series are asked to contact the Library. They may be given as gifts or
as loans (Loaned series can be returned whenever requested.) Especially valuable to the youngsters in their studies and
research would be the Life Nature Library, Life Science Library, Great Ages of Man, Emergence of Man, American Wilderness
and the Wild World of Animals.

The Jacksonville Jewish Community Council is a
viable, unifying force that encompasses the entire Jewish
community. Its structure reaches out to every Jewish
organization in this city and brings them together in a policy¬
making group to help and aid the Jewish people in our
community, in our nation and in Israel.
The Jewish Community Council was founded in the
mid 1930’s. The original aim of the Council was a means of
consolidating independent fund-raising efforts by many
outside agencies. It has grown and flourished into a
dedicated group of men and women who have as their goal
the broadening of Jewish life in many areas. This is obvious
by the far-reaching aspects of its committees.
In the area of fund-raising, the annual Combined
Campaign has excelled in providing funds for the United
Jewish Appeal and local and national agencies.
Our Community Relations Committee has brought
together the Jewish and general communities through the
efforts of an active Speaker’s Bureau and significant
mailings of pertinent materials to both the Jewish and
general communities. We maintain ongoing contact with
our elected political officials locally and nationally on
matters vital to the Jewish community at large.
The Adult Education Series, sponsored by the Council
and our four synagogues is now in its third successful year.
Through the Community Programming Committee,
programs are planned for Israel Independence Day as well
as for other events during the year.
The 1977-78 Sunday Spotlight Series will feature five
prominent artists in the fields of Jewish culture and the arts.
This program has been very widely received by the
community.
A Young Leadership Division has educational
meetings and encourages future development and
leadership in the community.
An active year-round Women’s Division has been a
vital area of the Council’s program, featuring education as
well as a successful fund-raising Campaign. In an effort to
make our community more cohesive, a Council of
Presidents was formed where the Presidents of all our
Jewish women’s organizations can come together and
develop ideas to make our community a more workable one
and most of all, a better one. A Shalom Committee meets
and greets newcomers to Jacksonville.
The Council also keeps a calendar for the Jewish
community for all organizations, which helps to avoid
conflict of activities.
Last, but not least, the aim and goal of the Council is to
extend the horizon of Jewish identity and to intensify a
desire for Jewish education to a more significant depth on a
community wide basis. We endeavor to eliminate
duplication, fragmentation and its consequences. A united
effort in this particular field benefits all segments of the
community.

ANNIVERSARY DAY — March 26, 1978
This year’s anniversary marked thirty-one years of
services to the aged and infirm of Florida. The services were
given in a garden setting on the banks of the St. John’s River
amidst well kept lawns and shrubbery. The three buildings
joined together in one continuous living space are modern
and blend into the environment.
The interior of the Home is gracious and is designed for
comfort as well as services which require nursing supervison.
The paintings and other art items add beauty to the general
decor of the Home. Although River Garden serves the ill,
frail, infirm and mentally impaired aged, it still stresses the
“wellness” rather than the illnesses which so many of the
residents are subject to suffering.
It is the positive accent on living which creates the
climate and tone of River Garden. It focuses on the social
components of living rather than the medical nursing aspects
of the nursing home. To give the quality of care we strive for,

SURVIVAL IS NOT ENOUGH!
The birth of Israel, brought about by the Zionist
movement three decades ago, has not yet fulfilled the
ultimate goal of endowing the Jewish people with freedom
from threats and oppression and has not brought full
security to Jewish life throughout the world. The
Jacksonville District of the Zionist Organization of America
maintains that we have no alternative but to remain vigilant,
recognize the dangers that beset Israel and to continue with
determination our protection of the Jewish homeland from
those who threaten her both here and abroad.
Many of us here in Jacksonville who care about Israel
and her future have turned our concern into commitment
and the commitment into involvement through the
informational and public action programs of the-
Jacksonville District ZOA.
In activities throughout the past year we have brought
to Jacksonville from the State of Israel men such as Dr.
Benjamin Akzin and Menachem Savidor as speakers on
Israeli-American relations, foreign affairs and politics. From
New York we brought to Jacksonville the noted journalist
and newsbroadcaster Moses Schonfeld as an expert
speaker on Jewish Public Information.
Our screening of the smash Israeli musical
“KAZABLAN” brought to Jacksonville a “MADE IN
ISRAEL” production which would not ordinarily have come
to this community. This entertainment success also served
as a fund raising project for the American Zionist Fund. Our
participation with the Jacksonville Chapters of Hadassah
brought to the community the Chevraia Players of the
University of Pennsylvania in their “The World of Sholom
Aleichem”.
To all who care about Israel and her future the personal
commitment to Zionism by membership in the Zionist
Organization of America is an obligatory responsibility. Just
the bare survival of Israel is not enough. Our business is to
get involved. A Zionist can do no less.

Lt. Meyer Leibovitz Post No. 199
The Jewish War Veterans of the United States this
year celebrate the 82 nd year of their founding. This
organization is the first veterans organization founded in
the United States and is still the only Jewish organization
authorized to speak before the Congress of the United
States. All other Jewish Organizations must speak thru the
J.W.V.
Israel is still the first concern of the organization,
working for peace and security in the Middle East. No other
organization works harder in the halls of congress towards
this end. Soviet Jewry and the restrictions imposed upon
them receive constant attention and support from all
members.
Last year’s major activity by the local group was the
locating and marking of the gravesites of local Jewish
veterans. This met with only minor success, and it is hoped
the focus can be instituted again next year. The group has
pledged to eventually find them all. They are due this honor.
This year the major activity will be to locate and
register as members all local Jewish veterans, including
those of the Korean and the more recent Viet Nam conflict.
All persons who know of a veteran are asked to contact any
of the members of the local post.
The post continues to meet in the Mt. Carmel library
on the 2nd Thursday of each month and any veteran is
invited to attend. Membership is open to all honorably
discharged current or former members of the armed forces
of the U.S., including those who have served for a period of
90 days for training or active duty. Individuals who have
served in the armed forces of other governments are invited
as associate members. Officers elected and installed this
year are: A1 Sinoff, Commander; Arnold N. Andriesse,
Adjutant; Max Becker, Quartermaster; and Lew Segal,
Vice Commander and Recruiting Officer.

Communal fraternities for the purpose of providing
burial and its attendant rites for the dead have existed
among the Jews ever since the Talmudic period. The
Hebrew term for such a fraternity is Hevrah Kaddisha (Holy
Society).
As soon as the society is notified by the local funeral
home of a Jewish death, out of respect for the dead, a
Shomer or watchman is notified and sits with the body,
especially at night, until the time of the funeral.
The practices of cremation, speeding up the
decomposition of the body, or embalming, the slowing
down the process, flowers at the funeral or public viewing of
the body are all discouraged by Jewish custom both
universally and locally.
The Jacksonville Hevrah Kaddish was formed in
January, 1974, and has been functioning ever since. It
consists of about twenty men and women who perform the
necessary tasks on a rotating basis. Anyone who would be
interested in becoming a member of the local society may
contact Joel Bressler, its President, for further information.

The members of the burial society used to attend at the
bedside of the dying person and receive his final declaration
or confession of faith (Viddui). They would wash and dress
the corpse, conduct the burial service, and finally, together
with the observance and performance of the traditional
rites, conduct the actual burial itself. In the case of the poor,
the burial society performed its services without any
charge. In all cases, however, whether there is a charge or
not, the members of the society fulfill their task as a sacred
duty or obligation (Mitzvah) which confers merit upon all
who take part therein. Since respect and acts of charity
shown to the dead were regarded by the Jews in all ages as
the highest form of charity and human service, the
members of the Hevrah Kaddisha have always been highly
respected in the Jewish communities; in many cases in the
past they were accorded special social and religious
privileges in return for their important and unselfish service.
The actual preparation of the dead for burial,
undertaken by the Hevrah Kaddisha, is known in Jewish
law as “taharah” (cleansing, purifying). During this Taharah
definitely prescribed ceremonies are observed,
accompanied by the reading of specific prayers. The body is
washed from head to foot in lukewarm water, dried and
then clad in the burial clothes (Tachrichin). During all this
ceremony the members of the society preserve a proper
gravity and respect. To prevent funerals from becoming
very expensive burdens upon the survivors Rabbon
Gamaliel 1, in the latter part of the First Century, ordered
that he be buried in a simple linen garment, and this
procedure was soon adopted by the great masses of the
Jewish people. Men are in addition wrapped in the same
Tallit (prayer shawl) which they have worn at services
during their life but with one of the fringes removed. As soon
as the body is fully clothed in the Tachrichin, it is placed in a
simple wooden coffin, again in an effort to provide
uniformity in the treatment of the dead and to prevent
excessive cost of burial.
If one wishes to honor their dead by purchasing a more
expensive casket, they are encouraged instead to give
the additional money to the deceased’s favorite loved one’s
charity.

U.S.Y. INSTALLS OFFICERS FOR NEW YEAR
Over 75 U.S.Y. members and guests attended the annual
Installation Banquet and Dance at the Jewish Center. Installed as
President of the United Synagogue Youth was Jay Plotkin, son of Mr.
and Mrs. Herbert Plotkin. Jay has been an active U.S.Y.’er and now
assumes the responsibility of leading the teenage group. In his
acceptance speech he promised “to bring back the true ideals of
U.S.Y.”.
Other officers include: Vice-Presidents: Programming, Terri
Katz; Religious, Scott Greenfield; Fund Raising, Allison Levy;
Corresponding Secretary, Frances Siegel; Recording Secretary,
Melinda Zisser; Treasurer, Lawrence Ansbacher; Parliamentarian,
Greg Kupperman; Member-at-Large, Randy Horwitz; Ninth grade
representatives, Barry Ansbacher and Cathy Levy. Bruce Horvitz
was the installing officer.
Selected as the recipients of the outstanding U.S.Y. Boy and
U.S.Y. Girl for 1976-77 were Roland Mizrahi and Marie Saraga.
Robert and Barbara Cantor are advisors to the organization.
Many outstanding programs and activities are being planned for Jewish teenagers in the 9th - 12th grades.
The entire Center family congratulates our U.S.Y.’ers for their achievements.
