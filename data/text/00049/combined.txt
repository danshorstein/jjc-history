Keeper of the “Fifth Commandment”
“Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long...”
It was this commandment which inspired the founding of River Garden Hebrew Home in 1946. It was this commandment
which nourished the love and care that over the years has made River Garden a nationally recognized model of excellence
in its field.
It is the commandment which underlies the tradition of meeting 100% of local Jewish community needs and of serving rich
and poor alike with excellence and caring.
River Garden Hebrew Home/Wolfson Health and Aging Center is on a 33 acre campus in Mandarin near the intersection
of 1-295 and Old St. Augustine Road. The design of the Traditional Care, Sheltered Care and Short Term Rehabilitation
pavilions recognizes that people with medical needs do not have to live in medical settings. The River Garden Home/
Wolfson Health and Aging Center will be a part of a larger community which will include: apartments, a town center, a
general store, a fitness center and a campus club.
Overall, River Garden is today and tomorrow a community where people live, love, and care; a community of many life
styles and of rich Jewish content, and a community which every day strives to live up to the mandate of the Fifth
Commandment.

Home-School Partnership
When school and home are joined together, third
personality or nature emerges. It is inherent in the
relationship. If we come to understand that such a
connection results, then we can have some control
over its development. We must help put this third entity
to work positively and direct its energies into useful
channels-and for good reason. It's the only way to be
certain that we help our children at home and at
school.
SOLOMON
The third personality is that action which results from
the parent-teacher relationship. The outcome of this
union can be either good or bad. There is never any in
between. If this relationship is good, then the results
would be good also. However, if the relationship
produces nothing, the results would most likely be
regarded as bad.
Of course, third personality development is not limited
to teacher-parent relationships. There is the child-
parent relationship, too. We are all aware of the effect
one child can have on another. One particular student
may be strengthened or weakened by a friend. We all
know children who act differently when they are with
certain friends.
DAY SCHOOL
OF THE JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER
Willard Kennedy, Principal
That is why personality development as a result of the parent-teacher relationship is so
important. We must make sure that our association will always promote the positive, the
strength side of the third personality. What a teacher may be unable to do alone, he or she
can do when joined with a parent--and vice versa. It is this partnership which can help us help
students realize their potential.
As parents and teachers we have a tremendous responsibility. The influence we have on one
another should never be minimized. The third personality which develops each time teacher and
parent meet must be put to work positively so that the result of the relationship will create
good things for students.
This is the goal of our educational institution-The Solomon Schechter Day School of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.

HEVRA KADDISHA OF JACKSONVILLE, INC.
In our times of joy, we do not like to reflect upon the inevitability of death and the pain and sorrow that it brings
to the family of the deceased. Jacksonville is fortunate, however, to have a dedicated group of volunteers that stands
ready, should the need arise, to assist the family of the deceased in their time of need and to assure that the deceased
is cared for with respect and according to the requirements of Jewish law.
From the time of death until the completion of the burial, the deceased should not be left alone. It is appropriate,
where possible, for members or friends of the family to serve as Shomrim, or watchers, to recite Psalms in the presence
of the deceased and to insure that nothing improper or disrespectful is done to or in the presence of the deceased.
If the family is unable to make these arrangements, the Hevra Kaddisha will provide this service for the family.
Prior to the funeral the members of the Hevra Kaddisha prepare the deceased for burial. This consists of washing
and cleansing the body of the deceased, performing the ritual immersion of the body, and reciting prayers asking
G-d to forgive the deceased for all sins and to grant the deceased eternal rest. The deceased is then clothed in simple
white shrouds and placed in a coffin selected by the family of the deceased. It is appropriate for the family to purchase
a plain wooden coffin and to avoid any pretentious ornamentation.
Where possible, both an autopsy of the deceased and embalming should be avoided. If this cannot be done, a rabbi
should be immediately consulted.
The members of the Hevra Kaddisha perform their services without compensation and often without the family
knowing which members are involved. It is a mitzva performed for the deceased which cannot be acknowledged or
repaid by the deceased—truly an act of loving-kindness.
All members of the Jewish community are invited to participate in some aspect of the work of the Hevra Kaddisha,
either in serving as Shorim, in making telephone calls to the members, in performing the purification rituals, in
providing food to and otherwise assisting the families, or in performing numerous other required activities. The
following persons presently serve as officers, directors, and members of the Hevra Kaddisha of Jacksonville, Inc.:

Message from Education
and Youth Director
Rabbi Dov Kentof
A great drought afflicted the land of Israel. The heavens were as brass,
the earth as iron. Water dried up at the fountains and cattle dripped in the stall.
The king called his people together so that the nation might beseech the Lord to
send rain upon the earth. Then the king stood forth and made his prayer, but the
sky was as brass and the earth as iron. And the lords and great men, the wise
men and chief captains, made their prayer; yet still the sky was as brass and the
earth as iron.
Then there stood forth an old man, poor and in mean clothing, and he
made his prayer, and lo! the sky was black with clouds, and there was a sound of
abundance of rain.
Then the king and his counselors and his captains, the priests and the
wise men, gathered round that poor old man, saying, "And who are you whose
prayer has prevailed with God, so that He sends rain upon the earth?" And he
said, "I am a teacher of little children."
We are all excited about the new year in our Education and Youth program
here at the Center. With the recruitment of new teachers and the introduction of
new, informal programs, we are looking forward to a year of excitement, learning
and creativity in our Afternoon Hebrew School, Sunday School and Hebrew
School. We again are looking to all the parents in the hope of support through
our AUTO organization, and we are convinced that through our close
relationship, children in our programs will benefit greatly in the coming year.
Let us wish you and your families a healthy, happy and prosperous year.
Alina and Dov Kentof

Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14 continued its proud heritage in 1989-90. The Troop, founded in 1919, is now in its
eighth decade of serving the youth of our community and riding high on a wave of success.
The Troop featured outdoor activities, interesting weekly programs, service to the congregation
and community, and dedication to advancement and the ideals of scouting.
A highlight of the year was the presentation to scouts Adam DePaul and James West the coveted
Eagle Scout Award. The rank of Eagle signifies excellence in character and achievement. The Eagle
presentation was made by Dan Wilensky at the annual Blue and Gold Banquet before several hundred
onlookers. Troop 14 has the distinguished honor of producing five Eagle Scouts in the past three years.
Troop 14 explored new arenas of excitement and learning this year. Among them was a ven-
ture to the Boy Scout High Adventure Sea Base in the Florida Keys. There, along with scouts congre-
gated from throughout the world, our scouts went snorkeling in the crystal clear ocean on coral reefs
abounding with colorful sea life, boating and fishing, explored a deserted island, and much more.
The Troop also accepted the challenge of the rugged Olustee Historic Trail Hike. The hike
required our scouts trek some 12 miles through the wilderness of the Ocala National Forest with trails
sometimes under water up to two feet in depth. The scouts were awarded for their efforts with local and
national Historic Trail Awards and attendance at the annual reenactment of the Battle of Olustee.
Other camping adventures this year included: exploring the popular Blue Springs State Park
where the scouts “ruffed it” in cabins with central air and heat and enjoyed water sports in the crystal
clear waters; two trips to Olena State Park, the second featuring a tubing trip down the nearby
Itchetucknee River; and Payne’s Prairie State Preserve, featuring visits to the Florida State Museum in
Gainesville and attendance at a University of Florida football game, following which the Troop took the
field for the annual troop game; a campout with the resourceful Webloes dens at Camp Echockotee; and
the annual campout with the Cub pack at the Center.
Program highlights this year included a visit by Marty Edwards who shared his knowledge and
insight on baseball card collecting and sports; a moot court over which Duval County Judge Morton
Kesler presided and scouts served as attorneys, witnesses and jurors; an insightful presentation by Dar-
leen Hutto of the Bold City Club of the Deaf and the cleverly contrived and well produced “Hanz and
Franz Scoutmasters” skit at the Blue and Gold Banquet of which the Saturday Night Live cast would be
envious.
The scouts’ efforts were not entirely nihilistic. Troop 14 served lunch to the residents of Mount
Carmel Gardens on Thanksgiving Day as has become an annual tradition. The Scouts performed road
clearing work and ground inmprovements at the JJC, built shelters for ducks which inhabit the Center
pond, collected clothing for the needy and landscaped a church, all under the planning and direction of
our Eagle Scouts. They also did conservation and environmental work at Anastasia State Park, and in
the Florida Keys.
Scouts attending summer camp this year included Bemie Echt, Jeff Anchel, Seth Bloom,
Barry Blumenthal, Danny Cook, Adam Dean, Larry Fiske, Eric Goldberg, Alex LeBeau,
Jordan Perin, Chris Parlato, Kevin Shafer, Shai Smargon, Brian Usoff and Andy Ussach.
Troop 14 is for boys grades 6 (or age 11) through 12. Those interested may contact Randy

Three Members to be honored on Simhat Torah, Sunday, October 22
Because of Israel’s love of the Torah, it has been referred to in Jewish tradition as Israel’s “bride”. A tradition evolved in ancient times to
honor the individual called for the last reading of the Torah —Hatan Torah , “Groom of the Torah.” The last person honored to begin the
Torah reading from its beginning is called Hatan Bereshit , “Groom of Genesis.” We also honor a woman who has made a distinctive contribu-
tion to our synagogue and Jewish life.
Hatan Torah — Ted Gross
Our Hatan Torah, Ted Gross, has a profound respect for knowledge and a devotion to study. He considers these the
key to the survival of the Jewish people and he has dedicated his life to teaching for this reason.
Ted Gross graduated from Yeshiva University and later received his Masters’ Degree in Education from the
University of Florida. In 1947, Ted was engaged as the principal of the Hebrew School programs for the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. Besides coordinating and teaching in the program, he was responsible for the innovation of many
programs. He admits that he is proud to have introduced Haftorah and Torah readings in Junior Congregation so that
pre bar/bat mitzvah students would be well prepared. And through his efforts, girls were able to become bat mitzvah.
In 1962, Ted Gross assisted in the creation of the Jewish Center Foundation School, now the Solomon Schechter Day
School.
The teachings of youth are never forgotten. To this day, Ted’s greatest joy is his continuing relationship with his
former students who he taught as children. Over the years, many have returned to Jacksonville to begin their professional careers. These
former students who, as a result of Ted’s influence, are still reading Torah at the Center. Before Ted’s arrival at the Center, all the Torah
and Haftorah readings were done by the rabbi or cantor. Ted admits with pride, “a distinguishing mark of the Jacksonville Jewish Center is
that it has the largest lay participation in Torah readings.” Today, Ted still helps the post bar mitzvah students prepare their Torah
readings. Some are the children of his former students. He strongly believes in advanced adult Jewish education and continually teaches
classes and gives talks whenever needed. He spends many hours coordinating the Torah readings on Shabbat and holidays.
Through the years Ted has always been busy with his many commitments, yet he has always made time to answer a halakhic question,
help a student or explain a reading. He has been described as one of the most knowledgeable Jews in Jacksonville. It is most fitting that Ted
is honored this holiday as “Groom of the Torah.” Mazeltov to Ted and his wife, Eunice, and his daughters, Carol and Ricki.
Hatan Bereshit — Seeman Zimmerman
Hardly a day goes by that Seeman Zimmerman isn’t at the Center. He’s either at a committee meeting, minyan,
checking the facility or watching a demonstration of maintanence equipment. When asked how he became so involved,
Seeman confides, “I saw the need and I just had to help.”
However, his Center involvement is not new. As a young boy, Seeman was very active in the Center’s Hebrew school
program, scouts, youth groups, Junior Congregation and Minyan Club. In fact, he received the Best Hebrew School
Student Award and the Junior Congregation trophy.
After serving time in the military and graduating from the University of Florida, Seeman came to Jacksonville to
work. He was asked by then-president Nathan Krestul to serve on the Center Board and gradually became active on the
house and finance committees. It wasn’t until his father died and he was saying kaddish daily for the eleven months
that he saw the need for someone to oversee the building. He accepted the chairmanship of the House committee and
became very deeply involved in the day-to-day activities. When it was decided to expand the building, Seeman was one of the key people
responsible for making sure the building was completed on time and that it was up to standards. When recalling the recent building
construction he proudly admits, “not one program had to be cancelled because of the construction.”
His success as house committee chair stems from his persistent investigation and consequent adaptation of more efficient ways to run the
Center. He never seems to lose sight of his goal—to help make the Center better. Seeman was delighted to see how smoothly and efficiently
the High Holidays services and activities came off. When asked what his hope was for the future he says sincerely, “I hope we can get out of
this tight budget situation so we can move on to the business of furnishing the building.”
We are grateful to Seeman for the inordinate amount of time and energy he has given to the Center, as well as his devotion to the daily
minyan.
Woman of Valor — Shirley Bielski
Simply put, Shirley Bielski’s presence in Jacksonville has made a difference in our lives.
In 1975, Abe Bielski brought his wife, Shirley, and their two children, David and Talie, back to Abe’s hometown,
Jacksonville, from Melbourne, Australia. For a while it was culture shock for Shirley. But within two years, Shirley
was at the Center teaching Sunday School and Afternoon School and was quickly on her way to becoming a very
involved and important Center member. Shirley readily admits, “the Center is home to me. It is because of the Center
that I accepted my new life here in the states.”
Before assuming her present position as Sisterhood president, Shirley served as vice-president of ways and means and
as vice president of programs, and as financial secretary and Torah Fund Chair. A long-time member of the Center Board
of Directors, she served for two years as membership chair and is presently a member of the youth commission, catering
and membership committees. While her children were in the Solomon Schechter Day School, Shirley was PTA president.
Shirley’s involvement in Jewish causes extends beyond the Center. She is a former division chair of UJA and served as a board member of
the Women’s Division. To each of her positions, Shirley has unselfishly given her time and creative talents.
Shirley is a gal who can’t say no. That’s been lucky for us but hard on her at times. However, she seems to thrive on the many creative
projects and causes in which she becomes involved. With the support and encouragement of her husband, Abe, Shirley is able to accept the
challenges her volunteer efforts require. Like a valuable diamond, Shirley has many facets—each glowing in its own characteristics. She is a
devoted wife, mother and daughter. By example, Shirley and Abe have shown their children, David and Talie, the importance of being a
caring and involved Jew. Both children are very active in Jewish youth groups, locally and at a state level.
Shirley Bielski is indeed worthy of the honor “Woman of Valor” in that she has made a significant contribution to the life of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, to the life of the Jacksonville Jewish community and to the individual lives of all of those whom she has touched
with her goodness and her many talents.
The entire congregation extends heartfelt congratulations to the honorees and their families on this very special moment in their lives.

Message from the Rabbi
A VIEW FROM AFAR
RABBI DAVID GAFFNEY
If “distance lends enchantment” it also lends perception. Many things can be seen, meaningfully,
only from a distance. This is true of towering mountains and spreading landscapes. It is also true
of one’s own life situation. The founder of Hasidism, the Baal Shem-Tov once observed that if a coat
hanger was burdened under the weight of the Peklach Tzores (packages of troubles) of some of his
acquaintances, he would always choose his own troubles in preference to those of others. Sometimes,
viewing the problems of another makes us grateful that we are not so severely afflicted.
New York is in many places dirty and sleazy. Viewed from the Empire State Building or from the
World Trade Center, the city is extraordinary. Paris, I am told, viewed from close is often dingy; seen
from the Eiffel Tower it is magnificent.
This proposition was compellingly communicated to me upon reading the report which surveyed
the diverse Eastern European Jewish communities. A group of Rabbis recently traveled through
Eastern Europe to ascertain the welfare and wellbeing of the Jewish communities there. Their
conclusions evoked a tapestry of emotions. Sadness in some cases, hope in others - and even a
challenge for the American Jewish community. cHlCl Inge Gaffney
The American Jewish community looks altogether different when seen from far perspectives. When one is in the midst of it, one is most
aware of its deficiencies...its squabbles, the failing of individual Jews, of Jewish illiteracy, inter-marriage, organizational anarchy and religious
indifference. When viewed from the depressingly low foothills of Eastern European Jewish communities, American Jewry looms like a
mountain.
Despite the new won freedom of the Eastern European communities European Jewry is enveloped in an atmosphere of sadness. The great
bulk of its people; the reservoir of faith, culture and population which was Eastern Europe, is no more. For many there is the frightening
and haunting memory of the Hitler years and of suffering and loss. I remember traveling with such a group some years ago to Roumania
and found that the actors of the Yiddish theater played to only a handful of American tourists. Many of the synagogues are becoming museums;
inter-marriage is mounting and in many communities the numbers of deaths in the Jewish community exceed births by many numbers.
It is precisely out of this background that the virtues of the American Jewish community are seen most clearly.
We enjoy two priceless assets. First of all, we have size. We are numbered in the millions rather than in the hundreds of thousands. Our
congregations are numbered by multiple hundreds rather than on the fingers of one hand. Even the large size of our congregations, so often
deplored, is a virtue whose worth cannot be calculated truly until one has seen the opposite - congregations and communities which are all
so small that they cannot afford the appropriate schools and programs that are part of congregational life.
More important still is the tremendous vitality of the American Jewish community. We have disagreements and hundreds of other faults
but there is life and activity and concern in our midst. People compete and argue but they also affirm and build and look to the future. They
commit themselves to the education of the next generation. It is a far cry from the passive acceptance which one may see in Eastern Europe.
Given this perspective there is much that we can be proud of.
The same can be said of our congregation. Sometimes distance lends enchantment. Yes...we have problems with deficits. We have
programs which need support and the direction of our congregation. We need our members to invest more significantly for the future...and
I use the word investment in the best sense of the word. We need the cooperation and the intellectual growth of a more informed parent
body. Our families need to be integrated more fully in the life...educational, social and religious of the congregation. As Rabbi I receive the
brunt of comments from members who tell me what is wrong and yet almost weekly in recent months as visitors would pass through the doors
of our synagogue on the occasions of the celebrations of B’nai Mitzvah, weddings and during holidays they would remark with unanimity,
“you have a beautiful congregation”, “your facilities are extraordinary”, “your services are warm and enjoyable and edifying”, “Rabbi, you
have a wonderful congregation, it is a mehaya (translation: life sustaining) to see how many lay people are involved.”
This is all true. When you consider our heavy investment in education; the intensive education our children receive in the Solomon Schechter
Day School, the quality of our Religious School, the depth of our program from our Pre-school through our Confirmation a sense of pride
should be in order. You have to be pleased when you see former students now involved as lay leaders, leading services, reading the Torah;
you can’t help but be pleased when you realize that we have a Minyan every morning and every evening through the year and that our families
are able to enjoy the milestones of life cycle events in the most beautiful of settings. How proud we should be. We have the support of an
extraordinary Sisterhood. Our people are served by Family Council, 55+, a new Men’s Club, P.T.A. and A.U.T.O. There are many adults
who continue with their Jewish education in our Adult Education Program. Our children in significant numbers go to Camp Ramah, visit
Israel and continue to be involved with our congregation even when they’re in college. We have a dedicated staff and a corps of committed
lay leaders. From this perspective there is much that we can look forward to in the future.
What is true of the American Jewish community may also be true of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. We have vitality and size. What else
can we want? Nothing, save, perhaps, a deeper sense of responsibility commensurate with these blessings and a deep seated resolution to
employ them with all our involvement, to share with our synagogue the material blessings that are our own, to know that by our contributions
we will make a sustained difference for Jewish life in the years ahead.
This very Yearbook is being dedicated to our own Hyman Selber because of his own enchantment with our congregation and because of
his vision for our synagogue. Having served as Chairman of the Yearbook for more than 55 years Hymie has been responsible through his
dedication to the Yearbook for raising substantial funds for the Jacksonville Jewish Center. In this fashion he has affected the lives of our
congregants; most especially teenagers and children who have been enriched by his dedication. We express our appreciation for all this in
our Yearbook dedication and wish Hymie and his entire family health and wellbeing in merit of his consecration to our congregation and to
the Jewish people.
As we begin a New Year, a view from afar might be tempting. Let us, however, also be thankful for the blessings that are closer to home.
From Inge and myself, to you and to yours, best wishes for a healthy, happy and fulfilling year.. .a year of perspective, of appreciation and
hopefully, of vision.

Established in 1893, the National Council of Jewish
Women is the oldest Jewish women’s organization in
America. NCJW has more than 100,000 members
in 200 Sections nationwide that are active in the
organiztion’s priority areas of Women’s Issues, Jew-
ish Life, Aging, Children and Youth, Israel, and Con-
stitutional Rights.
NCJW Jacksonville Section has been instrumental in developing and im-
plementing many significant programs in our community, currently
including:
P.A.C.E. - Practical and Cultural Education - a structured academic and
vocational program for teenage girls who have become involved in the
court system as status offenders.
Mental Health Forum - a one-day public forum of mini-workshops,
such as: coping with personal relationships, family life and stresses, co-
sponsored with the Mental Health Association.
Kosher Kart - delivery of balanced, hot meals to homebound individuals.
Guardian Ad Litem - a volunteer-program for court appointed child ad-
vocates to represent abused and neglected children in judicial hearings.
Tallahassee Institute - a conclave of concerned women advocating the
issues of NCJW priorities to legislative representatives.
Ship-A-Box - packaging and sending personal articles to children in
Israel.
Pre-School Vision Screening - Since 1971, Council has participated
in an eye screening program for the benefit of public and private
kindergarten students. Hundreds of children have been tested under this
program each year.
Social Legislation - Through programs and study groups, and in ac-
cordance with recommendations from national and state legislative chair-
women, this committee brings legislation to the member and recom-
mends actions.

MOSAIC: Jewish Life in Florida
is coming to Jacksonville
Museum of Science and History
March 17-May 31,1991
MOSAIC is the first comprehensive project to retrieve, document, and preserve the
history of the Jewish experience in Florida from 1763 to the present. The immediate
goal is a 2,000 square feet modular exhibit which opens in Miami October 19, 1990,
and travels to seven other cities in Florida through 1992. The exhibit contains nearly
400 photographs and 80 artifacts, loaned by Florida Jewish families, synagogues, and
organizations, and state historical archives.
MOSAIC, the traveling exhibit, will discuss the themes of where Jews came from, what
they did, the families and institutions they formed, how they interacted with and for their
communities, and significant contributions Jews made to the development of Florida.
Visitors will also gain knowledge about Jewish traditions and the heritage Jews strive
to pass on to future generations.
This old photograph shows the Finkelstein family who came from Pushalot, Lithuania, ana ran a popular
boarding house where Jewish life centered early in this century.
You will want to be involved in MOSAIC. Here's how:
• volunteer to be a docent at the exhibit
• help create the Jacksonville module, which tells the history of our community
• help plan community educational programming
• make a financial contribution - a minimum contribution of $100 makes you a charter
member with special privileges.

Sylvia Shorstein at 733-7607.

Jacksonville Jewish Center is Proud to
Dedicate this Yearbook to Hyman Selber
Among the many pleasures in his life, Hymie Selber
counts the Jacksonville Jewish Center. How lucky we are!
For fifty-five years, the name of Hymie Selber has been
synonymous with the Jacksonville Jewish Center year-
book. As business manager, Hymie was responsible for
soliciting ads and keeping the books. To him it was a labor
of love. It gave him pleasure to see the Center increase its
revenue each year.
Hymie is very humble. He gives credit to people like his brother, Philip Selber, Max Rubin,z'l,
Ethel Joel,z'l, Jack Dorman and especially, Esther Fishier,z’l, Center Sisterhood and the many
other volunteers and staff members who have made the yearbook such a successful program.
He readily admits that it was his wife, Bertha, z’l, and Hannah Setzer, z'l, who were the original
“dynamic duo” when it came to ad solicitations. They, literally, walked from store to store in
downtown Jacksonville soliciting businesses for yearbook ads. They would not take “no” for an
answer.
Hymie’s children, Ina Taffet and Joel Selber, remember that before their father left for work
in the morning he would leave a list of businesses for their mother to call on that day. To Hymie
and Bertha it was a year-round job and they took it seriously. It was their determination and
dedication that set the example for future yearbook solicitors.
Although Hymie retired from his business, he still takes the time to oversee every yearbook.
He continues to work closely with the editors giving advice and encouragement whenever
needed.
On April 11, 1975 the Jacksonville Jewish Center adopted a resolution which states:
"...IHymie’s] remarkable record of achievement is matched only by the success of the annual
Center Yearbook which has received national recognition as an outstanding chronicle of our
Synagogue-Center’s activities and programs. The lives of thousands of children, teenagers and
adults have been enriched because dedicated individuals such as Hyman Selber have raised the
funds to help make these programs possible.
...May Hyman Selber’s example of unselfish service serve as an inspiration to all of us.”
Hymie is a ba’al tzedakah —he is blessed with an open hand, sensitive heart and warm soul.
Many Jewish organizations in Jacksonville and Israel are recipients of his graciousness. The
Center is grateful that Hymie has shared his optimism, indomitable enthusiasm, burning deter-
mination and insight with the congregation. He has certainly made a difference in our lives.
Mazel tov to Hymie and his entire family.
-Editor

Jacksonville Jewish Federation
The Jacksonville Jewish Federation is the central fundraising,
planning, budgeting and coordinating agency for the Jewish com-
munity. It strives to address the needs of the Jewish community,
encouraging community participation. Its diverse committees offer
many opportunities for volunteer involvement.
The Federation funds and coordinates community relations and
education programs. It publishes a monthly newspaper of Jewish
news of local and national interest. The Federation’s Israel and
New York Teen Experiences and Young Leadership program
examine Jewish life and history and give participants an oppor-
tunity to experience their Jewishness in unique and meaningful
ways.
The Federation conducts an annual campaign that raises funds
for the United Jewish Appeal, as well as other national and
overseas agencies. Local beneficiaries are the Jewish Community President: Joan Levin
Alliance, Jewish Family and Community Services, the Kosher Executive VP: Alan Margolies
Nutrition Program, River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged and
the chapters of various Jewish organizations.

HADASSAH
Jacksonville, Florida Chapter
In America, the Zionist Women’s Volunteer Organization is Hadassah. Of all the movements for national liberation, none has
had so vital a women’s component as this one. Before anyone heard of equal rights, before women got the right to vote, the
women of Hadassah were buying land in Eretz Israel on which to build a hospital.
Over 1,000 Jacksonville members are making a mark on Jewish life today. They are working on great issues regarding the
resettlement of Soviet Jews and the protection of Israel’s health and youth by supporting modem, fully equipped hospitals (staffed
by experts in their field), Hadassah Institute of Technology and Youth Aliyah. In addition, we seek to safeguard American
democracy and provide knowledgeable leadership in the American Jewish community.
We are guided by the example of our founder, Henrietta Szold. The institutions and programs she nurtured flourish because of
the roots she planted. Hadassah began as a study group and dedication to learning remains one of our cherished traditions. None
of our programs would exist without the bedrock of Jewish values to which Hadassah is dedicated.
This year will be sparked by the continuing forward movement of every Hadassah service in Israel, expansion of the Hadassah
Medical Relief Organization worldwide, projects in the United States and community commitments in Jacksonville. We walk in
the footsteps of well over one million dedicated and energetic leaders who have brought our organization to its present size, status
and impact throughout the world. It is a proud legacy which is ours to continue and enhance. Won’t you join us? Please call one
of the listed numbers so that we may meet and greet you and wish you and yours a happy, healthy New Year.

Caring and Sharing...
What kind of synagogue is the
Jacksonville Jewish Center?
I'll tell you. Besides being a modern
and very functional facility THE
JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER is
made up of very special people. To
prove the point here is what happened
in just one week at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center.
If you wanted to see a balabusta at
work all you had to do was come by the
Center office one Monday morning and
you would have seen not one, but six
Sisterhood women ironing the taleism
they had washed the night before.
Why? Because they care about their
Center family. To them everything
should be clean and fresh looking for
the holidays and they made sure it was.
The torrential rains didn't dampen the
spirit of the Center's Chalutzim group
and their advisors, Marilynn and Ira
Goldfield, as they hand-delivered 45
high holiday baskets to newcomers and
new members on Sunday, September
24. Each basket was filled with a
hallah, a bottle of wine, fruit and a New
year greeting and welcome from Rabbi
Gaffney. This effort was coordinated by
Rabbi Kentof and the Center's 5th and
6th graders and their very special
parents. Two of our newest members
have been so impressed with the
Center's continuous hospitality that
they called the office on Monday and
said they wanted to give back to the
Center because it has given them so
much in the short time they were here.
P.S. they both volunteered for two
committees.
The effects of Hugo spread far beyond
its physical devastation. People from
everywhere rallied to help the suffering
families in South Carolina. As always,
the Center family once again came
through. In just a few hours on a
Tuesday evening, Horty Gendzier, Edith
Horovitz, Shirley Bielski, Susan and
Matt Frankel contacted Emanu-El
Synagogue in Charleston, S.C. and
arranged for a truck in Jacksonville to
carry: 150 challot; wine, food, paper
goods, candles, fruit, baby food and
diapers to the synagogue.

Center Men's Club
The year 5750 has seen the revitalization of the Men’s Club through the insight and hard work of a few good men. The
original goals of the Men’s Club were fulfilled with programs that educated, enlightened, and entertained; membership rolls
climbed steadily and included a broad cross-section of the Center population; and friendships were formed.
Highlights of the year included the Superbowl Party and the fishing trip. Members and their families who attended these
events had such a good time that they will be repeated annually. A presentation on anti-Semitism in the Soviet Union was
timely and inspiring. The update on pro-football’s chances in Jacksonville gave us all a new insight.
In the next year several challenges face the board. We must continue to provide programs which stimulate intellectually,
socially, and spiritually. We are planning to affiliate with the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs. Appropriate service projects
to the synagogue and Judaism will be established.
Assooy Kahalach to the founding board and Yasher Koach to the present board members. As it is written in Pirkay
Avot (v.26), “ Ben Hai Hai said, According to the labor is the reward.” We have been greatly rewarded.
I bid you a Shalom, Peace and Shana Tova. -Ira Grann, President

Joel Bressler Honored
On Sunday, November 19, 1989, Joel Bressler was honored at a brunch by
the Jacksonville Jewish Center for his ten years of service as acting cantor on
Shabbat and holidays. As founding member of the Hevra Kaddish, “Jewish
Burial Society,” he was the organization’s ongoing president since its creation
in 1976.
This tribute given to Joel Bressler was well-deserved and an expression of the
community’s esteem and affection it has felt for Joel for so many years. The
Jacksonville Jewish Center and the community are grateful to Joel for his many
contributions has has made to Jewish life in Jacksonville.
After the High Holidays in 1989, Joel Bressler moved to Potomac, Maryland
where he now makes his home with his new wife, Shirley.

gogues. It meets the second Sunday of the month from 2:00 to

Cub Scout Pack 14
Cub Scout Pack #14 continued its tradition of interesting and rewarding events throughout the year. Our
great group of boys, ranging in age from 2nd through the 5th grade, met in one of the four dens once a week. Pack
meetings with all the boys and their families were held once a month.
Cub Scouts aim high: at developing good character, spiritual growth, good citizenship, sportsmanship,
comaradarie with other boys, and a sense of personal achievement. Thanks to our extremely competent and caring
den leaders, the boys have earned many badges of achievement while having lots of fun. Several cubs have begun
work on their Aleph Badge which is awarded through the National Committee on Jewish Scouting.

speaker, Sam Kouvaris; a mock game show with parents who shared their knowledge of U.S. presidents; a repre-
sentative from the Mayor’s office in charge of the conservation project, “Keep Jacksonville Beautiful”; and a com-
munications expert demonstrating the latest innovations. We added two very successful Sunday family sporting
events—a soccer game and baseball game. Our two older Webelo dens camped out with the Boy Scouts. This event
earned the Cubs many badges and a true desire to continue in the Boy Scout program. Led by Sandy Kadish, the
Cub Scouts, Boy Scouts, and Brownies participated in a moving and rewarding Scout Sabbath. The year ended with
a favorite—our annual father-son campout, preceded by a family dinner and swim at the Center’s Recreational Park.

planning and organizing group. Our thanks to: Lanny Zimmerman who celebrated his 10th year as Cubmaster of
Pack 14. Please call the Center office if you would like to volunteer in the Cub Scout program.
Yours in Scouting,
Carole Goldberg, Pack Committee Chairman.

weekly news bulletin filled with vital information not usually
found elsewhere. ZOA Headquarters in New York City
serves as a cultural and educational center for Zionist activity

“Zionism with racism.”

ency whose entire agenda is the promotion of Israel. To
this end, young leadership missions were organized, dia-
logues with Christian Zionists established and cultural
programs continued at ZOA House in Tel Aviv. Renew
your commitment to Israel by joining with us.

President’s Message
When I was a boy growing up at the Center, I looked forward to the end of
services on Rosh Hashanah and the mad rush to the lobby to pick up the yearbook.
Seeing photographs and reading articles about myself and my friends brought back
the joy that was experienced throughout the year. How many of us catch ourselves
cracking a smile as we look at a photograph and remember how we felt at the time
it was taken.
It is well that we honor Hymie Selber for his many years of dedicated service to
the synagogue and for his efforts in producing the yearbook. Aside from raising
funds for the synagogue, the yearbook has provided our members with a means of
preserving precious moments in our congregational life. Reviewing the pages of our
yearbook reinforces the notion that the Center dishes out more than its fair share
of memories.
Our synagogue will soon be celebrating its 90th birthday. We should be proud of what our synagogue has
accomplished, and the quality of its religious programs, schools, activities. But we cannot stand on our laurels.
We must strive, everyday, to create new memories that will evoke in our members and their children feelings
of satisfaction, accomplishment, and enjoyment. Our synagogue should set a standard of excellence. And we
should try our best to uphold that standard.
The next yearbook will contain the congregational memories created over the forthcoming year. 1 hope your
memories and the memories of your children will meet, or exceed, your expectations. Only then can we say
that the synagogue has fully met its responsibilities.
May this forthcoming year bring you health, happiness, and peace of mind. Robin, Shira, Aliza and Zachary
join me in wishing you L’Shanah Tovah T’ikatavu.

Torah Lishmah
Genesis One constructed the history of life as inherently directed towards a better society. Yet there has
never been an age when mankind was not frustrated by endless conflicts and even greater means for self-
destruction. The modem world is teetering on the edge of total collapse. Perhaps the greatest challenge remains
the same: the recognition that life is a gift for which we owe thanks and effort to restore the origninal direction.
No person, no nation, no government has the right to distort God’s plan for its own benefit only, or to do as
they please. If this is true, it is a biblical lesson, not a scientific one! As we read the Torah from week to week
we find frequent examples of the basic truths. Those participants who help us read the Torah absorb these
reverential features which give us a more hopeful outlook for the future. It can also be helpful towards blunting
the impact of “heavy metal” on the personalities of our young people.
This past year saw a more intensive arrangement of class and havurah members taking responsibility for
all the readings on special occasions. We are especially grateful to the doctors and attorneys and other
professionals who take on the longer readings and perform on a professional basis as they would in their own
practices. Little did they know that when they entered the program it would become a lifetime occupation for
the highest goal in Judaism. Torah Lishmah- for its own sake, it own reward. We are thankful to those groups.
