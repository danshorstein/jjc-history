As we approach the end of the year 5695, and we look back upon the ever
darkening clouds that have covered the Jewish horizon throughout the past year, we
lift our eyes to God in prayer for the thousands of our co-religionists who are sub¬
jected to tyranny and persecution in many lands, who are being deprived of their
human rights and economic opportunities.
This is the time to realise the heavy obligations that rest upon American Jewry
who have the good fortune to live ,in this land of liberty and democracy. While we
must provide for the spiritual and social needs of our local community and work
for the improvement of our cultural and religious institutions, we must not forget
that thousands of our unfortunate brethren across the sea are stretching out their
hands to us and are calling for help.
As we gather in our synagogue to pray to God that He may forgive our
misdeeds, and purify our hearts, let us also pray that we may be imbued with the
courage and spirit to live up to the great obligations that rest upon us in this cruical
hour.
May God grant our prayers that all men form a single band to do His will,
with a perfect heart and to live together in peace, righteousness, and happiness.
May the year 5696 find us strongly united in our common endeavor to build a
community of intelligent, civic minded and loyal Jews.
Rabbi Morris David Margalis

A Tribute to Our President, Mr. Max Rubin
Mp Max Rubin, the president of the Jacksonville Jewish Center, is one of
those rare individuals whom God has favored with the gift of leadership.
For more than thirty years, since his coming to Jacksonville, he;, has exerted
a profound influence upon the life and development of the Jewish Community. Every
charitable, religious and educational activity of Jacksonville Jewry has felt the force
of his guiding and directing hand, of his wisdom, tact and leadership.
Because of Mr. Rubin’s fine appreciation of Jewish values and his high sense of
responsibility as a communal learer, he has stood steadfast as a rock in his struggle
to save the Center from finacial collapse and spiritual deterioration during! the years
of the depression.
Because of his excellent collobcration with Rabbi Morris D. Margolis during the
past three years, and because he has secured the cooperation of the officers and
members of the Board of Directors, the Center has increased in membership, has
developed a splendid Hebrew School and Sunday School, and has sponsored many
cultural and social activities for the benefit of the entire community.
Mr. Rubin deserves the admiration and cooperation of every member and
friend of the Center.
May God grant him and his family long life, good health and happiness and
courage to carry on his noble work and to exert his beneficent influence upon our
community.

THE JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER
The Jacksonville Jewish Center was built in 1927 under the leadership of Rabbi
Samuel Benjamin and Mr. Harry Finkelstein, its first president. The building
contains three parts; the main entrance leads into the auditorium; from a vestibule
in front of the auditorium stairs lead to the Center synagogue, which j.s above the
auditorium. In the rear, is a three story building containing class rooms, meeting
rooms, a prayer room, the Rabbi’s study and a library. Last year the whole building
Was completely redecorated.
The auditorium has a well equippel stage for concerts and plays with two sets
of scenery. On one side an opening leads into a well fitted out kitchen, and on the
other side, a door leads into the Center office. Reproductions of biblical paintings
decorate the walls., and rust colored damask drapes adorn- the large windows. The
auditorium is equipped with several hundred comfortable heavy chairs, and pos-
seses a fine dance floor.
The main synagogue has large windows of frosted panes. One pane on each
window is of stained glass and contains an inscription of the donor. The platform,
pulpit and Ark are made of dark walnut with, gold decorations. The synagogue
is provided with five hundred individual cushioned seats made of light walnut. In
the rear of the synagogue a balcony also contains 154 comfortable seats. The
floors are covered with a heavy coat of bakelite varnish, and the aisles are carpeted.
The classrooms are well equipped with modern individual desks, black-boards,
and are well ventilated.
Services are conducted daily morning and evening in the prayer room. On Sab¬
baths and Festivals services are conducted in the main synagogue. On the High
Holydays an overflow service is held in the auditorium, where men and women are
separated according to orthodox requirements. In the main synagogue, services are
of the conservative type. The traditional prayer book is used, the Hebrew being
supplemented by English prayers., and congregational singing. Sermons are de¬
livered by the rabbi in. English. Mixed pews, are permitted.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is affiliated with the United Synagogue of
America, and generally calls its spiritual leaders from among the graduates of
The Jewish Theological Seminary of America.

CALENDAR
In this year's directory we are
publishing a Hebrew-English Cal¬
endar which we know will be of
great use to the Jewish people of
Jacksonville, and will be frequently
consulted. On the Hebrew side the
calendar contains all Hebrew names
of the festivals, holidays and fasts.
The weekly portions of the Law
are indicated for every Sabbath,
and and a reminder to light the-
Sabbath candles (L. S. C.) or to
light the festival candles (L.F.C.)
apppears on the proper days.
On the English side will be found
the English names of the festivals
and holidays and a calendar of im¬
portant events of all the Jewish
organizations meeting in the Cen¬
ter. This feature, we hope, will give
each organization an opportunity to
plan for their respective undertak¬
ings without conflicting with their
sister organizations, and give them
ample time to prepare for these
events to assure their success.
Below the calendar appears brief
explanations of the holidays and
other explanatory.
THE CENTER LIBRARY

need of new books, especially books
of interest to Children. The library is
open daily during the Hebrew School
sessions, and is used by children wait¬
ing for their classes. Donations of
children’s magazines, illustrated books,
and other juvenile literature will be
greatly appreciated
The Junior Daughters of Israel will
maintain a library service three nights
a week for adults during the coming
season. An effort will be made to
procure a large number of current
fiction on Jewish subjects and other
books on Judaica.
Donations to the library fund are
welcome.

days. It is alao the beginning of the 10 days of Pentience, which end with Yom

dom on earth — the reign of freedom, justice and peace.

3. Yom Kipupr (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when we
strive to reawaken the latent spiritual power in our souls so that it may become

4) Sukket (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of thanksgiving after

5) Simshat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of Shemini Azeret.

year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos,—procession with the scrolls of the
Ark to the accompaniment of songs and dancing.
6) Rosh Chodesh is the term for the begining of a new month. As the Jewish
year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coincides
with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered the
first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodes of the month following.

CENTER HEBREW SCHOOL AND SUNDAY SCHOOL
The Jacksonville Jewish Center maintains one of the best equipped modern
Talmud Torahs in the Southeast. The curriculum provides for a complete six year
course which gives its pupils a thorough training in reading and writing of modern
Hebrew, and the Hebrew Bible, prepares them, to participate intelligently in the
traditional religious services, and imbues them with loyalty to the aspirations and
ideals of the Jewish people, and to Palestine as the symbol of the regeneration of
our race and its culture. Yiddish is also taught to the older pupils, in whose homes
Yiddish is still spoken.
The number of children attending the Talmud Torah last year was about 80,
Which was made possible by the addition of a Hebrew teacher to our staff, and the
provision of transportation for children residing in the Riverside section of the
town. About 40 per cent of the children received free tuition; because of the in¬
ability of the parents to pay. The school consisted of six classes: a beginner'
class for young children between the ages of 6 and 8 years; a beginner’s class for
children between 8 and 10 years; one elementary Hebrew class; one advanced He¬
brew class; one Chumosh class and a Bar Mitzvah class.
This year school opened about six weeks earlier than usual in order to pre¬
pare the children properly for the High Holydays.
The Center Sunday School had an enrollment of 165 children divided into 10
classes. The curriculum provides for an intensive nine year course in Jewish history,
customs and ceremonies, beginning with the kindegarten class and terminating with
the Post-Comíormation class. It must be stressed, however, that Sunday School at
its best, offers the Jewish child a very inadequate preparation for active and in¬
telligent participation in Jewish life and for an understanding of its heritage.lt
is only eiiecitive as a supplement to the Hebrew School education.
During the year the children of both the Hebrew and Sunday sponsored many
interesting events on the occasions of the major and minor festivals. The Chanu¬
kah entertainment and the Purim Carnival were notable successes. The Simchasi
Torah celebration was also a memorable event. Numerous special programs were
presented by the Sunday School during the assembly periods on Chamisho Osor,
Lag B’Omer, and patriotic occasions. Through their charity fund the children
contributed to local and national philanthropic organizations.
Seven children were confirmed at a very impressive Confirmation Service on
Shavouth evening, June 7, in the presence of a large congregation. The confirmants
were Minnie Femberg, Bernice Berkovitz, Irene Kristie, Abe Spiwak, Raymond
Cohen, Meyer Proctor and Paul Weiss.
During the year the Center schools published the Center Hi-Lights, a monthly
paper which contained articles and poems, written by the children and many other
interesting items. Many of the contributors showed considerable talent and dis¬
played a fine appreciation of Jewish values.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER CEMETERY ASSOCIATION
(Chevrah Kadishah)
The,' Jewish Centex Cemetery Association consisting of a committee of five
life members, supervises and directs all burials taking place at the Center Ceme¬
tery which is part of the Evergreen Cemetery.
Recently the Jacksonville Jewish Center has purchased its cemetery from the
Evergreen Association and is now sole owner of the property. Many improvements
Were recently made in the grounds and plans have been completed for a beauti¬
fication program of the Cemetery, which will at the same time meet with the re¬
quirements of Jewish tradition.
A nominal charge of $10.00 per annum will be made to every lot owner for
upkeep and improvement. Owners of single graves who desire this upkeep service
should communicate with members of the Chevrah Kadisha.
It is hoped that the Jacksonville Jewish Cemetery under this new arrangement
will become one of the most beautiful cemeteries in the city.
Members of the Chevrah Kadishah are Max Rubin, chairman; B. Baker, L.
Bucholt'Zy D. Moscovitz, and S. S'elber, who replaces Mr. D. Davis, former chair¬
man, who recently resigned from the Association.
Former members of the Chevrah Kadishah are the late Moses Feldman, Neal
Finkelstein, Joseph Witten and E. H. Pilton.
In case of emergency please communicate immediately with Mr. Max; Rubin
or Mr. Dave Moscovitz.

THE DAUGHTERS OF ISRAEL
The Daughters of Israel serve in the capacity of a Sisterhood to the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center, aiding the Congregation financially, and assisting in all Center
activities. In addition the Daughters of Israel maintain the Hebrew School and
Sunday School of the Center.
Meetings are held the first Wednesday of every month in the Center auditorium.
The organization is very proud of its accomplishments during the past year,
which was due to the splendid co-operation of its members and officers and the
fine leadership of Mrs. Jos. Hackel its president. Much credit is due to the Daught¬
ers of Israel for the improvements it made in the appearance of the Center Audi¬
torium. Rust colored damask drapes were hung over the large windows and the
walls were decorated with a set of beautiful paintings on biblical themes. The stage
curtains were also repaired and decorated to harmonize with the color scheme of
the auditorium. The outstanding events of the year were the Thanksgiving Dance
Officers of Daughters of Israel
under the capable chairmanship of Mrs. Heymaiy Selber, the New Year’s Dinner
Dance, which proved to be a most successful event socially and under the chairman¬
ship of Mrs. Ben Chepnick, and the annual Cabaret which was successfully presented
Under the able guidance of Miss Ethel Joel and Mrs. Selber co-chairman.
The Daughters of Israel sponsored also the Chamisho Osor, Lag B’Omer,
Chanukah and Purim entertainments as well as the children’s Seder, of the Sun¬
day School and Hebrew School. The teachers of the Sunday School were presented
with gifts at the end of the school year.
A list of the outstanding events for the year 1935-36 is to be found in the
calendar included in this Year Book.

JUNIOR HADASSAH
The local Junior Hadassah was organized in 1928 by Hadassah, the Women’s
Zionist Organization, and is one of 14 units comprising thei Southern Region. It
subscribes to the Hadassah program and attempts to attract all Jewih young women
ltn the community from 18 years of age and up.
Special Junior Hadassah projects are the maintenance of the children’s commun¬
ity at MEIER SHFEYAH, the support of the Nurses’ Training School connected
with the Rothschild Hospital in Jerusalem and the raising of a considerable sum
annually for the Jewish National Fund through the distribution of J. N. F. boxes
and collections on Flower Day and Maccabean Day (Flag Day).
Junior Hadasah is a vital factor in the community life for the maintenance of
Jewish traditions and ideals. To further its cultural aims, iteresting programs
are prepared for general meetings and informal ‘Oneg Shabbat’ groups are spon¬
sored jointly with Senior Hadassah. Junior Hadassah is looking forward with an¬
ticipation to the regional Hadassah convention which will take place in Jacksonville
during February.
Regular business meetings of the Jacksonville Unit are held at the Jewish Center
on the first Thursday of each month. All meetings are open to the public at large,
and eligible Jewish girls are especially invited to attend.

1) Shabbat Zakor derives its name from the special portion from the Torah, Deut.
xxv, 17-19, read that day, which begins with the word Zakor—“Remember
Amalek.” It was assumed hat Haman was an Amalekite.
2) FAST OF ESTHER commemorates the way when the Jews of Persia fasted
and prayed that they be spared from the persecution planned by Haman. This
year, because it occurs on Sabbath, the fast is observed on Thursday, March 5.
3) Purim commemorates the day when, through the intervention of Mordecai and
Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly escaped being victims of HamamiTs
plot to exterminate them.
4) Shabbat Parah: Preceding the Passover, proper steps to become ritually clean
had to be taken as preparation for the eating of the Paschal lamb. Hence
the law concerning the ashes of the red heifer, Parah Adumah, (Numbers xlx),
was read.
5) Shabbat Hahodesh derives its name from the Haftorah of that day, Exodus
xii, 1-20. This is the “portion about the month/’ referring to the special con¬
secration of the month of Nisan which is the most eventful in the history
of Israel.

YOUNG MENS’ HEBREW ASSOCIATION
After a period of several years, during which time
the Young Mcn’si Hebrew Association was dormant, a
group of Jewish citizens met on January 31, 1935, and
effected a re-organization. Officers were elected and
plans made for the re-opening of the building.
Since January, the Y. M. H. A. was the scene of
considerable activity. Basketball was the major sport
for both juniors and seniors, and several affairs were
sponsored which met with success. Plans are now being
formulated for a ball to be Held in the near future
which will be a get-together of all members.
The Y plans to organize a basketball league for
senior and junior members. A series? of debates and
lectures have been planned for this coming fall and it
isi hoped that the members of the organization will take an active interest in this
undertaking.
Election of officers will be held after January 1, 1936. In the meantime
determined effort is being made to sell the building with the purpose in mind of
obtaining new quarters for the orgaization.
The Y. M. H. A. hopes the Jewish citizens of Jacksonville will lend their sup¬
port to the organization’s activities, during the coming year.

JUNIOR CONGREGATION OF THE J. J. C.
The Center Junior Congregation was organized about a year and one half ago
by Rabbi Margolis and was under the supervision of Mrs. Margolis, whose guidance
has assured its success. The Junior Congregation holds services every Saturday
morning, using the traditional prayer book.
The service consisting of English and Hebrew readings has been arranged by
Rabbi Margolis with emphasis on the important parts of the Sabbath morning
ritual and Congregational singing.
A section of the weekly portion of the law is read by three children from the
English Bible, which is usually supplemented by a story told by the instructor
charge. The congregation elects officers every six months, including a cantor, a
shames, a president, vice-president, secretary and treasurer. On the occasion of
certain holidays, the juniors hold receptions after their services», with the assistance
of the mothers. When a Bar Mitzvah ceremony takes place in the adult congre¬
gation, the juniors go to the main synagogue for the Bar Mitzvah rites and the
concluding part of the service. (Musaf).
The number of children attending is around forty. During Passover week,
services were held on the first and last two days, and the congregation enjoyed
an attendance of about sixty children. Since the opening of the Hebrew School this
year in September the children are being prepared to conduct their own services
during Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur.
Present Officers are Fruma Blattner, president; Louis Safer, vice-president;
Ben Hoffenberg, secretay; Armand Davis, shames; Raymond Cohen, cantor.

PUSHALOTER AID SOCIETY
The Pushaloter Aid Society was organized 23 years ago by a group of women
who came from Pushalot, a small town in Lithuania,' 3 miles from Ponivez,
Kovner district.
The society consists of 20 members who have either come from Pushalot or
have married a native Pushaloter. They meet once a month in the homes of
members.
The purpose of the organization is to aid individuals) and institutions in
Pushalot who are in need of relief. Such aid is usually sent each year preceeding
the Jewish Holidays. The society has donated a window to the Jacksonville Jewish
Center when it was built. Last year it also made a contribution of 12 Prayer
Books for the Friday night services to the Center.
Next fall the society will celebrate the 23rd anniversary of its existence which
promises to be a very interesting event.

Athletic Association
With the closing of the local Y.M.H.A.
building at the beginning of last year the
officers of the Center felt it their duty to
cater to the needs of the boys and girls of
this town and provide for the recreational
and athletic facilities The Center Athletic
Association was formed under the leader¬
ship of Dr. Sam Witten, Sam Bergman,
Alfred Stein, Morris Leibovvitz, and Sol¬
omon E. Cherniak for the purpose of pro¬
moting athletic activities. The Center
furnished the financial backing, and rent¬
ed the Kirby-Smith gymnasius and pro¬
vided uniforms for the teams.
The season of athletic activities proved
very successful as evident from their fol¬
lowing report of the basket ball season
submitted by Morris Leibovitz, and the
report of the diamond ball season sub¬
mitted by Dr. Witten.
BASKETBALL SEASON 1934-35
Last basket ball season the Jacksonville
Jewish Center entered its first athletic
team in the sporting world. A basketball
team was organized early in Sept. 1934.
The practices were held at night at the
Kirby-Smith Gym. About 60 candidates
turned out for the first practice. After
two practices the squad wasi cut. So many
younger boys were enthusiaastic over the
game that we decided to organize 4 mid¬
get teams in order to give all the Jewish
boys a chance to play. A leaguue was
started between these 4 teams and each
team played each other twice. New uni¬
forms were ordered for the combined
Junior and Senior team from Harry
Finkelstein Co. They were orange with
blue letters (JJC) across the chest.
We entered the Junior City League
and were leading the league, but the
games originally scheduled for night
games were changed to afternoon and
we were forced to resign from the league
due to inability of boys to play in the
afternoons.
The team won 18 games and lost 4.
Among the teams conquered were: Yel¬
low Jackets, Springfield Tigers, River¬
side, Landon Baby Lions, Fernandina
High School, Tampa, (2 games), St. Aug¬
ustine Bulldogs, Seybold Baking Co. and
many others. The lost games to Barnett
National Bank, St. Augustine Bulldogs
in a return game, Springfield Tigers,
(return game) and the T. E. P. Fratern¬
ity of Gainsville. The biggest game of
the season was when the team travelled
to Tampa Y. M. H. A. The Tampa boys
were much larger and more experienced
but we went into the game full of fight.
It was the fastest ad most exciting game
that the Jewish population had seen in
the past five years. The local team final¬
yl won after two overtime periods by
the score 29 to 28. A dance was given
in our honor after the game.
Late in the season the city Y. M. H. A.
Was re-opened and all our home games
were played on that court.
Tampa came here for a return game
and were soundly trounced by a large
score. The next game of imporance to
us was when the Jewish Center beat
the Fernindina High School on their own
Beach Casino Court 18 to 11. The play¬
ers on the squad were:
Morris Leibovit, Jack S'entler, Morris
Spivak, A1 Goldstein, Cecil Wolf son, Paul
Hoffenberg, Ben Carnot, Louie Moscovitz,
Seymour Hoffenberg, Julius Fletcher,
Meyer Leibovitz, and Joe Mizrahi.
The Girl’s team was organized late in
the season and due to the lack of exper¬
ienced players, was greatly handicapped.

JEWISH PROGRESSIVE SOCIETY
The Jewish Progressive Society was organized under the charter laws of
Florida on the first of January, 1932 with twenty-six memebrs, and has steadily
grown in membership until it now numbers one hundred.
The aim of the society is to foster every humanitarian cause, and especially
to help bring about a better understanding between Jew and non-Jew. One of
the activities of the society during the past was to help fellow Jews, both transients
and local residents, financially and morally iii their hour of distress. The organ¬
ization recently formed an Emergency Fund for the purpose, of providing members-
with care and to help defray part of burial expenses.. Charitable contributions are
also sent annually to hospitals, orphanages, institutions of Hebrew culture and
other worthy organizations.
The society’s main interest at present is the defense of Jewish rights which are
threatened in many countries, and it cooperates with the national Anti-Nazi League
to champion human rights.
Membership dues are $5.00 per year, payable quarterly. All eligible Jews are
invited to join the society. Meetings are held the second and fourth Monday in
the Jacksonville Jewish Center at 8:30 P. M.

YOUNG JUDEA
Under the auspices of Mrs. M. D. Margolis, Young Judea activities were re¬
vived in Jacksonville last year. At present there are three clubs in existence which
are doing fine work. The Shoshanna Judeans consisting of girls between the ages
of 12 and 14 years was organized: by Mrs. Gabriel Dryfoos two years ago and
is now under the leadership of_ Mrs. Mike Foor. The Herzlian Judeans consisting
of girls between the ages of 10 and 12 are led by Miss Miriam Carlton and Miss
Ruth Moed. The Rothchild Judeans, a group of boys and girls between 14 and 18
years of age, are led by Miss Edith Weiss.
The clubs have been doing fine work throughput the year. They have contributed
to the Boy S'cout movement in Palestine, the Jewish National Fund, and have
studied current Jewish problems with special emphasis on the events taking place
in Palestine.
On Chamisiho Osor B’Shvat, the clubs participated together with the students
of the Center Sunday School in the ceremony of planting trees on the Center lawn.
They also sponsored a booth at the Purim carival conducted by the children of the
Center Hebrew School. During March, set aside as Young Judea month, the local
clubs were honored at a special Friday might service held at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center, with members of the clubs participating. Miss Edith Weiss and Mrs.
Gabriel Dryfoos, gave short inspirational talks.
Miss Kathryn Kass represented the Jax Young Judeans at the Southern Young
Judean Convention in Savannah Ga. last August. She brought back a glowing re¬
port which she will submit at a Special meeting of all clubs.
The local Young Judea clubs are looking forward to a successful year of ac¬
tivities during 5696.

JUNIOR DAUGHTERS OF ISRAEL
The Junior Daughters of Israel was organized last year under the auspices
of Mrs. M. D._ Margolis with Edith Wolf son as first president. Its membership
consists of young Jewish women between the ages of 18 and 23 years. The aim
of the organization is to help the Jacksonville Jewish Center in its various ac¬
tivities, social, religious, and cultural. One of its most successful undertakings
last year was the forward and backward dances held regularly every Saturday
night from 10 o’clock P. M. till 1 O’clock A. M. at the Center Auditorium. These
dances brought together the majority of the younger set in our community in a
Wholesome atmosphere. Members of the Daughters of Israel served as patrons at
these affairs.
With the proceeds of these dances, the Juniors donated a new wedding canopy
to the Center which will be formally presented on Friday November 8th, at a
special Canopy Service which will be sponsored by the Junior Daughters. The or¬
ganization also donated one dozen Friday night service Prayer Books to the Center

JACKSONVILLE ZIONIST DISTRICT
The local chapter of the Zionist Organization of America was re-organized
by Mr. M. Sheinbaum two years ago to further the aims and ideals of a Jewish
homeland in Palestine, and is considered the largest unit in the Southeastern dis¬
trict. The two Hadassah units are sister organizations.
The Jacksonville chapter is a regular contributor to all the Zionist Institu¬
tions in Palestine, and raised about $1500.00 for the Jewish National Fund during
the last two years.
The Zionists meet the first Wednesday of every month for a social ana cul¬
tural gathering at which time important speakers and interesting programs are
presented to the group.

Diamond Ball Season 1934-35
With the coming of spring, plans were
made for an all star Jewish team to rep¬
resent the Jacksonville Jewish Center
in one of the night Leagues — a team
which would be capable of bringing honor
to the Center displaying a high type of
Jewish sportsmanship. In response to the
first call about 70 fellows turned out ail
eager to participate in the game.
In order to enable all to participate in
this activity, a J.J.C. Diamond Ball
League was formed, which enjoyed a suc-
cesful season and an interseting schedule
of games. Many of the younger boys dis¬
played a zest and zeal which may carry
them on to greater Diamond Ball heights,
while the older men re-lived cherished
memories of fond days that used to be.
The league was composed of 4 teams:
The Boy Scouts, The Jewish Progressive
Club Team, The Nine Odd Team,, and
The Old Timers Team. A split season
was decided upon which had 6 games in
each half, all games being played Sun¬
day mornings at the Forest and Palm
Diamond Balll field.The Jewish Progres¬
sive Club easily won the first half with
five games and only one lost. Their su¬
perior play exeprienee and hustle under
Ben Carnot proved too much for the
others to cope with. Morris Leibovitz,
the J. P. S. hurler, was tossing them in
too fast and seemed to be headed for
a clean slate of victories until the last
game of the first half, when the Boy
Scouts handed him his first defeat.
The second half proved to be much har¬
der fought and the winner was not known
until the last game when the Nine Odd
nosed out the Boy Scouts to claim the
honors.
The play off between the J. P. S. and
the Nine Odd at the Jacksonville Beach
Diamond Ball field resulted in the Nine
Odds emerging victorious in 2 out of 3
games played, and thereby claiming the
championship and the Bono Trophy which
was presented by Dr. Sam Witten at
a banquet celebrating th end of a success¬
ful season .
The Bono Trophy is a beautiful silver
diamond ball perched on a black ebony
base with a silver shield on which is in¬
scribed “Diamond Ball Champion, 1935,
Ninie Odd Club, Presented by Bono Clea¬
ners.M
The Jewish Cultural League
The Jewish Cultural League was org¬
anized by Rabbi Morris D. Margolis of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center in Nov¬
ember 1932 for the purpose of fostering
organized cultural and social activities
among the Jewish young men and wo¬
men. of this city.
During its three years of activity, it
presented many interesting cultural pro¬
grams on a wide variety of subjects,
mainly of Jewish interest These pro¬
grams aimed to extend the knowledge of
Judaism among the members of the
League and to increase their appreciation
of Jewish life and its problems. Beside
outside speakers, members of the League
also presented papers on Jewish topics.
Several plays were given by the dra¬
matic group. Several social affairs were
sponsored by the League in connection
with Jewish Festivals.
Membership in the League is open
to any man or woman of Jewish faith
above the age of eighteen.

SENIOR HADASSAH
Hadassah, the Women’s Zionist Organization of America, is a national organi¬
zation, now in its twenty-first year, having chapters in 25 cities in the United States,
Through its chapter meetings, study circles, lectures, Hebrew Classes, choral and
dramatic groups, and other activities, every Jewish woman in America is given
an opportunity to express herself Jewishly-—to bring a renewed appreciation of
Jewish views into her home, at the same time contributing her share toward the
rebuilding of the Jewish National Homeland.
Hadassah responsibilities enbrace the spreading of Jewish culture and Zion¬
ist ideals in America, the maintenance of a health and medical system in Palestine,
and the building of the Hadassah University Hospital and its eventual development
into a great medical center, that will redound to the credit of Jewish womanhood.
Under the capable leadership of Mrs. Ben Stein, president of the local chap¬
ter, the Jacksonville Hadassah Unit has become a vital force in the Jewish commun¬
ity and is recognized as one of the strongest and, most efficent organization in
our city.
The past year was marked by many interesting and successful events. Mrs.
Ben Stein inspired the opening meeting with a report of the 20th Hadassah Con¬
vention in Washington, D. C. which she attended as a delegate from Jacksonville.
A brilliant and enjoyable affair was the annual dance at the Windsor Hotel under
the chairmanship of Mrs. Neal Finkelstein. At the membership tea in the home of
Mrs. Jac Rosenberg, twenty new members were introduced. The rummage bridge
party, under the chairmanship of Mrs. B. Roberts, and the rummage sale, conducted
by Mrs. M. Wolfson, raised a substantial sum for the H. M. 0 Fund. An interesting
and inspiring event was the Hadassah Sabbath, observed on November 30, at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, when Rabbi M. D. Margolis addressed a large con¬
gregation of Hadassah members on Henrietta S'zold, founder of Hadassah, who
celtbrated her 74th birthday last year.
A concert and dance on February 14th, under the chairmanship of Mrs. B.
Roberts, was a delightful affair1. The local chapter was honored last year by the
visit of Mrs. Edward Jacobs, national president of Hadassah. A luncheon was given
in her honor. Mrs. Jacobs made several talks to various organizations, and was a
guest at the card party held at the .home of Mrs. Harry Finkelstein, the proceeds
of which went to the Infant Welfare Fund.
The culminating successful event of the year was the annual donor’s luncheon,
with Mrs. Herbert Weiss and Mrs. Sam Weiss as co-chairmen, and Mrs. Leonard
Moss and Mrs. H. Gendzier asisting in the presentation of a beautiful program.
Mrs. Moss read an original toast to each member of the executive board and pre¬
sented them with novelty favors. A brief talk by Mrs. Matilda O’Donald and a
cleverly written skit “Hadassah March of Time”, was given by a group of members
and their husbands. A considerable sum was raised for the Hadassah Medical
Organization Fund, the largest unit of health work, which the Hadassah main¬
tains in Palestine.
The members of the organization are eagerly awaiting the interesting
functions planned for next year. Special interest is displayed in the forthcoming
Southern Regional Junior and Senior Hadassah Convention which will take place
next February in Jacksonville.
