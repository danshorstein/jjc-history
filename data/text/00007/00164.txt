Types of Judaism
As in all other phases of life, American Jewry evidences a wide diver-
gence of opinion in regard to religious tenets and practices. There are cer-
tain fundamental beliefs common to all Jewish groups, such as the belief in
one God, in the brotherhood of mankind, in the moral efficacy of the Ten
Commandments and in the continuity of Jewish religious life throughout the
ages. However, as regards customs and ceremonials, and the relative import-
anee of such matters as tradition and nationhood, the Jews may be divided
into the following three groups.
ORTHODOXY represents those Jewish people who seek faithfully to
observe the beliefs, practices and customs decreed by Moses in the Torah and
interpreted by recognized leaders, scholars and rabbis from generation to
generation. This group is by far the most numerous among the Jews in
America . . . REFORM JUDAISM, while acknowledging the same sources
and foundations as Orthodoxy, stresses the principles of change, growth and
evolution in Jewish as in all religious thought. It strives to give a new in-
terpretation to ritual and ceremonial observances in line with Judaism s funda-
mental principles of social justice, peace and human brotherhood. Midway
between Orthodoxy and Reform is CONSERVATIVE JEWRY. This rep-
resents the group that does not wish to adhere to the strict interpretations
and exacting standards of Orthodoxy nor follow Reform in its non-adherence
to traditional practices.
