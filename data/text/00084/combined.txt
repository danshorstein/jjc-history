Center Hebrew School
RABBI DAVID H. PANITZ
Principal, 1945-46
The foundation of the entire educational program is the
daily Hebrew School. Perhaps the greatest achievement of
the past year’s activities in this department was the increase
of the enrollment to a total of ninety-six children at the height
of the year.
The students range in age from six to thirteen and were
divided into six classes. Emphasis in the three elementary
groups was placed on the teaching of the mechanics of read¬
ing Hebrew and on the fundamentals of the Hebrew Huguage.
In addition to this curriculum, the three advanced classes
studied the early history of the Jewish people in story form.
The two higher classes also studied selections from the book
of Genesis and Exodus and the book of Joshua, with emphasis
being placed upon the content of the Sedras as a whole and
upon the mastery of the more familiar passages.
“KOLAYNU”
The children of the Hebrew School issued a paper,
“Kolaynu”, in which appeared original stories, poems, quizzes
and editorials. Issues appeared on Chanukah and Purim.
“Koloynu” was edited by Gary Bloom with the assistance of
Joel Bressler, Helene Zimmerman and Pearl Aronowitz.
The staff of the Hebrew School included Mrs. Carl Alpert,
Miss Gertrude Meyerhoff, Mrs. Jack Becker, and Mr. Harry
Kolitz. Rabbi David H. Panitz supervised the school as its
principal and Cantor Harry Bettman supervised the prepara¬
tion of the Bar Mitzvahs.

With the return of many Jev/ish veterans to Jacksonville,
Masada, Yeung Zionists of America, one of the strongest
young men’s organizations of the pie-war Jewish community,
was reorganized.
The reorganization movement, initiated by the local Z. 0. A.
and furthered by the able assistance of Carl Alpert, nationally
known Zionist figure, culminated in the election of Harry
Lasris as president at a meeting attended by twenty-five
members. Other officers elected at the time were Joe P.
Safer, vice-president; Manny Ehrlich, secretary; Louis Safer,
treasurer; Henry Halpern, Harold Bloom and Leo Moskowitz
on the Executive Board.
immediately following their reorganization, Harry Lasris and
Louis Safer were sent as delegates to the national convention
held in New York. At this convention, Louis Safer was
elected a member of the National Executive Council of
Masada.
Inspired by the report of the delegates as to the important
work that Masada could do in the establishment of the Jewish
Homeland in Palestine, the local group launched a membership
drive which almost doubled their membership.
Masada’s sp.ing elections found Louis Safer as president;
Harcld Blocm as vice-president; Irwin Gendzier, secretary;
Harry Koeningsberg, treasurer; and Joe P. Safer, Leo Mos¬
kowitz, and Julius Friedlin, on the Executive Board.
Masada sent two delegates, Twin Gendzier and Irving Scott,
to the Southeastern Regional convention held in Birmingham
on Labor Day, and at that convention, both delegates were
named to the Executive Board of the Southeastern Region.
Included in the program engaged in by the local group dur¬
ing the year were numerouse cultural and social activities,
besides their work in the Zionist field.
The only fund raising affair sponsored by the local group
was the presentation of the play “Out of the Frying Pan”' in
conjunction with Junior Hadassah. The great success of this
play which was enjoyed by several hundred persons, indicates
that this joint venture with Junior Hadassah will become an
annual event. Hans for a fall fund raising affair have al¬
ready begun as Masada intends to sponsor a minstrel show,
made up of old-time A. Z. A. peí forméis.
With the Zionist movement gaining momentum daily and
with a show down concerning plans for the Jewish national
homeland in Palestine drawing nearer, Masada will endeavor
to increase its activities this year s d that the young men of
Jacksonville will be able to take their place along side of the
young men mother American communities in demanding jus*
tice for the Jewish people of the world.

The local A. Z. A. chapter No. 272, continued its program
of presenting a well rounded schedule of activities for its
members. All phases of its “five fold and full” program
found expression in concrete activities.
In carrying out its religious program the chapter conducted
its annual Friday evening service at the Jewish Center with
members of the chapter participating. This organizaiton is
extremely proud of the fact that the sermon delivered at the
services by Sol Proctor, past president, received second place
ranking in the national A. Z. A. sermon contest.
In addition to the regular cultural activities, the chapter
participated in a number of oratorical and debating contests
and sponsored a Youth Rally in June, with the B’nai B’rith
Anti-defamation League as the topic of discussion.
The chapter was also active in its community service pro¬
gram, participating actively in the S. 0. S. food and clothing
drive. The eighth annual A. Z. A. Minstrel, under the direc¬
tion of Bill Singleton, proved to be a continuation of the
previous minstrel successes. The chapter also participated in
the B’nai B’rith Youth Commission Bazaar, held at the Center.
The social activities were climaxed by the affair in the nature
of a banquet held at the Mayflower Hotel Roof.
The group had a very active athletic year, taking second
place in the state-wide A. Z. A. Basketball tournament and
first place in the bowling tournament.
The local boys are very proud of the prominence that has
come to several of their members. Beryl Weinstein, a former
president of the local chapter, was elected national Vice
President of A. Z. A. and Sol Proctor was recently elected
state secretary.
With its new slate of officers and new advisors, the chapter
is looking forward to a year of successful achievement.

Lt. Meyer Leibovitz Post No. 199
Jewish War Veterans of America ,
Answering the need of an organization composed
of men with the common bond of service toward
their country in time of need, the local post of the
Jewish War Veterans of America has taken great
strides forward in the accomplishment of their ob¬
jectives since their reorganization in the late fall
of 1945.
After its organization, the post voted to call
itself the Lt. Meyer Leibowitz Post in honor of one
of the city’s outstanding young Jewish leaders who
lost his life in the war. It was felt that he best typi¬
fied the young Jewish men of the community. At
the same time, the post approved a tablet to be
placed in the Jewish Center carrying the names of
all the young men of the Jewish community of
Jacksonville who had paid the supreme sacrifice
in the great conflict.
To establish good will in the community between
Jew and Gentile, the Post unanimously voted to
contribute an exercise room to the new Y. M. C. A.
The gift was made in the name of the Post with the
thought behind it of promoting good fellowship
among the youth of the community.
The Post carried out a telephone campaign to get
out the vote on primary day, took an active stand
for OPA and so wired our representatives in Wash¬
ington when, that crisis arose, sent delegates to
Washington to march in the parade as a protest
against British action in Palestine, and is at present
gathering and compiling statistics on all Jewish war
veterans in the city to stand as a permanent record
of the war activities of the Jewish community of
Jacksonville.

The Pushaloter Ladies’ Aid Society was formed
over 30 years ago to aid worthy causes in Pushalot
Lithuania. All members of the Society were either
born in Pushalot, or were married to natives of that
city. Pushalot is a small community in Lithuania,
from whence have come a comparatively large group
of local citizens, who expressed their common land
of origin in the form of a society. Formerly there
was a men’s group as well as a ladies society, but
the former has ceased to exist for sometime now.
Until the Nazi invasion severed contacts with the
Jewish communities abroad, money collected from
the local members was sent to Pushalot at regular
intervals, for such causes as Passover, rebuilding
of the Synagogue, etc. Now that the war is over and
Pushalot is no more, the society is striving to con¬
tact survivors in order to aid them, no matter what
part of the world they happen to be. The society
also contributes to local charities.

Religious Activities of the Center
The Center occupies an unusual position in offering religious facilities
for both the Orthodox and Conservative elements of the community.
ORTHODOX SERVICES
For these orthodox adherents, the institution maintains a
separate congregation on the High Holy Days, featuring strict
adherence to the orthodox type of service, including segrega¬
tion of sexees. It also máintains a year-round cycle of ortho¬
dox services, such as the early Friday evening service (Kabala
Shabbos) and all holiday services. It has also aided the
orthodox group in all of its endeavors to promote a more in¬
tensive type of orthodox observance.
CONSERVATIVE SERVICES
On the other hand, while catering to the needs of orthodox
Jewry, the Center has with the same interest and vigor con¬
tinued to develop a conservative type of service for those de¬
siring this trend in worship. As a result, late Friday evening
services have been developed into the most successful phase
of the year-round religious activities of the Center. During
the past year the services were conducted by Rabbi David H.
Panitz and Cantor Harry Bettman. Several visiting Rabbis
also conducted services from the pulpit during the summer
months, among these were: Rabbi J. Leon Hurwitz of Butler,
Pennsylvania, Rabbi Samuel Geffen of Teaneck, New Jersey,
Rabbi Hyman Cohen of Meridan, Connecticut, Rabbi Harry Z.
Zwelling of New Britain, Connecticut and Rabbi Carl L.
Manello of Wichita, Kansas,
Organizations participating in the services included the
Jacksonville Zionist District, Senior Hadassah, Young Judaean
Clubs, Boy Scout Troop 14, Girl Scout Troop 2, Cub Pack
14, Daughters of Israel, Junior Hadassah, and A. Z; A.
RELIGIOUS STAFF
For the first time in a number of years, the Center last
year, had a complete staff to handle the religious duties of the
institution. Rabbi David H. Panitz conducted all services
and assumed the spiritual guidance of the congregation. Rev.
Harry Bettman officiated as cantor at all services and assisted
the rabbi in the various ritual ceremonies held during the year.
Rabbi Safer conducted the orthodox services on Saturday
mornings and acted in the capacity of second cantor, reader
and mohel.
Although the Center on one hand increased its staff, it
suffered a serious loss in the resignation of Rev. J. Robin from
the Center staff due to ill health. Rev. Robin had acted for
the past thirteen years as sexton. In addition to his various
religious duties, Rev. Robin also supervised the maintenance
of the building and handled the preparation of the bar
mitzvahs. His loss will be hard to fill.
With the resignation of Rabbi Panitz, Rabbi Harry Z.
Zwelling of New Britain, Connecticut, has been called to the
pulpit of the Center. Succeeding Cantor Bettman will be
Cantor Abraham Marton, who comes to the Center from the
Madison Jewish Center in Brooklyn. Cantor Marton is al¬
ready training and developing a Junior and Senior Choral
Group to participate in the Sabbath services.

The Senior Hadassah Chapter of Jacksonville,
organized over two decades ago, has grown to a
membership well over 500. Through the capable
leadership of its officers and with the cooperation
of the many loyal and devoted women, it is today
considered one of the outstanding model chapters
in the southeastern region.
As part of the biggest Zionist organization in the
United States, it is the spearhead for activity on be¬
half of the people of Palestine, and the tens of
thousands of refugees who hope to settle there.
Striving to make Palestine the most healthful area
in the Middle East, so that the Jewish pioneers may
continue their nationhood, strong of body, sure of
mind and high of hope - - Hadassah maintains such
projects as infant welfare clinics, hospital and med¬
ical centers, supervises school hygiene, provides
school luncheons and has vocational education and
recreational centers. It also cooperates in Youth
Aliyah and in conjunction with Jewish National
Fund reforestration and land buying.
Participating in the supporting of these phases,
the local unit of Senior Hadassah, sponsors a num¬
ber of outstanding affairs during the year, empha¬
sizing some special project.
Among these are the Thrift Luncheon for the
support of the school luncheons; the Linen Shower
whereby many useful linens are gathered for
use in the hospitals and for refugees, and the sew¬
ing circles that function throughout the year, display
their handiwork.
The social events of the year include a Member¬
ship Tea. when paid-up members are entertained,
a brilliant Donors Banquet, in celebration of the
completion of the Hadassah Medical Organization
fund drive, and a gala Fall Dance.
For its educational program, the chapter sponsors
a number of Oneg Shabbat gatherings during the
year, and conducts a novel Education Day program.
Through this medium Jewish history and culture is
brought to the membership in the form of book re¬
views, lectures, symposiums, and discussions that
lead to a better understanding and appreciation of
our heritageas a Jew.
To strengthen cooperative relationships within the
Hadassah family here, joint meetings are held
annually with the Business and Professional Womens
Division and with Junior Hadassah. The Seniors also
take an active part in the youth work of the comm¬
unity, lending guidance and assistance to the Young
Judaea groups and to the Youth Commission.

JOINT SERVICES
The Jacksonville Jewish Center and Temple Ahaveth Chesed
annually hold two combined religious services, in expression
of their unity of purpose in behalf of the Jewish community.
This past year the Center played host to the Temple for
Chanukah and the Center was the guest of the Temple for
Purim services. These joint services have contributed to the
creation of an ever stronger and more loyal Jewish com¬
munity.
JUNIOR CONGREGATION
In addition to the regular Saturday morning services, a
special Junior congregation for children, is held throughout
the school year. During the last year a splendid attendance
was maintained with an average of 30 to 35 children a weefc
participating in these services, which they themselves con¬
ducted. The sermonette is prepared and deliverd by one of
the boys or girls each week.
The Chazan and Readers are selected from among the
children, and the ushers are chosen from the participants in
the services. Each Sabbath at the conclusion of prayers one
chapter was read from “The Strange Adventures of Danny
Noar”, the story of a Palestinian lad who searches for the
Lost Ten Tribes.
The Junior congregation was under the supervision of Mrs.
Carl Alpert and Miss Gertrude Meyerhoff.
AIN YAACOB CLASS
Rev. B. Safer, who conducted the Ain Yoocob class on Sat¬
urday afternoons for a number of years, continued this activ¬
ity throughout the past year. This class consists of a group
of elderly men who gather each Saturday afternoon to study
the Hagadic portions of the Talmud contained in the book
known as Ain Yaacob.

Rosh Chodesh
L. C.—Light Sabbath Candles or Festival Candles y2-hour before sunset,
for which consult daily newspapers.
Rosh Hashonah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and lasts
two days. It is also the beginning of the 10 days of Penitence, which end with
Yom Kippur. Sincere prayer and meditation during these days help us to ex¬
perience the reality of God and to dedicate ourselves anew to the establishment
of His Kingdom on earth—the reign of freedom, justice and peace.
Shabbat Shuba derives its name from the Haftorah of that day. Hosea
xiv, 2-10 which begins with the word Shubbah-‘Return’, i. e., Repent.
Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when
we strive to awaken the latent spiritual power in our souls so that it may be¬
come an effective and directing force in our daily conduct.
Sukkos (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of Thanksgiving
after all the products of the field have been gathered in. The Sukkah symbolizes
the wanderings of our ancestors in the wilderness of Sinai.
Simchath Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of Shemini
Azereth. Then the reading of the Pentateuch is completed and begun anew for
the coming year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos—procession with the
scrolls of the Torah to the accompaniment of songs and dancing.
Rosh Chodesh is the term for the beginning of a new month. As the
Jewish year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coin¬
cides with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered
the first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodesh of the month following.

and holidays.
Below the calendar appear brief ex¬
planations of the holidays and special
Sabbaths, and various other explanatory
notes.

Foreword
It is with a great deal of pleasure that we present our
annual Jewish Center Year Book and Directory, this being
our 43rd annual presentation.
We wish to thank the numerous commercial advertisers and
those who extended their annual New Years greetings through
the medium of our book. We extend to them our heartfelt
thanks and appreciation for their help in making possible the
publication of this book. We wish to take this opportunity to
wish them a healthy, happy and prosperous New Year.
As the members of the institution know, proceeds from the
publication of this book go toward the maintenance of the
Center’s program, providing as it does for the educational,
religious, cultural and social needs of the Jewish community
of Jacksonville. With the tremendous increase in the size of
the book during the past several years, this source of income
has become an integral part of the financial set up of the
Jewish Center, we therefore take this opportunity of calling
to the attention of our membership the directory of advertisers,
which this year will be found on the pages directly following.
We hope that our members will take cognizance of the various
firms that are listed among our advertisers and we feel cer¬
tain that they will cooperate with the Center by patronizing
these firms that have cooperated so well with us.
As in the past, this Year Book contains the usual features
which are valuable for year round information and which form
a record of the activities of the past year. The Hebrew-
English calendar may be referred to on every occasion for
the exact date of a holiday or a memorial occasion and our
complete membership list is a constant source of information
for the Jewish Community. The complete statistics on births,
bar mitzvahs, marriages, deaths, confirmations, etc. are a per¬
manent record forming the historical record of a community.
The pages of this book record the activities of the various
affiliated organizations, outline past activities and set forth
the plans and aspirations of the Center for the future.
In conclusion, we wish to thank all of the organizations that
have so graciously cooperated with our staff in the publication
of this book.
Center Year Book Staff

Double Gold Stars
THEIR LEGACY IS PEACE
BY HELEN KANTZLER
Among the families who mourn the loss of 10,780 Jews
already known to have died fighting for America there are
known to be at least 90 Jewish families, each of whom sac¬
rificed two sons in the bitter war against the Axis. Accord-
ing to documented casualty lists of the National Jewish Wel¬
fare Board (JWB) Bureau of War Records, in one family
alone death struck three times, leaving the grief-stricken par¬
ents bereft of sons.
These are facts which proclaim the sacrifice of American
Jewish lads who gave their lives to leave a legacy of peaces
these are facts which do honor to the sorrowing mothers and
fathers whose bravery in suffering equals their sou s heroism.
From America’s towns and cities, from farms, offices, fac¬
tories, shops and schools, young Jews of this country joined
the armies of liberation to free the enslaved and oppressed.
Among the typical cases of brothers who died in service to>
bring freedom to the world—for lack of space, the story of
courage and sacrifice revealed by all the double gold star cases
cannot be told here—were the three Liebfeld boys from St,
Paul Minn. Lt. Sigmund Liebfeld, a Librator Bomber pilot
and vetran of three years in the Army Air Corps, was return¬
ing to his home base in England from a raid over Europe when
his plane was lost over the 'English Channel—just one month
after his brother, Morris, a Marine lieutenant, had been killed
in the Pacific during an assault on Saipan.
The bereaved parents, Mr. and Mrs. Louis Liebfeld, receiv¬
ed some measure of comfort from the knowledge that their
only surviving son, serving with the American forces in Eng¬
land, would not b e sent to the front lines. In cases of mul¬
tiple deaths in one family, the sole remaining son was not kept
in combat area. Sam, an aviation engineer, was thus recalled
from England and assigned to flight test duties in the IX S„
Risky as his assignments were—his work included the testing:
of jet-propelled planes—Sam escaped danger time and again*
only to meet death near Minneapolis in a plane crash during
a routine flight. Three sons had left, none came back home
again.
The story of the Emmer boys of Clayton, Mo., is especially
dramatic because of the coincidence of circumstances which
surrounded their death. First, 19-year old Raymond Emmer
was killed with a Browning automatic rifle in his hand, some¬
where in Germany, on the birthday of his brother, Captain
Wallace Emmer, ace flyer of Word War II, who held a record
of 18 enemy planes shot down in combat. A year later Cap¬
tain Emmer, determined to avenge the death of his closest
friend who was shot down over Germany, piloted his Mustang
over enemy territory. Flak hit his plane and he was forced
to bail out. He died of heart failure, a prisoner of the
Germans.
Double tragedy struck the home of Jacob and Mary Gans
of the Bronx, N. Y., too. Only a few days apart they received
War Department notifications that 22-year old Solomon,' a
lieutenant in the Infantry, had been killed in France, and that
their other son, Ralph, had died in England four weeks later.
Other double gold star families are the Wilners of Washing¬
ton, D. C., whose boys, Bernard, an army major, and John,
a tank company commander, died in the cause of freedom;
the Lowes of Jamaica, L. I., whose two sons, David and
Gordon, were killed in action; the Weissmans of Chicago who
lost both sons in Germany—Leonard, a lieutenant colonel in
the Medical Corps, and Louis, a navigator on a bombing
plane; the Steins of Mt. Pleasant, Tenn., whose son, Morris,
was killed in the Philippines less than a year after his brother,
Hyman, who was in France on D-Day, succumbed in the Battle
of Normandy; Mrs. Harris Lumberg of Long Beach, Calif.,
whose boys, Bernard and Milton Sahl, gave their lives for
victory over fascism; the Heismans of Philadelphia, who lost
two sons—Bernard, holder of a Silver Star for “gallantry in
action” and Herbert, killed in the North African campaign.
These and 34 others are the families who made the greatest
sacrifice of all, who gave two sons that the world might be
safeguarded against the evil of tyranny. Their children,
Jewish fighters from communities throughout the U. S., served
with their Christian brothers, generously, courageously and
without thought of the cost, that America might live in a world
without fear.

The Business and Professional Women's Division
of Hadassah is that portion of the Senior Chapter
which is unable to attend the regular monthly meet¬
ings because the membership is employed during the
day, but whose awareness of the Zionist problems
demand their participation.
These women do in the evening what Senior
Hadassah does in the daytime. The organization
concerns itself with the same projects as the Senior
Chapter. Last year for the first time, the B and P W
Division were allocated a separate quota and under
the capable leadership of Mrs. Herbert Panken,
most of these quotas were filled and oversubscribed.
Their members did exceptionally well in Blue Box
collections, collecting approximately 30 per cent of
the total collected.
At the organization’s meetings, which are held
monthly at the homes of various members, there
have been presented interesting programs, such as
news broadcasts, given by the members themselves.
This broadcast is a summary of world events for
that month. In addition, occasionally, there is sched¬
uled an outside speaker who is well versed in
Zionism.
Besides its Zionist program, the B and P W div¬
ision has devoted much of its time to local and
national activities, outstanding of which has been
its participation in the work of Hadassah.
Mrs. Harry Ruby, President of the group issued
the following New Year’s statement: ”In critical
times as these, we must stand as one. Having a very
small membership we invite any one interested in
our work who cannot attend the afternoon meet¬
ings to join and aid us by giving of their time
and energy to the establishment in Palestine of a
National Home for the Jews.”

The President's Message
HARRY GENDZIER
President
I wish, first of all, to give generous Kudos and praise as
well as thanks, to those officers and members of the Board,
who have willingly and freely given their time, and their
energies to the affairs of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. To
these men and women, all credit must go. Our institution
can be proud of their lay leadership, because despite all sorts
of difficulties, they have succeeded in making outstanding
progress.
Due to the fact that we have suffered from a lack of full
professional leadership many members may have lost proper
perspective of the present situation. For example, it is well
to note at this point that the enrollment in our Hebrew School,
Sunday School, and Kindergarten-Nursery have grown each
year. True cur turnover of part of the staff has been large,
but it is nonetheless true that we have been able to maintain
all of our services on a high level. #
The committees have functioned ably and have constantly
endeavored to improve the curriculum*’ of our school and the
standards of our institution. The Cemetery Committee de¬
serves special praise, because here too in spite of shortages
of man power they have maintained their usual high stand¬
ards of dignity and decorum in the hours of bereavement of
our members.
The Jewish Center Expansion Program is still Number One
on our agenda. Committees are now at work with architects
making drawings of contemplated building projects designed
to increase the facilities of the Center in all its branches of
endeavor. Regardless of what community programs might
come into being, we of the Jewish Center are firmly resolved
to achieve and develop the finest possible program for the
building of a knowing, proud and positive Jewish youth. Our
clubs, such as the Cub Pack, Girl Scouts, and Boy Scouts,
Page Eighteen
have been revitalized through active assistance of older lead¬
ers, and these leaders are due for fine praise. Their work is
done quietly, and only these that can compare the standings j
of years gone by and today can see the transformation.
The Daughters of Israel and the Men’s Club have expended
worthwhile efforts to carry on important projects, and will no
doubt continue to do so for the ensuing year.
We have every reason to be happy over our staff. Harry
Kolitz has not only been a good teacher, and a good executive
of the Day Camp, but is an inspiration to the youngsters and
will be even more able to accomplish things through more
experience. Cantor Marton has come to us as a cantor and
teacher. He has an excellent voice and repetoire, and is
already forming a choral group. This will enhance the beauty
of our services to a great extent and will be an inspiration to
all of us. Already the Men’s Club has sponsored the pur¬
chase of music and gowns for the choral group.
Your president accompanied by Mr. and Mrs. Joseph Hackel
attended the Convention of the United Synagogues of Amer¬
ica. We learned that our Community is part and parcel of
the total Jewish community and that our problems are neither
new nor isolated. Particular problems are prevalent every¬
where. However with a willingness to serve, to discuss, and
to adjust, we can further the ideals of Judaism, bring modern
services to the Congregation, and yet maintain our tradition
so that our elders will always remain with us.
Since this will be my last term of office, I feel duty bound
to present certain facts to the membership. I feel that we
give too much value for the little we have obtained in dues.
A thorough change in the dues schedule is inevitable. Costs
are ever increasing. Salaries and wages as well as other
expenses have almost trebled, yet some members feel that they
ought to pay as little as they can.
All credit must go to those members who pay all they can,
and see to it that the functions of the Center are maintained.
.
I do not believe in refusing any person who is unable to pay
for any services, but similarly, I feel that the time has come
when all members should shoulder their responsibility. They
should understand there must be no bargaining with a re¬
ligious institution. If we are to foster Jewish life, if your officers
and directors are to build a program that will make your
children proud of their heritage if we are to be able to engage
high ranking and competent staffs, you must give us the
tools to work with.
Again I state it has been a pleasure and a privilege and
indeed an honor to serve as your president.- I hope you will
accept the coming of a new year as a challenge to each of
you to work heartily for the welfare of the Jewish Center,
We have a fine record, yet we can constantly improve.
We are living in a troubled world. May God grant us
peace, health and happiness. May each of you be inscribed
for a good year, and many, many more good new years.

Though only one year old, the Youth Commission of the
local B’nai B’rith groups showed considerable progress during
the past year in developing and strengthening the three junior
divisions of the B’nai B’rith organization.
The B’nai B’rith group, which is the central executive group
guiding the work of all the youth groups, assumes the respon¬
sibility for the moral, cultural and financial support of the
several groups. It also aims to coordinate the work of the
groups into a single overall program.
The B’nai B’rith groups consist of the A. Z. A., for boys m
age group of fifteen to eighteen; the B’nai B’rith Girls, for
girls in the same age group; the B’nai B’rith Young Women,
for young women between the ages of eighteen and twenty-
lve. All three of the organizations maintain a well-rounded
program, divided into five phases of activity; religious, com¬
munity service, cultural, social and athletic.
All of the groups have compiled excellent war service
records and are still engaged in a number of post-war service
projects. The membership of the groups has been greatly
increased during the past year and several joint activities
sponsored. The outstanding project of the year was the
highly successful annual bazaar, the proceeds of which were
divided among the three organizations to assist them in carry¬
ing on their programs. The .B’nai B’rith Youth Commission
is composed of representatives from the men’s lodge and the
women’s auxiliary, together with representatives from the
youth organizations themselves. The work of the commission
is so divided that one man and one woman form the chairmen
of the five phases of the overall program.

The Summer Day Camp program was arranged
to give all the children that attend a well rounded
program consisting of arts and crafts, athletics and
sports, hebrew singing, indoor games and trips of
interest. The general principles that were achieved
during the camp were the stimulation of the in¬
terests of the children to cooperate as good sport-
men in all the activities, surrounded by a real Jew¬
ish atmosphere.
Some of the children were very shy, while others
Were of the extreme opposite. Byt the proper
handling and training, both groups were brought
to a level of cooperation and consideration, one for
the other. All the children that attended camp
gained from 7 to 1 0 pounds, because of the prop¬
erly supervised exercises, eating habits and rest
periods. The closing of camp saw a flock of healthy
looking sun tanned bodies to surplant the pale win¬
ter, schoolday faces which entered.
The children took various trips of interest that
Were very educational as well as entertaining and
relaxing. Some of the trips taken were to the follow¬
ing places: St. Augustine, Marineland, Jacksonville
Naval Air Station, The Children’s Museum, The
Zoo, The Airport and several trips to the Beach.
A number of the children developed a hobby as
a result of taking the handicraft work. Most of the
children learned how to play varous sports and
games correctly. A good number of the boys learned
the fundamentals of boxing. Hebrew, as the achieve»*
ment, was the biggest success.
There were 31 children that never had Hebrew
training prior to attending the Day Camp, and 25
of this number were developed far enough to be
considered for the advance beginners class. A few
of these children could even go on the second
grade if they had one more month of concentrated
study.
The entire staff of the Summer Day Camp, all
of whom were experienced teachers, and the pos¬
sessors of considerable experience with children,
performed an excellent job with the camp.
The progressive attitude taken by the Center in
regard to the Day Camp made it a great success, and
insures an even more successful season next year.

Young Men’s Hebrew Association
With the end of the war, the Young Men’s
Hebrew Association expanded its activities into
three new fields, the social field, the athletic field,
and the youth field, in the community life of the
city.
The many facilities of the organization have con¬
tinued to be placed at the disposal of those service¬
men still in our midst and several times during the
year affairs were held for the members of the armed
forces.
In its new fields of activity, the YMHA appears
to have been exceedingly successful. Socially, the
organization inaugurated a Wednesday night social
for adults. The affairs are in the form of buffet
suppers with a social hour taking place thereafter.
The attendance has grown steadily and the evening
seem to be filling a void in the social life of the
Jewish community.
Athletically, the YMHA sponsored a junior base¬
ball team during the winter and for the first time
in many years, the Jewish youngsters of Jacksonville
were provided with a sponsored and organized
team. During the Spring and early Summer, the
YMHA sponsored a diamond ball team in the crack
City league. This all-Jewish aggregation in an or¬
ganized league for the first time in almost a decade
drew favorable coimment from the press and public.
To help the youth of the community, the YMHA
organized a Junior ’Y* and at the end of the year,
this organization of teensters boasted of 62 mem¬
bers and was ever-growing.
The YMHA looks forward to the coming year
with a great deal of enthusiasm as the results of the
recent community v survey have definitely shown
the need of a central organization in the life of the
community. We feel that we have firmly entrenched
ourselves in Jacksonville and shall continue to
strive for the betterment of Jewish community life
in this city.

Dedication
REV. JOSEPH ROBIN
This 43rd annual issue of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
Year Book is this year dedicated to Rev. Joseph Robin, for¬
merly Sexton of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and beloved
communal worker.
In all of the years that the Year Book has been formally
dedicated to an individual, it can truthfully be said that at
no time has the community felt more joy and “nachas” than
in the dedication of this book to Mr. Robin. After more than
fourteen years as a member of the Center staff, Mr. Robin
resigned his position as Sexton because of ill health. So re¬
luctant were both the Center and Mr. Robin to terminate his
position, that the final decision was reached after more than
a year of consideration. In accepting Mr. Robin’s final tender
of resignation last year, the Center Board bestowed upon him
the signal honor, never before tendered, of an honorary life
membership in the institution.
If it is true that the character of an institution is primarily
determined by the men and women who are part of it, rather
than by its physical properties, then the Center owes much
to the friendliness, warmth, religious zeal and enthusiasm
characterizing the personality of Mr. Robin. His genial per¬
sonality and broadminded modern religious philosophy made
him one of the most beloved and revered members of the
entire community, loved equally as well by the young and
the old.
The discharge of his duties as “Shamus” represented only
a small part of the tremendous responsibility which he volun¬
tarily assumed on behalf of the Center. He almost single-
handedly collected the entire dues schedule of the Center, he
supervised the maintenance and operation of the building, and
supervised the maintenance staff. He provided for the gen¬
eral needs of Center members. In addition, for a period of
fourteen years, he prepared approximately 150 boys for ^heir
Bar Mitzvah rites. He has officiated on the High Holidays
as Baal Shacharis or Baal Musaf, as the occasion required.
He assisted the Chevra Kadisha (Cemetery Committee) in
performing last rites for the deceased. In fact, he was almost
indispensible to the successful operation of the institution and
his recent resignation has confirmed this opinion as the Center
even now tries to fill positions, the duties of which were for¬
merly discharged by him.
Aside from the multiple functions performed by Mr. Robin
for the Center, he is a devout Zionist and has given much
of his time to Zionist activities, his pet project being the
Jewish National Fund. It was largely through his efforts
that the J. N. F. has been developed to its present position
of prominence in the community.
On this occasion of the dedication of the Year Book to Mr.
Robin, we pray to the Almighty that he and his family may
be granted many years of life, good health and happiness.

Jacksonville Jewish Center Building Expansion Program
The past year has witnessed the crystallization of the
building plans of the Jacksonville Jewish Center for the future
expansion of its facilities. Although the project for expansion
is slightly more than one year old, much progress has been
made towards the realization of the projected program.
The first step taken during the past year was the collection
of the various pledges made during the 1945-46 campaign.
As a result much of the $75,000.00 pledged has been paid,
with prospects in sight for the immediate payment in full of
all outstanding pledges. Plans are now being completed for
the launching of the second phase of the campaign intended
to double the previously accumulated sum of $75,000.00. It
is planned by the campaign committee that the forthcoming
drive will be extensive and will be designed to reach practically
every member of the Center regardlss of the size of their
probable dcnaticn.
The second phase of the Center building program under¬
taken during the past year was the crystallization of the na-
tuie of the planned extension program. At an early fall
meeting, the Board of Directors of the Center passed a resolu¬
tion recommending that all future expansion of the Center
building facilities be undeitaken at the present location at
Third and Silver Streets. The following circumstances dictat¬
ed the conclusion reached:
(1) At the present time the Center owns approximately
159 feet on Third Street and approximately 160 feet on
Silver Street, exclusive of the alley now running through the
Center property. In addition, the Center owns a plot of land
in the middle of the block directly adjacent to its other prop¬
erty, this plot measuring approximately 70 feet by 70 feel.
Thus the utilization of the present location would obviate the
necessity of using any of the building funds for the purpose
of purchasing land, thus allowing for a much more consider¬
able building program.
(2) Secondly it was felt that by centering all further ex¬
pansion at the present location, the present building of the
institution, could be utilized, thus saving a considerable sum
of money, especially in view of the high cost of building at
the present time.
Page Fourteen
(3)The present location of the institution is centrally
located as far as the distribution of the Jewish population is
concerned. Earlier in the planning of the expansion program,
it was thought advisable to consider Riverside or Southside
as a more centrally located site for the Jewish community,
but a more detailed study showed it to be inadvisable to
attempt' to locate the site in one section of Jewish popualtion
to the detriment of other sections. Building in Riverside or
Southside today might be as much out of line with conditions
twenty years hence as a building program in the central part
of Springfield would have been some twenty years ago.
It was the concensus of opinion that the institution should
be located in a part of the community central to the entire
city and comparatively as easily accessible to all parts as la
one. The obvious site for such a location would be the
downtown section of the community. It was in this light that
the present location of the Center presented itself as an ideal '
site, because of its location on the boundary of what is fast
becoming the business center of Jacksonville. The present
downtown section of Jacksonville is definitely expanding
northwards and will no doubt in the future have as its north¬
ern boundary Hogan’s Creek. This trend will place the:
present Center site adjacent to the downtown section. At the
present time the Center is no more than twelve minutes walk,
from the heart of the downtown business area.
Another attractive feature of the present site is its location
in the heart of a section already replete with many civic and
church buildings, such as the State Board of Health, city
parks, municipal radio station, St. Luke’s Hospital, city play¬
ground, swimming pool and tennis courts, and a number of
the outstanding churches of the community. The future
character cf the neighborhood is thus assured.
Having reached the conclusion to make further expansion
at its present site, the Center Board then embarked upon its
next move in the crystallization of its plans. The Archirec-
ural film of Kemp, Jackson, Bunch and Mizrahi was retained
to draw plans for the expansion of the present building facil¬
ities and the erection of whatever new buildings are needed.
Mr. Ralph Mizrahi, a member of the firm and an enthusiastic
member of the Center, is already engaged in the process of
drawing the preliminary plans and sketches.

Jewish Progressive Credit Union
Time and again, many of the Jewish merchants,
for various and sundry reasons, had found it well
-nigh impossible to obtain business loans from their
local banks, when they were in dire need of funds
with which to liquidate their due bills so that they
could continue to receive muchly needed merchan¬
dise without any hindrances or delays in shipment.
Hence, on February First, 1 940, several of these
merchants met to discuss ways and means of obtain¬
ing assistance of this nature in the then ”trying”
times.
It was decided to form a Credit Union which was
to be sponsored by the Jewish Progressive Society
of Jacksonville, Florida.
Under a charter from the National Credit Union of
Madison, Wisconsin, a system originally founded
by Edward A. Filene, the Progressive Credit Union
was inaugurated to foster savings and promote in¬
vestments and loans,
The success with which it met is attested by its
rapid growth which, as of October First, 1946,
shows a membership of one hundred and eighty, and
assets approximating one hundred and ten thousand
dollars. Since the date of its birth, the Progressive
Credit Union has made five hundred and twenty-
five loans with an aggregate total of more than half
a million ollars.
Savings in the organization are known as shares,
and all shares up to one thousand dollars are
’’covered” by FREE Life Insurance on the following
percentages;
Up to 55 years of age -
100% of the amount of savings.
55 to 60 years of age -
75% of the amount of savings
60 to 65 years of age -
50% of the amount of savings.
65 to 70 years of age -
25% of the amount of savings.

President Harry S. Truman, in a letter to the recent 29th
annual meeting of the National Jewish Welfare Board, stated
in part: “Coming on the eve of the first anniversary ot
V-E Day, the keynote of your meeting serves to emphasize
what too many have forgotten—that the laying down of arms
and the signing of an armistice do not of themselves bring
peace, and that the effective transition from war to peace
requires the same kind of national unity and consecration to
duty as did the building of the arsenal of democracy and
the winning of the war.”
“From War to Peaee” has been the dominating theme for
USO-JWB programs in the Jacksonville area during the past
year. This program cannot be valued in terms of statistics
alone: Of the thousands of servicemen, “Guardians of
Peace”, who availed themselves of special USO-JWB social
and cultural activities arranged in Jacksonville; of the re¬
ligious services held regularly at the Naval Air Station; of the
Oneg Shabbats with USO-JWB junior hostesses; of Jackson¬
ville’s major Jewish organizations who have cooperated so
splendidly at the weekly affairs in the Jewish Center where
a new attendance record was reached during the past several
months; of the Jewish organizations’ activities at the Duval
Street USO Club; of the myriad types of personal services
rendered for hundreds of members of the armed forces; of
inter-faith relationship and good-will carried to all corners
of America by those who received a “good deal.”
It is a credit to the Jacksonville Jewish Community that
it has not—and will not—“let down” the members of the
armed forces who served valiantly in time of war and those
who have been called to the colors to preserve the ways ot
peace. The saying, “Once ye too were strangers in the land
of Egypt” has not been forgotten. To the youngsters away
from home for the first time in sarange surroundings, there
has been brought cheer and understanding so much so that
many have written that acksonville to them has been a “home
away from home.”

Jacksonville Jewish Center Building Expansion Program
(CONTINUED)
Thus the past year has seen considerable progress in !he
Center’s plans to expand its building facilities to house the
extended program of the institution. The present tentative
plans for expansion are aimed to meet the following geneial
requirements:
Synagogue
A synagogue capable of accomodating not less than 1200
worshipers and preferably 1500 so as to accomodate the pres¬
ent expanded membership of the institution. The present
edifice takes care of the seating requiremnts of only a portion
of the general membership. At present, each member receives
only two seats, irregardless of the size of the family, thus
negating the principle of family worship. The most deplor¬
able result of this situation is the separation of the child from
his parents during services.
In addition*'to expanding the size of the synagogue, it is
hoped that the enlarged structure will be so decorated as to
make for reverence and beauty and to provide an environment
conducive to inspiring and decorous services. A much larger
auxiliary chapel is also needed to meet the needs of the en¬
larged Junior Congregation and the daily minyon.
Auditorium
One of the chief requirements of the Center is an enlarged
auditorium capable of seating not less than 750 persons and
equipped to serve comfortably at least 500 persons for ban¬
quets and dinners. In addition to enlarged floor space is the
pressing need for adequate kitchen facilities, stage equipment,
motion picture equipment, cloak room accomodations, etc.
The present auditorium has shown itself on numerous
occasions to be inadequate for even the present needs of the
community, not to mention its future requirements. Recent
presentations of the lyceum series, appearances of well known
artists, and even normal school affairs have shown the gross
inadequate seating facilities of the present auditorium. Social
affairs such as dances, banquets, carnivals, etc., have been
restricted by the present auditorium facilities. Unless this is
remedied the Center will be unable to cater to future social
and communal affairs, but will witness the removal of these
activities to other accomodations.
Educational Facilities
The present inadequate educational facilities of the Center
present the strongest argument yet for the contemplated ex¬
pansion program. The minimum requirements for the future
consist of a modern educational building capable of meeting
the needs of twelve classes in session at one time, equipped to
provide for a Sunday School of 300 to 400 students, a
Hebrew School of 100 to 150, a daily kindergarten of from
50 to 75 children; and a Day Camp of 100 to 150 children.
Such facilities as private offices for principal and teachers,
library, storerooms, etc., must be provided. These require¬
ments are based on a minimum anticipation of future needs
for the next 25 years, which space of time is the minimum to
be used in planning for future needs.
The present practice of holding Sunday School classes in
such places as private studies, synagogue, daily chapel, lounges,
etc. is not conducive to the obtaining of the best educational
results. None of the present rooms used are large enough
nor or they properly equipped. The daily kindergarten does
not meet in the proper type of room, nor does the Day Camp
have the proepr facilities. These improved educational
facilities must be provided.
Educational and Social Needs
The Center must provide for its members and especially for
its children facilities for wholesome recreational and social
activities. The provision of such facilities is in keeping witha
one of the characteristic and traditional phases of the syna¬
gogue as a “Bes Haknesses’’ or House of Assembly for the
Community. The supplying of these activities is only a normal
function and role of the modern synagogue center.
Especially is this type of program necessary for the assur¬
ance of the success of the educational program of the Center,
which requires that Judaism be taught as a way of life.
The present facilities consisting of the recreational and lounge
facilities in the auxiliary home on Third Street, the basketball
court in the rear, and the Center Auditorium are still not up
to the standard required for a modern synagogue center.

Young Judean Group
1945-46 was an active year for Jacksonville’s six Young
Judaean Clubs. “The purpose of Young Judaea has been and
will continue throughout the coming year to bring to the Jew¬
ish boy and girl the love and understanding of Zion and
Zionism.”
The senior clubs enjoyed a Chanukah party collectively,
while the three junior clubs held individual parties. One ot
the high lights of the year was the Palestine Fair held a week
before Purim. This was held in the Jewish Center, and aíl
clubs had booths at which handicraft made by the members
was sold. There was also a Tel Aviv Cafe and a Puppet
Show, as well as a Palestine film shown. The proceeds of
the event went to Jewish National Fund, Youth Aliyah and
Karen Hanoar.
Herbert Sohn, a member of the Meyer Levin Club, and past
vice-president of the Southern Region, was appointed Presi¬
dent of the Southern region at a business conference of the
regional officers in Memphis.
Another outstanding event of the year was the Senior Clubs
debating tournament. Due to the fact that one of the debat¬
ers became ill at the closing contest, the tournament will be
concluded in September at the opening tea of the senior clubs.
A feather in the cap of the editor, Stanley Hammerman, was

the Young Judaea newspaper, which made three appearances

Closing the year for the senior clubs was a luncheon held in
the Seminole Hotel. Louis Safer, president of Masada, was
the main speaker. While the clubs operated collectively
through the Senior Council and had many affairs together,
each club carried on its own activities. The Chaim Weizmann
club held a chess tournament and the Henrietta Szold Club
held a swimming party and a weiner roast. Features of these
events were Palestinian folk dancing and singing. Another
project in which all clubs combined was the Choral Group
which sang at the Zionist Youth Commission Banquet in
November.
The Junior Clubs, which are the “Aliyah,” “Theodore
Herzl”, and Rothchild,” each had picnics and did handicraft
work.
Through a uniform and developed program, the Young
Judaean clubs of Jasksonville, which range in age groups
from 10 to 18, intend making this next year one filled to
capacity with a program led by adult advisors in debate and
argumentation, Palestinian arts and crafts, Palestinian singing
and dancing, athletics, and a clear understanding of Zionism
and Palestine.

The Cemetery Committee is one group working within the
Center whose work is not of the spectacular type but is yet
productive of the most worthwhile and needed services per¬
formed by the institution. Members of this Committee are
called on at all hours of the day and night to perform services
for members of the congregation in the hours of their be¬
reavement.
The Cemetery Committee made considerable progress in the
last several years in solidifying its financial position and in
making plans for its expansion. In keeping with this policy,
a tract adjoining the Evergreen Cemetery on its western
boundary was purchased. This new plot consists of approx¬
imately eleven acres and when developed will represent one
of the finest cemeteries in this vicinity. Plans for its future
development are now being made.
One change was made in the personnel of the committee
during the past year, with Jacob Becker succeeding I. M.
Lieberman, who resigned because of poor health. The posi¬
tion of Assistant Chairman of the Committee was established
during the past year and Oscar Magezis was named to that
office.

Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society
The year just completed was the most momen¬
tous in the history of the Lades Hebrew Sheltering
Aid Society. A number of years ago, this organi¬
zation set for itself the project of building and
establishing a home for the Hebrew aged of
Florida, and it is with a great deal of pride and
satisfaction that this goal was accomplished during
the past year.
The beautiful riverfront home has been com¬
pletely remodeled and renovated to suit the needs
of the residents, and is at present occupied to
capacity. The society is deeply grateful to its many
friends whose generous contributions made this
possible. The project assumed such proportions
that it was decided to maintain the home as a sep¬
arate organization, with both men and women
membership, with the Ladies society having rep¬
resentation on the board of the home. The Ladies
Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society has further pledged
itself to continue its work for the Home in the
capacity of a Ladies Auxiliary.
In addition to its work for the home, the Society
continued its work in aiding transients and local
needy people. Special food baskets were sent to
state institutions. The members have cooperated
heartily with the U. S. O., and have sponsored a
number of affairs for the men in the service.

Cub Scout Pack No. 14, of the Jacksonville Jewish Center,
concluded the most successful year in its history at a joint
Cub and Scout Banquet held in the Center auditorium on
May 5, 1946.
A number of extremely pleasant affairs have taken place
in the Cub Pack since it was reorganized in May, 1945. A
most enjoyable picnic was held at Rubin’s Camp in June,
1945, with food and fun and frolic galore. July saw the
Pack at a beach outing together with the members of the
Center Summer Day Camp. Many contests and prizes marked
the event. August was high-lighted by a fishing trip to Pear¬
son’s Camp; not much fish, but again plenty of fun. More
recently the entire Pack visited the 16th Fleet at Green Cove
Springs, and once again it was a tired but happy bunch that
returned home in the evening.
These activities and many others at the regular pack meet¬
ings were made possible by the wholehearted cooperation of
all the Cub Parents, particularly the Committee Chairman, Mr.

Canfor Abraham Marfon Joins Cenfer Staff
REV. ABRAHAM MARTON
Cantor
The Center feels itself fortunate in having obtained the services of the Rev.
Abraham Marton as Cantor and Hebrew School instructor. In addition to his cantoral
duties, Cantor Marton will direct the musical activities of the Center, including the
choir and choral groups, both adult and junior.
Cantor Marton is a native of Vienna, Austria, where he received his Jewish
education and musical training. He is a graduate of the Jewish Theological Seminary
of Vienna and of the Schubert Conservatory of Music in Vienna. His cantoral train¬
ing was received from his association with several of the better known cantors of
Europe including such men as Cantor Mandell, Cantor Frankel and Cantor L .Miller.
He has filled the position of Cantor in congregations at Youngstown, Ohio, Balti¬
more, Maryland, Lebanon, Pennsylvania, and Brooklyn, New York. Although one of
the youngest men in the Cantorate, Rev. Marton is ranked as one of the outstanding
Cantors in the country by the recent ratings of the Cantors Association of New York.
In addition to his Cantoral abilities, Cantor Marton is a well trained and experi¬
enced pedagogue, having acted as Principal of Hebrew Schools at Lebanon, Baltimore,
and Brooklyn. He is at the present time acting as principal of the Center Hebrew
School in the absence of a Rabbi.
Cantor Marton has already trained and developed a male choir for the High
Holy Day services and is now in the process of training a choral group for participation
in the regular,Friday evening services. A junior choral group is also being organized
under the guidance of the Cantor.
