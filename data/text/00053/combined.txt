What Being Jewish Means To Me
In reaction to the Jewish community’s concern with the issue
of “continuity”, the American Jewish Committee inaugu¬
rated a program called “Why Be Jewish?”. The purpose of
the program is to assure that there will be a critical mass of
Jews willing and able to lead creative Jewish lives. Without
these assurances, all other efforts for Jewish community
building are, at best, transitory. The “Why Be Jewish”
effort of the American Jewish Committee is a multi-faceted
program. This past year it included a series of advertise¬
ments in the New York Times and other major American
newspapers which highlighted the personal meaning that
Judaism has to a wide ranging variety of interesting people.
These essays include the reflections of United States Sena¬
tors, Nobel Prize winners, successful businessmen, writers
and novelists, as well as a number of young people who spoke
from their own unique perspective. One of the most compel¬
ling testimonies came from a college student whose message
was the winning essay in a national contest for undergradu¬
ates on “The Meaning of Being Jewish”. We have presented
it here, for your consideration on the eve of the High Holidays.
What follows are the personal reflections of Leonid Petlakh.
We are confident you will find his statement a thoughtful one.
Leonid Petlakh
Hunter College
Class of 1995
Growing up in Minsk, in the former Soviet Union, I knew my parents were Jewish by birth. Beyond
that, all I knew about Judaism was that the word “Jew” stamped in my internal passport was going
to spoil my life. It was a cause for shame and humiliation. I learned that lesson in the first grade, when
my classmates taunted me with cries of “zhid”, the derogatory word for Jew. Distraught, I ran home
to my parents. With heavy hearts they explained that a Jew in the Soviet Union was doomed to second-
class status in school, at work, in society. In those days, anti-Semitism was everywhere. And to make
matters worse, there were no teachers, mentors, or friends who could be positive Jewish role models.
Things began to change in 1987 as many Jews began to emerge from their spiritual hiding places, eager
to affirm their tong-oppressed Jewishness. One Saturday, out of curiosity, I joined a group of young
people who had gathered outside the only synagogue in Minsk, not to pray, but to draw strength from
being together. I returned to that gathering many times and, in the process, found I was becoming a
Jew.
In 1989 we left Minsk via Italy for the United States. During the four months we spent at the emigres’
transit station near Rome, I read everything I could on Israel, the Holocaust and Judaism. My purely
ethnic connection was gradually taking on a religious dimension. Entering college here, I found a gifted
teacher and mentor, and, to my great surprise, I was drawn to Jewish studies. Finally, positive Jewish
experiences were shaping my Jewish identity. Before I knew what was happening, Jewish learning
had become a passion.
I began to understand the richness of our heritage. I had fallen in love with Judaism. And last summer,
seeing Israel for the first time, I experienced another dimension of being Jewish—the excitement of
a modem and ancient Jewish homeland. I have now decided to become a rabbi, to continue learning and
then to teach. Being Jewish means much to me: it means advancing the good of my people by sharing
the beauty and depth of Judaism; it is to be involved in tikkláü o/am. the healing of the world for the
betterment of all people; and it is to be a rodef shalom. a pursuer of peace, in a troubled and often violent
world. I am grateful for the precious gift of freedom. As a Jew, I believe that freedom includes the
responsibility to preserve my precious heritage and to transmit it—something my parents and
grandparents were not allowed to do. My greatest wish is that I may some day teach my own children
the fulfillment of being a Jew.

Afternoon Hebrew School
Afternoon Hebrew School
The Afternoon Hebrew School of Jacksonville Jewish Center is designed for students in
grades 3-7 and provides an elementary education leading to Bar/Bat Mitzvah and continuation in
the high school program.
Meeting Mondays and Wednesdays and on Saturdays, "the basics" are at the core of the
curriculum, with stress on Hebrew reading, Jewish Social Studies, and current events relating to
Israel. Synagogue skills, holiday observances and knowledge of prayers are all part of the compre¬
hensive Afternoon Hebrew School program.

BBYO • B'nai B'rith Youth Organization
The B’nai B’rith Youth Organization (BBYO) is the largest youth organization
in the world. It consists of two unique chapters, Aleph Zadik Aleph (AZA) for
young men and B’nai B’rith Girls (BBG) for young women.
AZA, which stands for fraternity and harmony, focuses on building Jewish
fraternal bonds while taking part in projects that benefit the community.
BBG focuses on sisterhood and social action and encourages members to
take active roles in the community, as Jews and as concerned citizens.
Each group is actively involved in the Jewish community, volunteering at local
synagogues and attending regularly scheduled programs on Jewish ethics and
modem values. BBG and AZA also work together to make positive changes
in their communities.
BBYO also promotes a feeling of unity, togetherness and belonging through
chapter programs, conventions, retreats and summer programs.
If you are a Jewish teenager in grades 9 -12, we invite you to join BBYO.

A Message From Rabbi David Gaffney
One of my favorite High Holiday stories is a story of a young Jew who in 1913 attended
Kol Nidre services in a small synagogue in Berlin, determined that this would be his
last Jewish experience. This young man, Franz Rosenzweig, knew that he was a Jew,
but knew very little about Judaism. He had grown up in a nominally Jewish home, but
like so many of the intellectual Jewish families of early twentieth century Germany,
there was very little that was Jewish about it. Oh, yes, annually, the family would
attend High Holiday Services at a local Temple... but that was it. Rosenzweig had
nothing against Judaism: it just didn’t seem to matter to anyone he knew. He went off
to college, studied philosophy and for the first time met deeply religious Christians
and he began to envy them. They knew what they stood for.... they had a guide, a source
of inspiration to turn to when they were troubled.
Rosenzweig was challenged to defend his nominal Jewishness or convert to Christian¬
ity. He contrasted their deeply rooted faith with the shallow pretense, the floundering
inconsistency that he had seen in his Jewish home. Finally, he resolved to become a
Christian but decided that he would go to services one last time on Yom Kippur and say
goodbye to his Jewish past. He told his parents. . .and they were shocked. “How can
we change Judaism”, they asked, “so that you will be willing to accept it? What can
we do to make it easier, and more convenient for you?” When he declared his
conversion plans, they said, “not in our Temple. You won’t use our Temple as a
stepping stone to renounce your faith.” Rosenzweig decided to attend Kol Nidre
services at the small synagogue. However, when he came out a few hours later he was
a changed man. He found something in Judaism he never knew was there before. He realized that Judaism could answer
his spiritual questions and give his life deepened meaning. From that day on Franz Rosenzweig studied, thought about,
and lived Judaism. He resolved to be committed to its tradition and to its history. He became one of the outstanding
philosophers of Judaism and his writing helped to set in motion a German-Jewish cultural and spiritual renaissance. What
happened that night in that small synagogue to change this man’s life completely? What happened to make him see
Jewishness in a totally new light? It wasn’t the sermon. It wasn’t that the building was impressive ... or that the
Cantor and choir were so good.
What he found was something he had never seen before. . .a community of Jews who cared about their tradition. The
people in the synagogue were not strangers to what they were doing. They were not an audience going to synagogue
in September the way they would go to a symphony in December. What he had found was an intensity . . .a sense of spiritual
engagement that he had never seen before. It may have been that his father or mother asked him - “Couldn’t you remain
Jewish for our sake, even if there isn’t anything to it? We are asking so little of you. We are not asking you to observe
anything, to do anything, even to believe in anything Jewish. We are just asking you to survive.” He might have
answered, “That’s exactly the trouble. Why should I take seriously a religion which asks so little of me ... a religion
that is only a label with no content. I want my religion to make great demands of me, and you offer me a diluted diet
of ‘be a nice person and come back next year’.” Rosenzweig’s experience in that synagogue teaches us something
important. Judaism is not primarily an idea ... it is a religious community. What ultimately “converted” Rosenzweig
back to Judaism (if I can use that term) was the impact of a Jewish community—real live people turned on by the reality
of their Jewishness. What “converted” him was not any idea about Judaism ... or any idea about God. Even God is only
an idea until people make Him real in the world by doing Godly things, by basing their actions on what He stands for. He
had heard “ideas” about the Shabbat. . .and about Kashrut. . . and the Torah, but he had never seen a family really
experiencing Shabbat. . .really hallowing their home so that it becomes a place for the presence of God.
I tell you this story because I am continually telling myself this story. Every time I get up to conduct a High Holiday
service I ask myself, “What if there is a bright young person here. . . another Franz Rosenzweig, who came to our
synagogue to give Judaism another chance?” I tell you this story because it is not only important for me to know the
story. . .you have to know it too. Rosenzweig was saved for Judaism by a life-giving encounter with a congregation
of Jews who brought Judaism to life and made it real and powerful. This is where you come in. You may feel miscast.
I am hopeful that you might play the role of an inspired congregant, but you feel more comfortable playing the role of
Rosenzweig, and that is the crux of the issue. We are all Rosenzweigs. We read about Judaism. . . we hear about it.
. .we know some of its ideas. But not too many of us live it passionately, authentically, and comprehensively. Can we
blame our children for thinking Judaism is an occasional garment to be dusted off and worn for special occasions. We
send them to school to learn about Jewish life, and when that doesn’t turn them into Jews we blame the teacher, we
blame the synagogue, and we don’t realize where the problem lies. What shapes people into Jews is a vibrant Jewish
community. The synagogue cannot convert people, because the synagogue is only a building. You need a congregation
to convert people; a fellowship of people who say — “skin deep Judaism never did anything for us—we want to find out
if “soul deep Judaism is any different. If we strengthen one another and reinforce one another we might become that
community. Can we afford to allow our young Rosenzweigs to go another year without sensing what being part of a
committed community means? As we read the data and the statistics of the American Jewish community, we realize
that this is the time to act.
The Rosenzweig story is eighty years old. . .but the moral of the story is applicable. . .in our homes and in our lives
and in the lives of our children today. For all of us and for those we love, may this year be one of transformation and
renewal. May it be a year that blesses us with fulfillment. . .with health and with peace. Amen.
Rabbi David and
Inge Gaffney

Message from Rabbi Dov Kenfof
Message From Rabbi Dov Kentof
Long ago it was said, “An ignorant person cannot be pious; one who is bashful cannot learn (he is
afraid or ashamed to ask); he who is irritable an impatient cannot teach” (Avot 2).
Study and learning are Judaism’s cornerstones. When the Torah was accepted by the Children of
Israel at Sinai, it was only after they had called out Na’aseh v’nishma, we shall do and we shall
understand. Since then, knowledge and understanding have been the core of Jewish living.
The cultural history of the Jewish people can be traced through books: The Torah, The Bible,
The Talmud, The Siddur, the commentaries of Maimonides and Rashi, the Shulchan Aruch, and
the Zohar. These are just a few of the thousands of volumes that serve as signposts and mile¬
stones on the long journey of the Jewish people.
In order to understand Judaism’s relevance, one must study the vast sea of available literature all
throughout life. The sages understood this and taught that the person who didn’t increase their
knowledge, decreased it.
The same Mishna warns about taking the attitude of “I’ll
study when I find the time” stating that time may never
be found. Regular study and scheduled times for learning are essential if a person is to advance his/her
knowledge.
The sages advised that we know what to answer to those who would question or challenge the Jewish faith
and the Land of Israel. In order to do so, however, one must possess a thorough knowledge of our religion
and our homeland. This can be achieved only through study.
Some would argue that the only time such intensive, continuous study is necessary is when a person wishes to
become a Rabbi; but the Talmud states “Torah study is valued as much as all other mitzvot combined.” The
unending pursuit of God’s teachings can be nothing but beneficial.
Recently, the late Chief Rabbi of Israel, Avraham Yitzchak Kook, put it another way: “The main aim of study
is to mould man into his civilized form ... Man is civilized if he acts justly and honestly; to educate man to live
in that framework is the supreme aim of Jewish religious study. The surest way to achieve this aim is not
through an ethics-divorced religion, but through a thorough religious grounding. Without God and religion,
any system of ethical education will lack a solid base and constitute an unreliable instrument for civilizing
man.”
Cindy Marsh
Secretary
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Afternoon Hebrew School, Sunday School, and High School have always placed the most emphasis on quality of
education and strengthening the students’ love of Judaism. The combination of class time, a dedicated/caring staff, and meaningful activities work
together to make this possible. As a result, we produce exceptional students who are aware and conscious of their Judaism and have a love for it and
their synagogue.
We pledge to continue to reach higher and higher to attain our goals in the years to come.
The Jewish future lies in Jewish study. More Jewish homes need to be filled with more
Jewish books. Learning must become an integral part of every Jewish person’s life.
Educated people such as our teachers and spiritual leaders, as well as philanthropists, are to
be honored because as the Talmud observes, “The learned increase peace and happiness
in the world.”
Making Jewish education a priority in your home will help to make Jewish living a priority
throughout the lives of your children.

Committees
Education Committee
Stuart Appelbaum
Cheryl Blum, Chairs
The Education Committee is responsible for providing policy and overseeing the
Center’s educational programs and schools. These include the Early Childhood
Department (Pre-School and Day Care), the Solomon Schechter Day School, the
Supplemental School Department (Sunday School, Afternoon Hebrew School, and
Hebrew High School). The Jewish education of our children is one of the most
important functions of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Over400 children are served
by the schools of the Center.
The Education Committee is co-chaired by Cheryl Blum and Stu Appelbaum.
Members of the Committee in 1993-94 were: Trish Bettman, Horty Gendzier,
Sheldon Gendzier, Jan Glassman, Carole Goldberg, Dr. Larry Goldberg, Rhoda
Goldstein, Annetta Komblum, Jeffery Morris, Dr. Marco Rand, Ellen Rubens, Eliot
Safer and Arlene Yegelwel.
This past year was a very successful year for the Center’s schools. Enrollment
for the Solomon Schechter Day School reached record levels. During this year the
planning for our Middle School program, which begins in September 1994, was
accomplished, We are pleased that the Jacksonville Jewish Federation recognized
the importance of the Day School by significantly increasing our allocation this year.
The Early Childhood Department also showed significant enrollment gains this year.
A new brochure was produced to highlight our Pre-School and Day Care programs.
Our Supplemental Schools also had a very good year. Our Sunday School program
was greatly enhanced by our new staff members and the large number of students.
We are proud that the Center was part of the new Community Hebrew High School,
Beit Midrash Jacksonville, This program enabled our children to attend class with
Jewish children from other congregations. The faculty, which included Rabbis from
all of the Jacksonville congregations, provided enhanced learning experiences for
our children.
The Center’s schools could not be successful without the efforts of our
dedicated administrators and staff. They give so much for our most precious
resource - our children. The Education Committee thanks them for all their efforts.
Mitzvah Committee
Joan Waitz, Chair
The Mitzvah Committee of the Jacksonville Jewish Center is pleased to perform
the mitzvah of Bikkur Holim “visiting the sick” which is a religious imperative for
us as Jews. The ancient tradition finds its roots in a number of biblical and rabbinic
texts. InBereshit, the tirst book of the Torah, we leam that human beings are created
b’tzelerry Elohim, in God’s image. Judaism understands that to mean that we are
required to emulate the ethical attributes of God: mercy, kindness, forgiveness,
graciousness.
Modem society with its wondrous advances in medical technology, intimidating
procedures and sterile hospital environments, has, if anything, increased the need
for Bikkur Holim. The needs of the sick and aged require not only the best of
scientific knowledge, but also the loving care of family, friends, neighbors and the
synagogue community. If you have the time, please join this important committee.
Mitzvah Committee Members: Martin Brauner, Stella Finck, Beverly Geller, Sonya
Morris, Faye Price, Dorothy Shenkman, Joan Waitz.
Youth Commission
Robert Cantor,Chair
Horty Gendzier, Youth V.P.
The purpose of the Jacksonville Jewish Center Youth Commission is to over see
and set appropriate goals for all of the Center Youth Groups. Chalutzim, Kadima,
USY, Boy Scouts and Girl Scouts are all vital to the future of our people. The Youth
Commission sets proper standards for the programming of these groups.
The Scout programs help to instill proper values, to teach survival and develop
community skills.The Youth Commission helps these programs become meaningful
within the framework of Jewish life.
With guidelines from this committee, our young people have an opportunity to
socialize with other Jewish youth in a wholesome, value promoting atmosphere.

Committees
Religious Committee
Edward Tannen, V.P. Religious
The Religious Committee, after extensive study and discussion, approved a
revision in the religious ritual of the Jacksonville Jewish Center: permitting a woman
who is to chant the haftorah to read the maftir portion of the Torah. The new policy
was implemented almost immediately, as Cantor Fox trained a Bat Mitzvah
candidate to read her maftir portion within several weeks of the effective date of
the policy revision.
Under the able tutelage of Bob Bossen, we are seeing a number of the younger
members of our congregation assisting as gabbaim on the bema during the reading
of the Torah, since several of our members who previously performed this
important function are no longer able to do so.
Maintaining the daily minyan continues to be the main concern of the committee.
Although we still seem to be able to provide a minyan every morning and evening,
it is a hit-or-miss proposition from day to day for the minyan captains to assemble
a minyan. The Religious Committee cannot, by itself, keep the daily minyan
running. The entire congregation must participate if we are going to be able to
provide a minyan every day so that our members may observe the Yahrzeit of their
loved ones no matter what day of the year it occurs.
The Committee spends a great deal of time during the summer months preparing
for the High Holidays by distributing the honors for Rosh Hashanah and Yom
Kippur. We do our best to assure that all deserving members of the congregation
receive the honors they merit.
Membership Committee
Shirley Bielski, V.P. Membership
The Membership Committee of the Jacksonville Jewish Center is committed to
extending friendship, recruiting, retaining and most importantly, involving new¬
comers in the life of our congregation. Under the umbrella of the membership
committee are the mitzvah committee, the buddy committee and marketing and
public relations committees. In order to meet the needs of our multifaceted Center
family, our committee includes representation from all groups of the congregation,
from singles, to new Americans, and everyone in between. We are sustained and
strengthened by the entire membership of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
The membership committee has been responsible for the following mitzvot:
greeting congregants each Shabbat morning; hospital visitation; matching buddies
to newcomers; Shabbat dinners; reaching out to young singles in the community;
providing home hospitality to college students and Navy personnel during holidays;
honoring new members at Shabbat services and informal social get-togethers.
We are a family together during good times as well as difficult times. We mourn
the loss of family and dear friends whose memories will remain with us always, and
we rejoice in the celebration of life cycle events. In the past two years, under the
chairmanship of Janis Fleet and Brad Jolson, 94 people have joined our
congregation. During those two years we have seen the birth of 33 babies, 52 Bar
and Bat Mitzvahs and 13 weddings. There were also 10 more weddings celebrated
in other cities by the children of our members.
The Friday evening Shabbat Connections have brought together diverse groups
of our Center family at regular intervals during the year - this is a time to come
together for Shabbat dinners and informal services. Our 2nd annual “Christmas
Eve” Shabbat connection was a wonderful success.
Our goal for the coming year, under the chairmanship of Rhoda Goldstein, is to
involve more of our new members in the committees of our congregation. As
mentioned in our last Center Bulletin; “One really does not feel a sense of belonging
until one labors and works for the common good, the enrichment of Jewish life.”
Our buddy committee tries to match up newcomers with Center members to help
make the transition to Jacksonville a little easier. We extend our gratitude to Judy
Paul and Mary Zimmerman for all their efforts. Also to Sonya Morris, Joan Waitz,
Faye Price and their friends for bringing Center cheer to our congregants during
their stay in our area hospitals, and a special thank you to all our committee
members for their phone calls and dedication.
As we look to the future, may we all continue to face our responsibilities with
renewed commitment by reaching out to newcomers, strengthening existing
relationships and promoting Jewish continuity, thereby giving purpose to our
existence.

Brandéis University National Women’s Committee pro¬
vides financial support for the Brandéis University Li¬
braries, which serve a community of students and schol¬
ars, in education and research, united by their com¬
mitment to the pursuit of knowledge. Members act as
emissaries to support and enhance the image of Brandéis,
a Jewish sponsored, non-sectarian university where
excellence is the tradition.
The Jacksonville chapter was organized in September
1991 and has about 120 members. In addition to
fundraising projects, members participate in study groups
using college syllabi prepared by Brandéis University
professors. A visiting professor attends annually.
Membership in the National Women’s Committee is
non-sectarian and open to non-graduates of Brandéis
and to men.

Valued Volunteer
A Tribute
to Philip Selber
(Excerpt from speech
given by Rabbi Gaffney
at the Annual Meeting,
June 26, 1994)
"...There are many bright people who will make possible the expan¬
sion of scientific achievements and technological advances. There is
evidence, however, that what we lack is not the wisdom of the mind
but the wisdom of the heart. The candle continues to remain small.
The leadership that we need is the leadership that provides the
wisdom that nourishes the heart and the soul; a wisdom that comes
from a vision, from a Godly dedication and from the willingness to
realize that we build not only for now but that in our building we plant
seeds for eternity.
We are here this evening to honor such a man; a man who like the
great Jewish altars, enabling our community to continue to worship
God, to be true to its faith and to realize in our collective will those
traditions that give character to our life. Philip Selber has been that
khaham lev - “a man with wisdom of the heart’ who has by the
sheer force of his personality and by his will nurtured the flame of
Jewish life in our community so that it might expand with the chang¬
ing needs of Jewish life here in Jacksonville. This congregation ...
this Jacksonville Jewish Center is evidence of the builder he has
been.
And Philip did it by sheer will. He also did it with a generosity and
with an understanding that his deeds would secure Jewish life for our
congregation and consequently for our community. He has for so
long assumed these as personal burdens ... carrying it on his own
shoulders 4 not only the curtains and the boards - the secular aspects
of the life of the synagogue, but the menorah and the Ark and the
altar - its religious aspects as well."

Men*s Club
Dear Friends,
I have a tough act to follow.
Three years ago Rabbi Gaffney asked Ira Grann to start a Center Men’s Club
Chapter. Ira put together a board of directors, and began building the chapter
from scratch. He established the Men’s Club as a can-do service organization
that’s always ready to pitch in and help.
Neil Sandler took the hand-off from Ira and built the Jacksonville Chapter
into an important Center organization. He involved the Men’s Club in youth-
oriented activities such as Havdalah Under the Stars, Sukkah decorating and
donations to youth activities. He also made sure there were plenty of activities
where we could kick back and relax with the guys.
These two men left a tough act to follow, but a great tradition on which
to build.
We will continue book presentations to Bar and Bat Mitzvahs and
sponsoring the Golden Kippa Award. Our successful programs of past years
will be carried on. These include the River Garden fishing trip, the blood drive,
the Yom Ha Shoah candle project and cooking latkes for the Hanukkah
Carnival.
In August we kicked off the new year with a tubing trip to the
Itchetucknee River. We are also planning a mayoral candidate debate, a wine
tasting, a coffee tasting and an oldies dance.
This year “Boys Night Out” will feature a different activity each month.
For instance, we plan to be making trips to the dog track, a sports bar
and a guy’s action movie.
Gentlemen, come and join us for a year of fun, fellowship and good works.
If you don’t, man are you missing out!
Ken Organes

Day School Strengthens Its Program

Willard Kennedy, Principal
As the Solomon Schechter Day School enters another year of service to the community,
it marks a new milestone of achievement.
We have added a Middle School Division to our overall structure which now embodies
a Primary Department (Kindergarten through Third Grade), an Intermediate Depart¬
ment (Grades Four and Five); and now the Middle School (Grades Six thru Eight). In
addition, our enrollment is at an all time high.
Some ask why we continue to expand our services. The answer, simply put, is that
Jewish Day School education has been identified as one of the strongest forces in
preserving Jewish continuity in America. Students who attend a Jewish Day School such
as our Solomon Schechter establish a firm foundation based on knowledge of source
material, Hebrew language, peer involvement, and values clarified through the prism
of Judaism. They leave our school knowing who they are based on their study of history
and religion as well as identification with all of the holidays on an intellectual and
emotional level.
Schechter students who enter other schools, both public and private, for the first time
after leaving us are proud and confident of themselves both as Jews and academic
achievers. Our students have been eminently successful, not only as scholars, but more
importantly as citizens of the schools they have attended.
This is a fulfillment of our mission statement. These accomplishments are our reason
for existence. These reasons are why you support our school and allow us to serve the
youth of this community.

Center Cemetery
Charlotte Fialkow and Blanche Slott, chairpersons
The Center owns and operates two cemeteries, both adjacent to the Evergreen Cemetery in North Jacksonville. Both
are beautiful pieces of property with large shade trees and mature landscaping. The original cemetery is referred to as
the “Old Cemetery.” The new cemetery is known at the Center Memorial Park. The Center provides complete
maintenance of these grounds. Both cemeteries are open daylight hours every day except Shabbat and Jewish holidays.
A Memorial Service is conducted each year on the Sunday between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur at each of the
cemeteries.
Just as with other life cycle events at the Center, burial and mourning rites are reserved for Center members. Therefore,
only full members may purchase a plot in one of the Center cemeteries. Associate members are not eligible to purchase
a plot. For more information regarding the purchase of cemetery plots, please call the Center administrative office.
We thank the volunteers who serve on this important committee: Charlotte Fialkow and Blanche Slott,co-
chairmen; Sid Berkowitz, David Goldring, Dr. Jeffrey Goldstein, Jeffery Morris, Nathan Sandler, Michael

Chevrah Kaddisha
One of the most sacred tenets of Judaism is that the dead be treated
with as much dignity as the living. Many communities have burial
societies to insure that standards of caring and dignity are afforded to
all.
OFFICERS 1994
President
Nathan C. Sandler
Jacksonville has a Chevrah Kaddisha comprised of wonderful, com¬
passionate people. However, with the growth of the community, our
numbers are far less than needed. Additional people would allow us
to perform our tasks in the proper manner without overworking
anyone.
The ritual tasks include: 1) the Tahara... traditional washing and
shrouding of the body and placing it in the coffin, all accompanied by
appropriate prayers; 2) making arrangements for the Tahara; 3) being
a Shomer, one who watches the body until the funeral; 4) transporta¬
tion for the Shomer.

HEBREW HOME/WOLFSON HEALTH • AGING CENTER
This is the Fifth Commandment and the reward. The theme of
respect and responsibility for our forebears is central to the
organization of Jewish life.
It was this commandment which inspired the founding of
River Garden Hebrew Home in 1946. It was this commandment
which nourished the love and care that over the years has made
River Garden a nationally recognized model of excellence in its
field.
The success of River Garden is based on the tradition of
meeting 100 percent of local Jewish community needs and of
serving rich and poor alike with excellence. To be served, all
you need to do is call.
To enable River Garden to continue to fulfill its mission
of caring for all, regardless of ability to pay, the River Garden
Foundation became active in 1992. The Foundation trustees
encourage and facilitate major giving and endowment support of
the Home.
River Garden’s programs of short-term rehabilitative/
respite care, long-term residential care, Alzheimer’s and Adult
Day Care, serve over 180 people on any given day. These
programs are guided by a devoted, professional full-time staff of
over 200. This staff includes physicians and beauticians; nurses,
social workers, pharmacists, activities specialists; physical, occu¬
pational and speech therapists; dieticians, etc. In addition, scores
of dedicated volunteers help create a special caring community.
River garden is located on a 33 acre Mandarin campus.
By the end of 1994, these programs will be augmented by The
Coves, an independent retirement community.
River Garden is a community where people live, love
and care; a community of many lifestyles and of rich Jewish
content, and a community which every day strives to live up to
the mandate of the Fifth Commandment.

Something Wonderful Happens
■v At The Center Everyday
About the cover: Sefer Torah, personal-sized with wooden spindles and wood and ivory finíais. It is typical of central and eastern Europe
of the period. This Torah was brought to Moffit, Florida, (near Zolfo Springs) from Russia by Marcus Joseph Weinkle, 1908. In 1887,
when Marcus was called to serve in the Czar's army, the family announced that he had died of a high fever and actually buried him. After
dark, he crept out of the grave, picked up the bundle hidden for him, and smuggled himself out of the country. Marcus was so religious
that when he immigrated to Florida he brought his own Torah with him. He did not know where he would find a minyan. In Moffit he ran
a lumber mill and conducted services for the Jews in the area.
Loaned by Jacksonville Jewish Center.
The Weinkle Torah will be part of the permanent core exhibit at the MOSAIC Jewish Museum of Florida. Grand Opening will be in
March of 1995, 301 Washington Avenue, Miami Beach, Florida.
