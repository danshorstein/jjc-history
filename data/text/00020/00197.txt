♦All days of the Jewish year begin at sundown. Therefore, each holiday
really begins at sundown of the day preceding our symbol.
1 | ROSH HASHANAH (the Jewish New Year), a solemn two-day
A holiday when the Shofar (ram’s horn) sounds forth in the
^ synagogue to call the people to repentance and to rewaken re¬
sponsibility to the Lord.
TZOM GEDALIAH (the Fast of Gedaliah), indicated here by
my the Hebrew characters for “tzom." On this day, Jews mourn
for Gedaliah, a Jew whom the conquering Babylonians un¬
expectedly appointed Governor of Palestine. The Jews saw
in this a sign that their nation would rise again, but Gedaliah was
ruthlessly assassinated.
YOM KIPPUR (Day of Atonement), holiest of the Jewish
L 4e I year. This is a time of solemn self-evaluation—-a time for
confession, repentance, and asking pardon of the Lord. Tradi¬
tion decrees, too, that on Yom Kippur, Jews forgive and ask forgiveness
of their fellowmen. On Yom Kippur Eve there is a service of great and
haunting beauty, which never fails to deeply stir those who hear it. As
the doors of the Ark are opened, the congregation stands—and is at once
caught up in a reverent and expectant hush. The sacred Torah scrolls
are taken out of the Ark, and held before the congregation. Then the
white-robed cantor begins the ancient chant, Kol Nidre (all vows), his
voice soaring and descending to an old, old melody which has become
famous throughout the world for its lucid poignancy. On the following
morning, the congregation gathers again in the synagogue, where they
remain, throughout this austere fast day, in continuous prayer. On
Yom Kippur, as on other holidays, the prayers are read from the Machzor
(holiday prayer book) which we show as our symbol, above.
