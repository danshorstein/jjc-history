giving commemorates the harvest in the Holy Land. It is
• ^ therefore symbolized by the Esrog and Lulav (citrus and palm

everywhere build Sukkot (temporary shelters)—often hung with fruit and
other decorations— to remind them that the Israelites lived in open huts
during their desert wanderings, after the Exodus from Egypt.
SHEMINI ATZERET (Eighth Day of the Festival). Because the
Jews were, and in many places still are, a pastoral and agri¬
cultural people, a short prayer for rain is said daily during
a large part of the year. But on Shemini Atzeret, a holiday of the har¬
vest season, the synagogue service gives particular prominence to special
prayers which ask for rain to bring full crops in the coming year—hence
our rain-drop symbol.
ÍÉ SIMHAT TORAH (literally, “Rejoicing in the Law") —On
this final day of the holiday season, the annual reading of
the Torah (Five Books of Moses) is completed and begun
anew. The sacred scrolls of the Torah are carried in joyful procession
around the synagogue. Children love this holiday because they take part
in the procession, carrying colorful flags, and receive gifts of sweets.
