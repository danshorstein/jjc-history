Founded in 4917, the JFCS is the 2 man casework
agency of the Jewish Community. Organized as the
United Jewish Charities, by the merging of three in¬
dependent groups, Jacksonville became one of the first
Jewish communities its size, to have a special agency
to deal with family problems and problems of poverty.
In 1922 it assumed the name of Jewish Welfare Asso¬
ciation. It was incorporated as the Jewish Welfare
Society in 1929 when it was one of the founders of
the Community Chest. It was professionalized in 1946.
Its current director came in 1952. It assumed its
present name in 1962.
Its main service is counseling on an individual, fam¬
ily and group basis. It is a licensed child placing
agency providing adoption services, including work
with unmarried Mothers, and foster home care. It
provides short term financial assistance (that in some
cases extend for several years) to local residents. It
also provides transient services, day camp scholarships
and processes applications for interest free college
loans from the Jewish Childrens’ Services. Its services
to the Aged, who take up more than 40% of the clients,
includes local intake for the River Garden Hebrew
Home, service in their own home and Friendly Visit¬
ing. Its outreach programs include monthly meetings
with the Jewish patients at the State Hospital at
McClenny and weekly sessions with prisoners at Rai-
ford. A youth rap and encounter group was begun in
1971 and is flourishing. Its resettlement of New Amer¬
ican program, financed entirely by the Jewish Com¬
munity has been dormant for the past several years.
(Literally, as this was written, the phone rang request¬
ing help in locating work for the head of a recently
arrived family from France.) It is hoped that in the
coming year there may be the reuniting of families
who sponsored relatives living in Russia.
The number of families receiving services from the
agency continues to grow 282 in 1969, 308 in 1970 and
357 in 1971. While most situations required only coun¬
seling, in 1971 a total of $7983 was spent for 48 indi¬
viduals in 28 local families in direct grants and medical
and dental relief. Free medicines and medicines at
wholesale cost were supplied through the generosity
of a friend for those needing it. These financial needs
continued to rise in the first half of 1972 and although
the budget was increased by $500 we are faced with
a potential deficit of $2000 in this area.
A special word of thanks must be included to our
many dedicated volunteers who helped us provide
services for people. Norman Moss has for years pro¬
vided a branch office of the agency in his store for
our work with transients and who is now an enthusi¬
astic participant in our Raiford program. Walter Levy
has been giving us two days a week for the past two
years to provide transportation and deliver medication
to our homebound, to sort and deliver the greatly in¬
creased amount of donated clothing (almost $60,000
worth in 1971), to help locate work for our clients and
to help coordinate our Raiford program. Mrs. Rochelle
Geiger who increased her volunteer work to 3 days a
week in various programs of the agency.

A gleaming 17-story structure at 5846 Mt. Carmel
Terrace is eloquent testimony to the determination
and faith of the Jacksonville Jewish Community Coun¬
cil to the Senior Citizens of the Jacksonville Jewish
Community—as well as those of the non-Jewish Com¬
munity. It also testifies to the concern that this Gov¬
ernment has for its citizens in providing modern,
comfortable, safe low-cost housing for those who have
reached retirement age.
Mount Carmel, from its attractive lobby, through its
well-lighted corridors and into the well-planned apart¬
ments, represents the latest conveniences for the
Senior Citizen.
The first residents have already moved into their air-
conditioned, well-lighted, modern "hpartments and more
are moving in daily. Mount Carmel Gardens has a ca¬
pacity of 208 apartments and applications will be pro¬
cessed until a waiting list is established.
Programmng for the residents of Mount Carmel Gard¬
ens will be planned to fulfill the desires of all the
tenants. Many Jewish organizations have made plans
to hold their meetings in the spacious auditorium of
the apartment building.
Mount Carmel is truly a grand addition to the Jewish
Community of Jacksonville.
The offices of the Jewish Community Council are in
Mount Carmel Gardens. The Jewish Community
Council is the sponsoring agency for Mount Carmel
Gardens.

River Garden Auxiliary, originally Ladies Hebrew
Sheltering Aid Society, was founded fifty-one years
ago, and today is the largest Jewish women’s organiza¬

many years ago, women of vision and courage dreamt
of providing a home for the aged, giving them shelter,
loving care, companionship and medical care. Their
dream has become a reality and the Auxiliary today
continues to endeavor to provide these services and to
maintain a personal “Home” atmosphere despite the
fact that the Home has grown into an institution
providing care for over 150 residents.

It is with a great deal of pride that I write this
message to the members, friends and supporters of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Another year of growth, accomplishments and
challnges has been completed. Dedicated leaders,
members and staff, together have deepened and
extended the activities and programs of our congrega¬
tion.
In a generation whose primary concern is the
future of Judaism, it is gratifying and comforting to
know that we are part of a Synagogue-Center com¬
mitted to the enrichment of Jewish life, Jewish educa¬
tion and Jewish youth.
As we strive to insure the continued growth of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, it is good to know that
our friends, many of whose names appear within these
pages, have joined us in our endeavor.
May the cooperative efforts of all concerned grow
from strength to strength and Jewish lives be made
more meaningful and secure through the work of our
congregation.
On behalf of the entire staff of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center, I feel privileged to extend warm
greetings for a happy, healthy and prosperous New
Year.

It is for the sake of our children that we live
and exist, and for that reason one of the most im¬
portant goals of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
is to train our children and imbue them with the
zeal and fervor of the sages, prophets and philoso¬
phers of Israel.
There is a wonderful story of the Hasidic Rabbi
who returned from a strenuous day’s labor and
said to his wife: “I must sleep or I shall faint.
No matter who comes, do not disturb me.” In a
little while he appeared at the bedroom door with
an anxious face to modify his request. “I made a
mistake”, the Rabbi said, “If a child comes, awaken
me.
Our goal at the Center is to bring to our children
the teachings of the Torah by combining MID¬
RASH and MAASEH, study and practice.
To accomplish this goal an active and multi¬
faceted program of Jewish study and practice in¬
cluding EDUCATIONAL, RECREATIONAL, SO¬
CIAL and CULTURAL ACTIVITY is offered.
Nursery School and Kindergarten, Solomon
Schechter Day School, Religious and Sunday
School, Minyan Club, Youth Choir, Kadimah,
United Synagogue Youth, Day Camp, Camp Ra-
mah and ATID are the vehicles of growth and
maturity offered under the auspices of the Jack¬
sonville Jewish Center.
The resources, manpower and creative talents
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center are dedicated to
the proposition that the torch of our ancestors
must be passed to our offspring in a way which will
enable them to carry on in a better and grander
fashion for the glory of God, the Jewish people
and all mankind.

THE GOOD IS HARDER
.... but it’s worth more
The good is more, not less.
And we really know what it is.
For who does not want
-- to find joy in life
- to fulfill his talents
-- to love and to be loved
-- to be respected
-- to have a good family?
You have every right to want all of these -- and the powers to seek and enjoy them all.
What, then, interferes with your achieving what you want most?
Aren’t you reasonable . . . sensitive . . . responsible . . . hardworking . . . loving? Of
course you are.
Yet aren’t you . . . sometimes . . . also impulsive or inconsiderate or lax or harsh?
It is in the conflicts we all experience, being
-- reasonable, yet sometimes impulsive
-- sensitive, yet sometimes inconsiderate
-- responsible, yet sometimes lax
-- loving, yet sometimes harsh
that you have the opportunity to choose what will enlarge you or make you smaller;
what will build your life or take away from what you want most out of life.
In Your Family
Of course you want and need love, understanding and attention from your husband or
your wife.
Yet you diminish your own marriage when you cannot be reasonable and sensitive
enough to continue the effort to give what you also want most to receive . . .
Each recognizing the other as a person, not as a possession.
And we as parents know it is often easier to spoil a child than to educate him; or to
be harsh than to be loving.
You can be sure that, if all you ask of your child is obedience . . . and you do not have
the patience to honestly listen to him . . . you are undermining your own influence on
his growth.
Each of you, parent and child, must learn to respect the other’s individuality.
It may not always be easy to do this.
Yet isn’t it worth it?
LOUIS PORTNOY - WALTER PORTNOY

Hearts” — This slogan for the 1971-72 ORT Day
membership drive represents the ORT effort through¬
out Israel and the Diaspora to aid fellow Jews through
vocational rehabilitation. Although education for a
livelihood is the basis of the ORT program, other fact¬
ors such as the rise in anti-Semitism, alienation of our
Jewish youth from their traditions, and shifts in the
Jewish population, give further cause for ORT’s global
existence.
Briefly the ORT program consists of:
1. Building and/or maintenance of over 700 voca¬
tional installations, including schools in Switzerland
and Israel for teacher training.
r
2. Training more than 65,000 students annually.
3. Providing for the health, social, recreational and
religious (including Bar Mitzvahs) needs of the stu¬
dents.
4. Providing basic and apprenticeship courses as
well as those on highly technical levels.
5. Assisting underdeveloped nations in setting up
training centers.

near future of establishing a pilot school in a Jewish
poverty area in the United States. With the increasing
needs of Israel and the Jewish communities in France
and Italy especially, financing the ORT program is a
major concern of the local chapter. Many fund raising
events are held throughout the year, the largest being
our annual Fall Art Auction.

organization, but in ORT this is particularly true. For
every two persons joining ORT another child can be
accepted in an ORT school. Our meetings are usually

JACKSONVILLE CHAPTER 219 OF HADASSAH
Hadassah was founded in 1912 by Henrietta Szold
and this year has been ■celebrating sixty years of dedi¬
cation and progress.
From its beginning, Hadassah offered women in¬
volvement in a great adventure. Hundreds of thou¬
sands from every state in the United States, including
Alaska and from Puerto Rico have been magnetized
towards Hadassah since then. This is because every
year, during the past six decades, some program of
healing, research or education, some shared experience
needing compassion and courage which started earlier
as a dream, began coming true.
Perhaps the greatest achievement of all is the meld¬
ing in Hadassah’s work of two rich, still evolving tra¬
ditions. One, rooted far back over the centuries, comes
from moral and religious concepts, common needs,
joys, sufferings, longings and hopes of the Jewish peo¬
ple. The other springs from that fierce advocacy of
man’s rights of “life, liberty and the pursuit of happi¬
ness" which - no matter what the challenges of this
and other times - is still the essence of the American
search for realization of the still unfulfilled American
dream.
The year 1971-72 was one of the most successful
in Jacksonville Hadassah’s history. All quotas were
met and oversubscribed. Our plus Medical Research
goal was completed and the name of the Jacksonville
chapter will be permanently inscribed on a device to
be placed in the Hadassah Hospital at Ein Karem,
Jerusalem.
Our meetings were outstanding, ranging from a
wine tasting party for paid up members to a female
Israeli lawyer discussing human rights on Education
Day. I don’t think any of us will forget the warm at¬
mosphere of “An Evening with Monte Hall.”
Our Summer book reviews are still very popular
and entertaining. The Hadassah’s Bazaar, located at
411 Main Street has been refurbished and revitalized
and continues to be a source of profit and satisfaction.
In the coming year we are very happy to welcome
a young woman’s group in addition to our day and
evening group.
Hadassah meets the second Tuesday of each month
from October through May. One of our groups is just
right for you. Won’t you join us?

The Jewish Community Council is the social plan¬
ning, community relations and fund raising body serv¬
ing the entire Jewish community of Jacksonville and
its environs. Its representative Board of Directors is
composed of delegates of all the major Jewish reli¬
gious, philanthropic and service organizations in the
area, plus members-at-large.
The annual community-wide United Jewish Wel¬
fare Fund Campaign provides funds for the support of
local, national and overseas needs, including the
United Jewish Appeal, and the Israel Emergency
Fund.
Local beneficiaries receiving funds are River
Garden Home for the Aged; the Jewish Family &
Children’s Services. The Council is the sponsoring
agency for the Mount Carmel Gardens.

vice is maintained by the Jewish Community Council
to enable all Jewish organizations in the community
to clear dates for all their events.
The Council publishes the COMMENTATOR, a
newspaper published monthly. It is the official paper
of the Council and its member organizations.
The Council conducts community programs in cele¬
bration of special events and, through its Youth Divi¬
sion, promotes various programs for the Jewish youth
of all ages. The Council also sponsors two softball
teams in the City Recreation League.
The Council is a member of the Council of Jewish
Federations and Welfare Funds, the National Jewish
Comumunity Relations Advisory Council and the
Community Planning Council of the Jacksonville area.

The Center Day Camp offers a diversified sum¬
mer program for all Jewish youth, ages three through
fourteen years. The Camp has the finest facilities in¬
cluding many acres of wooded area for primitive camp¬
ing and nature trails, an Olympic swimming pool, a
well equipped playground, softball diamond, basketball
court and areas for all active sports.
The 1972 season provided to be most successful,
with campers enjoying many new program ideas, elect¬
ing those in which they were most interested each after¬
noon. Softball, an all-time favorite for both boys and
girls proved to be most popular. The Center “Stars”
competing in the newly formed Southside Day Camp
League won the first place trophy. Other popular
activities were cheerleading, archery, instructional and
free swimming, waterskiing, music, arts and crafts.
Camp-outs, trips to the Children's Museum and
Marine Science Center at Mayport, roller skating, ice
skating, as well as the traditional end-of-Camp Color
War, helped to give each camper an enriched and re¬
warding summer.
Highlighting the season was the visit of Suesakee
Yamakawa and his group of Japanese visitors to Jack--
sonville as part of the “Experiment in International
Living Program.” We were fortunate to have them
select our Camp for visitation. Their many talents in¬
cluded the teaching of Japanese songs, the art of Ori-
jami (folding of tissue paper) and exhibitions of diving
and magic.
Blanch Slott
Camp Director
As a Synagogue sponsored Camp, our campers
gained Judaic experiences in an informal atmosphere
by participating in morning services, chanting the Birhat
Hamotzi and Birhat Hamazon at meals. Shabbat is
observed by lighting the Shabbat candles, chanting the
Kiddush and having a special creative program. Each
summer campers solemnly observe Tisha B’Av with a
processional of the flaming torch and Torah at our
own replica of the Kotel ha Maravi (Western Wall).
Our goal for the future is to continue to improve1
our facilities and program for the best in Day Camp¬
ing for all Jewish youth.

It was with profound humility that Moses, on the peaks
of Sinai, uttered the plea “Lord, see, You ask me to raise
this nation; who will you send with me?” Seemingly, Moses
recognizes that he cannot lead without a people, that his
noblest intentions will be hopelessly frustrated without a
congregation. Similarly, the life of a rabbi, his work and
concern, is inevitably linked with the quality, depth and
character of the community he serves.
The nature of this reciprocal relationship is best de¬
picted by a story which is told of the Hasidic master, Hayyim
of Zaus, who reputedly was able to turn the hearts of his
disciples to God. This is the tale.
A man lost his way in a great forest. After a while an¬
other lost his way and chanced on the first. “How can we
find our way,” he asked. “I don’t know,” said the first, “but
I can point the way that leads deeper into the thicket. From
there let us join and find the way together.”
“So my congregation,” the rabbi said, “let us look for
the way together.” We stand on the threshold of a New
Year charged with the challenge of renewal. We face num¬
erous difficulties but each augurs possibilities for
growth. Each problem is an opportunity. From my own
perspective I sense a current of excitement within our
membership; a new determination to put the past
behind us and to turn with renewed zeal to the tasks
which lie ahead - and these tasks are manifold.
RABBI DAVID GAFFNEY

Cub Scouting, as an introduction to the Boy Scouts
of America continues to be one of the outstanding youth
programs offered at the Center. Boys between the ages
of 8 and 10 years find many new and challenging ex¬
periences in Cubbing.
The Cubs in each Den become a close unit as they
work together to develop new hobbies, take field trips,
explore nature and work on craft projects and skits.
The annual Rocket and Pinewood Derbies give the boys
the opportunity to design and make their own rockets
and cars and to enter them in competitive racing.
In honor of Boy Scout Week, the Cubs participate
in the late Friday Sabbath Service and the annual Blue
and Gold Banquet. To close a most successful year, all
members of Pack 14 and their families gathered at the
Center Southside Site for an evening of swimming,
sports, games and cooking over an open fire.
The Cub program would not be possible without
the interest and dedication of many. Our thanks to Cub-
master Sidney Engelhardt for his many years of service,
to Mrs. Fred Kupperman, head Den Mother, and to
the Den Mothers who served with her. Thanks also to
A1 Wolfson, Webelos Leader; Stanley Ruby, Committee
Chairman; and the members of'his committee: William
Newman, Murray Husney, Carl Miller and Ted Freed¬
man, Institutional Representative.
Individual Dens meet weekly at the homes of Den
Mothers. Monthly Pack Meetings are held on the last
Thursday of each month at the Center. New members
are always welcome.

Message from the President
At this time of the New Year, we traditionally
give thanks as we review the past and look forward
with prayers to the future.
We are very thankful for our good fortune in
having Rabbi David Gaffney as our spiritual leader.
His particular strengths in working with teenagers and
college students have become evident in the short time
that he has been with us. His previous successes and
his extremely intensive Jewish scholastic background
give all of us a great feeling of confidence for the fu¬
ture.
Through the devotion and dedication of our con¬
gregation, we are confident that we can build the en¬
tire new synagogue center complex at one time. Our
plans call for the construction of the synagogue audi¬
torium-social hall, 16 classrooms, multi-purpose audi¬
torium, administrative facilities, library-resource
center, youth center and the necessary appurtenances
such as kitchen, lobbies, etc. We believe that ground¬
breaking will take place during the coming spring and
we will be in our new home before the end of 1974.
The privilege of taking part in such a magnificent and
beautiful “Mitzvah” normally comes along once in a
lifetime.
Our day school has another record breaking en¬
rollment in both the nursery-kindergarten division as
well as the 6 grades of the elementary division. With
the addition to our staff of Rabbi Gaffney, a most
gifted teacher, and several new outstanding Hebrew

For the past two years Rabbi Dov Peretz Elkins
has been the spiritual leader of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. The following is his last message to the congre¬
gation in the Bulletin of July 15, 1972:
Since this will be the last column I shall write for the Center
Bulletin, I feel compelled to render to the congregation a “State of the
Union” report, reviewing the accomplishments of the past two years.
Let me list some of the developments, changes, and innovations which
I consider significant:
1) In the realm of education, the most significant development was the
expansion of our Solomon Schechter Day School from three grades
to six grades, and from approximately 100 students to over 200 stu¬
dents. Other less dramatic but equally significant improvements in
the educational system of the Center were begun, and will, hopefully,
come to full fruition during the next few years.
2) In the realm of our religious program, the following things came into
being: Shortening and up-dating of Shabbat morning services (Torah
reading study guides, original prayers, less repetition); extensive use
of experimental and innovative liturgy both during the High Holy
Days and on Sabbath evenings during the year; Monday morning
Father-Child Minyan Breakfasts and Shabbat afternoon Family Sha-
losh Seudot programs for Day School classes; establishment of the
Academy of Judaism, a 16 week course for prospective converts to
Judaism; camp-style Shabbat services at our Southside Site during
summer months; Simchat Torah evening services in the Setzer
Auditorium with record crowds dancing and singing in wild religious
ecstasy.
3) In the field of youth work, these things happened: forming of a
local chapter of Leaders Training Fellowship, with monthly meetings
at my home on Shabbat afternoons; regular and on-going communica¬
tion with college students through letters, ATID newspapers and
campus visits during which I invited Center students to dinner and
conversation; on-going contact with youngsters in all our youth groups
and school departments, such as weekly lunches with Day School
classes.
4) In adult education, we initiated classes and discussion groups on the
Southside (Plantation Apts.) for young parents of Day School chil¬
dren, which were well attended, and well received; we organized a
series of public lectures in cooperation with all the synagogues of
Jacksonville, a first in total community planning.
5) In community relations we were able to accomplish the following: a
bright new face and format for the Center Bulletin; closer relations
with the Jewish Community Council and other Jewish organizations
and institutions; a spate of activity for the welfare of Soviet Jewry
(marches, lectures, petitions, etc.); countless appearances before civic,
interfaith, and educational organizations, in person and through mass
media such as television and newspaper.
All in all it is a record that gives me great satisfaction and pleasure.
It has been a privilege and an honor to serve God in the capacity of
Rabbi of this synagogue, and I am grateful to do so. I pray that the
things I have tried to do will continue to reach fulfillment, and that
each of you will continue to become better and more devoted Jews. I
pray that God will bless you with good health and long life. Shalom,
lehitraot!
