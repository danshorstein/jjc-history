The program of the Jewish Welfare Society can be broken down into six overlapping categories: (1) Family consultation and
case work; (2) foster home and adoption placements; (3) Application processing for the River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged
and follow-up case work with the residents; (4) Resettlement, naturalization and migration assistance; (5) Transients; (6) Prison
program.
The family consultation and casework services continue to be the heart of the agency’s program as the great majority of cases
come for counselling, planning and other services that do not involve the direct outlay of funds. This past year there was a sharp in¬
crease in service to older people.
The agency placed three children for permanent adoption and one child in a foster home during 1957. The number of children
available for adoption puts the Jacksonville Jewish applicants in one of the most favorable positions in the United States.
The addition of a new worker, Mrs. Marcia G. Arnold, to the staff has made it possible for us to intensify casework with the resi¬
dents of the River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged and to broaden some of our other services.
There was considerable activity in the New American migration programs. The new Americans were among the first to feel the
effects of the recession and re-employment has been a difficult area to cover.
The number of transients helped during this past winter was markedly lower than other years, but with the return of normal weather,
this picture will change.
The agency, under the leadership of Rabbi Israel L. Kaplan, continued its work with the prisoners at Raiford.
The Jewish Welfare Society is the Family and Casework Agency of the Jewish community. It is a Community Chest — United
Fund agency with its local program mainly supported by the Chest. The Jewish Community Council provides two-thirds of the salary
of a social worker and assumes the entire cost of the resettlement of new Americans.
