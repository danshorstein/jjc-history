Know Your Center
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is
“The Conservative Synagogue in Jacksonville”
Scope of Activities
Introduction
For over half a century the Center has provided a
modern program in Jewish experience, ever increasing
its services and facilities. Our Year Book reviews the
programs and activities we make available for religious
inspiration, education and Jewish living. These are only
too well known to our loyal membership. For the benefit
of new members and newcomers to the city, we set forth
our program in brief. We also call attention that the
name “Center5 is to be distinguished from organizations
by similar names in other communities that are primarily
recreational Centers. We are a “Synagogue-Center55.
Religious Services
Daily: Mornings, 7:30 A.M. (8:30 on Sundays)
Evenings, at sunset (including the eves of Sab¬
baths and Festivals)
Sabbath and Festivals:
Evenings, 8 o'clock
Mornings: Early service, 8:45; Reading of
Torah, 9:30; Musaf, 10 to 11:15 A.M.
High Holidays: Members and their families, service¬
men and out-of-town guests are accommodated in our
air-conditioned Auditorium which seats 1400. (We do
not “sell seats55 as the expression goes in some com¬
munities. )
Personal Observances
Our religious leadership supplements public worship
with services on personal occasions at the Synagogue and
home, such as: Circumcision, naming of girls, Pidvon
Ha-ben, Bar Mitzvah, Bas Mitzvah, Confirmations, Mar¬
riages, Conversion to Judaism, Bereavement.
Education
Daily Kindergarten: Monday to Friday, 9 to 12 noon.
Week-day Hebrew School: Classes for boys and girls
meet on alternate afternoons of the week in three
divisions:
1. Primary Grades: “Play way to Hebrew ", for pupils
6 to 8 years of age.
2. Elementary Grades.
3. Advanced Grades.
Sunday Religious School:
Sunday mornings, 10 to 12 noon; Pre-Kindergarten
through 10th Grade, Confirmation Class.
Junior Congregation:
Sabbath and Festival mornings, services held in separate
Chapels according to age.
Youth Services:
Sabbath and Festival mornings at 10 o'clock for senior
youth.
Minyan Club:
Sunday mornings at 8:45 for boys of Bar Mitzvah
age, followed by Breakfast sponsored by the Center
Men’s Club.
Adult Courses:
Lecture series are presented as available. Institutes on
specific subjects are arranged in convenient units of
study. Special classes and discussion groups are ar¬
ranged as called for.
Extension Services
Affiliated Organizations:
Sisterhood, Men's Club, Couples Club, Amity Club,
Mr. and Mrs. Club, Young People’s League. Each of
these organizations is described in appropriate sections
of the Center Year Book.
Youth Activities:
United Synagogue Youth, organized in two chapters,
Center Youth League and Charles Palot Club; Major’s
Pre-Teens, Tween-Agers—for high school, Junior high
school and grade school youth.
Scouting: Boy Scouts and Cub Pack, Girl Scouts and
Brownies.
Athletics: Programs for youth and adults, men and
women, conducted at our Gym and outdoors, also at
various communitv facilities.
J
Social Events: Scheduled by the various organizations on
special occasions or in connection with regular worship
and meetings.
Community Service: The Center’s facilities are available
not only for the celebration of personal events, such as
weddings, and Bar or Bas Mitzvahs, but are also used
by youth and adult organizations, civic groups and inter¬
faith groups.
Cemeteries: Memorial Parks located at Evergreen Avenue
and Winona Drive and at 40th Street, East of Main St.
are owned and maintained by the congregation.
Center Bulletin: The “Center Bulletin" appears weekly
during the active season and is mailed to our member¬
ship and to interested persons whose names and ad¬
dresses are submitted to the office.
Center Year Book: For a fuller description of the Center
program and activities, the Center Year Book published
annually on the eve of Rosh Hashanah offers a bird's
eye view of events for the preceding 12 month period,
vital statistics of the membership, Hebrew Religious
Calendar for the coming year, directory of community
organizations and other departments.

Standing Committees
The programs and activities of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center are under the direction of the standing Committees
of the Center listed here. In many instances they are
composed of a number of sub-committees. Respective
chairmen welcome inquiries about the particular depart¬
ment in their charge. They also invite volunteers for
personnel on their Committees. Similar offers of infor¬
mation and affiliation are made by the Presidents of the
affiliated organizations and the Chairmen in charge of the
projects supervised by the Committees that function for the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.
ADULT EDUCATION
Morty Rosenkranz, Chairman; Fosters interest and presents
programs of study and discussion for the adults in the
Congregation.
ATHLETICS
Lawrence Richter, Chairman; Initiates and supervises ath¬
letic programs for groups of all ages in cooperation with
Education and Youth Committees.
BUDGET
Joe Becker, Chairman; Makes an annual audit of finances,
analyzes income and expenditures and recommends the
balance between resources and appropriations for the
coming year.
BUILDING FUND
Philip Selber, Chairman; Responsible for planning and
construction of our expanding Synagogue-Center and
secures the funds to meet the outlay incurred.
CEMETERY
Max Rubin, Chairman; Supervises Memorial Parks owned
and maintained by the congregation and assists with
funeral arrangements.
EDUCATION
Dr. Nathan J. Schneider, Chairman; Sets policies for the
program of Religious education in the Hebrew School,
Sunday Religious School, Kindergarten, Junior Con¬
gregation, Pre-Junior Congregation, Minyan Club and
Summer Day Camp.
FINANCE
Hyman Selber, Chairman; Coordinates the sources of in¬
come for the support of the congregation and super¬
vises all expenditures.
FRIENDS OF THE SEMINARY
Jack Becker, and Philip Bork, Co-Chairmen; Enrolls con¬
tributing members for the support of the Jewish Theo¬
logical Seminary of Amercia and branch organizations
of Conservative Judaism in America.
HOUSE
Manuel Goldstein, Chairman; Formulates house rules, fees
and schedules, supervises personnel for maintenance of
all buildings and equipment.
MEMBERSHIP
Irwin Gendzier, Chairman; By disseminating information
and by personal calls on newcomers to the city and
non-affiliated residents.
RELIGIOUS
Nathan Freidlin, Chairman; Reviews practices of Ritual
and Worship, arranges for daily and Sabbath and Festi¬
val services, supervises the ushering and assists in all
religious programming.
YEAR BOOK
Hyman Selber, Chairman; Solicits advertising, gathers ma¬
terials and edits Center Year Book.
YOUTH
Robert Wolf, Chairman; Coordinates programming of the
Center Youth League, Charles Palot Club, Boy Scouts,
Cub Pack, Girl Scouts, Brownies, Tween-Agers, Major's
Pre-teens and supervises use of our facilities by our
youth organizations.

Rabbi’s Message
The Center Year Book serves many purposes. Greeting
the Jewish Religious New Year is one of them. Through
its columns we exchange good wishes and call upon the
Almighty for His blessings upon our members, friends and
dear ones. It achieves even more.
Our Year Book helps resolve some imponderable ques¬
tions that are bound to invade our consciousness as we face
a new year:
J
What sort of year is before us?
j
Shall it be a year like any other year?
J é J
What is really new about a new year?
Hold several editions of the Center Year Book side by
side and you will be impressed by the profound meaning
that a year spells out in one’s life.
The casual observer will thumb through the pages of
the congregation’s annual record and notice a constant
pattern. He will see striking similarities. The same de¬
partments, the same activities, the same features. To this
casual observer the page of Bar Mitzvah boys and Bas
Mitzvah girls in this year's book is exactly like its counter¬
part in last year's. Photographs of Religious School classes
or of young people at an activity are exact replicas of
those he had seen before.
Fortunately, we are not all “casual observers’'. Those of
us who are involved in the Center's activities and work
intimately with the individuals participating in them, know
the difference. The sameness vanishes upon closer in¬
spection; personal concern and interest bring out finer
details.
Surely the photographs are not “all the same" to parents
of the hundreds of children who appear on the pages of
this Year Book. This holds true also of children whose
parents are highlighted because of their significant roles
in the dedicated service to the synagogue.
Superficially, one Year Book may look like another. In
its essential character it assumes living personality and a
vitalitv of its own.
j
No, one year in our lives is not just like another! Should
anyone be inclined to succumb to the monotony of events
and happenings, the Center Year Book will help him re¬
gain perspective. In fact, the least amount of considered
thought would lift us from being lulled into humdrum and
even-toned regard of life.
On the approach of this past year I took occasion to
reflect in one of my messages on personal milestones that
the year marked. I spoke of the quarter century of matri¬
mony and the silver anniversary of my ordination in the
rabbinate. Half of that span of years my family and I
shared with our beloved congregation. The Confirmation
Class this past Shavuot was the 13th that I had inducted
into its status within the congregation. I called it my “Bar
DR. SANDERS A. TOFIELD
Mitzvah" class. In the space of the past 12 months my
family and I had both joy and sorrow. This year too was
not just another year.
While it is not possible humanly to penetrate into the
future and know what sort of year is before us, there is
no question that no year is just like any other year.
Basically this is the meaning of observing the beginning
of a new year with religious worship.
With appropriate humility for what remains to be ac¬
complished, for tasks that fall within the realm of our
responsibility as moral individuals and as members of a
congregation, that we have not fulfilled to our utmost
ability. With a sense of profound gratitude that the Di¬
vine Power guiding our destinies provided us with many
satisfactions and moments of exhilaration. With con¬
sequent hopeful spirit and implicit reliance on God’s love
for his children and creatures. With intercession that the
Divine will direct the world into happier spheres of human
relations. With all these sentiments combined into a
genuine outpouring of soulful worship, the ushering in of a
New Year should prove an exhilarating experience.
The brief “L’Shanah Tovah" prayerful wish we express
to one another on Rosh Hashanah embraces some such
reflections conveyed in this message. I affirm here some¬
thing that should be obvious but often overlooked. Short
statements of religious content and intent may become per¬
functory social amenities. Let us make a conscious effort
to avoid this pitfall. Let us reflect on the God-given bless¬
ing of life and the periods of time in which it is realized.
Let us hold sacred the year ahead and step into it with
spiritual dedication.
Pray the Almighty, it be a year of blessing, happiness,
well-being, peace.

President’s Message
When I look back over the period of almost two years
that I have had the honor of holding the distinguished
office of President of our Congregation, I see a period of
time that has been most enriched by heartwarming occur¬
rences and gratifying experiences that can be had in no
other way than by occupying high office in a synagogue.
Every Sabbath service in the synagogue is an en¬
lightening experience in Jewish life. There is the naming
of a baby, there is a Bar Mitzvah or Bas Mitzah, there is
the presence of a prospective bride and groom and, yes,
there are those who are present to recite the Kaddish in
memory of the departed. The full gamut of life appears
before us at the synagogue. There are the children in the
Junior Congregation service on the Sabbath and there
is the soul stirring sight of their numbers as they come into
the synagogue in a body on the Saturdays that a Bar or
Bas Mitzvah is being celebrated. And there is the look of
surprise that registers on the faces of the people who see
this sight for the first time as if to ask, is it possible that
so many children are interested in attending Sabbath
services.
For myself, there is the satisfaction in knowing that I
have had the opportunity to play some part in making
these things possible. I would make no attempt to lead
vou to believe that the office of President of such a
j
congregation as ours is all sweetness and light, all honorary
and formal. There are problems that are worrisome and
there are tasks that are time-consuming. But all of this
is far outweighed by the deep satisfaction that comes
from performing a community service from which such
tangible results are apparent.
Our congregation has continued on the path of
progress which has marked its existence since the begin¬
ning. The past year we have completed the construction
of our School Building which has relieved a situation of
overcrowding and inconvenience. At the same time the
work of remodeling the Synagogue Building was finished.
We are now in the process of acquiring property which,
while it will not entirely remedy our automobile parking
problem, will help considerably to alleviate it.
We have made necessary increases in our staff so that
we can offer a most complete program to our membership
and their families and so that we can have a more efficient
operation of the Center, its programs and its physical
facilities.
All of these things have been accomplished because
there is a spirit of loyalty on the part of the membership
BURTON I. GROSSMAN
of our congregation. No few men, no set of officers, no
spiritual leader could have made these things come to pass
without the support of our congregation. I believe that
we are fortunate in that our membership senses its obliga¬
tion to a tradition and a faith that has inspired Jewish
people since the beginning of time. I know that the
loyalty, the sense of responsibility and the desire to provide
our families with facilities of which we can all be proud,
and a program which is most constructive, will enable us
to go forward during the coming year with the same
enthusiastic support which has permitted the Jacksonville
Jewish Center to develop into the outstanding institution
it is today.
On behalf of all of the officers and the membership
of the Board of Directors and the Board of Trustees I
extend to the entire membership of the Center and to all
our friends sincere wishes for a Happy and a Good
New Year.
uLe Shanah Tovah Tikatevu"

Ilie Center Men’s Club
The Center Men’s Club is looking forward to its 15th year of existence, the 1959-60
season, with renewed dedication. All these years the Men’s Club has bent its energies towards
being of service to the Jacksonville Jewish Center in its manifold programs. In this primary
function the Men’s Club expects to match and exceed the record of the year just past in
the coming year.
Looking over the record of last year, selecting the highlights as samplings of what thz
Men’s Club does, we find an interesting variety of activities. At the very beginning of the
season we cooperated with the Synagogue and its Religious School departments by offering
souvenir flags at Simhat Torah Hakafot services to the children. On October 19 we hold
our Paid-up Member Dinner when Milton Felder received the Sam Bryan Award for
outstanding service to the organization. On November 20 we participated in the annual joint
meeting with the Temple Brotherhood. Our annual Blood Bank Drive was held in February.
In the field of adult Jewish education in which the Center Men’s Club has cooperated
with the Center’s affiliated organizations over the years, this past year we helped sponsor
the series of classes and discussions conducted by Rabbi Tofield. It consisted of two courses:
“Sabbath Institute for Men” and later “How to Conduct the Seder” Institute.
We again sponsored a Religious School picnic at the close of the school year. On May 24
at the closing Assembly of the Religious School we presented a series of awards. Recipients
appear in the photograph on this page, together with other honorees. Among our standing
projects is the sponsorship of the breakfast for the Minyan Club Sunday mornings. Its
activities are portrayed in other parts of this book.
The new administration which includes officers, board of directors and executive com¬
mittee composed of men who have been outstanding in their zeal for the organization is
poised to initiate many new activities while continuing with the traditional programs. We are
sponsoring a Bowling League already in progress. At monthly dinner meetings we will again
serve delicious meals and present on the program distinguished speakers on religion, sports
and politics. Dinner meetings are held the first Thursday of the month.
All men interested in affiliation may contact one of the officers or come to visit us
at regularly scheduled dinner meetings.

Religious Education and
Youth Activities Section
The 36 pages that follow immediately in this Year Book are devoted
to Religious Education and Youth Activities, including Athletics, also
closely associated with the work of these two departments. In addition, the
pages further on in the book devoted to general activities also portray
events and programs that properly belong in this section.
Religious Education
The Religious School system at the Jacksonville Jewish Center keeps
pace with the most advanced thinking in the area of religious education
for congregational schools that is advocated and developed by the Joint
Commission on Religious Education sponsored by the United Synagogue
of America and the Rabbinical Assembly of America. The new Classroom
Building completed in the past year and curricular improvements intro¬
duced hold promise for a season of educational achievement that will bring
about inspired Jewish living. While we present an imposing record mostly
by means of photographs, the picture remains only general. Members
of the congregation and parents especially are urged to take active part in
the programs of the Religious School. They will thus gain a genuine
appreciation of the enormous effort that our Religious Education depart¬
ments require and receive.
youth Activities
With the appointment of an Activities Director this department looks
forward to a season of even greater accomplishment than in the past.
Mr. Fischer came to us this past summer and directed the Summer Day
Camp. That project showed substantial progress over the summer. As
the coming season unfolds, the various youth projects sponsored by the
congregation will find their programs intensified and amplified. What we
said above about the portrayal of the work of the Religious Education
departments in this Year Book is equally relevant to the Youth Activities.

Girl Scouts and Brownies
The Center Sisterhood sponsored two Brownie Troops this year. Brownie Troop 346,
consisting of eleven girls, 7 to 9 yrs. of age, was organized in Arlington. The leader was Mrs.
Joe Fraden, assisted by Mrs. Kenneth Reich.
During the year they had several cook-outs, they made a trip to the zoo, made bean
bags at Singer Sewing Center, went to Popeye Playhouse, bowled, painted ceramics and
made flower vases for the residents of the River Garden Home for the Aged. They presented
an Israeli dance for Thinking Day and took part in Friday night services at the Center.
The year was concluded with a closing luncheon for Brownies and their mothers.
Brownie Troop 96 consisted of Southside girls. Their leader was Mrs. Sidney Stillman,
assisted by Girl Scout Barbara Ann Stillman. Ten girls were in the troop and eight “flew” up
to become Girl Scouts next year.
Troop activities included cook-outs, visiting the Little Theater, a Hanukah party and
taking part in Friday Night Services. The girls also presented a Closing Tea at Mrs. Still¬
man’s home with a Mother’s Day theme.
Girl Scouting is a movement created for the improvement of girls which enables them
to reach womanhood with maturity. It teaches them to cope with world problems through
their fellowship with other girls under the capable leadership of the mothers who serve
unselfishly for the benefit of youth.

Recognition of the “Home” as a haven for the aged and infirm has been widely accepted by the Jewish communities of Florida.
Thirteen years of devoted services to over 200 residents have demonstrated the worth and need of a Jewish Home for the Aged in the
State of Florida.
The story of the growth of the Home could be told in two different ways. The first is its physical growth from a wooden frame
residence housing eleven residents to two modern brick buildings providing all the facilities, including air-conditioning, for 72 residents
who enjoy comfortable and gracious living. The emphasis upon individualizing services has resulted in planning each building or wing
to meet the special needs of the individuals in that section. Whether incidental personal services or intensive nursing care is required, each
area is geared to encourage the individual to live to the maximum of his capacity.
The other aspect of development is the changing concept of services in a Home for the Aged. The Moshav Zkenim of yesteryear
provided food, lodging and little else. Today we recognize the needs of the aged in the “Home” are the same as those in the community.
Added to the basic services of shelter and care is an enrichment program to round out the patterns of daily living. Whether it is the
weekly Bingo game, a social affair, Chapel services, Bible study, Hobby Shop or publishing their own periodical, the end result is a warm
and rich environment which enables the older person to continue living an active and interesting life.
Life at River Garden continues to be dynamic and meaningful because the Home has become a miniature community with all the
resources that exist in a community: This enables the residents to continue in sharing the rewards as well as the responsibilities for com¬
munal living.

The year 1958, was a busy and important one in the 41 year history of the Jewish Welfare Society. The first six months of 1959
give promise of an even busier one.
For the first time in eight years, and after searching unsuccessfully for more than a year for a graduate school trained worker, we
had, for the entire year, a case worker as well as a director on the staff. This made possible the resumption of full case work service for the
applicants and residents of the River Garden Home, the joint undertaking of a limited but fairly impressive rehabilitation program, and
fuller service for the increasing number of requests from the local Jewish community. The case worker left us but in June we were able to
hire Mrs. Dawson Oppenheimer, a graduate school trained worker, on a part-time basis. We have promise of hiring a full time worker
in June, 1960.
Even though it meant deficit financing, we were able to meet the more numerous requests for financial assistance during the slight
recession of business adjustment in the early part of 1958. This helped motivate a careful re-examination of our policies, especially on
financial assistance, and our assumption of responsibility for children who are not suitable for adoption as well as those who are. In 1958,
we continued our enviable position of having infants to place for adoption and placed three. In the first six months of 1959, we had none
to place. In 1958, we had one child in our custody and supervised another in foster care. In the first six months of 1959, we had two
children in our custody and supervised a third.
Administratively, we changed from a large “membership board”, plus an executive committee, to an executive board, with active
committees. This appears to be much more effective.
In the first six months of 1959, there was a notable increase in the number of marital counselling cases and serious parent-child prob¬
lems—especially with teenagers, that came to our attention. Some of the problems were of long duration and it was only after much
damage had been done, that help was sought. May we take this opportunity to encourage you, the members of the community, when
faced with a complicated social or psychological problem, to make early use of our service.
Our best wishes for a happy and prosperous New Year.

The Jacksonville Chapter of Hadassah is composed of approximately 800 Jewish women who meet the second Tuesday of each month
to serve together in the ranks of the American Zionist movement. Hadassah’s members join in efforts to affect the national Hadassah
program of health, vocational and education services to Israel and the dedication of our local women to active participation in the life and
expression of American community affairs.
With more than 310,000 members in 1250 chapters and units in the United States and Puerto Rico, Hadassah today is the world’s
largest Zionist organization. While its pioneering efforts in medicine and social welfare have permeated every facet of Israeli life, it has
conducted significant activities on the American scene to foster creative and intelligent living through education programs stimulating
understanding of the Jewish cultural heritage. At the same time, Hadassah participates in efforts to safeguard democracy and support of
the United Nations.
Since its founding, Hadassah has raised more than $115,000,000.00 in behalf of the Jews of Palestine and Israel. This is a tremendous
contribution—one that was made possible only through the selfless devotion of Hadassah’s dedicated members. Together with Z.O.A.,
Hadassah supports the Jewish National Fund for the rehabilitation of the land of Israel and Zionist youth work through Young
Judaean Clubs.
Outstanding events of the coming year will be the opening luncheon to be held at the Center on September 8, the Membership Tea
on November 10 and a Tourish luncheon which will be the featured attraction of the month of January. The month of February will high¬
light Hadassah Hospital supplies, while March and April will be devoted to programs honoring contributors and donors to Hadassah’s
projects. The closing meeting will be held May 12th.

Young Judaea
Young Judaea concluded a successful year of activity with two Junior clubs and two
Senior clubs; Judaeanettes, Ruthettes, Zionettes and the Estherettes, under the direction of
the Youth Commission with Mrs. Norman Moss as Chairman and Mrs. Irving Brukner
as City Director.
City-wide events included the Annual Sabbath Service held at the Center during
December 1958 at which time the Presidents of each club participated and the parents acted
as hostesses. In March 1959 the Annual Israeli Fair for the benefit of the Scouts in Israel
was held on the grounds of the River Garden Home for the Aged. A talent show was
presented in addition to the many booths set up for display and for the sale of many
articles. The third annual event was the closing affair, the Bikkurim Festival and Banquet
held at the Center. Awards and honors were given to various members. Tel Yehudah
scholarships were given to Judy Bossen, Marilyn Halpern, Elaine Brukner and Byron Kolitz.
An outstanding event of the year was the Zionettes’ performance of Israel songs and
dances at the Stephen Foster Memorial Festival at White Springs in May. The Zionettes
also participated in Israeli dances at the Flannah Senesch Evening Group of Hadassah in
January and the Estherettes presented a medly of Israeli songs. The Winter Conclave was
held in Daytona Beach. Jacksonville Senior Judaea was host to Eddie Berman, National
President of Young Judaea, at a large rally in the Center Auditorium.
The newest group to join Young Judaea is the Ruthette Club for 11 yr. old girls and
is directed by Mrs. M. Goldstein. Nancy Moss served as Sr. Judaean leader with this group.
The Judaeanette Club for boys and girls ages 10, 11 and 12 met under the direction
of Mrs. Brukner and Mrs. Mitchell Schmer with Elaine Bruckner as Youth leader. Each of
the groups had a full year of activities and social events.
During the summer Camp Judaea was held at Camp Blue Star, Hendersonville, N. Car.
and was attended by Kay Kluglose, Judy Wilensky, Evelyn Goldstein and Phyllis Chamison.
Mrs. Irving Brukner retired as City Director of Young Judaea at the Bikkurim Festival
and Miss Sybil Horovitz was appointed new City Director. Mrs. Norman Moss, Chairman
of the Youth Commission, also retired and will be replaced by Mrs. Charles Kluglose.

Hemisphere.
In seven years 90 chapters have been established with a membership of 55,000. The money secured through memberships and contri¬

The Women’s Committee notes the development of Brandéis with pride. The women throughout the country have made incalculable
contributions by fostering good will for Brandéis in their respective communities. The university has expanded its student body, gathered
a distinguished faculty and realized its building program. With the establishment of Brandéis, Jews in America for the first time have been
enabled to serve as the host to students of other religious affiliations, as in the past colleges and universities sponsored by other faiths had

The Jewish Religious Calendar
GENERAL EXPLANATIONS
The Jewish Religious Calendar is the result of many
stages of development. In ancient times the unit of time
was the month, determined by the phases of the moon. The
Hebrew Calendar was thus lunar. However, since religious
festivals are observed annually at given seasons the calendar
also became solar. The two systems of measuring time were
harmonized by means of leap years in the following man¬
ner. The lunar month consists of 29/2 days. In seven of
a 19 year cycle an extra month is added. The year 5720
for instance, has only 12 months while last year had a
13th month.
The Jewish Religious Calendar that appears in the
following pages of the Center Year Book is arranged ac¬
cording to the months of the Civil Calendar, the Hebrew
date appears in the lower left corner under the large
number.
Fridays and the eve of Festivals, as well as the first day
of Festivals, carry the symbol L.C., “Light Candles”. The
hour that follows this symbol is 18 minutes prior to sunset,
based on information furnished by the United States Wea¬
ther Bureau for Jacksonville.
Rosh Hodesh, “Beginning of the Month”, is observed
with special prayers in the service for that day and with the
Blessing of the New Month on the Sabbath previous. When
the new moon appears in the middle of the day the out¬
going month consists of 30 days and the last day is con¬
sidered the first day Rosh Hodesh for the coming month.
The Sid rah, the portion of the Torah for the week, is
indicated on Saturday’s date. Special Sabbaths are named
and are explained below under the Hebrew month in which
they occur.
See Explanatory Notes in Last Year’s Center Year Book for the
historic associations and religious reasons of the Holidays, Festi¬
vals, minor Feasts, Fast Days and special Sabbaths.
SPECIAL OCCASIONS
Sabbath. Two Candlesticks and the Kiddush cup; loaf
of twisted Hallah bread in the background, inscription:
“Sabbath holiness”.
Rosh Hashanah. Shofar, with set of scales symbolic
of divine judgment in the background. Inscription: “Happy
New Year” on top; “Repentance” together with the two
Hebrew words in the next panel occur in the Unetane
Tokef prayer.
Y om Kip pur. Prayer Book with the verse “On this
day shall Atonement be made for you” inscribed on its open
pages; Charity plate; with a tall taper alongside. Inscrip¬
tion: “Prayer and Charity,” completing the quotation “Re¬
pentance, Prayer and Charity lead to Grace and Salvation.”
Sukkot. Lulav (palm branch) set into a holder with
myrtle and willow branches, an Etrog (citron) lies along¬
side. Inscription: “Thou shall rejoice in thy feast.”
Hanukah. Menorah with candles; shield with letter
“mem” for Maccabees and cruse of oil in background. In-
scription: “Rock of Ages, my Salvation”.
Purim. Megillah scroll of Esther; in background,
crown and scepter. Inscription: “Peace to all his seed.”
Passover. Four cups, embellished with grape leaves
representing the fourfold promise of redemption. In baek-
ground, three matzohs. Inscription: “Let my people go
that they worship me.”
Shavuot. Torah scroll in decorated cover with silver
ornaments. In background; stalks of wheat and grapevines
symbolic of Bikkurim, first fruits. Inscription: “Torah of
Truth.”
