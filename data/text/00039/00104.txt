HARRY GENDZIER
"A Man For All Seasons”
"To be a happy and worthwhile
individual, you must share your life
with others” This idea was taught
to Harry Gendzier by his mother and
has guided him in his dedication to
the Jewish Community. To under¬
stand Harry, one needs a little
background sketch of this unique
character.
The year was 1916 and war raged.
In the deserts of Palestine,
Britons, Arabs and Turks were
waging sporadic war over a land once
ruled by biblical kings.
In the trenches of Russia, Jews
were dying, sometimes at the hands
of attacking Germans, sometimes at
the hands of their Russian
countrymen.
And in New York City, 19 year old
Harry Gendzier, inflamed with
pride, anger and hope, was writing
an impassioned story he called,
"Awake, O, Israel."
"Pack your cudgels ye sons of
Maccabees, falter not," wrote
Gendzier. "Live and hope ... but then
work . . . send this message to your
brethren across the sea. Make of our
race as it was before The Race.
Jerusalem, let us meet there, United
and Peace Loving. Shalom, Shalom."
That was 32 years before a State of
Israel existed and 52 years before
Harry and his wife, Belle, first saw
the promised land.
AN ACTIVE YOUTH
Growing up in New York City,
Harry was a familiar figure around
the Peter Cooper Club, an
Educational Alliance and Jewish
Settlement house that a fellow
named Eddie Cantor spent a lot of
time in, and a youngster named
David Sarnoff. At a young age, Harry
became interested in the Zionist
Youth groups and that is when he
wrote the aforementioned article
which was published in several
magazines, including Jacksonville’s
first Jewish Publication, "Spring
Festival."
Leaving New York, Harry came by
boat to Jacksonville in the fall of
1917, planning to enroll in law
school in Gainesville. He learned
classes would not start for several
months so he went to work as a shoe
salesman here.

During the depression, the
Gendziers owned a shoe business.
One day Harry Gendzier was having
trouble with the old car he drove. He
was talking with a man who was
driving a new sleek Buick. Harry
asked him what business he was in.
"The rag business", said the Buick
owner.
In 1937 Harry left the shoes
forever and with the barest of capital,
launched the Florida Rag Company,
a recycling business.
HARRY’S DREAM
All the while Harry was busy
making a living, he was very
concerned about the young Jewish
adults. As one of the 75 members of
the Orthodox Synagogue, B’nai
Israel. Harry felt that the restless
young adults of the congregation
were not able to enjoy the
overcrowded facilities. Harry
Gendzier, looking to the future, felt
as though Jewish life in Jacksonville
was deteriorating ... the attachment
remained with the synagogue only
for the High Holiday services;
births; deaths; marriages. He and
other congregants felt that it was
time for change. The synagogue
should hold the center of the cultural,
social and religious aspects of Jewish
life, ail under one roof. The
leadership was ready to go along
with the ideas. Rabbi Samuel
Benjamin, a rabbi of the
Conservative movement, was asked
to lead them toward Conservative
Judaism.

1926. At that time there were 200
members of Congregation B’nai
