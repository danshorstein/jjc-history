As I write this, the Men’s Club is 18 months old. Or maybe young. Growing up is an endless series
of circles. This is true for people and for organizations. With every bit of work there is growing. With
every bit of growing there is learning. For every bit of learning it takes work.
This year saw a lot of work make a great many things happen. The Men’s Club was accepted for
membership in the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs. We had our first dance. The Club observed its
first Men’s Club Shabbat. We sold twelve dozen Yellow Yahrzeit Candles to help commemorate Yom
Ha-Shoah. We had several brunches with speakers and our second Superbowl party. We also helped
out at some Center functions as hot dog heaters. Then, a program full of ups and downs, there was
the second fishing trip.
As we grew this year, and experimented with programs, we learned what works, and how to make
them work better. The leadership, while there are some changes, continues to grow together and become
stronger. We have learned from each other and from our members. Hours of work has lead to growth
and learning. We hope we have translated that into fun and help where we were needed. Our work for
the Center is a labor of love. Kahlil Gibran said in The Prophet , “work is love made visible.”
My thanks to the outgoing board. My best wishes to the incoming board. And a special thanks to
Jane, Beth, and Renee for permitting me the time for this project.

AUTO
(Adults United To Organize)
AUTO, the Supplemental School organization, gives children and parents an opportunity to share Judaica ex-
periences together.
It has always been a Jewish philosophy to protect our
“kinder” and give to them what we never got! Those
times of protection are the links of an unbroken chain in
which our children need to experience and share the
knowledge of what survival is about. We are moving for-
ward with new and exciting programs involving the leader-
ship and intuition of our children with parents being the
bumper guards on the sides.
We are hoping to be able to guide instead of protect,
teach and share instead of lecture and brag. A school
newsletter by our students is one way we plan to open up
a new line of communication. We are very concerned with
the welfare of our children and have taken our first steps
in the marathon. Please be there to help our runners stay
in the race! We need your support!

Keeper of the “Fifth Commandment”
‘Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long.
It was this commandment which inspired the founding of River
Garden Hebrew Home in 1946. It was this commandment which
nourished the love and care that over the years has made River
Garden a nationally recognized model of excellence in its field.
It is the commandment which underlies the tradition of meeting
100% of local Jewish community needs and of serving rich and
poor alike with excellence and caring.
River Garden Hebrew Home/Wolfson Health and Aging Center
is on a 33 acre campus in Mandarin near the intersection of 1-295
and Old St. Augustine Road. The design of the Traditional Care,
Sheltered Care and Short Term Rehabilitation pavilions recognizes
that people with medical needs do not have to live in medical set-
tings. The River Garden Home/Wolfson Health and Aging Center
will be a part of a larger community which will include: apartments,
a town center, a general store, a fitness center and a campus club.
Overall, River Garden is today and tomorrow a community
where people live, love, and care; a community of many life styles
and of rich Jewish content, and a community which every day strives
to live up to the mandate of the Fifth Commandment.

Message from the Rabbi
REFLECTIONS ON THE HOLIDAYS
RABBI DAVID GAFFNEY
Rabbi Shlomo of Karlin, a great Hassidic Rabbi, once startled his disciples by asking them
a rather unusual question: “What is the most serious sin a Jew can commit?”
His students reflected for a while and then suggested their respective answers: “murder,”
said one; “idolatry,” said the second. Other answers were given.
The Rabbi shook his head after each answer and when all the disciples had been heard,
he stated his own. “The most serious sin a Jew can commit is to forget the Biblical verse: “You
are children of the Lord your God” (Deuteronomy 14:1).
I find this an insightful story because it states that when we look upon ourselves as children
of God, we invest our lives with fullest potentiality. . . that we achieve as we believe.
What we think of ourselves has a powerful impact on what we make of ourselves. The
decisive area of our accomplishments is located in our own mind. The Hassidic Rabbi anticipated
the insight of William James, one of the leading pioneers in the field of psychology who wrote
that “the greatest discovery of my generation is that human beings can alter their lives by alter-
ing their attitudes.”
Our severest handicap is a distorted self-image When we think of ourselves as incapable, unlovable, unsuccessful, we tend to become
all these things. However, when we have a positive self-image, our achievements will reflect that self-appraisal. We tend to become
what we imagine ourselves to be. Our thoughts and our attitudes are the controlling factors.
That is why the “sins” enumerated during the High Holiday service describe not only how we sin against God and how we sin
against our neighbors, but also how we sin against ourselves. The same Rabbi once pointed out that “the worst thing the evil urge
can do is to make us forget that we are the children of God.”
God is not affected by our wrongdoing, but we are. We are diminished; we lose the significance of our lives. The whole thrust
of the High Holidays is an appeal to turn to Judaism and to discover who we are and what our life means. . . that we are God’s partners
in the creation of a better self and a better world.
Simply put, a positive attitude and a positive self-image is so very vital and so very important. You must believe in yourself. This
should be a goal, a philosophy, an attitude, and a way of life.
The Siddur—our daily prayer book—contains this philosophy. It expresses in one of the preliminary blessings: Praise be to You,
Lord our God, ruler of the universe, who fashioned me in Your image.” What a magnificant statement. To begin each morning with
this blessing is to be reminded of our unique, intimate, and personal relationship with God, thus reminding us of our unlimited potential,
our sacred responsibility and our inherent dignity. When we think along these lines, we not only become better people... we are ready
to face the personal and collective challenges that we have as individuals and as Jews.
Think of how extraordinary the past year has been. It was a year in which we saw a successful conclusion to one of the most
insidious threats in the person of Saddam Hussein. That we were able to deal with that challenge and to see a successful conclusion
of the Gulf War, was made possible, in great measure, by our country’s assertion of its interest and of the interests of other nations
who want peace. The State of Israel dealt, not only with the problem of Saddam Hussein, but also with the extraordinary challenge
of the absorption of hundreds of thousands of Soviet Jews. The Jewish community, worldwide and most especially in the United States,
recognized that we are living in historic times and proved, for the most part, to be worthy of the challenge.
During the month of June, we witnessed the extraordinary miracle of the absorption of 14,000 Ethiopian Jews who had been given
up for lost and who found themselves received and welcomed in Israel. . . another challenge. Israel was able to do this because it is
clear about its mission. . . that no longer will any Jew find him/herself homeless in the world and not know that there is a place that
they can call home.
Here, at the Center, we are on the threshold of a major watershed in our own congregation’s history. We have, over these past
months, been conducting a very successful campaign called “The Last Mile” with a mission to bring an end to the indebtedness of
our mortgage payments. We do not want our children to carry the burden of our sizable mortgage. We need to eliminate this debt
and free ourselves of this heavy burden so that we can focus our energy on strengthening existing programs and other pressing needs.
This, too, is our challenge.
But then, Jewish life is always a challenge. Other generations had to pay in sacrifices more precious and more dear than those
we are asking of our congregants. We take so much for granted, our health. . .our family. . .our loved ones. We, too often, take the
Jewish community for granted as well as the legacy that we have received from the past. What the above quoted Rabbi was suggesting
was that the most serious sin is to forget that “we are children of God;” that we are the architects of our destinys; that we are in com-
mand of our lives. It all depends upon our approach, our attitude and our sense of purpose and direction.
The High Holidays come to urge us to make every attempt to do better with what we have and to fulfill our potential as human
beings, as members of families, as members of our congregation, and as members of our people. We each have boundless resources
of mind and spirit waiting to be tapped and vast capacities striving to be utilized. There are challenges out there, no doubt, but we
would sin, grieviously, if we did not believe that we are up to the challenge.
As we begin this new year, we begin with a prayer that each of us will remember this message—that we achieve a positive self-
image, that we discover those gifts with which we have already been endowed and fulfill our highest potential, and that we are able
to use these challenges as stepping stones to broader horizons of creative living. It is in this hope that we share in common a prayer
for Shanah Tovah—a New Year rich in health, fulfillment and achievement.
Rabbi David
and Inge Gaffney

WHAT OUR SYNAGOGUE MEANS TO US
Over 440 children were educated in our
Synagogue this past year. Our schools are
grooming the Jewish leaders of the future.
From Sisterhood luncheons, to wedding
receptions, to youth conventions, to commit-
tee meetings, to Bubbee-Zaydee and Young
Family Shabbat programs, our building is
home to a variety of activities. There are life
cycle events, daily services and holiday
celebrations which would keep our doors open
everyday. There is not a day that goes by in
the life of our congregation when our building
is not being used to perpetuate our Jewish
heritage.
We are proud of the contributions of our
Synagogue to the quality of Jewish life in this
community and we will continue to do so as
long as our Synagogue is healthy. Through
your support, we will solidify this leadership
role.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH FEDERATION
The Jacksonville Jewish Federation is the central fundraising, planning,
budgeting and coordinating agency for the Jewish community. It strives to ad-
dress the needs of the Jewish community, encouraging community participa-
tion. Its diverse committees offer many opportunities for volunteer involvement.
The Federation funds and coordinates community relations and educa-
tion programs. It publishes a monthly newspaper of Jewish news of local and
national interest. The Federation’s Leadership Development Program examines
Jewish life and history and gives participants an opportunity to experience
their Jewishness in unique and meaningful ways.
The Federation conducts an annual campaign that raises funds for the
United Jewish Appeal, as well as other national and overseas agencies. Local
beneficiaries are the Jewish Community Alliance, Jewish Family and Com-
munity Services, the Kosher Nutrition Program, River Garden Hebrew Home
for the Aged and the chapters of various Jewish organizations.

The Jewish Community Alliance has as its mission to strengthen Jewish iden-
tity and to provide to the Jewish community services which will enhance the quali-
ty of life and help ensure the continuity of the Jewish people. Therefore the JCA
will: 1) serve as a common meeting ground for all people and all segments of the
Jewish community; 2) provide meaningful group and individual experiences which
the members of the community view as enhancing the quality of their lives; 3)
strengthen family life through programs for all members of the family, recognizing
that some families consist of only one person.
To carry out its mission the Jewish Community Alliance provides programs
in recreation, creative and performing arts, physical education and sports, child care
and preschool, informal education and social group activities. The JCA strives to
make available to people of all ages, opportunities for authentic Jewish experiences,
to develop community based leadership and to work toward building a sense of
community among all Jews.
Although the JCA is created by and established primarily for the Jewish com-
munity, membership is open to all who wish to join regardless of race, sex, religion,
national origin, disabilities or military service.

Education and Youth
Director: Rabbi Dov Kentof
You and I have been sending our children to Jewish
schools. We have invested time and efforts. Has it all been
worthwile? Have the returns justified the investments?
But what returns should we expect? How can we
measure and evaluate them? Educational returns are so
intangible.
Indeed they are, but not as intangible as we may think.
We should and we do have some ways of measuring them.
It is, of course, very simple to measure objectively the
achievements of our children in terms of subject-matter,
such as Bible, Hebrew, history, etc. We do possess the
technical equipment and devices of measuring accurately
what our child has learned at any given time in any of the
school subjects.
But, you may agrue, knowledge is not enough; it is at-
titudes that count; it is the cultivation of character, of the
religious personality, and the like, that is significant.
Quite true. We all realize that mere cold-blooded
knowledge is not enough. Knowledge has no value unless
it glows with the impulse to action. Knowledge must be
dynamic, it must serve both as a motor and a rudder. It
should galvanize its possessor into action; it should also
guide and direct such action.
Character or personality is in the final analysis a mat-
ter of conduct, of behavior and deeds, of performing
habitually certain actions rather than others. But conduct
may have no ethical or social value unless it is undergirded
by knowledge. Besides, there is an old saying: Nothing sue-
ceeds like success. Achievement in knowledge and abilities
generally go hand in hand with the development of attitudes
and character. The glow of successful achievement
generates a favorable attitude toward the subject matter
and often results in the building of abiding interests.
Photo left: Cindy Marsh,
Secretary
Photo right: Dov is
always surrounded by
kids of all ages.
Indeed many centuries ago our Rabbis declared: LO
AM HA-ARETZ HASID, an ignorant man cannot be a good
man. When the question was raised some 18 centuries ago
as to the relative merits of conduct and learning the Sages
decided that learning is preferable, “for learning leads to con-
duct.”
Our ancient Sages had indeed a keen pedagogical in-
sight. They were right in maintaining that character or con-
duct has to be built on a background of knowledge, and
that it is the inevitable by-product of all learning; it cannot
be developed in an intellectual vacuum. They were further
correct in stressing that learning is an ongoing life-process.
“He who does not constantly add to learning perishes
spiritually” is a famous Rabbinic saying based on sound
pedagogy. Knowledge cannot be cold-storaged, to be us-
ed, when need arises. Unless it is constantly refreshed by
the accumulated wisdom of the past and the new resources
of the present, it shrinks, withers, dies.
Hence, the study of Judaism must be continued, ac-
cording to the traditional view, all through life. “On the day
of judgment,” says a famous medieval sage, “a man is first
questioned regarding his devotion to the study of Torah and
then regarding his other deeds.”
The conclusion should be obvious. Our school should
provide a certain minimum of knowledge in each grade and
at each stage. But above all, our school should endeavor
to cultivate an ability and a desire to go on learning. Our
school should aim to develop abiding interests in the study
of Judaism.
Here is a challenge to our synagogue and school. I pray
that we will be able to meet this challenge.
Let me wish you and your family a happy and healthy
new year.

Three Members Honored on Simhat Torah, Friday, October 12
Jack Silverman, Hatan Torah
Jack Silverman puts his car in “automatic pilot” and goes directly to the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. Whether it’s for daily minyan, Shabbat Services, a Center Board meeting,
Religious committee, Adult Education Hebrew classes, 55 + , Mr. and Mrs. or the raffle rehear-

Our Hatan Torah also serves as a gabbai at services, seeing to it that the Torahs and
other ceremonial objects are in their proper places and safely stored. He voluntarily arrives
early Saturday mornings so he can get the prayerbooks ready for service leaders and the
Shabbat worshippers. The personal attention he gives to his volunteer efforts and genuine
concern he has for his synagogue and his family have made Jack Silverman a most ad-
mired and loved man.
We are proud to honor Jack for his dedication to Jewish ideals and his constant devo-
tion to the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Mazel tov to his wife, Mollie; his daughter, Barbara;
his son, Billy; his grandsons and other members of the family.
David Mizrahi, Hatan Bereshit
When asked to describe David Mizrahi in one word, most people used the term,
“mentsh.” His relatives, friends, and people who have served on committees with David
all agree that he is an “upright, honorable person; someone of consequence; someone to
admire and emulate.”
David has been a long-standing and active member of the Center Board. As chairman
of the Religious committee for the last six years, David has handled issues with sensitivity
and diplomacy. He is responsible for the distribution of the synagogue honors on Shabbat
and holidays and oversees the daily minyan. David works closely with Rabbi Gaffney and
synagogue lay leaders on issues relating to Jewish ritual, worship and synagogue practice.
Born and bred in Jacksonville, David received his entire formal Jewish education at
the Center. As a teenager he held the distinguished position of Southeast Regional Presi-
dent of USY. It is no surprise that David is so involved. He comes from a long line of
presidents. His father, Ralph, is a past president of the Center as is his brother Charles;
his mother, Evelyn, is a past Sisterhood president.
We honor David for his dedicated service. Mazel tov to his wife, Judy; his sons, Steven, Ronald and Dennis; his parents,
Evelyn and Ralph Mizrahi; his brother, Charles and sister, Debbie and other members of the family.
Barbara Safer, Woman of Valor
While some people are content to watch the passing parade, Barbara Safer insists
on marching in it. Her involvement and dedication to enhancing Jewish life in Jacksonville
are evidenced by the many leadership roles she has held.
Our Woman of Valor has been involved in all aspects of Jewish life at the Center as
well as the Jacksonville Jewish community. During her tenure as Center Sisterhood presi-
dent, Barbara’s commitment to Jewish education and the advancement of women were
apparent. She encouraged women to become more knowledgeable Jews so they could take
more active roles in Jewish ritual. She served on the Education Committee, Adult Educa-
tion Committee, was a Kadima advisor with her husband Eliot, is a past president of the
Solomon Schechter Day School PTA, was Membership co-chairman for the Center and
continues to be a valued and active volunteer for all Center special events.
Barbara is a past president of Shirl-Li Hadassah and is Donor Chairman of River Garden
Auxiliary. On the national level, Barbara is presently a National Board Member of Women’s
League for Conservative Judaism, a recipient of Hadassah’s National Leadership Award
as well as the National Young Leadership Award for the Jacksonville Jewish Federation. She is also a Florida Branch
vice-president.
Her strength of character, organizational skills and tenacity to get the job done have made her a sought after leader
and committee member. There’s a saying around town, “If Barbara Safer is on the committee, it will be a success.”
We honor Barbara Safer for her loyalty to her synagogue, family and friends and for her dedication and commitment
to Jewish life. Sharing her honor are her husband, Eliot and sons, Morry and Mathew.

Willard Kennedy, Principal
Principal's Message
The Solomon Schechter Day School of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center exists today as the outstanding educational institution it is
because of the persistence and vision of the congregation and its leader-
ship, both lay and professional, throughout the years.
Beginning in 1961, families were attracted to a day school which
stood for intellectual excellence and Jewish tradition. The embryonic
school grew year by year with the support of Rabbis in the area, of laymen
who shared its ideals and purposes, and a growing number of gifted
and dedicated parents.
Upon this base, the school is entering its thirtieth year of serving
the youth of the Jewish Community.
One may ask, what sort of family sends a child to Schechter, and
why is their number growing? They may belong to a Conservative
synagogue, or an Orthodox or Reform one, or none at all. Some parents
are Rabbis, Jewish educators, or academicians, most are not. For the
most part, they are pulled by the attraction of the Schechter program
rather than pushed by dissatisfaction with local school conditions. They
are looking for quality education which takes the Jewish religious dimen-
sion of learning and growing at least as seriously as other aspects.
Schechter parents realize that intensely Jewish neighborhoods, where
the streets and store windows teach something about Jewishness, don’t
exist for their children in our community. They look to our school to
provide a sense of being comfortable with one’s Jewishness, and to pro-
vide a world where Jewish observance and Jewish seriousness are
normative.
The Solomon Schechter Day School looks back with gratitude to
all those who sustained it during the early years, are maintaining its
identity today, and look forward with confidence and enthusiasm to
the years ahead.

Boy Scout Troop 14
Boy Scout Troop 14 had another memorable year in 1990-91.
Founded in 1919, Troop 14 is in its eighth decade of serving the
youth of our congregation and the community.
Troop 14 is dedicated to the lofty ideals of the Boy Scouts
of America. The program features regular camping and outdoor
activities, weekly meetings, personal advancement and projects
of service to others.
The year was filled with exciting outdoor adventures. The
scouts camped at the breathtaking Manatee Springs and Blue
Springs State Parks where the scouts enjoyed swimming in the
crystal clear waters and canoeing. The boys joined the Webloes
of Pack 14 at Boy Scout Camp Echockottee where all displayed
exceptional skills in the art of angling, much to their culinary
delight. The Troop joined other scouts from throughout North
Florida for a fun-filled weekend at Scout World while earning
recognition for a fine camping display. The energetic scouts visited
the pre-Civil War bastion, Fort Clinch, and completed the rugg-
ed, 16-mile Florida Historic Trail Hike in record time.
The year’s camping highlight was attending a week-long trip
to Camp Daniel Boone in the beautiful mountains of North
Carolina near Ashville. The entourage enjoyed white water raf-
ting, canoeing, mountain climbing, archery and riflery, though no
member of the troop participated in the 50-mile llama trek this
year. The scouts in attendance included: Ian Fiske, Greg Dacks,
Shai Smargon, Doug Weinstein, Michael Slonin, Brian Bell, Adam
Dean, Zack Hatfield, David Ayotte, Brian Usoff, Jeff Anchel,
Michael Edwards, Randy Echalico and Ryan Sussman.
The scouts camped at Walt Disney World’s Fort Wilderness
and visited the Magic Kingdom. The Scouts visited the Hillel
Jewish Student Center in Gainesville joining the University of
Florida students for Sabbath services and a Gator football game,
experienced the Florida State Museum and explored the unique
Devil’s Millhopper sinkhole.
The Beaver Patrol led by patrol leader, Morry Safer, was
awarded the first annual Harry I. Yergin Award for the Outstan-
ding Patrol at the Annual Blue and Gold Banquet. Brian Levine
and Morry Safer delighted the banquet audience with their hilarious
and creative presentation of Hans and Franz, as the madcap
strongmen of Saturday Night Live turned rappers.

CENTER CEMETERY
The Center owns and operates two cemeteries, both of which are adjacent to the Evergreen Cemetery in North Jackson-
ville. Both are beautiful pieces of property with large shade trees and mature landscaping. The original cemetery, usually
referred to simply as the “Old Cemetery,” has the graves of the founding families of the congregation, dating back to the
turn of the century. The new cemetery is known as the Center Memorial Park. The Center provides complete maintainence
of these grounds. Both cemeteries are open daylight hours every day except Shabbat and Jewish Holidays. A memorial
service is conducted each year on the Sunday between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur at each of the cemeteries.
Just as the celebration of weddings, bar/bat mitzvahs and other life cycle events at the Center are limited to its members,
the professional services of the Rabbi and other Center personnel for burial and mourning are also reserved for Center
members. Therefore, only members may pur-
chase a plot in one of the Center cemeteries.
Associate members are not eligible to pur-
chase a plot. For more information regarding
the purchase of cemetery plots, please call the
Center administrative office.

19914992?

Zionist Organization of America
Jacksonville District
The Zionist Organization of America, Jacksonville District, continues
its more than 44 year old custom of encouraging the community to
plant trees in Israel. This gives a person a tangible way to experience
what Zionism is all about. Trees can be planted to commemorate any
occasion.
ZOA monitors activities in our nation’s capital, the White House,
Congress, and all other relevant agencies. Besides publishing The
American Zionist and ZOA Insider, there is the Zionist Information
News Service which provides a weekly news bulletin filled with vital
information not usually found elsewhere. ZOA Headquarters in NYC
serves as a cultural and educational center for Zionist activity in
America. ZOA has accepted the challenge of the Conference of
Presidents of Major American Jewish Organizations to fight against
UN Resolution 3379 which equates “zionism with racism.”
ZOA is one of the few Zionist organizations with a constituency
whose entire agenda is the promotion of Israel. To this end, young
leadership missions were organized, dialogues with Christian Zionists
established and cultural programs continued at ZOA House in Tel Aviv.
Renew your commitment to Israel by joining with us.

Center Raffle
“What We Did For Love”
On January 20, 1990, a standing-room-only crowd
of more than 600 people were treated to a musical pro-
duction called, “Broadway Comes to Jacksonville.”
Favorite show tunes and dance numbers filled the
auditorium. Our talented Center members persented a
top-notch production for one very good reason—the
education of our children. We thank all the cast and crew
for a wonderful evening. Our appreciation to Marilyn
Garber, director; Shirley Bielski, assistant director;
and Debra Setzer; producer. Topping ticket sales were
Alan Gordon, Linda DuBow, Rhonda Passink
and Isabel Balotin, followed closely by Martha
Savage, Stuart Hecht, Jeffery Morris and
Shirley Bielski. Our thanks to all our other sellers
who helped us reach our goal. Ticket sales coordinator,
Charlotte Fialkow, kept all the records and did a
fabulous job. We are grateful to all the ticket buyers who,
year after year, support our fund raiser for education.
Trying it all together were coordinators, Mel Jawitz,
Esther Weiss and Carla Bernard.
The top winners were: Alan and Renee
(Lola) Gordon, $7,500; Beth and Mitchell Trager,
$1,000; Sue and Ron Elinoff, $500; VCR - George
Cohen. Congratulations and thanks to all.
