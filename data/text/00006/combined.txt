With its fine group of leaders Jun¬
ior Hadassah has made great strides
during the past year toward establishing
itself as the most outstanding group of
young Jewish girls in the city.
Opening its year of activity with

a substantial number of new members
were added to the rolls of the chapter,
Junior Hadassah carried through an elab¬
orate series of social affairs: formal and
informal dances, ,card parties, and an¬
nual Donor s Affair, all of whi ch helped
to strengthen the social bonds uniting
all Hadassah workers.
Endeavoring to improve the cult¬
ural status of its members, Junior Ha-
Jassah sponsored a series of study groups
which formed convenient units for the
discussion of Jewish problems and Zion¬
ist ideals. Many interesting lectures were
delivered to these groups by different
speakers.
The purely Zionist activities of Jun¬
ior Hadassah during the past year were
many and fruitful. A special program
was devoted to Youth Aliyah, the project
ol transferring youths from persecution-
ridden Europe to Palestine. Another pro¬
gram was devoted to initiating graduates
of Young Judea into Junior Hadassah.
By far the most inspiring event this
year was the very successful Regional
Convention whi ch was held in conjunc¬
tion with Senior Hadassah. A fine group
of girls coming from the neighboring
states as well as members of the local
Junior Hadassah participated in th e many
social and cultural events of the Con¬
vention. The highlight of this event was
the luncheon held on Sunday at the
Roosevelt Hotel. J he guest speaker, Mrs.
Moses P. Epstein brought a vital and in¬
spiring message to the assembled dele¬
gates.
With all quotas lor Junior Had¬

satisfactorily filled, Junior Hadassah
brought to close a success ful year oi act¬

JACKSONVILLE POST No. 199
A valuable addition to the Center-affiliated organizations is the Jewish
War Veterans and its Ladies Auxil iary.
Organized four years ago, the Post has been active in promoting patriotism
and the ideas of democracy.
Members of the post meet twice a month on the second floor of the Center
rear wing. I hroughout the year, members of the post pay good will visits to
local neighboring hospitals and distribute gifts and magazines to their Jewish
and non-Jewish patients. On national holidays, the Post participates in parades
and other patriotic manifestations.
The Ladies Auxiliary of the Jewish War Veterans meets
nights as the post and assists its members in all its undertakings.

Mrs. Joseph Strauss, President
Jacksonville Chapter of
Hadassah
Has Outstanding Year
Hadassah is the Women s Zionist Or¬
ganization of America whose purpose it
is to assist in the upbuilding and main¬
taining of Palestine as a national Jewish
homeland. The marvelous accomplish¬
ments there in the past 25 years have
been greatly due to this group of women.
Had assah has undertaken the task of
making Palestine a country both habit¬
able and healthful. Through the estab¬
lishment of a net-work of medical clinics,
hospitalization, nursing service and mod¬
ern methods of sanitation, Hadassah has
practically abolished the dread of malaria
and trachoma and has made Palestine as
livable as any place on earth.
Hadassah s work reaches out to desti¬
tute children from Europe and makes it
possible for them to find safety in this
new home. Through Youth Aliyah, it
has helped transfer, settle and educate
in Palestine some 8,000 of these young
people, who are beginning to show how
valuable a contribution they can make
toward achieving a democratic victory.
Between October 1940 and 1941 a total
of 1250 were able to get to Palestine. De-
spite war conditions, continued immigra¬
tion into Palestine is still possible through
Youth Aliyah channels.
One of the most important phases o(
the work of Hadassah is the Child Wel¬
fare program, the aim of which is to check
malnutrition among the school children
of Palestine by providing them with nour¬
ishing meals. 4 his project is now (eed-
ing 25,000 schoo I child ren through its
school luncheons and providing a year-
round check up on child health through
its 47 infant welfare stations and its
school hygiene program in clubs, kinder¬
gartens, elementary and secondary
schools throughout Palestine. Through
the I hrift Luncheons here, the local
chapter raises its quota for this fund.
Linens and clothing gathered through
the annual linen shower and the sewing
groups that meet throughout the year,
supply many useful garments for hospi¬
tals and refugees. The Palestine Supplies
committee also is sending to Palestine
some 30 tons of supplies — drugs, equip¬
ment, food and clothing each month.
In the new land areas that have been
acquired by the Jewish National Fund
in the crucial period of 1941-42, Hadas¬
sah has a considerable share in which
it takes justifiable pride. Through its box
collections here, and the sale of tree cer¬
tificates, this organization gives valuable
assistance to this fund.
I hrough its Donors affair and the an¬
nual fall dance, the Jacksonville Chapter
contributes its share for the Hadassah
Medical Organization fund, which is
used to maintain Hadassah s vast med¬
ical and public health system in Pales¬
tine. Because of the war emergency and
because of the thousands of impoverished
refugees who are reaching Palestine,
Hadassah has had to expand its health
program. 1 hey have provided for hun¬
dreds of extra hospital beds and have
established a large hospital fund for the
purpose of giving free hospitalization to
indigent patients.
I his year that has reached so high a
mark of service in Palestine is also rich
in development and service on the Amer¬
ican scene. In accordance with Hadas¬
sah s program ol education, the chapter
here conducts a study group, a monthly
Oneg Shabbat gathering, and each month
presents a specific phase of Hadassah s
program by one of the committee chair¬
men.
In the realm ol defense work, a survey
shows that the majority of our member¬
ship has participated in at least one of
the many phases: first-aid nutrition, air
raid shelter, Red Cross sewing and knit¬
ting and bandage rolling. Our chapter has
made contributions to the Red Cross, to
the Blood Bank, to the U. S. O. and
with magazine subscriptions to the U.
S.O. We also worked with the Junior
League in collecting blankets and were
success ful in obtaining 86 blankets from
our members for this project. A number
of dances were given for the service men,
and the Business and Professional Wom¬
en’s Division sponsored some very en¬
tertaining house parties for the men in
the service.
Our chapter was very active in the
sale of tuberculosis seals, contributed to
the administrative fund of the nursery
schools maintained here for children of
working parents. Individual contributions
were made to the Council for the pre¬
vention of cancer, through the efforts of
a chairman appointed by our chapter.
An inspiring and stimulating contribu¬
tion to Jacksonville Jewry was our South¬
eastern Regional Conference, which
brought us Mrs. Moses P. Epstein, Mi ss
Edith Bukspan and many other brilliant
speakers.
Membership is the foundation upon
which Hadassah s entire program rests
and its progress is revealed in the addi¬
tion of 67 new members during the past
year.
I he Business and Prof essional Wom¬
en’s Division is a part of Senior Hadas¬
sah and this group has made remarkable
strides in the past year. The membership
has increas ed from 30 to 52 and they
have participated in every Senior Hadas¬
sah phase, by assuming a definite quota
for every project and in every instance
fulfilling the pledge.

JEWISH WELFARE SOCIETY
1942
Dave Davis, President
Throughout the years of its existence,
the Jewish Welfare Society of Jackson¬
ville, an agency of the local Community
Chest, has endeavored to serve the Jew¬
ish needy of the community. It has been
the aim of the Society, not only to relieve
the immediate, pressing needs of the less
fortunate, but also to rehabilitate them
to the point at which they are able to
meet their own situations. During the
past year about five hundred (500) cases
received assistance from this organization.
Problems dealt with have included un¬
employment, old age, chronic and acute
illnesses, dependency of children, trans¬
portation and insufficient income.
The Jewish Welfare Society continues
to co-operate with the National Refugee
Service, Inc., of New York. Funds for
this purpose are supplied by the Jack¬
sonville Jewish Community Council. J he
organization is endeavoring to see to it
that every American not only accepts the
new-comer, but also recognizes his value
as a citizen with much to contribute to
the democratic way of life. And in their
turn, today, many refugee boys are serving
in the United States Army, while scien¬
tists and inventors among them are giv¬
ing freely of their learning and experience
in order to further the war e ffort.
Since America entered the war vast
changes have also taken place in local
conditions. Millions of men either al¬
ready are, or will be in the armed serv¬
ices by the end o f the year. This condi¬
tion has, of course, reduced unemploy¬
ment sharply. But while the Social
Agencies have less unemployment with
which io contend, new burdens have
been added through war time responsi¬
bilities.
Many have asked the question, How
will conditions affect the Jewish Welfare
Society?” And Mrs. Estelle G. Stern,
the executive secretary, answers by stat¬
ing: 'There has been marked increase
in the cost of living and we have had to
increase the allowances to our families
and unattached people. Many Jewish
doctors and dentists, who have given
their services gratis to our clients, have
volunteered for war duty. We will have
an increase of cases of middle-aged men
who, through priorities of war materials
wi ii i ose their positions in small business¬
es. It will be very difficult to find foster
home care for our children, because fam¬
ilies are too upset to take in children, even
if an increased rate for board is offered.
Nursing home care has increased and it
wi II be difli cult to secure practical nurses.
We are faced with a housing problem.
It is almost impossible to lind apartments.
At the annual meeting ol the Jewish
Welfare Society honors were conferred
on D avid Davis and Miss Cassie Joel.
Mr. Davis, who has been president of
the organization for twenty-two (22)
years, was presented with a wrist watch
from the board members, while Miss
Joel, chairman of the hospital committee,
who has been with the association since
its inception, was given a radio.
J he Jewish Welfare Society wishes
to take cognizance of the excellent co¬
operation received from the following
local agencies: the Ladies Hebrew Shel¬
tering Aid Society; the Duval Family
Welfare Board; the Duval County Hos¬
pital; the Girl Scouts; the District and
State Welfare Boards; the Legal Aid
Association the State Rehabilitation De¬
partment; the Tuberculosis Association,
and the Duval County Health Unit.
A fine opportunity is offered those
wishing to assist in the unselfish work
of this organization, and at the same
time, to remember their loved ones on
occasions of either joy or sorrow, through
the FI ower Memorial Fund. When the
fragrance of Howers is lost and when the
beauty of material things has departed,
the memory of your tender charity will
live, hallowed by the reverence of those
whom you have made better and happier
by your loving sympathy.”

Mr. Selber took a special interest in the physical
welfare of the Center building, supervising all repairs
and improvements and? being handy with tools, often
doing odd jobs himself. He took care of the grounds
and annually built the Center Sukkah.
His greatet service to the Center, however, was
the splendid transformation he brought to the Center
Cemetary grounds since he undertook supervision in
1937. The much neglected property was converted in¬
to a beautiful park. The roads were improved, city wat¬
er was brought in and every barren spot was made
green with grass and beautiful shrubbery. Mr. Selber
gave to this work every minute of his time for several
years and earned the lasting gratitude of the institution.
This 39th edition of the Jacksonville Jewish Cen¬
ter year book is dedicated to Mr. Simon Selber, one of
the most loyal and most devoted members of the insti¬
tution.
Mr. Selber came to America in 1904 and settled in
Jacksonville seven years later. He immediately joined
the old B Nai Israel Synagogue, but poor health pre¬
vented him from taking an active part in the a ffairs oí
the congregation.
In 1928, the year the Center was com pletedt Mr.
Selber became a mourner. I he observance o f the K a-
dish, morning and evening, brought him into intimate
contact with Center activities. 1 he daily exercise of
walking to and from the Synagogue greatly improved
his health. Since that time, Mr. Selber, as an express¬
ion of gratitude for his regained hea ith , devoted all
of his time and energy to the welfare o rti íe Jacksonville
Jewish Center. He was an active member and chair¬
man of a number of important committees, such as the
Religious Committee, the Education Committee, the
Ho use Committee) and the Finance Committee.
His devotion to the Jewish Center has inspired
the loyalty of his children to the institution. They be¬
came indefatigable workers for the Center and promin¬
ent leaders in the community.
On the occasion o I the N ew Year, the officers and .
members of the Center, extend to Mr. Selber and his
family their very best wishes for a year of good health
prosperity and happiness.

The outstanding characteristic of the High Holidays is that they concern themselves primarily with the
Jew as an individual. All other festivals have either national or agricultural origins, and serve to strengthen
the bonds between the Jew, his people and its past. Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur bring home to the in¬
dividual Jew the meaning of his daily struggle for existence, his relationship to his fellow human beings and
to God, the Creator and Ruler of the universe.
The great hold that these Days of Awe (Yomim Noroim) have upon us in this age of irreligión may be
partially explained by the fact that the ideals and emotions stimulated by them probe deeply into the private
life of every Jew and lift him to a high spiritual level.
If the ritual of introspection and self-purification of these solemn days were accepted by the world at
large, it would bring inestimable good to humanity and help to dissolve the world’s ills.
In a world corrupted by greed, hatred and bloodshed, Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur, with their call to re¬
pentance, their prayers for universal peace and the establishment of the Kingdom of God on earth, offer a
valuable message to humanity.
Stosty líaslyonalt
Rosh Hashonah aims to improve human nature. 1 he
prayers, the ritual, the music, and the sounding of the
Shofar are calculated to elicit from the worshipper the finest
and highest expressions of his personality. They impress
upon him the vanity and uselessness of envy, greed and
hate, and stimulate in him emotions of tolerance, love and
the proper understanding o f the real values of life.
During these days, God, as it were, sits in judgment
over us, reviewing our good and bad deeds. This, more
concretely, takes the form of every individual searching his
inner self and resolving to eliminate that which is cruel and
u gly in his life. The New Year ritual prepares him to meet
the coming year cleansed in spirit and determined to foil ow
the path of the good life so that he may find favor in the eyes
of God and man.
fmtt 2Ctppur
One o f the prime functions of Yom Kippur is to break
down the natual resentment on the part of human beings
to admit that they are wrong. I he solemn Kol Nidre service
is opened with a petition to God that he may permit us
to pray in the company of the transgressors, a term which
may be applicable to every member in the congregation.
The public recitation of the long catalogue of sins, among
which we are sure to find some that apply to ourselves,
the requests for forgiveness, which fill the prayer-book, the
custom of asking forgiveness from those whom we know
that we have wronged, all of these are calculated to bring
about a mood in which the worshipper acknowledges his
errors and sins. This is the first step necessary in making
any improvements, for without acknowledging error there
can be no correction of error.
Yom Kippur also serves to demonstrate the supremacy
of the spirit over the fl esh. 1 wenty four hours of fasting
is not only a denial of the natural hungers of the body but
also a test of a man’s willpower to withstand the temp¬
tations to which he may be exposed in the year to come.

I en years cigo, September, 1932. Rabí ji Morris D. M ar-
golis, a young graduate of the Jewish I heological Semi¬
nary of America arrived in Jacksonville to assume the
spiritual leadership of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. 1 he
task that lay ahead of him was an arduous one. Tbe
community was divided into factions. 1 here was still
friction between the orthodox and conservative elements in
the synagogue, occasioned by the period of transition from
the old BN ai Israel Congregation to the new Jacksonville
Jewish Center which was completed in 1928. Frequent
changes in the pulpit caused divided hearts and created per¬
sonal grievances. I he Center had no definite policy and no
established traditions. I he Sunday School an cl He! )rew
School were in a chaotic condition. 1 here was bitter feel¬
ing between the two congregations in the community.
Jacksonville Jewry in general was permeated by an attitude
that Charity begins at home, which greatly hampered the
functioning, let alone the grow th, of those groups in the
community that tried to do Zionist, fraternal, and charitable
work in cooperation with national organizations. In addi¬
tion, the cramped budget of the Center necessitated by a
small membership and years of depression, made it im¬
possible for the institution to engage an adequate staff.
1 he young Rabbi was entrusted with the job of functioning
as rabbi, cantor, choir director, principal of the Sunday
School and Hebrew School, as well as teacher. Indeed,
it was a formidable task. Instead ol being discouraged, the
difficult ies of his first position became a challenge to Rabbi
Margolis. He gave to it every ounce of energy, every
moment of his time, and every thought of his waking hours.
1 oday, despite the struggles and hardships he had en¬
dured, Rabbi Margolis may well look back with satisfaction
upon the f irst ten years of his ministry. As rabbi and
leader he may we 11 feel that h is efforts have been a beneficent
and lasting effect upon the community. His preaching, his
sincere and eloquent presentation ol the message of religion

have helped to contribute to a lessening of friction between
groups. Jacksonville is one o f the very few communities in

Another Center Project--A Kindergarten
Speaking of the modern nursery school,
Dr. Margaret Mead recently said: It
is a most essential new invention in our
changing society, an invention meant to
cushion the shocks from a changing
world. in the crisis which our civil¬
ization faces, she added, pre-school ed¬
ucation is more important today than
ever before. First, because upon the child¬
ren of today will fall the task of rebuild¬
ing the world, and to do this they must
be stronger , more secure, more self-
reliant and self -con fident th an any gen¬
eration before them. And second, be¬
cause our own morale depends upon the
success with which we prepare our child¬
ren for the future: 'There is no better
way to keep our morale high and our
nerves steady than to be alert, in every
community, to care for our children, to
protect and strengthen them against the
future. ’
If th is be true to the world in general,
how much more so is it of the Jewish
world where so many millions have been
uprooted by tyrannical forces and where
all have been profoundly shaken by the
march of events. 1 he Jewish world will
indeed, need rebuilding physically and
spiritually, and the major part of this
gigantic task will have to be done by the
American-Jewish child of today. To se¬
cure the future of Jewish life, therefore,
we must be alert and farsighted now and
realize that in a society so deeply malad¬
justed our children must grow up
stronger, more secure, more self-reliant
and self-confident than any generation
before them.
But while educators are coming to re¬
alize the great importance of pre-school
education, little or nothing has until re¬
cently been done in this field by any of
the Jewish educational organizations in
America. A modern Jewish Nursery
school scientifically controlled was sim¬
ply non-existent.
What, however, at first seemed but a
dream to be realized in the distant future
assumed concrete form two years ago as
a result of the vision, determination and
iniative of Ivriah, The Wo men s Di-
vision ol the Jewish Education Asso¬
ciation of New York City, now the Jew¬
ish Education Committee of New York.
The Beth Haveled, a new experiment in
pre-school Jewish Education, was organ¬
ized, and the impetus given for the de¬
velopment of Jewish Nursery schools.
This school seeks to create an integrated
American-Jewish curriculum that will
meet the needs of the very young child.
Research in the field of child develop¬
ment and mental hygiene has demon¬
strated the importance ol the experiences
of young- children. 1 hese experiences
largely determine their mental health and
their attitude towards themselves and
their enviroment in later life.
It is, moreover, very important that the
child be properly introduced, early in life,
to the particular culture to which he be¬
longs, if that culture is to be adequately
assimilated and to become a source ol
strength and creative expression. In the
case of the Jewish child, these early ex¬
periences must especially serve as a back¬
ground lor a wholesome adjustment to
his bi-culture development.
J he organization of such type ol
school is under consideration by Mr.
Rosenberg, our Executive Director. Par¬
ents interested in this project are urged
to communicate with the Center Office.

The Daughters of Israel serves in the capacity of an
auxiliary to the Center and its main activities have for
their objective the furtherance of all Center projects and
institutions. The Daughters of Israel is represented on the
Board of Directors of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and
its members play an important part in the life o f th e com¬
munity.
Through its various committees, the Daughters of Israel
fast year participated actively in the Jewish Community
Council campaign, the Red Cross work and defense activi¬
ties. Especially active was the U. S. O. Committee which
assisted the Jewish Welfare Board in sponsoring dances and
social affairs for the U. S. service men stationed in the
vicinity of Jacksonville. I his committee also sponsored
a number of social hours following the Jewish religious
services conducted in Camp Bfanding on Friday nights.
Aside from these special activities occasioned by the war
emergency, the Daughters of Israel carried on its regular
work of assistance to the Center. A large sum of money
was raised from the two annual events, The Th anksgiving
D anee an d The Cabaret and Minstrel. The latter event
was one of the most memorable in the history o f the organ¬
ization. For the first time the Daughters of Israel presented
a full fledged, all girl minstrel, in connection with their
cabaret, entitled, Burnt Cork and Tinsel. Its tuneful
melodies, colorful costumes, its dancing and perfect timing
won the admiration and warm applause of the packed
auditorium. Mrs. M. J. Reiser, who directed the minstrel,
received much praise for this splendid performance. I he
minstrel was repeated a number of times at the request
of various organizations.

THE KADDISH
Its origin is mysterious; angels
are said to have brought it down
from heaven and taught it to men.
About thi s prayer the tenderest threads
of filial feeling and human recollection
are entwined; for it is the prayer ol
the orphans! When the farther or
mother dies, the surviving sons are to
recite it twice daily, morning and
evening, throughout the year of mourn¬
ing, and then also on each recurring
anniversery ol the death — on the
Yahrzeit.
It possesses wonderful power.
Truly, if there is any bond atrong
and indissoluble enough to chain
heaven to earth, it is this prayer. It
keeps the living together, and forms
the bridge to the mysterious realm
of the dead. One might also say
that this prayer is the watchman and
the guardian o f the people by whom
alone it is uttered; therein lies the war¬
rant of its continuance. Can a people
disappear and be annihilated so long
as a child remembers its parents? it
may sound strange: in the midst of
the wildest dissipation has this prayer
recall to his better self many a dis¬
solute character, so that he has be¬
thought himself and for a time at least
purified himself by honoring the mem¬
ory of his parents.
Because this prayer is a ressurec-
ion in the spirit of the perishable in
nam because it does not acknowledge
death, because it permits the blos¬
som which, withered, has fallen from
the tree of mankind to flower and
develop again in the human heart,
therefore it possesses sanctifying pow¬
er.
To know that when thou diest,
the earth falling on thy head will not
cover thee entirely;to know that there
remain behind those who, wherever
they may be on this wide earth? whe¬
ther they may be poor or rich, will send
this prayer after thee; to know that
thou Ieavest them no house, no es¬
tate, no field by which they must re-
membeer thee, and that yet they will
cherish thy memory as their dearest
inheritance — what more satisfying
knowledge canst thou ever hope for?
And such is the knowledge bequeathed
to all of us by the Kaddish.
L. Kompert

indigent families.

efforts towards the fulfillment of this much needed project.

war effort.

On a peaceful Sunday morning, a dastardly attack
was made on Pearl Harbor and our nation forced into a war,
the like of which is unknown in the long and bloody history
of mankind.
It is the biggest of all wars. Not only in the number
of nations engaged, not only in the number of men involved
not only in the area of action covering land, sea, air and the
depths beneath the sea, but it is also the biggest in civilian
casua (ties and in mass murders. What is worse is that the
very foundations of our civilization are at stake.
We enter the new year with the manpower and resour¬
ces of our nation rapidly being transformed into an all out
war effort to win the battle of human liberty. The millions of
men who have left their homes for service with the armed
forces, and the mounting casualty lists are grim reminders
that the cost of war is blood, sweat and tears.
We shall win the war and we s hall win the peace .
I hat was the pronouncement of our President, and it is our
duty to strain every point and to make every sacrifice that
will help to attain those objectives in the shortest possible
time.
I he war has placed additional responsibilities on the
Jacksonville Jewish community and on our institution. I hou-
sands of men of our faith are stationed at Camp Bfanding and
at the Naval Air Station. We have co-operated with the
Jewish Welfare Board and other organizations to the fullest
extent in taking care of the recreational and spiritual needs
of these men. We deem it a privilege to have been able to
serve them.
The war also made us realize the urgent need of a
Y. M. H. A. in our community. During the early part of
the year, we bo ught the building adjoining the Center, and
later the lot back of it, all of which has been turned over
to the Y. M. H, A. for its use. Since our dividends are
measured largely in public service we are greatly satisfied
with the results of our efforts. When we see the number of
peoole, particularly young people, who are using the beau¬
tiful and well equipped Y. ML H. A., we feel that we have
made a very fine investment.
Jewish education is still our major problem. To increase
our educational facilities, we have engaged Mr. Samuel H.
Rosenberg, an able and experienced educator, to direct our
Hebrew and Sunday Schools. He comes well recommended
and well qualified for the work. We ask the cooperation of
all our members and parents in particular, to help Mr. Ro¬
senberg in his difficult task. J o a great extent the kind of
Jewish education we give our children, will determine what
kind of Jews they will be.
I want to thank the Rabbi and other members of the
staff, my fellow officers and directors for the splendid assist¬
ance and cooperation that they have given me durin g the
past year.
Let us enter the New Year fully resolved to carry out
the great tasks that lie before us. Let us pray that victory
for the United Nations will soon be forth coming ana that
the N ew Year will bring to all of us, peace, good health,
happiness and prosperity. Amen.

Military Maids is composed of young women in our Jewish community who
have reached the age of seventeen or ov er. 1 hese girls have banded together to
help serve the men in the Armed Forces. Each week on Thursday, a bus load of
members attends dances in Starke, and contributes to making the Starke Jewish
Welfare Board activities a success. Members also attend Saturday night dances,
beach parties, horseback riding parties, and other social activities held in Jack¬
sonville for service men. Among the outstanding committees of the organization
are the entertainment and membership comittees. The entertainment committee
helps plan the Saturday night affairs, which are held in cooperation with the other
Jewish organizations in Jacksonville. The membership committee checks on all girls
who wish to apply for membership in the organization, and sees that the following
nine rules of theclub are adhered to.
1. Attendance at twelve three hour periods of entertainment entitles a girl to a Mil¬
itary Maid pin and special recognition.
2. At least one affair a month must be attended to hold card.
3. Girls are expected to set and maintain the highest standard of conduct.
4. There shall be no drinking of aIcholic beverages before or at any function.
5. No smoking on dance floor.
6. Each girl must present membership card at all functions
7. Membership cards are not transferable.
8. Girls must be at least 17 years of age to be eligible for membership.
9. Membership cards may be revoked if rules are not followed.

BELOW IS A LIST OF THE USUAL EXCUSES GIVEN BY PARENTS FOR NOT SENDING THEIR CHILDREN
TO HEBREW SCHOOL. DO YOU KNOW THE ANSWERS?
Excuse 1: “Child is too busy.”
Answer: Time must be found for the child’s Jew¬
ish education, for it is of supreme importance to the
future happiness of the child, his family and his com¬
munity. How about the tens of thousands of Jewish
children who are enrolled in Hebrew schools? They
manage to find time. Certainly the child’s Jewish ed¬
ucation is more important than instruction in music
and other special fields. The class time of the child
can be adjusted so as not to conflict with his other
activities.
Excuse 2: “Child is too young.”
Answer: A thorough Jewish education requires at
least six or seven years of continuous study. The
child’s Jewish education should begin when his Public
School education begins. The young child is highly
impressionistic and it is important that he be under
the influence of a Hebrew school during his early
years. There are thousands of children between the
ages of six and nine in Jewish schools. The classes
for younger children meet immediately after Public
School so that the children may be home early. The
program of instruction for younger children includes
songs, stories, games and other activities which make
their learning pleasant and interesting.
Excuse 3: “Girls do not need Jewish education.”
Answer: These are modern times, when girls re¬
ceive as good a general education as do boys. Our
girls should be treated as the equals of boys also in
their Jewish education. The Jewish girl of today is the
Jewish mother of tomorrow. Upon her will depend the
kind of Jewish homes we are to • have in the future.
Hers will be the responsibility of bringing up the next
generation. She must be adequately prepared for
these tasks. Tens of thousands of Jewish girls are en¬
rolled in Hebrew schools and are benefitting greatly
from this Jewish education. Do not deprive your girl
of her due.
Excuse 4: “There is plenty of time to prepare the
child for Bar Mitzvah.”
Answer: If Bar Mitzvah is to mean something to
the boy, he should study in a good school for several
years. At least two to three years are necessary to
give the boy a minimum preparation for Bar Mitzvah,
Excuse 5: “Child is studying privately”
Answer: A well-organized school is the best place
for the child. The school has a systematic course of
study, which is taught by trained teachers and is sup¬
ervised by a principal. Private instruction is usually
inefficient and unsatisfactory. In a school, the child
makes better progress because he is studying with
other children, and is stimulated by them. The holi¬
day celebrations, religious services, singing and other
activities make the learning interesting and enjoy¬
able. He not only acquires Jewish knowledge but also
learns how to live as a Jew. The child needs the social
milieu which the school provides.
Excuse 6: “None of the child's playmates go to He¬
brew School
Answer: Child will acquire new friends in the He¬
brew school and thus widen his circle of playmates
and acquaintances.
Excuse 7: “Child is attending Sunday School.”
Answer: It is impossible to obtain a satisfactory
Jewish education by attending only once a week.
There is so much of Jewish history, religion, Hebrew
and other subjects to be studied. Many of the better
Sunday schools are now beginning to organize week¬
day classes for their pupils.
Excuse 8: “Other children dropped out.”
Answer: Very often pupils drop out because their
attendance is poor and therefore they make no prog¬
ress. Children who attend regularly and do their work
well usually continue until they are graduated.
Excuse 9: “Tuition rates are too high.”
Answer: There is no cost for the Jewish education
that your child will receive, in our Talmud Torah.
Tuition is free for children of our members and to
children whose parents cannot afford to be members
of our center.

THE SYNAGOGUE
A unique creation of Judaism is the
Synagogue, which started on its world-
mission and made the Torah the common
property of the entire people. Devised in
Exile as a substitute for the Temple, it
soon eclipsed it as a religious force and
a rallying point for the whole people,
appealing through the prayers and Scrip¬
tural lessons to the congregation as a
whole. The synagogue was limited to
no one locality, like the Temple, but
raised its banner wherever Jews settled
throughout the globe. It was thus able
to spread the truths of Judaism to the
remotest parts of the earth, and to invest
the Sabbath and Festivals with deeper
meaning by utilizing them for the in¬
struction and elevation of the people.
What did it matter, if the Temple fell
a prey too the flames for a second time,
or if the whole sacrificial cult of the
priesthood wi th all its pomp were to
cease forever? The soul of Judaism lived
indestructibly in the House of Prayer anal
learning.
K. Kohler, 1917

The life of a Jewish community depends largely on the
number and quality of its lay leaders and communal workers.
Mr. I. M. Lieberman belongs to that invaluable few in our
midst whose keen interest in Jewish affairs and whose bound¬
less devotion to Jewish causes makes them indispensable to any
community effort.
Mr. Lieberman made America his home 37 years ago when
he arrived in New York City from Brailowa, Russia. His first
public service was the organization of the Brailower Aid So¬
ciety of New York which is still in existence. At the end of one
year he settled in Hartford, Conn., where he was instrumental
in forming the first Austrian Lodge of the Independent Order
Brith Sholom, which under a modified name is still active in
that city. For reasons of health, Mr. Lieberman came to Jack¬
sonville in 1915, and immediately became active in Jewish
affairs. He was one of the organizers of the Jacksonville Brith
Sholom Lodge which played an important role until its disolu¬
tion in 1931. Mr. Lieberman then reorganized the group into
the Jewish Progressive Society of which he was president for
many years? and which did benevolent and social work.
Being an ardent Zionist, Mr. Lieberman deplored the lack
of Zionist work in Jacksonville. After a long and hard struggle
he succeeded in convincing the membership of the Progressive
Society to adopt the Zionist platform and engage in Zionist ac¬
tivities. In 1939 a seperate Zionist District was organized with
Mr. Lieberman as president, while the Progressive Society also
under his guidance, adopted the project of the Progressive
Credit Union. The latter contributes greatly to the welfare of
the small Jewish business man in Jacksonville.
During the past ten years Mr. Lieberman has been very
active as a member of the Board of Directors of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center, and is now a vice-president of the institution.
He has always been among the first to volunteer his services
when any large project was undertaken. He is at his best at
social function, banquets? concerts, and dances. He is then the
jolly, solicitious, and amiable host completely forget¬
ful of self and concerned only with the welfare of his guests.
Mr. Lieberman s ineterest extends to every worthwhile Jew¬
ish cause. He is an indefatigable worker in the annual campai¬
gns sponsored by the Jacksonville Jewish Community Council.
For this work he was last year awarded a silver plague. Mr.
Lieberman attended many Zionist and JDC conventions,
and is on the national council ol the ZOA and the JDC. He
is also vice-president of the Southeastern Zionist Region.
Being a 'regular, good fellow Mr. Lieberman has made
many friends among his Christian neighbors and business as¬
sociates. Fie has been active in civic organizations such as the
Chamber of Commerce, and the Lions Club which this year
has entrusted him with the chairmanship of the Sight Conser¬
vation and Blind Committee. In 1940 Governor Spessard Holl¬
and bestowed on him the hororary title of Lieutenant-Colonel.
On the occasion of his sixtieth birthday his family honor¬
ed him wi th an inscription in the Chaim Weitzman volume
of the JNF Golden Book. As an expression of his loyalty to
the synagogue, Mr. Lieberman has this year undertaken the
membership campaign and pledged himself to secure sixty new
members for the institution.
His sixtieth birthday finds Mr. Lieberman in the prime of
lile, and a most valuable and respected member of the com¬
munity. The members and officers of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center extend to him and his family their best wishes for many
happy years to come? and pray that he may contiune to enjoy
good health and that he may be successful in all his undertak¬
ings.
