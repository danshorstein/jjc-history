Center Youth Department
A Message from the Director
The children of the Jacksonville Jewish Center are very special young people.
Children who know that getting together is a beginning, keeping together is
progress and working together is success.
Enthusiasm, commitment, initiative and friendliness are the qualities of
leadership our children have developed. By their deeds and actions, each
encouraged a higher standard of interest and participation with all whom they
work and play. Working and playing together, has insured that our groups have a
very successful year. The children don’t often realize that they are learning and
growing as they have so much fun.
Our advisors bring experience, wisdom and knowledge to our organizations. They
teach the children to be responsible, resourceful, original and creative. They are
committed to youth groups rich in Jewish values and traditions.They work hard to
instill a sense of pride in all of our young people.
We at the Center, strive to grow along with our children. We must encourage them
continue their Jewish and community social action participation and guide them
in their efforts to become educated, open minded Jewish adults.
I look forward to helping guide our children in these important endeavors.
Thank you advisors and children alike for a fabulous year.
L’Shana Tova to you and your families.
— Gayle D. Daily s
Center Youth Commission
The Youth Commission oversees all youth organizations and programs sponsored by
the Jacksonville Jewish Center including Chalutzim, Kadima, USY, Scouts and the
Center Day Camp. The committee is made up of youth group advisors, presidents of
our clubs and caring Center members who believe that the future of Conservative
Judaism can be secured through participation in innovative programs designed to
develop upcoming Jewish leaders.
In addition to membership in Center Youth Groups, the Youth Commission
encourages our youngsters to attend United Synagogue summer programs, such as
Camp Ramah, USY - on - Wheels, Ramah Seminar in Israel and USY Poland/Israel
Pilgrimage. These provide excellent opportunities for our children to share
wholesome fun and uniquely Jewish experiences with their peers from all over the
United States. The Youth Commission assists applicants with a variety of
scholarships.
Through our College Outreach program, our college undergraduates stay connected to
the synagogue and community. Four times a year, at Rosh Hashanah, Chanukah,
Purim and Passover, care packages are sent that bring warm wishes and promote
holiday observance. Help us to stay in touch with your children by keeping us
updated on their school mailing and email addresses. Thank you to Beth Fleet and
Ellen Rosner coordinating this important work.
Gayle Dailys

Installation of Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
The sanctuary was filled on the evening of Sunday, May 15, 2005
as we installed Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner as the spiritual leader of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Rabbi Lubliner began his tenure at the Jacksonville Jewish Center last
August. His warmth, enthusiasm and broad spectrum of knowledge
immediately endeared him to the congregation. Through his manner and
considerable good humor, he has fostered a welcoming atmosphere in our
synagogue, where people can regularly learn lessons of Torah and Jewish
tradition, and find direction and faith for their lives. “To feel at home in one’s
synagogue is great,” Rabbi Lubliner observed, “To make Jewish teaching
come alive in one’s home and daily life is even better.” Rabbi Lubliner, his
wife, Susan, and their sons Avichai, Elior and Itamar, are welcome
additions to the Jacksonville Jewish Center and the community.

A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
Moving at the Speed of Sound
There is a beautiful parable told by the master homileticist, Rabbi Yaakov ben Zev Wolf Kranz
(1741-1804). Better known to the generations as the “Maggid of Dubno,”
this talented preacher once told the following story about the deeper meaning of the
Shofar we sound during the Days of Awe.
There was a village far off in the provinces where the people were quite simple and
unsophisticated. Once, one of the villagers had occasion to spend the night in the big city.
Suddenly he was awakened from his sleep by the loud beating of drums. “What does this mean?”
he asked in fright. His host replied that a fire had broken out and that the beating of drums
served as the city’s fire alarm. Nodding his head in understanding, the man returned to sleep.
When he returned to his village he excitedly reported what he had seen in the city. “They have a
most marvelous arrangement,” exclaimed the man. “Whenever there is a fire in the city they
beat their drums until the flames are extinguished. The villagers thought this was a splendid
idea, and immediately purchased drums for use in the event of a fire. Shortly thereafter, when a
fire did break out in the village, the inhabitants quickly took up their drums and pounded them so
loudly and strongly that it was a pleasure to listen to them. In the interim, however, the flames
devoured half the buildings of the village. A stranger, who happened to be visiting from the city,
laughed ironically and said to the brigade of drummers, “Why, you foolish people! Do you not
realize the impossibility of putting out a fire just by beating the drums? The instruments are
meant only to call out the fire fighters so that they may come and extinguish the flames; the
noise of the drum won’t frighten the fire away!”
This, then, is the meaning of the Shofar. It is merely a device to arouse us from lethargy, “to
awaken sleepers from their slumber,” in the words of Maimonides. The Shofar, however, cannot
bring repentance and true amelioration to our lives any more than an alarm clock can push us out
of bed and dress us for work. The value of the Shofar’s sounding is not gauged by the skill of the
one who blows it, nor is it measured in the pleasure we experience when we hear an
exceptionally long Tekiah Gedolah. Rather, its effectiveness can only be determined by what we
do with ourselves long after we have listened to its plaintive call.
If this is true of the Shofar in particular, it is equally true of the High Holy Days in general. This
season serves as an invitation to self-scrutiny, rather than as a substitute for it.
Whether or not our lives in the coming year are notably better in a spiritual sense depends
not on the three days of Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, but on what we do in the weeks and
months following the Days of Awe.
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
The Irish poet, William Butler Yeats, once wrote, “In dreams begin
responsibility.” As we prepare to welcome 5766, let us inspire one
another with the dream of a community in which each home serves as
the satellite of a Jewish Center wherein the values and teachings of
Torah always find living reality and loving resonance.
Susan and our boys, Avichai, Elior and Itamar join me in wishing
each and every one of you a year of health, blessing, and goodness.

communal soul searching, an opportune moment to articulate new visions and refine old ones for

should we address in days ahead? How can we build upon our

Center Sisterhood
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Sisterhood is a wonderful group for you to invest your
heart, energy and time in. Our Sisterhood provides many opportunities for you to grow
as a concerned Jewish woman. We have wonderful educational programs including
Sisterhood Shabbat, Women’s Minyan, Sisterhood Seminars and frequent book reviews.
Our Judaica Shop, staffed by Etta Fialkow, chair,
and many volunteers, is an ongoing source of
funding. There is a wide selection of beautiful
art, jewelry, religious articles, Judaic books,
Shabbat candles and many more interesting
objects. We are able to special order merchandise
for Bar and Bat Mitzvahs, weddings, and births. If
you have a few hours on a weekly or monthly basis,
we would love to have you on our staff. Working in
the Judaica Shop is a great way to meet new people
and make new friends.
Our fundraising projects such as baking and selling hamantaschen, Rosh Hashanah
flowers and cards, Leagrams, Nachas Tax, Donor luncheon and much more, annually
support our synagogue’s Building Fund, Education and Youth programs, as well as
provide summer camp and college travel scholarships. This year we have made a
substantial pledge to the Above and Beyond Campaign, designated for upgrading the
Center Kitchen.
The Women’s League Torah Fund Campaign supports and enhances The Jewish
Theological Seminary of America, the Ziegler School of Rabbinic Studies and the
Schechter Institute for Jewish Studies, which provide Jews worldwide with the opportunity
to study and learn. Benefactors receive a beautiful pin in appreciation of their generous
contribution. This year the pin is “Shalom - Peace,” designed by Eytan Brandes. We
were pleased to have honored two very valuable members of Sisterhood, Gayle Bailys and
Stella Finck, with the Horty T. Gendzier Award at the annual Torah Fund Luncheon.
Membership in Sisterhood continues to grow. Our luncheons and evening meetings
are well attended and feature a wide range of interesting and entertaining
programs. Sisterhood has something to offer women of all ages.
Our very own Barbara Safer received a National Award
for Community Service from Women’s League for Conservative
Judaism this year. It was a wonderful and well deserved honor.
We are very proud of Barbara for her continued devotion to
Women’s League.
This position of Sisterhood president is both challenging
and rewarding. As I begin the final year of my term, I look
forward to working with the many talented women - officers,
committee chairs, past presidents and members- who are
always willing to pitch in and make all events successful.
Barbara Safer receives award.
I would like to wish everyone, L’ Shanah Tovah on behalf of my husband, Sandy,
and daughters, Lindsay, Whitney, and Jillian.

From the Ba’al Tefilah
A famous Hassidic story tells of an illiterate shepherd boy who was brought to Yom
Kippur services at the synagogue of the Ba’al Shem Tov. Not knowing the order of the
prayers in the Mahzor ; the boy sat mute until, during the Neilah service at the close of the
holiday, he took his flute from his pocket and blew on it a loud, shrill note.
His father and the other congregants were appalled. However, the Ba’al Shem Tov
informed all present that the kavannah - the pure intention —of that note was, in fact, the
catalyst that brought the prayers of the entire congregation into the presence of the
Almighty. This story illustrates the importance of kavannah — of intention and feeling in
prayer. Rabbi Shimon ben Nathaniel says in Pirkei Avot, “... do not make your prayer a
prescribed routine but a plea for mercy and grace before the Omnipresent...” And yet, we
have a prescribed liturgy, ordained by the Rabbis. How do we account for the apparent
contradiction? We do so by praying the prescribed liturgy with feeling and intention.
The job of the Shaliach Tzibbur - the representative of the congregation — is much like
the flute of the shepherd boy — to bring the prayers of the congregation to God. It is a
daunting task. One must be inspired, while inspiring others. The Shaliach Tzibbur must
concentrate on melody, pronunciation and voice, all the while putting real feeling into them.
The Hineni prayer, recited by the Hazzan before the Musaf prayer on the High Holy Days,
expresses the emotions of the Cantor who prays for Divine help in balancing all of these
roles, and who recognizes his own inequality to the task.
I am both honored and humbled by the opportunity to serve this congregation as Ba’al
Teñllah. To stand at the bimah where I was called up as a Bar Mitzvah , and to officiate at
services, lifecycle events and community observances, is a gift for which I am thankful to
our Creator. I eagerly look forward to another year of serving God and His People. I also
look forward to working with Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner, with all of the wonderful staff of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, and to spending another year with this wonderful congregation.

From the Ritual Director
The Talmud credits Moses with the public reading of the Torah. The cycle of Torah
reading begins in the fall right after the Festivals and ends the following fall,
on Simhat Torah.
Following the Babylonian tradition, we read a parsha, portion, of Torah every
Shabbat, completing it in one year. However, on festivals and holidays,
there are special portions of the Torah that are read. These portions deviate from
the order of the yearly cycle. Instead, the subject of the portion read relates to the
particular holiday in some fashion. Rav Moshe Chayim Luzzato,
in his Derech Hashem (4:8) explains further the concept of reading the Torah.
He writes, “the reason is that the Torah consists of something that was given to us
by God to read. It was furthermore designated so that His holy light should be
transmitted to us through such reading. On certain special days, it is also appropriate
that special portions be read, relating to the concepts of those days. In this way, the
special holy light of these days is strengthened through the power of the Torah, which is
the strongest power that we have.”
I would like to thank the many members of our congregation who have added their
voices to the process that is Torah. Through their dedication and devotion, we have
maintained a high level of participation in reading Torah. The commitment and
encouragement of our synagogue organizations and their leaders will ensure that this
quality standard will be continued for many generations to come.

Scouting
Cub Scout Pack 14
Happy birthday Pack 14! With 66 years of continuous membership, Pack 14 was
recognized by the Northeast Council as the oldest pack in north Florida. We
received a framed Norman Rockwell piece with a letter of recognition. Both are
proudly hang in the Center’s Neal F. Portnoy Youth Lounge.
We started last year off with a big bang, or should I say a loud noise. At our first
pack meeting, the boys and their parents learned how to make shofars .
Camping is what Cub Scouts like best. We began our camping trips with an old
favorite, Fort Clinch, known for its history and beautiful beach. When we
camped at Manatee Springs,
the manatees were out in
force. At Ocala National Forest,
home to the Florida Black Bear,
we fished by day and looked out
for bears by night. We enjoyed
trips to Silver Springs and the
Suwannee River. The Florida
Caverns were more spectacular
then we could have imagined.
One of the most exciting
campouts of all was at the Olustee
Battlefield Historic State Park.
Smoke filled the air, as the guns
and cannons resonated all around us, while we relived the Civil War at the Battle of
Olustee. It was a thrill to camp on the battlefield with thousands of reenactment
soldiers, who lived and cooked just like back in the time period that they were
portraying.
Thank you Charlie DuBow and his dad, Michael, for arranging a Pack 14 tailgate
party at Alltell Stadium. Jaxon Deville came and played football with the boys until it
was time for our very own private tour of the stadium.
The third annual Balloon Night had its ups and downs, but despite windy conditions,
our balloons zoomed around the sky in ways that I have never seen.
The Pinewood Derby is always a highlight of the Cub Scout year. This year’s setup
was really something. The cars that the scouts made were once again just incredible.
The race was a exciting as the Daytona 500. Great job Pack 14
contestants, winners and helpers!
Our second annual tubing trip down the Ichetucknee signaled
the end of another wonderful year. Twice the number
participated in a funfilled day complete with swimming and
cookout.
At the 2005 Blue and Gold Banquet, four, 5th grade Webelos
crossed over to Boy Scouts to continue in scouting.
Thank you Elissa Vining and Missy Frisch for a fine evening
and thank you to the Jacksonville Jewish Center for your help
throughout the year. Let me be the first to say I am looking
forward to next year.
Scouting builds friendship, character and strength. Help us build Scouting.
Get involved! Join today. Call me at 262-0207, to enroll your child
in a great part of this program.
— Marty Pett

The Jacksonville Jewish Federation serves as a central resource for our Jewish community
and is dedicated to providing for the welfare of every Jewish person living in Jacksonville.
Through a partnership with the United Jewish Communities, formerly known as UJA, we are
equally committed to the survival of and quality of life for every Jew living in Israel and
throughout the Diaspora.
The five major local recipients of funds raised by the Federation are the Jewish Community
Alliance, Jewish Family and Community Services, River Garden Hebrew Home, Jacksonville
Jewish Foundation and the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School. We are proud of the invaluable
services provided by these five outstanding Jewish institutions. It is a privilege to assist them
in trying to meet their goals.
Jewish education is a priority, and the Federation is proud to support the Martin J. Gottlieb Day
School, Beit Midrash Hebrew High and the Torah Academy of Jacksonville. Through the Joan
Levin Gift of Israel program and “birthright israel,” Federation is helping to make the dream of a
trip to Israel a reality for Jewish teens and college youth in our community. All of the synagogues
in our community receive funding from the Federation campaign.
The Federation is involved with community relations, leadership development and Missions to
Israel. We are working in cooperation with our local agencies, UJC and the synagogues to
address the vital issues and challenges in the entire arena of Jewish continuity.
We are currently engaged in a Long Range Strategic Planning Process in cooperation with the
synagogues and major local agencies. One of the initial outcomes of this process is
Federation’s assuming responsibility for our community’s Hebrew High School program, Beit
Midrash.

Hadassah
“Hadassah has always been an important bridge to peace. By
treating all people without regard to nationality or religion,
Hadassah has shown the world what peace in the Middle East
will look like.” — Yitzchak Rabin, 1994.
“Thank you, Hadassah, for giving my daughters their father
back.”— Survivor of massacre at a Moshav in Israel, 2002.
Judaism teaches that it is not enough to avoid iniquity or
remain silent in the face of injustice. We must actively pursue
righteousness and justice at home, in the community, and in
the world. In the spirit of tikkun olam, Hadassah began as a
study group in 1912 by Henrietta Szold, who gathered together
like-minded women to bring public health services to pre-state
Israel. Today, more than 300,000 American women (49,000 in
Florida) help repair the world by supporting Hadassah’s
projects and programs. In Israel we save lives at our hospitals
at Ein Karem and Mount Scopus, build self-esteem and
employment skills at Hadassah Career Counseling Institute,
rescue children at risk through Youth Aliyah, and educate at
Hadassah College Jerusalem. Across America, our members
promote health education, perform community service, and
teach Jewish values. Hadassah advocates on issues ranging
from Israel-US relations to First Amendment protections to
genetic discrimination to women’s rights and health concerns.
We are mothers and daughters, sisters and friends. We are
attorneys, physicians, social workers, nurses, bankers, real
estate brokers, and teachers. Jacksonville’s 1,000 Hadassah
members proudly invite you to join our wonderful organization
and experience the joy of doing mitzvot. Please call me to
discuss which of our four groups, Shir Li, Tikva, Aaronson
Nurses’ & Health Care Professionals’ Council, or Yamit will be
able to welcome you as a new or returning member.
Jodi Seitlin, Chapter President (904) 254-2322

Special Events
Dedication of the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School
More than 500 people gathered on Sunday, January 30, at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center to celebrate a community milestone, the
naming of the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School, the cornerstone of the
Above & Beyond Endowment Campaign.
The program included a musical interlude by the Day School
Choir, as well as messages from Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner,
Rabbi Dov Kentof, Principal Willard Kennedy and others.
Bruce Horovitz, Center Executive Director, presented a plaque from
the United Synagogue of Conservative Judaism as a tribute to the
Martin J. Gottlieb School and its tradition of educational excellence. A
luncheon reception by Margo’s Catering followed.
The naming of the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School is a
fitting memorial to Marty Gottlieb, philanthropist, community leader and Day
School graduate. His parents, Mel & Debbie Gottlieb, are longtime supporters of the
Day School. Because Jewish education was also important to Marty & Shari Gottlieb,
they enrolled their own children, Hana and Noah, in
the Day School, as well.
Founded in 1961, and first known as the Foundation
School, the Solomon Schechter Day School of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center is the first step in
preparing our children for their religious role in
Jewish life as well as their role as successful and
contributing members of our society.
The school began with one first grade class. As the
need arose, more classes were added until, in 1970, the school increased to a full six-
year elementary program. By 2002, a complete Middle School program had been
added.
Today, the school has a total enrollment of more than 160 in programs and classes
ranging from Kindergarten through the Eighth Grade. The school offers an
integrated curriculum of Judaic experiences, Hebrew language and General Studies
- all aimed at creating informed, intelligent American Jews, cognizant of their
history and heritage and proud of their tradition and values.
Martin J. Gottlieb grew up
around the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. He began his
education at the Center’s
Solomon Schechter Day
School from which he
graduated in 1984. He
became a Bar Mitzvah on our
pulpit, and participated in
teen activities. This
synagogue, this school, this
community, were precious to
Marty, a sentiment he learned
from his parents, Mel &
Debbie.
The Martin J. Gottlieb Day School will continue a tradition of excellence as an
accredited Solomon Schechter School, offering an advanced Judaic Studies
curriculum, as well as a superior and fully accredited General Studies education.

Our Jewish identity is important. The JCA seeks to create a positive and affirming
environment of Jewish culture and heritage. We strive to welcome all and share our Jewish
traditions.
The mission of the JCA is to (1) contribute to the continuation of Jewish life, culture, and
community (2) provide a common meeting ground for the Jewish community (3) enhance the
quality of life for all segments of the community and (4) strengthen family life. The JCA
fulfills its mission through an integrated range of high quality programs for people of all
ages including: childcare and family programs, fitness and recreational facilities and
activities, Jewish education, culture and arts programs, and by providing a place for family,
“neighborhood,” and community gatherings.
The JCA takes pride in working with the Jacksonville Jewish Center and other local
synagogues and agencies in conducting special educational programs and celebrations.
New classes and programs are always being added to meet the diverse interests of our
members. We serve the Jacksonville Jewish community with programs like the NAEYC
accredited Gan Yeladim preschool and kindergarten, after school care for elementary age
youth, the Challenge Program, Yiddish for Fun, adult Torah study group, classical music
appreciation, pottery classes, children’s birthday parties, yoga, pilates, aerobics, Middle
School Night Out, Great Books discussion group, swim instruction, gymnastics, swim team,
tennis teams and clinics, aqua aerobics, youth, teen and men’s basketball leagues, Parent
Central program, Jacksonville Teens Volunteer program, Theatre of Youth, summer camp
accredited by the American Camping Association and so much more . . .
The JCA serves people of all ages from 6 weeks to senior adults. We invite you to be a part
of the JCA. A sliding fee scale is available to make membership affordable to all. Come by
for a tour, or take part in one of our many programs - after all, the JCA is Where You Belong!

Boy Scout Troop 14
The scouts of Troop 14 continued the scouting tradition at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center with another year of outdoor adventures.
This year the troop went on campouts to Riverbend District Camporee,
Battle of Olustee civil war reenactment, Kings Bay, Georgia Nuclear
Submarine Base and a week of camping at Boy Scouts of America’s Camp
Thunder in Georgia.
Scouts from Troop 14 also participated in BSA High Adventure Camps.
Eddie Norton and Sam Schwartz spent a week at Sea Base, sailing a 72’
schooner in the Bahamas. Andrew Vining and Kyle Vining spent three
weeks at Philmont Scout Ranch in Cimarron, New Mexico, backpacking
through the mountains. Eddie Norton, Michael Epstein, Adam Schwartz
and Sam Schwartz spent two weeks representing North Florida Boy Scout
Council at the 2005 National Boy Scout Jamboree, with 40,000 scouts from
around the world.
All the scouts made great strides in advancement this year.
Michael Epstein, son of David Epstein, Eddie Norton, son of Missy & Karl
Frisch, Kyle Vining, son of Elissa & Scott Vining and Joseph Yarish, son of
Paul Yarish, earned Boy Scouting’s highest honor, the rank of Eagle.
Troop 14 looks forward to continuing its proud tradition of providing Jewish
youth with a Boy Scouting experience. If you enjoy camping, sports and a
variety of outdoor activities, and are between the ages of 11 and 18, we
invite you to attend our meetings at the Center on Thursday nights. The
troop also welcomes participation from adults interested in providing
leadership assistance.

A Gold Seal Community
“Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long ...” It was this
commandment which inspired the founding of River Garden Hebrew Home
in 1946. It was this commandment which nourished the love and care
that for more than fifty years has made River Garden a nationally recognized
model of excellence in its field. It was this commandment that made it
necessary to create a Home that is welcoming to families and
encourages its use as an extension of family space.
River Garden residents are a part of the community, not apart from it!
The River Garden Hebrew Home has also become the anchor of a growing
system of elder services including the Home, The Coves, The Therapy Center,
Family Care Support program and Home Health Agency. The success of River
Garden is based on the tradition of meeting 100 percent of local Jewish
community needs and of serving rich and poor alike with excellence. The
River Garden Foundation accepts donations to help River Garden continue to
meet its mission. This would not be possible without substantial charitable
support from the community and the work of The River Garden Foundation to
stimulate planned gift annuities, trusts and bequests.
River Garden’s programs of short-term rehabilitative/respite care, long-term
residential care, Alzheimer’s/Adult Day Care and outpatient therapies serve
hundreds of people a day. These programs are guided by a devoted,
professional full time staff of over 230. Staff includes physicians and
beauticians, nurses, social workers, pharmacists, activities specialists,
physical occupational and speech therapists, dieticians, etc. In addition,
scores of dedicated volunteers help create a special caring community.
Located on a 35 acre Mandarin campus containing lush Florida
oak and pine trees, a private lake, meandering walking paths, River Garden is
the perfect setting for residents and visitors. River Garden is a community
where people live, love and care. It is a community of many lifestyles and of
rich Jewish content, and a community which every day strives to live up to the
mandate of the Fifth Commandment.

Simhat Torah Honorees
On Simhat Torah, we bring to a close the annual cycle of the reading of the Torah and begin anew.
Because of our perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to as Israel’s “bride”. An ancient
tradition evolved of honoring worthy men by calling them to the Torah for the last reading, Hatan
Torah, and the beginning portion, Hatan Bereshit. In our synagogue tradition, we also honor the
contributions made by an Ayshet Hayil , “Woman of Valor”.
Our Hatan Torah
William Rose is a quiet, modest man, but his presence behind the scenes at the Center
has a major impact on our congregation. The High Holidays are enhanced immeasurably by his dedicated
participation. It is William who annually puts the appropriate prayer books at every one of the 2000
seats set for the season. Like magic, the prayerbooks change from Shabbat siddur to High Holiday mahzor
and back again for each service. The Center never had a more devoted shamis.
Born in Jacksonville, William attended Hebrew school and joined youth groups
that met at the Center. He earned the rank of Eagle and is a member of the Order of the Arrow.
As a young adult, he was Assistant Scout Master for Troop 14. Upon graduation from Andrew Jackson
High School, William enlisted in the U.S. Navy and served in the Pacific during World War II.
William returned to Jacksonville to work in the family grocery business. In 1951, he married Betty Sager
and soon had two daughters. After a long and successful career in wholesale foods, William retired and
began working harder than ever volunteering for a myriad of tasks at the Center, as well as providing
transportation for the elderly and disabled for the Jewish Healing Network.
Mazel tov to William and Betty, daughters, Margaret and Allison,
granddaughter, Rachel, and all the members of his loving family.
Our Hatan Bereshit
Jack Mizrahi continues the long tradition of synagogue leadership and devotion to Jewish education
that was begun by his grandfather, Salim Mizrahi. The Sefardic Torah in our aron kodesh bears the family
name, a testament to their unswerving loyalty to Judaism and the Jacksonville Jewish Center. A sterling
example, Jack has a deep love of Hebrew learning, a dedication to religious practice, well developed
pulpit skills and solid administrative abilities. His strong moral compass directs his participation in a
variety of roles to the betterment of the congregation.
From Kindergarten through Confirmation, Jack earned honors from Center schools. A youth leader, he
was USY chapter president, SE Region USY vice president, went on Israel Pilgrimage and received the
SE Region Scholarship Award. As a Boy Scout of Troop 14, Jack received the Ner Tamid Award.
A graduate of Wolfson High, Jack attended the University of Pennsylvania, where he received a degree
in Economics from the Wharton School, He worked his way up the ladder in his family’s
ladies apparel business, and now is president of his own Ridgefield Corporation.
Jack has always found time for his family and of course Jewish education. He has taught Sunday School
and Beit Midrash, served on the Center Board and committees, has been a Minyan Captain
since 1987 and worked tirelessly on many community advisory committees.
Mazel tov to Jack & Nancy, their children Sara, Dena, Jonathan and Debra, and the entire Mizrahi family.
Our Woman of Valor
Jeanine Rogozinski is a powerhouse of energy, enthusiasm and determination. Blessed
with beauty and brains, she makes every endeavor a complete success - and in high heels. “No” is not in
her vocabulary. She answers the call to volunteer with “when can I start.” Enlisting the aid of others is
easy for Jeanine. Her ready smile and upbeat personality make it impossible to say “no” to her. Best of
all, she applies all of her talents in service to the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Add to the list loving wife
and mother, devoted family member, synagogue and civic leader and you have our Woman of Valor.
Born in Dallas, Texas, Jeanine has taken up the challenge of leadership set by her parents in their own
congregation. She is immediate past president of Center Sisterhood, served as VP of Ways & Means and
Programming, and chaired numerous committees and events. She received the Women’s League
Leadership Award. A 10-year Center Board member, she currently sits on the Executive Committee.
With a degree in marketing, Jeanine really shines when given a fund raising project to direct.
From the Day School PTAs American Flag pin program, to the 2001 Center Raffle for Education,
Jeanine has helped raise thousands of dollars for local and national charities and for Center schools
Mazel tov to Jeanine & Chaim and their children, Rachel, Erin, Jonathan and Danielle.

Daily Minyan Captains
Traditionally, public worship requires a minyan , a quorum
of ten men. Only in the presence of a minyan may the Torah be
read or the Kaddish prayer be recited.
Our dedicated minyan captains ensure that there is a minyan
twice every single day of the year. It is often a hard
and thankless job. It requires hours of phoning to
coordinate attendance.

Marco Rand, Chairman
Our new rabbi is completing his first year here in
Jacksonville, and the Religious Committee is proud to
continue working with him to enhance our synagogue’s
religious life. Rabbi Lubliner has been an

Executive Director’s Message
The walls are vibrating at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. If you listen
closely. . . what you hear will be truly inspirational.
Pause for a moment and you will hear the sound of Talmud being taught on a
Monday evening. You will hear the echo of men and women davening twice
daily in the Witten Chapel. You will hear the sounds of children planning a
mitzvah project, actively engaging in the act of Tikkun 01am.
You will hear the sounds of Jewish music.
You will hear a young teen preparing for his or her Bar or Bat Mitzvah.
You will hear congregational leaders planning for the future.
Walk through our building and at any given moment, you will hear the sounds
of Jewish life. They are like no other. You will hear the sounds of compassion,
the bursts of laughter, the outreach to others and on occasion
that “still, small voice.”
Bruce Horovitz
Executive Director

Center. Don’t take them for granted. Rather, take an opportunity and come hear

your voice one that vibrates in the walls of our facility.

Jacksonville Jewish Center. May our walls continue to vibrate in the year to
come with many new sounds and many new voices.
Edith, Sam and Max join me in wishing
you a happy and healthy new year.

A Message from the Religious School Director
In 2004, the Jacksonville Jewish Center Religious School was designated a
“Framework for Excellence” Model 1 school by the United Synagogue of
Conservative Judaism, in recognition of exceptional academic quality.
To maintain our high standards, we continually enhance our curriculum and
programs. This year, we have focused our attention on our Hebrew
curriculum in the younger grades.
Consecration marks the beginning of a Jewish child’s formal
Hebrew education. Until now, the initiation for Religious school
students began in grade 3. Therefore, Religious School students celebrated
Consecration in 3rd grade at ages 9 and 10, along with Day School
students in 1st grade, who are ages 6 and 7.
Under the Framework for Excellence curriculum, we now begin teaching Hebrew
language and prayer in our Sunday School at a younger age,
beginning with reading readiness skills in Kindergarten. Specific skills needed for
the Consecration service will also be incorporated.
Lois Tompkins
Religious School Director
We are proud to announce that beginning this year, all students of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center will be ready to celebrate Consecration in the
1st grade. To complete the transition, students entering grades 2 and 3 in the
upcoming school term 2005-06 will also be eligible for this year’s celebration.
Youth Congregations
Meet Saturdays, 11a.m.-Noon
The restructured curriculum has many other benefits for our students.
It allows us to parallel levels a little more closely, so students who enter the
Religious School from a Jewish Day School will be able to fit into our program more
comfortably. Supplemental courses for more advanced students
will still be offered in each grade level of the afternoon school,
ensuring that all of our children are challenged and continuing to advance each
year to the best of their ability.
Pre-Junior Congregation
Kindergarten-2nd Grade
Led by Karen Morse
Junior Congregation
Grades 3-6

learning, progress at any level of Hebrew, while still enjoying the company of their
age-group, in a fun and stimulating Judaic program. Our dedicated teachers
challenge and nurture the students. Sunday Family Education events and regular
weekday assemblies establish a family connection, teaching the importance of
community, Torah and prayer. Frequent
visits from Rabbi Lubliner and other
synagogue professionals add to the
enjoyment of being a valued part of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.
The goal of our program is to instill a love
and understanding of our rich cultural

life-long Jewish learning and living.
— Lois Tompkins

Hevra Kaddisha
The Hevra Kaddisha is one of the oldest and most respected Jewish institutions.
It’s duties were so important that in early times it was one of the first
organizations formed by a new Jewish community.
This is true of the Jacksonville Hevra Kaddisha, an independent society that
serves the entire Jewish community. Dedicated to preparing the deceased with
respect and dignity, our members are some of the most dedicated and sincere
individuals in our community. They seek no reward, only the satisfaction of
performing a mitzvah that requires no “thank you.”
The Hevra Kaddisha is self-supporting and has grown in strength over the years
making it possible to serve all in need, regardless of financial means.
Members are available 24 hours a day to offer our services and comfort. One
need only contact any of our officers for assistance.
The respect, love and consideration that is afforded
individuals while living must also be given to the deceased.

President’s Message
It is hard to believe that a year has passed since I became the president
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. This has been an exciting and
fulfilling year for me and, I hope, for the congregation.
The dedication of our entire Center staff has enabled us to not only maintain
our busy schedule of operations, but to expand them with new programs.
I cannot express how grateful I am to Bruce Horovitz and the administrative
staff and to Richard Ford and the maintenance staff. Bruce has announced that
he is leaving his professional capacity at the Center to join his dad in business,
and we wish him well. I have selected a search committee to find a new
Executive Director with the goal of finding the very best person to continue
building on the great foundation that Bruce has given us.
Last year we began an historic, new campaign to increase our endowed funds
from approximately $3,500,000 to $10,000,000. This was initialized by the
very generous contribution from the Gottlieb Families through the naming of
the Solomon Schechter Day School in memory of Martin J. Gottlieb. This year,
our plan is to complete the Above and Beyond fundraising campaign by the end
of December. The names of the new contributors, as well as those contributors
who have increased their overall contributions to the Center, will be included
on an enhanced dedication wall. We will also have a special weekend in the
spring to thank the contributors and to dedicate the new wall.
Even though the year has gone by rapidly and very successfully, we still have
some areas to address. Planning for our future involves ideas, proposals for
programs, correcting problems, and monetary considerations. We have begun
the process to search for a second Rabbi. This will take time and funds and we
need the help of all of our congregants to make this work. Please feel free to
contact me at any time with your ideas, thoughts, or complaints.
As I said last year, we must focus on membership. Every member of the
congregation is on the membership committee, officially chaired Shirley
Bielski and Talie Zaifert. Please give our congregation a little bit of your time,
so that we can increase our membership and also provide the programs needed
by all of the members. We have the facilities, and we have the professional
staff necessary. Now we need our congregants to come forward with the ideas
and programs that you would like to become a reality.
Seeman Zimmerman
President
In closing, I must say that I have enjoyed the past year as
president and I look forward to the coming year with confidence
and enthusiasm. I want to thank my wife, Nancy, for being so
supportive, along with the rest of my family, relatives, friends,
and, most of all, our congregation. Your encouragement has
made the job much easier. I want to thank Bruce Horovitz for all
of his help and direction, and Rabbi Lubliner for his advice and
involvement in all aspects of the synagogue.
L’Shana Tova Tiketavu,
Seeman Zimmerman

Committees and Organizations
Center Men’s Club
“Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life”
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Men’s Club is dedicated to the enrichment of
synagogue life and the Jacksonville Jewish community. The Men’s Club provides an
opportunity for Center men to work together supporting, sponsoring, and participating
in programs that bring meaning to our slogan, “Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life”.
Our Men’s Club is an active member of the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs,
an international organization dedicated to the ideals, philosophy and traditions of
Conservative Judaism. Through the support of the FJMC, we distribute hundreds of
Yellow Holocaust Memorial candles every Yom HaShoah to our congregation’s families.
This year we will again offer the successful “Hearing Men’s Voices” program, a series
of workshops dedicated to creating a forum for discussion and sharing of issues
relating to Jewish men in today’s world. Thought provoking workshops covering
family, religion, society and health will be led by experts and professionals.
We are committed to supporting Center youth with programs like the Yad Shel
Chai (Hand of Life) award that recognizes and rewards our youth for reading Torah.
We also provide gifts for the Confirmation students, and present a Kiddush cup
to all Bar and Bat Mitzvah. We support Center youth organizations by donating our
time at conventions, as well as financial resources.
Center Men’s Club has established the Horty T. Gendzier Summer
Scholarship Fund, which provides the opportunity for children to attend Camp Ramah
or other Jewish enrichment programs and honors the memory of Horty T. Gendzier,
who was so instrumental in the pioneering of these programs. Our annual Men’s Club
Man of the Year banquet provides the greatest contribution to the Fund, along with a
portion of membership dues. This annual “roast and toast” event, which is full of fun
and surprises, has quickly become a much anticipated part of our schedule of activities.
We are visible friends of our Center. We sell and build sukkot for our Center
families and help to decorate and maintain the Center’s Goldman Sukkah. You can
find Men’s Club members at the Center’s annual Family Picnic, grilling and serving
refreshments. We are the ushers who greet you at morning services every Shabbat and
holiday. Men’s Club members sponsor, lead and participate in Men’s Club Shabbat
and Armed Forces Shabbat, as well as co-sponsor Adult Education Sunday Seminars.
Over the years, many of our programs have earned Center Men’s Club Torch
Awards from the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs and we are actively developing
award-worthy programs for community service and other activities.
The Men’s Club is a great way for friends to have fun. We have gone on deep
sea fishing trips and had family outings to the Jacksonville Suns baseball and
Barracudas ice hockey games. In the fall, we “kick off” our new year with our annual
Membership Party, featuring lunch and raffles, while we watch the Jacksonville
Jaguars play an away game on the BIG screen TV. Our recent pick-up softball games
have been a huge success, and we will keep on playing.
All this and more is waiting you if you are at least 18 years old, male, and a
member of the Jacksonville Jewish community. Join now, we need your help and
appreciate your support!
— Dave Schmerer

We grieve for the thousands who lost their lives or loved ones in Hurricane
Katrina’s tragic wake. May God, who ever remains close to the broken-hearted,
enfold, protect and comfort those who survived the maelstrom only to face an
uncertain future.
In the spirit of these Days of Awe, as we contemplate the frailty of human
existence, let us also find strength in the imperishable strength of the Divine
Image implanted in each of us. In the face of our nation’s great suffering, the
words of the Haftorah for the Day of Atonement challenge us to heed the moral
imperative of helping God’s despairing children:
Share your bread with the hungry, take the homeless into your home.
Clothe the naked when you see him, do not turn away from those in need.
Then cleansing light shall break forth like the dawn, and your wounds shall
soon be healed.
And you shall rebuild ancient ruins, restoring old foundations laid long ago.
You shall be known as the rebuilder of broken walls, the restorer of dwelling
places . —Isaiah, 58:7,10,12
