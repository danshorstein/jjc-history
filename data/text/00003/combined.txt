Troop 14 Basketball Champions
During the basket ball season Troop 14 entered a team in the Boy Scout
Basket Ball League. Under the captaincy of William Katz Troop 14 went through
the season undefeated, winning the championship of the League with ease. They
scored 2 victories over Troop 41, by scores of 42 to 2, and 33 to 5; 2 victories
over Troop 19 by scores of 19 to 1 and 12 to 5; 2 victories over Troop 6 by scores
of 26 to 24 and 10 to 6. They also played one outside game with the A. Z. A.
Fraternity and won that game by the score of 17 to 16. In winning all of their
games during the season, credit must be given to every member on the tear* as
each one played his hardest and above all they played together.
The members on the team were: Irvin Surasky, Capt. William Katz, Simon
Rothstein, Melvin M. Margo], Melvin O, Margo), Meyer Leibovitz, Norman
Moscovitz and Paul Weiss.

JUNIOR DAUGHTERS OF ISRAEL
The Junior Daughters of Israel was organized 2 years ago under the auspices
of Mrs. M. D. Margolis with Edith Wolf son as first president. Its membership
consists of young Jewish women between the ages of 18 and 23 years. The aim of
the organization is to help the Jacksonville Jewish Center in its various acti¬
vities, social, religious, and cultural. One of its most successful undertakings last
year was the forward and backward dances held regularly every Saturday night
from 10 o’clock P. M. till 1 O’clock A. M. at the Center Auditorium. These dances
brought together the majority of the younger set in our community in a wholseome
atmosphere. Members of the Daughters of Israel served as patrons at these af¬
fairs.
With the proceeds of these dances, the Juniors donated a new wedding canopy
to the Center which was formally presented by the Junior Daughters. The organi¬
zation also donated one dozen Friday night service Prayer Books to the Center.
Many of its members are also active Sunday School teachers. The Juniors will
continue their practise of holding basketball dances after out-of-town games.
Meetings are held every first and third Monday evenings of the month. All
eligible girls are invited to join the Junior Daughters of Israel.

THE CENTER SYNAGOGUE
The Jacksonville Jewish Center was built in 1927
under the leadership of Rabbi Samuel Benjamin, and
Mr. Harry Finkelstein, its first president. The building
contains three parts; the mam entrance leads into the
auditorium; from a vestibule in front of the auditorium
stairs lead to the Center synagogue, which is above the
auditorium. In the rear, is a three story building contain"
ing class rooms, meeting rooms, a prayer room, the Rabbi’s
study and a library. Last year the whole building was
completely redecorated.
The auditorium has a well equipped stage for con"
certs and plays with two sets of scenery. On one side
an opening leads into a well fitted out kitchen, and on
the other side, a door leads into the Center office. Re"
productions of biblical paintings decorate the walls, and
rust colored damask drapes adorn the large windows. The
auditorium is equipped with several hundred comfortable
heavy chairs, and possesses a fine dance floor.
The main synagogue has large windows of frosted
panes. One pane on each window is of stained glass and
contains an inscription of the donor. The platform, pub
pit and Ark are made of dark walnut with gold decor"
ations. The synagogue is provided with five hundred in"
dividual cushioned seats made of light walnut. In the
rear of the synagogue a balcony also contains 154 com"
iortable seats. The floors are covered with a heavy coat
of bakelite varnish, and theaisles are carpeted.
The classrooms are well equipped with modern indi"
vidual desks, blackboards, and all are well ventilated.
Services are conducted daily morning and evening
in the prayer room. On the High Holy days an overflow
service is held in the auditorium, where men and women
are separated according to orthodox requirements. In the
main synagogue, services are of the conservative type.
The traditional paryer book is used, the Hebrew being
supplemented by English prayers, and congregational sing"
ing. Sermons are delivered by the rabbi in Engish. Mixed
pews are permitted.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is affiliated with
the United Synagogue of America, and generally calls its
spiritual leaders from among the graduates of The Jewish
Theological Seminary of America.

Center Institute of Jewish Study
Last year Rabbi M. D. Margolis or¬
ganized the Center Institute of Jewish
Studies with the aim of presenting sev¬
eral courses in Judaism to adult groups.
As a first year experiment the Institute
made a fair beginning. Classes were
conducted every Tuesday evening for a
period of four months from November
to the middle of March. The attendance
varied between 10 to 20 pupils at every
session. Classes were conducted in Jew¬
ish History, using “A History of the
Jews/’ by Paul Goodman as the text.
Rabbi Margolis presented also a series
of lectures on Customs and Ceremonies,
discussing the various customs and rit¬
uals connected with birth, Bar Mitzvah,
and Wedding Ceremonies. Interesting
discussions followed each lecture in
which all the pupils participated. New
courses are being planned for the coming
year and a large enrollment is expected
for the Institute.
One of the memorable occasions in
conection with the Institute of Jewish
Studies, was the celebration of Hanuk-
kah in the home of Mr. and Mrs. Henry
Rosenvaig, which was enjoyed by a fes¬
tive gathering of pupils and their friends.
Among those who attended the courses
of the Institute were the following:

the month Adar. As a reminder of this duty the passage from Exodus xxx, 11-16,
was read on the Sabbath preceding.

Junior Congregation of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
The Center Junior Congregation was organized two years ago by Rabbi Mar-
golis and was under the supervision of Mrs. Margolis whose guidance assured
its success. The Junior Congregation holds services every Saturday morning, using
the traditional prayer book.
The service consisting of English and Hebrew readings has been arranged by
Rabbi Margolis with emphasis on the important parts of the Sabbath Morning
ritual and Congregational singing.
A secton of the weekly portion of the law is read by three children from the
English Bible, which is usually supplemented by a story told by the instructor
m charge. The congregation elects officers every six months, including a cantor,
a shames, a president, vice-president, secretary and treasurer. On the occasion of
certain holidays, the juniors hold receptions after their services, with the assistance
of the mothers. When a Bar Mitzvah ceremony takes place in the adult congre¬
gation, the juniors go to the main Synagogue for the Bar Mitzvah rites and the
concluding part of the service. ( Musaf)
The number of children attending is around forty. During Passover week
services were held on the first and last two days ,and the congregation enjoyed
an attendance of about sixty children. This year the children are being prepared
again to conduct their own services during Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER CHOIR
The Center Choir was organized by Rabbi Margolis 2 years ago and disting¬
uished itself in many ways. The choir consists of two sections, the Men’s Choir who
sing during the High Holydays, and the Mixed Choir who sing during the year at
the late Friday night services. The Men’s Choir made a fine showing on Rosh
Hashonah and Yom Kippur ,leading the congregational singing, and rendering
well a number of compositions.

several special Sabbath services. Their singing was especially impressive at the
joint service of the Center and the Temple, held in March.
An invitation is hereby extended to all who like to sing to join the Center Choir.

F. T. E. CLUB

organized five years ago at the Jacksonville Jewish Center for social and charitable
purposes. During the past year, the Club experienced the most active and profit¬
able season since its organization. Many enjoyable affairs were given includnig
its annual fall and spring dances held in November and February respectively.
In honor of its June and. mid-year graduates, a houseparty was held* at the Beach.
The Club has been foremost in aiding the Jewish Center and other charitable
organizations of the city.
Various affairs are geing planned for the fall and winter seasons of 1936.
which inculde the annual dance held after the Florida-Georgia football game in
November, and the Anniversary celebration which wil be held in November.

ROTHCHILD YOUNG JUDEAN CLUB
The Rothschild Young Judean Club, a group of boys and girls between the
ages of 15 and 18 years of age, are lead by Miss Edith Weiss.
The R. Y. J. C. has been doing fine work throughout the past year. It has
contributed to the Boy Scout movement in Palestine, the Jewish National Fund, and
has studied current Jewish problems with special emphasis on the events taking
place in Palestine and with reference to the Jewish Youth.
The R. Y. J. C. sponsored a side-show at the Purim carnival conducted by the
children of the Center Hebrew School. During April, set aside as Young Judea
month, the club was honored at a special Friday night service held at the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center, with members of the club participating.
With various outings, cultural meetings and Floridian tours, the R. Y. J. C.
enjoyed a pleasant and interesting season.
The club plans to enter the National Judea Play Contest, by presenting a series
of short plays sometime during the month of November.
The club has secured a permanent club room at the Center, in which they will
have a book case, containing books which will circulate among the members.
The club issued a newspaper, “THE R. Y. J. C. JOURNAL’’ which contained
club news, special features, editorials, and comments on club members. The next
edition will be issued at the time of the presentation of the plays.

JEWISH WELFARE SOCIETY
In 1918 several small charitable organizations amalgam¬
ated under the name of tne United fiebre w Charities. The
aim of the Society was to help constructively the neeay
people in the community, both local and transient. Later,
the name of the organization was changed to the Jewish
Welfare Society.
The founder and first President was the former Julius
Hirschberg who, with a Board of loyal workers, collected
funds and took care of those needing relief and advice.
In 1924 the Society became a member of the Jacksonville
Community Chest and has been financed by that efficient
organization ever since. The budget for 1935 was $3,875.88
of which amount the Chest gave 3,352.14.
No matter what the need among the Jewish people of
Duval County the Jewish Welfare Society is ready to serve,
'through the wide range of its service the Society strives
toward rehabilitation both of the individual and the family
and the cohesion of the home.
The president, David D’avis, has been in office contin¬
uously since 1922. His has been a work of devotion and
untiring effort for the benefit of the organization. Mr. Morton
R. Harschberg, son of the founder, is 1st Vice Presidnt
and takes an active interest in the welfare of the agency.
Mi. Joseph. H. Glickstein, the Treasurer, has efficiently
served the Society for many years.
Aside from the splendid cooperation of the Officers
and Board of Directors there are several groups who de¬
serve special mention for without these the great work
or tne agency could not have been accomplished. Special
praise is clue: those who contributed money, food and cloth¬
ing; physicians, specialsts, dentists, and Independent Drug
Company, who took care of the medical needs of our clients;
all Social Agencies and Institutions in the City, County and
State, the Needle Work Guild for many garments, and the
attorneys who have rendered legal services gratis for many
years. Special thanks are due Kabbis Kaplan and Margolis
and the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society for their
helpful cooperation.
Last year the society rendered relief in various forms
to 273 cases, which presented many problems, such as un¬
employment, insufficient income, illness, Transients, etc. 255
meals were given, 146 lodgings, weekly rents were paid to 83»,
and material weekly relief to 35, clothing was given to 79
and cash relief to 73, drugs were supplied to 35, hospitaliza¬
tion to4, medical care by private physican was given to 20,
transportation was supplied to 10. Other services rendered
by the organization include among other things assistance to
immigrants, Passover supplies to indigent families and pris¬
oners, dental and optical care.
The most distressing phases of the work were lack of
employment facilities, lack of facilities for the care of men¬
tal diseases and lack of hospitalization for the chronically
ill and imfrm persons.

THE DAUGHTERS OF ISRAEL
The Daughters of Israel serve in the capacity of a sisterhood to the Jack¬
sonville Jewish Center, aiding the congregation financially and assisting in all
Center activities. The main purpose of the Daughters of Israel, however, is the
maintenance of the daily Hebrew school and Sunday School of the Center. By
providing every Jewish child with the opportunity to receive a Jewish religious
education, the Daughters of Israel is an important factor in the preservation and
spiritual growth of the local Jewish community.
Last year at the end of the season the Daughters of Israel gave the Center
a check for $1500.00 which helped materially in the refinancing of the Center
Building. Besides this sum of money the Daughters of Israel also contributed a
large gas stove, which considerably increased the facilities of the enlarged kitchen.
Members of the Daughters of Israel catered the delicious Purim S'oodah. A number
of current books were also donated to the Center library.
! ►
MRS. JOSEPH HACKEE, President
The outstanding events of the year were the Thanksgiving Dance with Mrs.
Sam Datz and Mrs. A. E. Selber as chairmen; The New Year’s Dinner Dance,
under the capable management of Mrs. Ben Chepnik, Ethel Joel and Mrs. A. Sager;
The Daughter’s of Israel Sabbath, The Children’s Seder, with Mrs. T. M. Schneider
as chairman and the Closing Luncheon and Installation of officers.
The Daughters of Israel also sponsored through its religious committee, under
the chairmenship of Mrs. Oscar Magezis and Mrs. Max Rubin, all the entertain¬
ments and celebrations of the Hebrew School and Sunday School, such as Simchas
Torah, Chanukah, and Purim, entertainments. The teachers of the Sunday School
were presented with gifts at the end of the school year. All brides were presented
with Jewish Cook Books. Confirmands were presented with Bibles and prayer books.
The Daughters of Israel are looking forward to a successful year of activities
under the capable leadership of Mrs. Joseph Hackel who is beginning her third
term of service.
A list of the outstanding events for the year 1936-37 is to be found in the He-
brew-English calendar included in this year book.

YOUNG MEN'S HEBREW ASSOCIATION

without Jewish residents the building has lost its usefulness. All attempts in recent
years to revive the Y. M. H. A. have failed. Plans are under way to sell the

JUNIOR HADASSAH
The Junior Hadassah is the Young Women’s Zionist organization of America,
organized for the purpose of mobilizing the forces of American young Jewish
womanhood in the heoric effort of our people to effect a solution of our common
Jewish problem. We accept the premise that full-expression for our people is
difficult, if not impossible, outside of Palestine. We therefore look for a solution
of our economic, cultural and social problems to practical translation of Zionism
as embodied in the Basle program: “Zionism strives to create for the Jewish people
a publicly recognized, legally secured home in Palestine.”
Junior Hadassah’s Problem is Two=Fold:
1. Practical efforts in Palestine through the support of the model home and
school for children in Meier Shfeyah; its financal and administrative support of
the Nurses’ Training School in Jerusalem; the operation of a citrus grove at Pardess
Anna; and participation in the work of the Jewish National Fund.
2. A cultural program aimed at making Jewish life in America self conscious
and creative; to spread a knowledge of Zionism among the Jewish young women
of America so that they will be aware of the new life being created in Palestine,
hnd of its relation to Jewish life here.
In this two-fold program, there is room for every Jewish young woman in
America.
The year 5696 was for the local chapter one of the most successful years, both
socially and financially. The year was ushered in by the first annual Yom Kippur
dance held in the Center which proved to be a most successful event. The Regional
Conference which will long be remembered was one of the outstanding gatherings
bf southern Hadassah Members. Both the Senior and Junior chapters combined
forces and filled every minute of the proceedings with interest and enthusiasm.
Another important event was the visit of Miss Hannah Goldberg of Boston, national
Vice president, who inspired a large audience at the Center.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER CEMETERY ASSOCIATION
(Chevrah Kadishah)
The Jacksonville Center Cemetery Association is managed oy a committee of
five men elected for life, which supervises and directs all burials taking place in
the Center Cemetery, which forms part of the Evergreen Cemetery.
Last year the Center purchased the Cemetery from the Evergreen Association
and is now sole owner of the property. This change has made it'possible to make
many improvements. The addition of Mr. S’. Selber as a member of the committee
in place of Mr. D. Davis who resigned, was indeed fortunate. Mr. Selber took
charge of the difficult task of improving the grounds of the much neglected prop¬
erty. After six months of hard work Mr. Selber has transformed the Cemetery
into a beautiful park. Some of the work done by Mr. Selber during that short
period of time is as follows: He laid out and installed 800 feet of lineal water
pipe giving many convenient outlets to reach every lot in the Cemetery. City
water was brought in through these pipes. Sixty loads of black muck were de¬
posited on barren grounds. Thousands of bunches of St. Augustine grass were
planted so that every bare spot was covered. Walks were cut out around each lot
and single grave sections. Beautiful shubbery was planted in many advantageous
spots. The roads were improved. Every member of the Center who visited the
Cemetery in recent months, has expressed admiration and gratitude to Mr. Selber
for his splendid achievement. The proverbial neglect of the Center Cemetery is now
a thing of the past. We may well be proud of our beautiful cemetery.
These improvements were financed by a nominal charge of $10.00 per anum,
made to every lot owner.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH COMMUNITY COUNCIL
The Jacksonville Jewish Community Council was organized at a mass meeting
held at the Jewish Temple on Mjay 15, 1935, which was addressed by Edward M.
Cohn of Atlanta, Ga., Southern Field Kep. of the National Council of Jewish
Federations and Welfare Funds. After discussing the purpose of the organization
representatives of all local Jewish groups voted in favor of the council and be¬
came members of the new organization. While the aims of the Council are mani¬
fold it was decided that it should concentrate all its efforts at first to unite all
fnd raising campaigns, for national and overseas charities, into one welfare fund,
sponsored by the council.
During the summer of 1935 Rabbi M. D. Margolis who was elected secretary,
communicated with all the charitable agencies that had conducted fund raising
campaigns in Jacksonville, during the preceding three years. By December 1935
eighteen organizations had given consent not to solicit funds in Jacksonville, but
to accept an annual allocation from the Councils welfare fund. The Joint Distri¬
bution Committee and the United Palestine also agreed to join the united campaign.
Under the chairmanships of Rabbi I. L. Kaplan and Rabbi M. D. Margolis the
Welfare Fund Campaign was conducted during the months of February and March,
yielding about thirty five hundred dollars in pledges. On March 26th at a meeting
of Representatives of all local Jewish factions allocations were made to thirty
nine national and overseas charitable agencies. On September 1st, 1936 the books
of the organization showed that the Welfare Fund had received in cash $3,232.69,
and distributed $3,059.92., about $300.00 is still to be collected from pledges .Two
hundred and sixty five doners contributed to this fund. The minimum membership
fee in the Welfare Fund is set at $10.00, although smaller contributions were
also accepted. Judging from the fine response of the Community to this campaign,
which suffered from inexperience, and lack of workers, it is to be expected that
the Jacksonville Jewish Community Council wll become next year a much stronger
and better organization, which will unite all Jewish people of the community in
carrying out its worthy objectives.

concluding part of the service. ( Musaf)

Th e end of the year 5696 finds the Jewish people steeped in despair. Those of us
who read the Jewish Press and keep abreast of world events as they affect the Jewish
people, are overcome by a sense of hopelessness. The list of countries where Jewish
life has become difficlut or unberable has increased during the past year. The unrest
in the Holy Land and the threatened danger to Jewish immigration has had an espec¬
ially depressing influence.
Into this dark picture the Jewish New Year should bring to the Jew a note of hope
and courage. Rosh Hashonah, according to Jewish tradition celebrates the "Harath
Olam", the day of the world's coming into being, or the dawn of human history. If we
want to get a proper perspective of ourselves and regain our courage and faith, we,
the Am Olan (eternal people) must see our present troubles from the point of view
of eternity, God's yardstick of time. Measured by the four thousand years of Jewish
history, our present day troubles assume an entirely difficult aspect. Indeed we are
living today in the most glorious period of Jewish history, when the visions and dreams
of our prophets and sages become realities before our very eyes.
We are not as helpless as some may think and our plight is not as desperate as it
appears. The present Palestine situation, finds its counterpart in a similar situation over
two thousand years ago, when our people returning from Babylonian exile and attempt¬
ing to rebuild their national home met with treachery and sabotage "And the builders
were building while each man had a sword girded about his loins". Let us realize that this
generation was given the opportunity to effect the greatest miracle in the annals of
human history, the restoration of a people to its land from which it was banished for
nearly twenty centuries. Let us realize that we are fortunate to be charged with the
task of carrying out the greatest act of justice for a long-suffering and oppressed peo¬
ple, our people !
Let the sound of the Shofar this New Y ear, rally every Jew about the banner of
Israel and evoke in us greater loyalty, courage and zeal to carry on our struggle with the
forces of darkness and bigotry, and for the restoration of Israel to his ancient homeland.
May the year 5697 bring blessings of health, prosperity, peace and good will to
the Jewish communities and all the citizens of this great land of liberty.
Rabbi Morris David Margolis

1) Shabbat Hahodesh derives its name from the Haftorah of that day, Exodus
xii, 1-20. This is the “portion about the month,” referring to the special conse¬

2) Shabbat Hagadol always precedes Passover. It derives its name from the
importance of the approaching festival and from the further fact that the Haf¬

led bread. The celebration of the Passover with the beautiful ceremony of the S'eder
on the first and second evenings, has helped to foster in the Jewish people the
hope of living again as a nation in Palestine.

JEWISH PROGRESSIVE SOCIETY
The Jewish Progressive Soociety was organized under the charter laws of
Florida on the first of January, 193.2, with twenty-six members, and has steadily
grown in membership until it now numbers over one hundred.
Mr. I. M. Lieberman was the founder of this society which was built upon the
remnants ot the disbanded BTith Sholom Lodge. The organization during the past
four years had only fraternal and charitable aims, however last year, the Progress¬
ive Society took a step forward by accepting the Zionist ideology by affiliating itself
with the Zionist Organization of America. Two dollars of the $5.00 dues per member
is sent annually to the Zionist Organization of America. The remainder is used for
local and national charities.
During the past year 14 members and non-members in financial distress, were
assisted by the organization. The cociety also sent donations to the Hias, The
Denver Institutions, Atlanta Orphanage, etc. The Progressive Society is a member
of the Jacksonville Jewish Community Council and participated actively in its
welfare fund campaign.
Among the outstanding events of the society during the past year were a
number of interesting speakers, including District Attorney Harel, and a debate
in which the members of the society participated, and the annual banquet and
installation of officers, an enjoyable affair.
With the enlarged aims of the society the organization looks forward to an
increase in membershipp from among local Zionists and a year of interesting
activities.

CENTER HEBREW SCHOOL AND SUNDAY SCHOOL
The Jacksonville Jewish Center maintains one of the best equipped modern
Talmud Torahs in the Southeast. The curriculum provides for a complete six
year course which gives its pupils a thorough training in reading and writing of
modern Hebrew, and the Heorew Bible, prepares them to participate intelligently
in the traditional religious services, and imbues them with loyalty to the aspirations
and ideals of the Jewish people, and to Palestine as the symbol of the regeneration
of our race and its culture. Yiddish is also taught to the older pupils in whose
homes Yiddish is still spoken.
The number of children attending the Tlmud Torah is about 80. Transpor¬
tation is provided for children residing in the Riversde section of the town. About
40 per cent of the chldren receive free tuition because of the inability of the parents
to pay. The school consisted of six classes: a beginner’s class for young children
between the ages of 6 and 8 years; a beginner’s class for children between 8 and
10 years; one elementary Hebrew class; one advanced Hebrew class; one Chumosh
class and a Bar Mitzvah class.
The Center Sunday School has an enrollment of 165 children divided into 10
classes. The curriculum provides for an intensive nine year course in Jewish history
customs and ceremonies, beginning with the kindegarten class and terminating
with the Post-Comformation class. It must be stressed, however, that Sunday
School at its best, offers the Jewish child a very inadequate preparation for active
and intelligent participation in Jewish life and for an understanding of its heritage.
It is only effective as a supplement to the Hebrew School education.
During the year the children of both ths Hebrew and Sunday sponsored many
interesting events on the occasions of the major and minor festivals. The Canu-
kah entertainment and the Purim Carnival were notable success. The Simchas
Torah celebration was also a memorable event. Numerous special programs were
presented by the Sunday School during the assembly periods on Chamisho Osor,
Lag B’Omer, and patriotic occasions. Through their charity fund the children
contributed to local and national philanthropic organizations.
Seven children were confirmed at a very impressive Confirmation Service on
Shavouth evening, in the presence of a large congregation. The confirmants were
Minnie Feinberg, Bernice Berkovitz, Irene Kristie, Abe Spiwak, Raymond Cohen,
Meyer Proctor and Paul Weiss.
During the year the Center schools published the Center Hi-Lights, a monthly
paper which contained articles and poems, written by the children and many other
interesting items. Many of the contributors showed considerable talent and dis¬
played a fine appreciation of Jewish values.

1) Rosh Hashonah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and lasts two
days. It is also the beginning of the 10 days of Penitence, which end with Yom
Kippur. Sincere prayer and meditation during these days help us to experience the
reality of God and to dedicate ourselves anew to the establishment of His King¬
dom on earth — the reign of freedom, justice and peace.
2) Shabbat Shubath derives its name from the Haftorah of that day. Hosea xlv,
2-10 which begins with the word Shubah’-'Return’, ie,’ Repent.
3) Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when we
strive to reawaken the latent spiritual power in our souls so that it may become
an effective and directing force in our daily conduct,
4) Sukkos (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of thanksgiving aftei
all the products of the field have been gathered in. The S'ukkah symbolizes the
wanderings of our ancestors in the wilderness of Sinai.
5) Simchat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of Shemini Azeret.
Then the reading of the Pentateuch is completd and begun anew for the coming
year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos—procession with the scrolls ofthe
Torah to the accompaniment of songs and dancing.
6) Rosh Chodesh is the term for the beginning of a new month. As the Jewish
year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coincides
with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered the
first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodes of the month following.

SENIOR HADASSAH
Hadassah, the Women’s Zionist Organization of America, in its Senior and Junior
organizations, register 50,000 women devoted to service in and for Palestine. During
24 years now, the span of a generation, Hadassah has been carrying on a campaign
for the dissemination of information and knowledge on Palestine and Zionism and
for the gathering of funds to help in the building up program of Palestine.
In the course of years, Hadassah has spread a network of curative and pre¬
ventive health institutions throughout Palestine, including the only tuberculosis
hospital in the country; has waged successful war against native diseases, notably
trachoma and malaria, has raised standar ds of medicine, introduced modern meth¬
ods of treatment, advanced health education among the masses, trained graduate
nurses, given specialized post graduate courses to many physicians, and helped
establish sick benefit societies and rural medical service. Through its manifold
activities, benefiting all races and creeds, Hadassah has improved the health and
lowered the death rate of the people of Palestine.
The immediate tasks of Hadassah are: The completion of the Rothschild-
Hadassah Hospital, which is being built on Mount Schopus, Jerusalem, by the
Hadassah and the American Jewish Physicians Committee; S'upport of the Youth
Aliy ah—Juvenile immigration into Palestine from countries of persecution; the
redemption of the Haifa Bay land for the Jewish National Fund.
Mrs. Ben Stein, president of the local chapter for the past three years, has
devoted much time and effort to the work of the local organization, and her dis¬
tinctive success as a leader and ardent Zionist is manifested by the banner year
which the organization enjoyed during 1935-36.
Assisting Mrs. Stein in carying out the program of the past year were the fol¬
lowing officers: 1st vice-president, Mrs. L. Klepper; 2nd vice-president, Mrs. Her¬
bert Weiss; Recording Secretary, Mrs. Nat Cohen; Corresponding Secretary, Mrs.
Perry Kantor; Executive Secretary, Mrs. Louis Witten; Treasurer, Mrs.
Harry Katz; financial Secretary, Mrs. Ira Stein.
The outstanding event of the year, was the Southern Regional Conference of
Senior and Junior Hahassah organizations, held in this city February 9 and 10. Mrs.
Alexander Lamport of New York dominated the convention with her inspiring
addresses and delightful personality. Memorable events at the convention were:
The Oneg Shabbat held in the Center, Saturday afternoon, honoring delegates and
Visitors. ,It was characterized by inspiring talks and singing of Palestinian songs.
The hostesses on this delightful occasion were the Daughters of Israel; The reception
in the Temple Home, Sunday afternoon, given by the Temple Sisterhood in honor
of the distinguished guests and visitors; The inspiring address of Dr. T. Z. Cason,
who displayed a thorough understandng and knowledge of the work done in Palestine
and especially of the work of the Hadassah; The address by Rabbi Israel L. Kaplan,
who described the cooperative colonies in the Holy Land and the back to the land
movement among the Jews. The convention was a great success and increased the
enthusiasm of members and non-members for the great work of building up the
Jewish National Homeland, and especially the health work of Hadassah.
Other outstanding events of the year were the opening Luncheon under the
chairmanship of Mrs. Ira Stein and Mrs. Dave Harris; the Fall Dance, under the
chairmanship of Mrs. Harry Katz; the December meeting at which Mrs. Ben Stein
gave a report of the national Hadassah Convention which she attended, and Rabbi
M. L'. Margolis gave an enlightening lecture on Jewish Customs and Rituals; The
Hadassah Sabbath held in the Jewish Center in honor of Mrs. Szold’s seventy-fifth
birthday, when Rabbi Margolis spoke on Youth Aliyah; The Donor’s Banquet
addressed by Mrs. Heilman, guest speaker, of Atlanta.
The membership of the Hadassah has increased during the year from 96 to 131.

PUSHALOTER AID SOCIETY
The Pushaloter Aid Society was organized 24 years ago by a group of women
who came from Pushalot a small town in Lithuania, 3 miles from Ponives, Kovner
district.
The membership of the society consists entirely of women who have either
come from Pushalot or have married a native Pushaloter. They meet once a month
in the homes of members.
The purpose of the organization is to aid individuals and institutions in Push¬
alot who are in need of relief. Such aid is usually sent each year preceding the
High Holydays. The society donated a window to the Jacksonville Jewish Center
when it was built. It has also contributed to the Center a number of Prayer
Books for Friday night services, and several useful kitchen utensils.

The enlarged edition of the Jacksonville Jewish Center Yearbook,
which the Center published last year for the first time, made a splendid
impression upon the Jewish Community of Jacksonville, as well as upon
our Gentile neighbors. Many favorable comments were made by Jews
of other communities who have seen the book and admired its useful
contents, and beautiful appearance.
With the inclusion of this year of other Community efforts, such as:
The Jacksonville Jewish Community Council and The Jewish Welfare
Society, The Center Year Book may well claim to be the community
"Pinkas," containing a complete record of the activities of every func¬
tioning Jewish organization in Jacksonville.
The officers of the Jacksonville Jewish Center for the year 5696 left to right, (sitting):
Max Rubin; President, Harry Finkelstein, Hon. President, B. Baker, 2nd Vice-President. (Standing):
Jos. Hackel, Auditor, David Moscovitz, 1st Vice-President, Jos. Wilensky, Sec'y and Max Rose,
Treasurer.
We hope that the calendar included and the list of events scheduled
for the conning year continue to bring about a greater co-operation be¬
tween organizations affiliated with the Center.
We know that, as during the past year, the directory of names ad¬
dresses and telephones of all members of Jewish organizations will be
welcomed as one of the most useful features of the book.
We hereby express our grateful appreciation to all the merchants
who have purchased advertising space in this year book. We urge all our
friends to patronize our advertisers whose good-will and co-operation has
made this book possible.
NOTICE: The Jacksonville Jewish Center is not connected with and does not derive benefit from
any local publication except this Year Book and the Daughter's of Israel program
published in connection with their annua1 cabaret.
