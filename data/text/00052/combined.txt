CONSECRATION
Celebrated January 23, 1933

Students of the First Grades of the Solomon Schechter Day
School and Aleph Class of the Afternoon School
demonstrated their knowledge and budding devotion to
Judaism in a lovely program during the Shabbat services.
They received a siddur, a certificate and a sweet treat that
is traditionally given to mark the beginning of their formal
Hebrew education.

SISTERHOOD BAT MITZVAH
Celebrated April 30 - May 1, 1993

For almost three years, the members of this class studied
an intensive and varied curriculum with Rabbi David
Gaffney. The program of instruction included an in-depth
study of the prayerbook and its structure; a mature
understanding of the background of Jewish holidays and
their traditional practices; Jewish history and a study of
Zionism; discussions of philosophical issues including God,
good and evil, synagogue practices and kashrut.

To mark this special milestone, the Bat Mitzvah class par-
ticipated fully in all Shabbat services during the weekend. They also shared their personal reflections of this experience
with the congregation. This was truly a simcha for the entire Center family.

Supplemental School Programs

 

Afternoon Hebrew School

The Afternoon Hebrew School of Jacksonville Jewish Center is designed for students in grades 3-7 and
provides an elementary education leading to Bar/Bat Mitzvah and continuation in the high school program.

Meeting Mondays and Wednesdays and on Saturdays, “the basics” are at the core of the curriculum, with
stress on Hebrew reading, Jewish Social Studies, and current events relating to Israel. Synagogue skills, holi-
day observances and knowledge of prayers are all part of the comprehensive Afternoon Hebrew School pro-
gram.

Girl Scouting

What a great year for Girl Scouting at the Center! Not only was a brand new Brownie troop formed, while Brownie
Troop 882 flourished, but also we were delighted to welcome the return of a Junior Girl Scout troop.

Although the three troops worked independently, several activities were shared. A joint “Baby Shower” was held
for the Lighthouse Learning Center in Orange Park. The proceeds - a mountain of rattles, diapers and formula - greatly
benefitted the agency which takes care of severely physically and mentally handicapped infants and children.

Brownie Troop 882, under the leadership of Beth Shorstein,
had a busy and fulfilling year. While the girls loved the arts and
crafts, hiking, singing and games, the highlight of the year was
the campout at Chowenwaw in Green Cove Springs. Even
though the entire east coast of Florida was ravaged by a severe
storm, the Brownies made it through the night, evacuating only
under protest! As a testimony to their continued interest in
scouting, all eleven third graders have bridged and will be Junior
Girl Scouts this fall. Call Beth Shorstein, 262-7343, Joanne
Rosenberg, 269-6911.

 

Junior Girl Scout Troop 5, under the leadership of Jan Lipsky,
has had a great first year exploring the five worlds of interest-
people, the arts, well-being, the outdoors and the world of to-
day and tomorrow! The Juniors tackled the Photography badge
first. They learned to load
35 mm cameras, shoot photo
stories, process and print film
and even held a photo show of
their work. They worked
together on Computer Fun and
on other badges in both a
group and independently. The
year was capped of with a camp-
ing trip to Kateri Springs in
Orange Springs. The weekend
was devoted to disability
awareness, proving that we can
concentrate on how much we
are all alike and not on our dif-
ferences. Call Jan Lipsky,
268-9225.

 

 

Brownie Troop 40 led by Beth Dollar and Louise Levine has
an eventful first year. Community service projects included visiting
the sick and donating food to the Jacksonville Food Bank, Help-
ing Hands to benefit the Salvation Army, planting trees at the
Jewish Center and participating in the Children Helping Children’s
“baby shower” to benefit Lighthouse Learning Center. The girls
jazzed it up at the Orange Park Jazzercise, had a great time at
a joint troop hayride and cook-out and ended up the year with
a horseback riding party. They earned badges and hopefully
learned something about themselves and others. Call Beth
Dollar, 268-8740.

Amidst the difficult challenges of this past year, Jewish Family and Commu-
nity Services continued its mission to support individuals and families at times
of crisis, to substitute for their family if they were isolated or vulnerable, and to
educate and inform to promote healthy, effective family life. The Agency
entered the year financially and programmatically sound. From this strong
operating base, we efficiently delivered a broadened array of quality services.

Our mental health services received record numbers of new clients, many of
whom were families emotionally and financially strained by the faltering econ-
omy or the elderly bearing the brunt of government cuts. Both are growing,
susceptible populations who turned to us with immediate needs requiring crisis
intervention through counseling and with food and rent assistance.

The New American Program successfully resettled another 211 Jewish
refugees, prepared for more, and tntervened on behalf of scores of previously
settled immigrants who agonized for family and friends remaining amidst polliti-
cal turmoil in the former Soviet Union.

Through our many workshops, Jewish Family and Community Services per-
petuated its link with the community.

The ability to successfully perform our mission despite the adversity of this
past year is directly attributable to the dedication of our Board of Directors, the
loyal support of the Friends of Jewish Family and Community Services and the
commitment of our highly regarded professional staff who have shouldered
additional burdens and responsibilities.

Through the effort of all these fine people we maintain our resilience against
a dark economic picture, less money and expanding needs. The Agency will
uphold its commitment to create and deliver exemplary programs and services
to see our community through the rough times and into a future
filled with opportunities and new challenges.

Iris T. Young

Excitement and Inspiration Mark
Congregation’s Pilgrimage to Israel

When Judah HaLevy, one of the great medieval Spanish
poets wrote his Ode to Zion, he expressed his personal
longing to visit Israel in the following way — “If I could see
you, O Zion, I would be a harp for your songs.” The last
phrase — “The harp for your songs,” was incorporated as
the refain in the very popular Israeli song — Jerusalem of
Gold.

For Judah HaLevy, travel to Israel was difficult. His poem
conveyed a Jewish hope. What was impossible for him
became a reality for a group of the Jacksonville Jewish Cen-
ter who visited Israel with Rabbi and Inge Gaffney in June.
Beginning with the El Al flight to Ben Gurion Airport and
morning services that had everyone on a “high” (-30,000
feet in the air) the tour was a never ending pilgrimage of
excitement and inspiration capped by an extended stay in
Jerusalem. Congregants had an opportunity to visit impor-
tant historic places that were the scenes of our most impor-
tant Biblical and historic stories. Rabbi Gaffney also made
arrangements to visit absorption centers; a school for newly
arrived immigrants from Ethiopia (see picture), a private
meeting with members of the Knesset and more .. . and
more.

Israel was not only sights . . . it was also a soul stirring
experience, whether at Masada or Friday evening services
at the Kotel . . . the Western Wall. All in all these were two
weeks never to be forgotten; an experience that enriched
the lives of all its participants.

By the time you read this message I will have completed my first one
hundred days as president of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Although it
is only a brief time, there have been moments of great fulfillment and per-
sonal satisfaction, as well as many challenges.

It is a great honor to provide direction and leadership for the Jack-
sonville Jewish Center. | follow a long list of very devoted, hard working
and fine presidents. | am especially proud to follow Jeffery Morris, who
served this congregation for three very successful years. Every community
has its share of reliable and conscientious people. These are families and
individuals who, through the years, establish themselves as leaders of an
organization or community. Jeffery Morris is one of those people. It is Stuart Hecht
most appropriate that this yearbook is dedicated in his honor.

 

I would like to start this New Year with an outstretched hand to you —
the members of the Jacksonville Jewish Center — with an invitation to
you to become involved. I welcome your comments and promise to
respond to your concerns. My family and | look forward to sharing many
simchas, holidays and events with you and your family. |

Madeline, Elizabeth, Allison and Stephanie join me in wishing all of you
a very happy and healthy new year.

L’Shanah Tovah

Stuart Hecht

Doris Goldstein
Solomon Schechter Day School
P.T.A. President

One of the most important things that links us
together as a community is our concern for our chil-
dren. We want to share with our children what is pre-

cious about our past and prepare them for what will be

different about their future.

The PTA has a single goal: the enrichment of our
children’s education. Not a week goes by at the
Solomon Schechter Day School and Early Childhood
Department without the PTA’s helping hand. Parents,
teachers, students and administrators come together

through PTA as a united force for helping their schools:
- Fun — and educational — programs like Book-A-

Bration, the “Critter Encounter” from the

Jacksonville Zoo, trips to theaters and other field

trips.

- Volunteer health programs like vision screening

and Red Ribbon Week for drug abuse prevention.

- Help with holiday observances like Model

Seders, Consecration and Shabbat Boxes.

- “Extras” for the classroom like a computer and

software for Hebrew word processing for the Day

School. ... sand tables, stencil machines and picnic

tables for the Preschool.

- Art enrichment programs like Kaleidoscope — a

whole day for the arts — and regular classroom

visits from the “Picture Lady.”

- And much more.

Every child benefits from the PTA’s volunteer
efforts. Every parent benefits when he or she gets
involved with PTA. Because the PTA aims squarely at

enriching our children’s school day, the time and ener-

gy spent with PTA brings immediate, positive returns.
With your help, we can do so much. Best wishes
for a joyous and healthy New Year.

Supplemental Hebrew School
A.U.T.O. President

This year has been a busy and rewarding one for
A.U.T.O. — the Supplemental School PTA. With the
creative ideas of Rabbi Kentof and the many hours of
work by dedicated volunteers, we have striven to
enhance our school’s efforts to enrich the Jewish
education of our students.

We started with a fund-raiser, selling delicious
honey cakes. In the fall, we increased our membership
at open house and instituted “room mothers,” who
secured volunteers for the three booths A.U.T.O.
sponsored in the Hanukkah Carnival. They will be
invaluable in future endeavors.

Preparation for Consecration was a joint effort with
Solomon Schechter PTA and was a huge success.
Beautiful Siddur covers and adorable costumes for the
presentation were provided for the students through the
time and efforts of these parent groups.

For Tu B Shevat, we prepared bags of fruit and
nuts for each student in the Afternoon Hebrew and
Sunday Schools to take home and share the signifi-
cance of the holiday with their families.

With A.U.T.O.’s help, several of the Afternoon
Hebrew classes were involved in the true meaning of
Mishloach Manot by making gift baskets for Purim.
These children beautifully decorated baskets filled
with fruits and goodies which were then delivered by
USYers and others to new and prospective members
of the Center. An orderly and organized costume
parade was planned by A.U.T.O. and Day School
PTA for the celebration of Purim in the synagogue.

For Passover, we baked matzot and set up
model seders with the Afternoon Hebrew students.

The annual Teacher Appreciation Dinner, spon-
sored by A.U.T.O. in honor of the teachers of the
three Supplemental Schools, was a delightful evening
enjoyed by teachers, staff and spouses.

These worthwhile projects are made possible by
the fundraising and volunteer efforts of the members
of A.U.T.O. Please join and give your time and ideas.

CENTER CEMETERY
Charlotte Fialkow and
Blanche Slott, chairpeople

The Center owns and operates two
cemeteries, both adjacent to the Ever-
green Cemetery in North Jacksonville,
Both are beautiful pieces of property
with large shade trees and mature
landscaping. The original cemetery is
referred to as the “Old Cemetery.”
The new cemetery is known as the
Center Memorial Park. The Center
provides complete maintenance of
these grounds. Both cemeteries are
open daylight hours every day except
Shabbat and Jewish holidays. A
Memorial Service is conducted each
year on the Sunday between Rosh
Hashana and Yom Kippur at each of
the cemeteries.

Just as with other life cycle events at
the Center,burial and mourning rites
are reserved for Center members.
Therefore, only full members may pur-
chase a plot or be buried in one of the
Center cemeteries. For more informa-
tion regarding the purchase of ceme-
tery plots, please call the Center
administrative office.

HEVRAH KADDISHA

Nathan Sandler, President

For almost 2000 years, Judaism has
cared for the deceased with as much
dignity as one would have in life. Bur-
ial societies exist in communities to
ensure that standards of loving-kind-
ness and dignity are maintained for all.

While Jacksonville has a Hevrah
Kaddisha, its membership is smaller
than desired. Additional members
woukd enable us to enhance the per-
formance of our tasks which include:
(1) Tahara, the traditioan washing,
shrouding and placing of the body in
the coffin; (2) making arrangememnts
for the Tahara; (3) being a Shomer,
one who watches the body until the
funeral; (4) transportation for the
Shomer.

Being a part of the Hevrah Kaddisha
means performing a mitzvah for
another Jew who can never thank
you. This is the greatest mitzvah of all.
Please calll your Rabbi or Nathan San-
dler, if you wish to take part.

Dear Friends:

The importance of education is paramount in Judaism. The building of the
sanctuary was certainly of great consequence to the Children of Israel, but the
Talmud nevertheless rules, “Children who attend school are not to be removed
from the study of the Torah even for the building of the sanctuary.” So, throughout
Jewish History, parents have made sacrifices to ensure that their children received a
good education.

The obligation to educate our children is addressed in the Torah to the parent,
as stated in the first passage of the Shema — v’shinantam I’vanecha, “and you shall
teach them diligently to your children.” But, in the case of orphans, this was not
possible, and the first century Rabbi Yehoshua ben Gamla ordained that the educa-
tion of children be a communal responsibility.

The Talmud discusses the question, which is of greater importance, study or
action, and unanimously concludes that education and knowledge have priority,
since they teach the correct manner of action and observance. Ignorance has always
been frowned upon in Judaism: an am ha’aretz (a “person of the soil,” an ignora-
mus) was considered the lowest category of individual.

Certainly, the age of bar/bat mitzvah is not the end of one’s Jewish education.
Rather, it is the signal for further and more serious study, now that one is obliged to
fulfill religious obligations with a fuller understanding and appreciation of their pur-
pose.

The Hebrew word for education is chinuch (“consecration”), aimed at teaching
a child not merely how to make a living, but how to live. Parents who see education
only as a means to secure a reasonable livelihood or a successful career for their
children provide but a fraction of the total education called for by Jewish tradition.

“Train a child in the way he should go, and even when he is old he will not
depart from it,” declares Proverbs. The proper purpose of education is to instill into
a child’s mind the ethical and moral values of Judaism; the active observance of the
mitzvot through knowledge of the sources; a strong sense of identification with the
Jewish people and Eretz Yisrael together with concern for all mankind.

The Jacksonville Jewish Center Supplemental School is committed to providing
quality education. We hire the best staff and use the best textbook material avail-
able.

In addition to traditional classroom subjects, Hebrew and Judaica, we include
discussions on Jewish life today and how it affects our children. We also experience
learning in surroundings other than the classroom and schedule camp-outs, field
trips and other outdoor activities to round out our program.

Our Pre/Jr. Congregation classes are of top quality and are led by our staff.
Services are led by the students under the guidance of staff members.

Our youth program, consisting of three clubs, Chalutzim, Kadima & USY, have
a tradition of excellence in youth programming, conventions and retreats.

I hope that your children will take part in all the wonderful activities offered to
members of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and I wish all of you a Happy and fulfill-
ing New Year.

Youth Activities

 
 
 

USY ON WHEELS This six and a half week cross country tour affords teenagers, ages
1412 to 18, the opportunity to see many of the exciting sites in the U.S. and Canada while
living a fully Jewish life. Each bus of 45 teens has a chance to visit their peers and experience
Conservative Judaism in North America. Emily Goldberg represented her Chapter.

USY ISRAEL PILGRIMAGE This is a unique

opportunity for young people ages 15 to 18 to visit Israel. Based
in Jerusalem, each of our groups travels the country experienc-
ing their Jewish heritage. Some of the highlights of the trip are
the JNF forest project, lectures by outstanding personalities and
the Tzedakah Awareness program. In addition to spending a delightful summer with their Israeli peers, our Pilgrims also develop
lifelong friendships with their traveling companions throughout the United States and Canada. An additional tour of Poland was
included in an Israel/Poland Pilgrimage. Center USYers who participated this year were: Rachel Abramson, Bernie Echt, Adam
Frisch, Jay Goldstein, Faye Gordon, Stacie Marks, Rachel Mizrahi, David Moskovitz, Morry Safer, Todd Sager, Lisa
Schneider, Josh Sekine, Ben Setzer, Freddie Sherman, Laura Witten, Jonathan Zisser.

CAMP RAMAH

Camp Ramah provides the opportunity of a total Jewish living
experience for children and teenagers in the relaxed atmosphere
of asummer camp. Values built on a shared experience and emo-
tion reach our sons and daughters in a manner impossible in a
formal school program. Over the years, RAMAH has helped in-
still a deeper commitment to Judaism in many of its participants.
Twenty-six Center students attended this year.

The Center believes that benefits accrue to the congregation,

bl not just to the individual. We have therefore made monies
available to reduce the financial cost of relied students regardless of need. These monies are not part of the regular Center
budget, but rather are raised by donations to the Myra Thier Lazarus Ramah Fund, and Sisterhood, and matching funds
from Jewish Federation.

Two families have helped us greatly. The Gordon Family has established a scholarship fund in memory of the beloved Robert J.
Gordon. A long list of deserving boys and girls have been able to go to Ramah because of this scholarship. Abe Selber established
in his will a perpetual trust for specific Jewish education purposes. With the full support of his children, Iris and Franklin, who
have never lost interest in the Center, the proceeds of the Abe Selber Trust have been used to send campers to Ramah.

We were proud to have sent one of the largest delegations to Ramah this year.

 

LTI Leadership Training Institute/Summer Encampment

Summers are topped off with a unique experience at Camp Blue Star for one week
during the month of August. Teenagers from 7th grade and up are encouraged to
take the opportunity to participate in this program which includes informal classes
and discussion groups, cultural activities and special evening activities in addition to
a full range of camp sports and social activities. The camp provides two unique
programs: 1) Kadima, grade 7 & 8 and 2) USY, grade 9 through 12. Those
attending were: Rachel Abramson, Herschel Bettman, Sandy Drashin, Liza
Furman, Eric Goldberg, Kevin Kaplan, Josh Kornblum, Lauren Marshell,
Mike McNett, David Moskovitz, Tillie Moskovitz, Josh Rubens, Marcy Rubens,
Morry Safer.

A Message From Rabbi David Gaffney

 

As we celebrate Rosh-Hashanah and Yom Kippur we may be reminded that
the New Year was always associated with the venerable practice of making reso-
lutions. We should not make light of our New Year resolutions for they spring
from the knowledge that we are free and responsible creatures, able to grow and
correct ourselves. While it is true that “you can teach an old dog new tricks,” we
are not dogs and what is fundamental to our human essence is that we are not
pre-programmed, but have the capacity for change.

None of us is what he or she should truly be. The gulf between what we
could be and what we are, between our vast potentialities and our limited
achievements, underscores the need for repentance, which translated Jewishly
means to return to God and an upreach to the highest within ourselves. We need
not remain the willing captives of the past. Life does not demand that we be per-
fect. It only requires that we grow.

It is in this spirit that I share with you some resolutions for Rosh-Hashanah
that you might consider.

- Don't bear the burdens of the past with you into the New Year. Resolve to
commit the past with its mistakes and its failures to God’s mercy and then strive
for greater spiritual progress in the future. So many of us move into the New Year

 

weighted down by the memory of our mistakes and the sins of the past. While it Rabbi David and
is true that we all live with the consequences of our actions — that we “reap what Inge Gaffney
we sow’... it is also true that God’s forgiveness can turn even that reaping into a

redemptive and healing process. We call this Teshuvah — which is translated as
repentance. The word accurately means self-renewal.

- Resolve to cultivate a character too noble to cherish petty resentments and grudges. Some people ponder over
past injuries, squeezing out of them every drop of misery they contain. It is inspiring, if not surprising, to come across a
fully mature human being who is strong and generous enough to be impervious to resentment, to bitterness and to
revenge.

- Resolve to be tender with the young, compassionate with the aged, sympathetic with those who strive and toler-
ant of those who are weak. We have all been each of these at some point in our lives. As one playwright wrote — “I
don't ask for your pity, just your understanding — not even that — no. Just for your recognition of me in you and the
enemy .. . time — in us all.”

- Resolve to strengthen and deepen in the year ahead the ties that bind you to family, to friends and to dear ones.
These relationships are like the rich gifts — not cheaply bought. It demands time, thoughtfulness, self-sacrifice and out-
going affection. Too often we take our families for granted. Expressions of appreciation and love heighten the depth of
each of our relationships . . . with our spouses, with our families and with our friends. We need to reaffirm the priority
of our family and its claim on our devotion, concern and time.

- Resolve to develop inner spiritual resources so that happiness and peace will not be at the mercy of every chance
intruder. This will help us to become too strong for fear, too noble for anger and too rooted in faith to be overcome by
trouble. We will, however, never achieve this unless we keep regular appointments with God and, in the words of the
Psalmist, “seek to be, always, in His presence.” We do so as we observe our joyous Shabbat days, as we celebrate our
holidays, encounter His presence in prayer and in sacred deeds — Mizvot.

- Resolve to make a difference . . . to add to the sum total of human welfare so that it can never be said that we
lived selfishly. There are opportunities for service all about us. Israel, most decidedly, needs our support. So do the
institutions of Jewish life. The well being of the synagogue can be advanced and strengthened by our presence and
participation. There is anguish, poverty, want and need voiced everywhere in our society and we can ease this pain by
caring. As we reach out to bless others, we ourselves are blessed. As we feed, we are fed; as we give, we receive; as we
bring joy to those in distress, we find happiness and joy within our own hearts.

- Resolve to enlarge and expand your understanding of what Jewish life is all about — its values and ideals, its his-
tory, its observances and practices, its sacred teaching and great literature. The richest treasure and legacy of countless
generations of Jewish devotion and commitment is available to us for the asking. We must always be learning Jews.
Study is a lifelong quest that we undertake, not only for our knowledge but also for our personal enrichment.

These resolutions, though they may not all be fully realized, can serve as a challenge to us, for they constitute an
ideal to guide us.

As we pray together, aspiring for a better year and for a better life, may we be sustained and encouraged by the
resources of the spirit offered to us by our synagogue and by the values it has preserved over these many years.

We begin Rosh-Hashanah and Yom Kippur with the prayer that God’s blessing attend us all. May He watch over
us, keep us in health, grant us growth, fortify us with courage and renew in us a steadfast heart as we pray... . as we
make resolutions for the year to come.

To all of you, from Inge and myself, a year of achievement and satisfaction, health and peace, joy and love. Amen .

TORAH READING
1992-93

Ted Gross, coordinator

The emphasis on fair and equal
treatment for all people is rooted in
the basic Torah laws concerning the
stranger (alien), the orphan and the
widow. Comment on Exodus 22:20 -
in the alien, men discovered the idea
of humanity. It is not strange, there-
fore for the newest member of the
Supreme Court to have a record of
jurisprudence reflecting these values.
From year to year, from generation
to generation, Torah principles have
fostered an innate respect for social
consciousness This is true even of
those who only pay attention to the
public reading of the Torah Scroll.
How much more is the potential for
commitment of those who do the
actual reading!

We are thankful to those who
devote time and energy to becoming
regular Torah readers, to those who
return again and again when their
particular portions come up for
repetitive reading and to those who
have volunteered the skills learned in
the Center program to assist in other
congregations. We are also thankful
to the Religious committee and its
chairman, Edward Tannen, for spe-
cial assistance.

LIBRARY COMMITTEE

Minnie Schreiber, chairman

The Henry Kramer Library and
Resource Center continues to be
first in Juadica collections, according
to the Jacksonville Business Journal.
New and timely Judaica books are
purchased each year by contribu-
tions from Center members. A spe-
cial bookplate recognizes the donor.

The Solomon Schechter Day
School PTA Book-A-Bration pro-
gram furnishes new books for the
children’s section. Family and friends
buy books in honor of the birthday
of Day School students as well as for
other occasions. So many new books

were added to this collection this
year that a new and larger card cata-
log holder had to be purchased from
various library contributions.

A paperback book holder was
donated by the Sachs Family in
memory of Debby Sachs.

The demand for new books by
young readers created a need for
new shelves. The Center Men’s Club
donated the materials and talent to
build these shelves.

Progress is being made in organz-
ing the Yiddish and Hebrew Collec-
tions on the third level of the
Library. Members of the congrega-
tion who read Yiddish and Hebrew
would render invaluable assistance
in this area.

The Henry Kramer Library has an
extensive collection of books on a
wide variety of topics. It is accessible
to all members on Sunday morn-
ings. In addition, the book cart is
made available on special Shabbat
and Holiday mornings after services
for the convenience of worshippers.
Leisure reading of Judaica is a won-
derful way to become better
informed.

The committee performs many
hours of volunteer service. This
insures the Library collections are in
order and easily available to the con-
gregation. Members are invited to
join this enjoyable endeavor.

Robert Cantor, chairman

The purpose of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center Youth Commission is
to over see and set appropriate goals
for all of the Center Youth Groups.
Chalutzim, Kadima, USY, Boy Scouts
and Giirl Scouts are all vital to the
future of our people. The Youth Com-
mission sets proper standards for the
programming of these groups.

The Scout programs help to instill
proper values, to teach survival and
develop community skills. The Youth
Commission helps these programs
become meaningful within the frame-
work of Jewish life.

With guidelines from this committee,
our young people have an opportunity
to socialize with other Jewish youth in
a wholesome, value promoting atmos-
phere.

programs are Traditional Long Term Care, and Alzheimer/Dementia Care.

offers respite care.

In addition, an Adult Day Care Program allows community members access
to River Garden’s excellent facilities and services on a daily basis. Retirement
Housing and Home Assist services are in the offing. River Garden is a rich
community resource providing not only care to the frail elderly, but training
for many professionals including physicians, nurses, gerontologiosts and others.

Beautifully landscaped grounds complete with lake, fishing pier, childen’s
playground, shuffleboard court and outside picnic areas provide ample
opportunity for families and other visitors to enjoy quality time together.

Words like quality time, gracious, beautiful, and excellent are not usually
used when describing a nursing home . . . but River Garden is different. River
Garden is a nursing home that feels good. While few look forward to needing
nursing home care, it is reassuring to know River Garden is available should
it be needed. Community members are encouraged to visit the Home and
personally experience its ambiance. For further information or to arrange a

MITZVAH COMMITTEE

Bikkur Holim literally means “visiting
the sick” and is a religious imperative
for us as Jews. The ancient tradition
finds its roots in a number of biblical
and rabbinic texts.

In Bereshit , the first book of the
Torah, we learn that human beings are
created b’tzelem Elohim, in God’s
image. Judaism understands that to
mean that we are required to emulate
the ethical attributes of God, mercy,
kindness, forgiveness, graciousness.

Modern society with its wonderous
advances in medical technology, intim-
idating procedures and sterile hospital
environments, has, if anything,
increased the need for Bikkur Holim.
The needs of the sick and aged
require, not only the best of scientific
knowledge, but the loving care of fam-
ily, friends, neighbors and the syna-
gogue community.

“To minister to the sick, is to minister
to God.”
- Abraham Joshua Heschel

Experience for a lifetime is the goal of the Solomon Schechter
Day School. Our classrooms are dynamic places where careful atten-
tion is given to individual learning styles, and group sizes and teach-
ing approaches are varied so each child will be reached. A dedicated
faculty leads students through an enriched curriculum in which texts
are only one source of information.

A solid foundation for learning is laid by developing the unifying
tools which allow mastery of concepts. Students learn to read with
comprehension and skill, to write with fluency, and to think critically.
The most current materials and instructional methods, through both
group an individual instruction, continuously challenge students to
reach their potential.

The richness of Jewish tradition permeates the school day.
Through: observance of daily prayer and kashrut, in holiday obser-
vances, and through teaching of Mitzvot, Judaics becomes an inte-
gral part of everyday life. Hebrew study goes far beyond just being a
language program with the goal of fluency.

The foundations of the school are solid. It has a thirty-two year
record of academic achievement and support of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. We have doubled our size in the last ten years with-
out sacrificing quality.

Now we turn our visions to the future. We plan on establishing
our Middle School Program during this current year with a carefully

We invite you to share in the excitement of this commitment to
meet the needs of a lifetime.

A Havurah is a small group of congregation members (families, couples, singles) who
meet regularly for activities or experiences of their mutual choosing. The groups are var-
ied but all gather together to enjoy life as Jews through socializing, education, discussion
of Jewish issues, worship or sharing of Jewish holidays and celebrations.

For information call Havurah Coordinator Etta Drashin, 737-3318.

About the Cover

The beautiful cover of the
yearbook is an illuminated page
from the Darmstadt Haggadah

from Heidelberg, Germany,
dated 1430.

The text is from the Ha
Lachmah Anyah which intro-
duces the Maggid section of the
Seder which tells the story of
Pesach.

 

This is the bread of affliction a i c
which our ancestors ate in the Elaine Metlin
land of Egypt. Data Entry

Let all who are hungry enter and eat.

Let all who are needy, come and celebrate Pesach.

Now we are here. Next year may we be in the land of Israel.
Now we are enslaved. Next year may we be free.

This overture to the poor and needy is so important that it was
written in the language of the times, Aramaic, so that it would be
understood by all.

The theme of hospitality is threaded through the Seder, through
Pesach, through all our festivals. We open our homes to those who
are homeless and we open our door to Elijah, symbol of the weary
wayfarer. To allow any Jew to suffer deprivation on this day would
be a mockery to Pesach, for it would mean that we had forgotten
that we were once slaves in Egypt. We read this passage to under-
score that many of our fellow Jews are still afflicted, that there is
still hunger in the world but that we are confident that the redemp-
tion will come and will come soon.
