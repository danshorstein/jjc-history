As we review the past year and
recall that it brought us the his¬
toric event, the rebirth of our
Jewish Nation—the State of Israel
—we pause and reflect.
Countless generations have
dreamed, hoped and prayed that
this miracle would take place,
that righteousness would prevail
and justice would ultimately tri¬
umph. All this has come to pass
in our day and we are mindful
and deeply grateful for God’s
Blessings. With our courage
strengthened and our faith re¬
newed, we await the day when all
the dark clouds that are now on
the horizon, will forever be dis¬
persed and in their stead a glorious
rainbow will appear bringing peace
in its wake.
We ask how it affects our pro¬
gram and assay an appraisal of
our efforts, that we may better
determine our future course. We
realize that the tangible results
are dependent upon the numerous
intangible ones, and conclude that
if we are to contribute to their suc¬
cess in some measure, we must
equip ourselves for the many fu¬
ture tasks that offer a challenge to
us.
Concerned as we are primarily
with the need for Jewish educa¬
tion, we must continually stress
this fact. It can not be a theoreti¬
cal matter for discussion, but must
become the practical purpose and
force motivating all our endeav¬
ors, if we are to pursue a forward
course towards an eventual goal.
As Jewesses, we recognize our
obligations and our duties. Through
our actions, we seek to express our
debt of gratitude to the Almighty.
Willingly, we accept responsibili¬
ties, only to find that at times we
are not prepared to render a fuller
service to our people because of
the inadequacy in our former
training. We resolve to correct this
and strive to improve the deficien¬
cy. What we outline for ourselves
is prompted by our hopes, our
dreams and our desires to prepare
a generation for a richer and hap¬
pier Jewish Way of life.
The inspiration we receive
through our Sisterhood work,
fires our imagination and gives
birth to many ideas. These ideas
can become productive and fruit¬
ful when they are nurtured spirit¬
ually and put into effect practical¬
ly. Women who grapple with such
problems and offer to remedy
them, possess the attributes of
fine Jewish Womanhood. Such
women are not content to follow
a desultory pattern nor an effec¬
tive program. Such women dis¬
count frustration and strain with
impatience to achieve a worthy ob¬
jective. Such women abound in
our Sisterhood and they are a
credit and benefit to themselves,
to us and our Jewish people.
In the year 5709 which is just
unfolding, with the Almighty’s
Help, may such women become
the standard bearers for Israel, a
nation at peace.

While the Y. P. L. is the young¬
est member of the Center’s fami¬
ly, it shows promise of becoming
an important factor in the local
Jewish community.
The Young People’s League of
the Center had its start at an in¬
formal meeting attended by local
youth leaders and representatives
of the Center Men’s Club. The
conference culminated in plans for
a Youth Night to be held on March
23rd. Mr. Samuel Ribner, Nation¬
al Director of Y. P. L. addressed
the gathering.
The Regional Conference of the
United Synagogue of America pro¬
vided the impetus for launching
the organization. Mr. Ribner again
addressed the group at a luncheon
held April 11th at the Roosevelt
Hotel. The feature of the after¬
noon meeting was a symposium on
“What the Jewish Youth Expects
From the Synagogue”, led by Mor-
ty Rosenkranz, Ben Hoffenberg,
Herbert Solin and Sheldon Gend-
zier. A temporary committee was
appointed at this time with Irwin
Gendzier serving as chairman.
