THE BUILDING EXPANSION FUND
The year 1947-48 brought further evidence of
the Center’s need of expanded and additional facili¬
ties. Fortunately progress was also made in bringing
the Building Expansion Fund closer to its goal.
Several years ago it became evident that the pres¬
ent structure cannot accommodate the steadily grow¬
ing number of members and the increasing enroll¬
ment in our Religious Schools. At first it was be¬
lieved that improvement and enlargement of the
present building would meet the need. As the com¬
mittee delved deeper into the project they were
convinced that a more thorough going expansion was
called for. Larger amounts and more space were
necessary. The plan has therefore been revised. It is
now proposed that the present Synagogue auditorium
he remodeled and air-conditioned, rooms be en¬
larged and remodeled to serve specific purposes on a
scale recommended by progressive school adminis¬
trators.
Even though the facts have already been stated on
previous occasions and in different form, it might
still be well to review here what the plans include.
1. Auditorium with seating capacity of 1500 for
programs, 500 for athletic exhibitions, and 450 for
dining.
2. Adequate stage for full-scale theatrical pro¬
ductions.
3. Facilities for showing of moving pictures.
4. Regulation basketball court and gymnasium.
5. Large restaurant-type kitchen.
6. Recreation facilities, i. e., handball court, exer¬
cise rooms, lockers and showers.
7. Special class rooms, junior game rooms, and
arts and crafts facilities.
8. Meeting and entertainment facilities for or¬
ganizations.
9. General lounge, ladies lounge and game rooms.
10.Rabbi’s study and administrative offices.
Additional ground was acquired adjacent to the
Center’s property along Third Street and permission
obtained from municipal authorities to re-locate the
alley now separating the main building from the pro¬
perty we now own on Silver Street. These negotia¬
tions were in the hands of the Real Estate Commit¬
tee, Ben Setzer, Chairman and Sam Fletcher, Co-
Chairman. Ground now owned by the Center will
accommodate an additional building that will house
a large auditorium for athletic and social functions.
On the High Holidays the same auditorium will pro¬
vide seating for about 1500.
The need for an improved and expanded Center
is great. Temporarily, residential buildings located
on the ground owned by the Center are used far be¬
yond their normal capacity. On Sunday morning
there is not an inch of unused space. Center leader¬
ship is vitally concerned over the inadequacy of these
facilities for our Schools and for the youth activities.
With this awareness a Victory Dinner was ten¬
dered the Congregation by the Building Expansion
Fund Committee on March 17th at the Roosevelt
Hotel Ballroom, attended by 400 of the Center’s most
loyal members. Sam Wolf son and Ben Yoffee were
Co-Chairmen, Philip Selber was Master of Cere¬
monies. Rabbi Harry Halpern of Brooklyn, New
York, gave the address of the evening. The guests
were welcomed by Joseph Hackel and Harry Gend-
zier, Chairman of the Building Expansion Fund re¬
ported on the progress made to date and urged that
the necessary funds come forward to bring the drive
to a victorious conclusion.
The verbal statements of the occasion were supple¬
mented by an attractively printed brochure with
sketches of suggested exterior and formal plans.
Ralph Mizrahi, member of the Board and of the
architect firm of Kemp, Bunch and Jackson helped
with the preparation of these studies.
The pledges made at the dinner brought the Fund
very close to its goal of $250,000.00. Payments made
on previous pledges and those reported on March 17th
total about half of this amount. It is that the balance
will come in in even more rapid pace because our
membership is becoming increasingly aware that
construction must begin soon.
“The picture of the next generation of Jackson¬
ville Jewry—what will it be?” was the challenging
title of the prospectus distributed at the dinner. The
question is still to be answered. Adequately, gener¬
ously and thoughtfully. To those who read this ac¬
count and who are conscious of their responsibility
to find an answer to the question is repeated the plea,
“Help the Center keep pace with the growth of
Jacksonville and the progress of American Judaism.”

year. In the Diaspora it is an occasion for stressing the signifi¬
cance of the upbuilding of Palestine.

by every adult Jew, entitling him to vote for delegates to the
Zionist Congress.

Sabbath Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of Purim,
a special portion of the Torah is read beginning with the word
“Zakhor”—“Remember Amaelk.” Haman is presumed to have
been an Amalekite.
Taanit Ester commemorates the day when Queen Esther and
the Jews of Persia fasted and prayed that God would save them
from their threatened annihilation plotted by Haman.
Purim commemorates the day when, through the intervention
of Mordecai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly
escaped being victims of Haman’s plot to exterminate them.
Shabbat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning the
ashes of the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual of
purification of our ancestors in preparing for the Passover Festi¬
val.
Shabbat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read, em¬
phasizing the significance of the month of Nisan in which Israel’s
great deliverance occurred.

foreword
THE Jacksonville Jewish Center originated almost a half century ago as Con¬
gregation B’nai Israel. For over 20 years it has carried the present name
at the present location, West Third and Silver Streets and has been the Conserva¬
tive Synagogue in Jacksonville.
The Center’s Rabbinic leadership has come from the Jewish Theological
Seminary of America for over a generation. Rabbi Sanders A. Tofield is on the
Executive Council of the Rabbinical Assembly of America and is a member of
several of its important committees. Cantor Abraham Marton is a member of
the Cantor’s Assembly sponsored by the United Synagogue.
The Center is affiliated with the United Synagogue of America and many
of its leaders are also active in the Conservative movement on a national level.
When the Southeastern Region of the United Synagogue was formed last year,
the Center was headquarters of the Conference and Joe Becker was elected
Vice President and Joe.Hackel, Treasurer. At the Biennial United Synagogue
Convention held in Chicago, Harry Gendzier became a national Vice President.
All branches within the Jacksonville Jewish Center are affiliated with cor¬
responding national bodies of the Conservative movement. The Sisterhood,
Daughters of Israel, is a member of the Women’s League and its President,
Mrs. Jacob Becker, is Vice President of the Florida Region. Last year the Cen¬
ter Men’s Club joined the National Federation and the youngest member of
the Center Family, the Y. P. L., formed this year, is also part of the national
organization of the same name.
Progress recorded in the pages of this Year Book is in keeping with the ad¬
vancement on a national level of the Conservative movement as a whole, which
is the fastest growing Jewish movement in America.

As we review the past year and
recall that it brought us the his¬
toric event, the rebirth of our
Jewish Nation—the State of Israel
—we pause and reflect.
Countless generations have
dreamed, hoped and prayed that
this miracle would take place,
that righteousness would prevail
and justice would ultimately tri¬
umph. All this has come to pass
in our day and we are mindful
and deeply grateful for God’s
Blessings. With our courage
strengthened and our faith re¬
newed, we await the day when all
the dark clouds that are now on
the horizon, will forever be dis¬
persed and in their stead a glorious
rainbow will appear bringing peace
in its wake.
We ask how it affects our pro¬
gram and assay an appraisal of
our efforts, that we may better
determine our future course. We
realize that the tangible results
are dependent upon the numerous
intangible ones, and conclude that
if we are to contribute to their suc¬
cess in some measure, we must
equip ourselves for the many fu¬
ture tasks that offer a challenge to
us.
Concerned as we are primarily
with the need for Jewish educa¬
tion, we must continually stress
this fact. It can not be a theoreti¬
cal matter for discussion, but must
become the practical purpose and
force motivating all our endeav¬
ors, if we are to pursue a forward
course towards an eventual goal.
As Jewesses, we recognize our
obligations and our duties. Through
our actions, we seek to express our
debt of gratitude to the Almighty.
Willingly, we accept responsibili¬
ties, only to find that at times we
are not prepared to render a fuller
service to our people because of
the inadequacy in our former
training. We resolve to correct this
and strive to improve the deficien¬
cy. What we outline for ourselves
is prompted by our hopes, our
dreams and our desires to prepare
a generation for a richer and hap¬
pier Jewish Way of life.
The inspiration we receive
through our Sisterhood work,
fires our imagination and gives
birth to many ideas. These ideas
can become productive and fruit¬
ful when they are nurtured spirit¬
ually and put into effect practical¬
ly. Women who grapple with such
problems and offer to remedy
them, possess the attributes of
fine Jewish Womanhood. Such
women are not content to follow
a desultory pattern nor an effec¬
tive program. Such women dis¬
count frustration and strain with
impatience to achieve a worthy ob¬
jective. Such women abound in
our Sisterhood and they are a
credit and benefit to themselves,
to us and our Jewish people.
In the year 5709 which is just
unfolding, with the Almighty’s
Help, may such women become
the standard bearers for Israel, a
nation at peace.

A Message
FROM OUR RABBI
The New Year 5709 dawns with a light of a new
era in Jewish history. Israel is once more an honored
name among nations; the white and blue flag waves
hospitably against the skies of world capitols.
Israel’s miraculous rebirth is particularly inter¬
woven in the religious experience of the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center fellowship. In keeping with the
Conservative outlook in Judaism we have sponsored
the Zionist ideals in our community through every
means in our command, the pulpit, the classroom,
and public service facilities. Leaders of the Center
were foremost devotees to the cause of Yishuv Eretz
Yisroel all through the years of Zionist fund-raising
efforts and public enlightenment.
This edition of the Year Book is dedicated to the
Republic of Israel, out of abiding love of Zion, and
out of the profound conviction that the soulful ties
between Zion and ourselves are only in their initial
stages of cohesion.
Life for the Jews in a world with a state of Is¬
rael differs vastly from the one in which only a
scattered Israel existed. On this premise we base
our attitude towards religious observances, our edu¬
cational objectives, our life as citizens and cultural
beings. In short, the basis for our survival in the
human family. We are. under moral obligation to
begin the New Year with resolute effort to compre¬
hend and to lend reality to this outlook.
In order to be energized by the magnetic forces
shuttling between the two poles of our existence,
Israel and American Israel, we must free ourselves
from pre-conceived notions and naive expectations.
There is room for a fresh examination of the ques¬
tion, How can Zion and Zionist Jewry promote each
other’s welfare? Because old theories persist even
after logic and history rendered them fallacious we
cannot be too alert in orienting ourselves to the
events of the hour.
We must continue to reject the identification of
Zionism with refugeeism. Just as strongly we oppose
Shelilat ha-Galut, the negation of a future for Ju¬
daism outside the boundaries of the Holy Land. In
some quarters, Jewishness is still identified as a mode
of worship. Without inspiration from the marvellous
RABBI SANDERS A. TOFIELD
revival of Jewish peoplehood as symbolized by Is¬
rael, their Israelite persuasion looks pallid.
Secularists interpretations of Judaism seem to be
returning to their battle posts. For awhile they
neglected their traditional opposition to Jewish re¬
ligion and to Hebraic culture bound up in the love
of Zion. Now one of their spokesmen raises the ques¬
tion in a recent article, “Are we to be Israel’s colony
culturally?” In all conscience we say, yes. Diaspara
Jewry—in America, particularly—look for the seeds
of faith and culture sprouting in the native soil of
Jewish genius to become fruitful in lands where
Jews cultivated patriotic loyalty.
The vision of traditional Judaism in America is
becoming more sharply focused in the light of current
history. Energies spent on saving the remnant of
our people now have to be applied towards keeping
ourselves wide awake to the spiritual renacens. From
our own institutions, our own religious fellowships,
our own knowledge and practice of Israel’s tradition
will come the ability to drink of the “living waters”
flowing from the rocks of normal Jewish life in Is¬
rael.
Zion without the love of her children is a deso¬
late metropolis. World Jewries without a Zion in her
full splendor are motherless waifs. Happiness will
prevail in the House of Israel when each plays the
rightful role.
God help us attain a large measure of this happi¬
ness in the coming year.
Rabbi Sanders A. Tofield

minder to light the Sabbath and festival candles, (L. C.) ap¬

Translations of Hebrew names of holidays are given in paren¬
theses on the general calendar. Civic and patriotic days are
shown in the same section. Brief explanations of the holidays
and special Sabbaths, and various occasions are printed under
bold headings with each month.

NOTES ON TISHRI
L. C. Light Sabbath or Festival candles y2 hour before sunset,
for which consult daily newspapers.
Rosh Hashanah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year
and lasts two days.
Shabbat Shuva the Sabbath occurring between Rosh Hashanah
and Yom Kippur, takes its name from the first words of the
Haftorah, “Shuvah, Yisrael—Return, O Israel.”
Yom Kippur the Bay of Atonement, is a day of fasting and
prayer, a day for confession of sin and supplication for for¬
giveness. “For transgressions between man and God, repent¬
ance on Yom Kippur brings atonement. For transgressions be¬
tween man and man, Yom Kippur brings no atonement until
amends are made.”
Sukkot the Feast of Tabernacles commemorates the period
when our forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their
wanderings through the wilderness of Sinai after their Egyp¬
tian bondage.
Simhat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) celebrates the com¬
pletion of the reading of the five books of Moses. On this day,
the reading is begun anew for the coming year.

THE CANTOR
CANTOR ABRAHAM MARTON
Reverend Abraham Marton assumed his office at the
Center with the High Holidays of 1946. In the year of
service that he is rounding out he has earned the applause
of Center worshippers for melodious rendition and modern
interpretation of the traditional liturgy. He is assisted by
a Choir of men and women whom he has trained to
professional standards.
The musical work of the religious school is under his
direction. Among his planned projects are a junior choral
group and a class in Hazanut for leaders of children’s and
youth services.
He is a member of the Hebrew School staff, offering
courses in the daily school, training Bar Mitzvah candi¬
dates, and directing the Sunday morning service of the
Minyan Club. In this sphere he has shown as much pro¬
ficiency as in the musical phases. He has been called upon
for musical programs by various synagogue and communal
organizations, to the delight of the respective audiences.
MUSIC AT THE CENTER
Jewish musical activities at the Jacksonville Jew¬
ish Center, consisting of the Jewish Choral Society,
the High Holy Day special choir and the regular
Sabbath and Holiday choir reached the heighth of
their development under the direction of Cantor
Abraham Marton.
The highlight of the activities of the Jewish Choral
Society, a mixed group of 37 voices, was the concert
of Hebrew and English liturgical and secular music
given Sunday evening, May 2, 1948, at the Morocco
Temple under the auspices of the Ladies Hebrew
Sheltering Aid Society. The varied program of litur¬
gical compositions, folk songs and general music
presented at the time was well received by an en¬
thusiastic audience. In addition to the May concert,
the group performed on a number of other occasions
in presenting a maximum number of public renditions
of Jewish music. Leo Moskowitz is president of the
group.
From the ranks of the Choral Society were drawn
the members of the High Holy Day Choir, whose as¬
sistance contributed in large measure to making the
liturgical portions of the High Holy Day services
more inspiring and devotional. The third important
arm of the Center musical program is the year-round
mixed choir which participates in Friday evening
and special holiday services.
Cantor Abraham Marton’s devotion to the cause
of providing richer and more beautiful music to
the community has resulted in the development of
these varied activities to their present appreciated
position of importance. Much credit is also due to
the individuals who have given so generously of
their time in making these groups the fine musical
organizations that they are.

Another phase of the Center’s
program of character and citizen¬
ship building are the Girl Scout
and Brownie Troops sponsored by
the Daughters of Israel.

showed marked progress in all
phases of their program. Four in¬
dividual troops were organized and
each undertook projects in keep¬
ing with their age level.
Troop 2 had as their main pro¬
ject the making of stuffed toys to
be presented to the children of the
Hope Haven Association. Another
project undertaken by the troops
was the adoption of several fami¬
lies in Palestine.

very successful in its two-fold pur¬
pose. The girls were shown that
their sponsoring organization has
their interest at heart and the lead¬
ers were inspired by the coopera¬
tion of the parents, evidenced by
the large attendance. Mrs. Leo
Michelson acted as master of cere¬
monies at this affair.
The Brownie Troops under ex¬
cellent leadership advanced con¬
siderable. Theirs was mostly handi¬
craft projects and outdoor past-
times.

Girl Scout movement in the Cen¬
ter is closely connected with its
kindred groups, the troops parti¬
cipated in the annual closing ban¬
quet given by the Boy Scout Com¬
mittee. Many of the girls and lead¬
ers appeared in uniform.
Members of the troops are also
students of our Hebrew and Sun¬
day Schools and take part in the
religious activities of the Schools.

individual they also appeared of¬
ficially twice when the annual
Sabbath dedicated to the Girl
Scouts of America was observed
on October 24th and at the annual
service dedicated to Mothers when
they presented floral momentos to
all mothers present.
