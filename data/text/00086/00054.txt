The Jewish Religious Calendar (Luah), which has proven a
valuable appendix to this publication, is printed upon successive
pages with corresponding dates of the general calendar.
On the Jewish side of the calendar appears the Hebrew names
of the festivals, holidays, fast days, and the weekly portion
of the Law (Sidra) read each Saturday from the Torah. A re¬
minder to light the Sabbath and festival candles, (L. C.) ap¬
pears opposite the proper days.
Translations of Hebrew names of holidays are given in paren¬
theses on the general calendar. Civic and patriotic days are
shown in the same section. Brief explanations of the holidays
and special Sabbaths, and various occasions are printed under
bold headings with each month.

NOTES ON TISHRI
L. C. Light Sabbath or Festival candles y2 hour before sunset,
for which consult daily newspapers.
Rosh Hashanah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year
and lasts two days.
Shabbat Shuva the Sabbath occurring between Rosh Hashanah
and Yom Kippur, takes its name from the first words of the
Haftorah, “Shuvah, Yisrael—Return, O Israel.”
Yom Kippur the Bay of Atonement, is a day of fasting and
prayer, a day for confession of sin and supplication for for¬
giveness. “For transgressions between man and God, repent¬
ance on Yom Kippur brings atonement. For transgressions be¬
tween man and man, Yom Kippur brings no atonement until
amends are made.”
Sukkot the Feast of Tabernacles commemorates the period
when our forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their
wanderings through the wilderness of Sinai after their Egyp¬
tian bondage.
Simhat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) celebrates the com¬
pletion of the reading of the five books of Moses. On this day,
the reading is begun anew for the coming year.
