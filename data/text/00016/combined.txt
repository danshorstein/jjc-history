United Synagogue Youth
CENTER YOUTH TEAGUE
"Bringing Youth back to the Synagogue." These words sym-
bolize the aims and efforts of United Synagogue Youth. With a
wide program of cultural, social and religious activities, U.S.Y.,
appears as one of the fastest developing youth groups in the
United States and Canada.
Beginning its ninth year of development, C.Y.L. (Center
Youth League—Senior Chapter of U.S.Y.) again participated in
the Leadership Training Institute, held in Hendersonville, North
Carolina; the National Convention in Chicago, Ill. and the Re-
gional convention. These conventions combined with our own
socials, study groups and Sabbath service participation, help inspire
the members to acquire a better understanding of Judaism.
The Charles Palot Club (C.P.C.), the younger division of
U.S.Y. is now in its fourth year. It also strives for the same aims
as put forth by the older group. C.P.C. provides the basic founda-
tion and fundamental leadership for those entering C.Y.L.
The financial assistance of the Center Sisterhood and the Cen-
ter Men’s Club have enabled us to attend the conventions and
enjoy local events. C.Y.L.-C.P.C. are indeed grateful to those
organizations and to the Center Youth Activities Committee for
their wonderful cooperation and assistance.
Our deepest appreciation to all those who have helped
throughout the years as our advisors. They have inspired every
member with loyalty and warm feeling for the principles and
practices of Judaism.
On page 91 will be found pictures of the Regional Conven-
tion to which our U.S.Y. Chapters were host last spring.

The Mr. and Mrs. Club is now in its fourth year of existence as a social club
affiliated with the Jacksonville Jewish Center. The Club has brought together many
of our old established Jacksonville residents and many newcomers to the city in a
bond of friendship and sociability. We are extending a welcome to those who are
desirous of becoming associated with the Mr. and Mrs. Club.
Harold Pausman
The sincere untiring efforts on the part of the Officers, Board of Directors and the various committees who served willing-
ly since the inception of our Club have established one of the city’s leading social clubs.
The outstanding gala events of our Club, including the Installation affair, the Invitational Affair whereby our membership
are permitted to bring local guests, and our New Year’s party have always been successful affairs at no additional cost to our
members. In addition to these affairs, every month we have a small get-together and on every third Saturday night a dance with
a live orchestra and the serving of food, which is handled by a designated committee.
In electing our Officers and Board of Directors, we have maintained a policy to have both husband and wife serve together,
creating a stronger working body to provide ideas and benefits for our Club.

THE JEWISH RELIGIOUS CALENDAR
The Jewish Religious Calendar is the result of many stages
of development. In ancient times the unit of time was the
month, determined by the phases of the moon. The Hebrew
Calendar was thus lunar. However, since religious festivals are
observed annually at given seasons the calendar also became
solar. The two systems of measuring time were harmonizd
by meaos of leap years in the following manner. The lunar
month consists of 29^ days. In seven of a 19 year cycle an
extra month is added.
The year 5718, for instance, has only 12 months while last
year had a 13th month.
The Jewish Religious Calendar that appears in the following
pages of the Center Year Book is arranged according to the
months of the Civil Calendar, the Hebrew date appears in the
lower left corner under the large number.
Fridays and the eve of Festivals, as well as the first day of
Festivals, carry the symbol L. C, "Light Candles.” The hour
that follows this symbol is 18 minutes prior to sunset, based on
information furnished by the United States Weather Bureau for
Jacksonville.
Rosh Hodesh, "Beginning of the Month,” is observed with
special prayers in the service for that day and with the Bless-
ing of the New Month on the Sabbath previous. When the
new moon appears in the middle of the day the outgoing month
consists of 30 days and the last day is considered the first day
Rosh Hodesh for the coming month.
The Sidrah, the portion of the Torah for the week, is in-
dicated on Saturday’s date. Special Sabbaths are named and are
explained below under the Hebrew month in which they occur.
TISHRI
Rosh Hashanah, Jewish Religious New Year.
Fqst of Gedalia, commemorates the assassination of the
governor of Judea who attempted to restore Jewish independ-
ence after the destruction of the First Temple by the Baby-
lonians.
Shabbat Shuva, Sabbath of Repentance, derives its name
from the Prophetic reading, "Shuva Yisrael,” "Return, O
Israel.”
Yom Kippur, Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and
prayer, a day for confession of sin and supplication for forgive-
ness. "For transgressions between man and God, repentance
on Yom Kippur brings atonement. For transgressions between
man and man, Yom Kippur brings no atonement until amends
are made.”
Sukkot, Tabernacles, commemorates the period when our
forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings
through the wilderness of Sinai.
Hoi Hamoed, Intermediate Days of the Holiday, are ob-
served as semi-festivals.
Hashanah Rabba, the day on which prayers with the refrain
Hosanah ("O Save Us”) is recited.
Shemini Atzeret, Eighth Day of Assembly, marks the con-
elusion of the fall festivals.
Simhat Torah, Torah Festival, is the second day of Shemini
Atzeret and celebrates the completion of the annual reading
cycle from the Five Books of Moses and the beginning of read-
ings for the New Year.
KISLEV
Hanukah, feast of Dedication, commemorates the rededica-
tion of the Temple by the Maccabees in 165 B.C.E. The Macea-
bean triumph over Syrian forces was the first victory for a peo-
pie’s right to the freedom of worship. Lights are kindled in
the synagogue and in the home on every one of the eight
evenings.
TEBET
Asar ah BeTebet, Fast of the 10th of Tebet, commemorates
the beginning of the siege of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in
586 B.C.E. which led to the destruction of the first Temple.
SHEBAT
Hamisha Asar BeShebat, also known as Tu BeShebat, 15 th
of Shebat, is Holy Land Arbor Day or New Year of the Trees.
Shabbat Shekalim, the Sabbath when every adult Jew in
the days of the Temple was reminded to pay his poll tax of a
half a shekel on the first day of Adar.
ADAR
Shabbat Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of
Purim a special portion of the Torah is read beginning with
the word "Zakhor”—"Remember Amaelk.” Haman is pre-
sumed to have been an Amalekite.
Purim, commemorates the day when, through the inter-
vention of Mordecai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia nar-
rowly escaped being victims of Haman’s plot to exterminate
them.
Shabbat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning
the ashes of the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual
purification by our ancestors in preparing for the Passover
Festival.
Shabbat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read,
emphasizing the significance of the month of Nisan in which
Israel’s great deliverance occurred.

The Jewish Religious Calendar, Cont.
NISAN
Shabbat Hagadol, the Sabbath which precedes the great
Festival of Passover, derives its name from the Haftorah which
contains a reference to the "Great Day of the Lord."
Pesah, Passover, celebrates Israel’s deliverance from Egyp-
tian bondage. The festival lasts eight days during which matzot
are eaten in the place of leavened bread. The celebration of the
Passover with the beautiful ceremony of the Seder and the first
and second evenings has fostered by Jewish people the ideals
of liberty and freedom.
IYAR
Yom Ha’atzma’ut. The new State of Israel declared its in-
dependence on May 14, 1948, which was the 5th of Iyar ac-
cording to the Hebrew calendar.
Lag B’Omer, the date of a victory by Bar Kokhba in the
final Jewish struggle against the Romans. He was supported by
the sage Rabbi Akiba and his disciples. It is known as "The
Scholars Festival" and marked by field day outings.
SIVAN
Shavuot, Feast of Weeks, completes 7 weeks from the sec-
ond day of Passover on which the Omer (a measure) of the
new barley was brought to the Temple. It was also the day
when Bikkurim (first fruits) were presented. Tradition as-
signed to Shavuot commemoration of the Covenant established
at Sinai between God and Israel and the giving of the Ten
Commandments.
TAMMUZ
Shivah Asar BeTamuz, Fast of the 17th of Tammuz, that
commemorates the breach in the walls of Jerusalem by the
Babylonians in the Year 586 B.C.E.
AB
Shabbat Hazon, precedes Tisha B’Ab derives its name from
the first words of the Haftorah "Hazon"—the vision of Isaiah,
which foretells the destruction of Jerusalem.
Tisha B’Ab, the Ninth Day of Ab, is a fast day which marks
the destruction of the First Temple in Jerusalem by the Baby-
lonians in 585 B.C.E. and also of the Second Temple by the
Romans (70 C.E.)
Shabbat Nahamu, Sabbath of "Consolation", the firs word
of the Haftarah is "Nahamu," Comfort ye, My People.
ELUL
Selihot, "Penitential Prayers," recited at midnight in prep-
aration for the holiday season, "The Great Days of Awe."
HOLIDAY SYMBOLS
Framing the doors of the Holy Ark in our Synagogue are
a series of panels depicting eight sacred occasions of the Jewish
year. These occasions are listed in the calendar and are de-
scribed in the explanatory notes. Following is a description
of what they represent as they appear from top to bottom,
right to left.
1. Sabbath. Two candlesticks and the kiddush cup; loaf
of twisted Hallah bread in the background, inscription: "Sab-
bath holiness."
2. Rosh Hashanah. Shofar, with set of scales symbolic of
divine judgment in the background. Inscription: "Happy New
Year" on top; "Repentance" together with the two Hebrew
words in the next panel qccur in the Unetane To kef prayer.
3. Yom Kippur. Prayer book with the verse "On this day
shall Atonement be made for you" inscribed on its open pages;
Charity plate; with a tall taper alongside. Inscription: "Prayer
and Charity," completing the quotation "Repentance, Prayer
and Charity lead to Grace and Salvation."
4. Sukkot. Lulav (palm branch) set into a holder with
myrtle and willow branches, an Etrog (citron) lies alongside.
Inscription: "Thou shall rejoice in thy feast."
5. Hanukah. Menorah with candles; shield with letter
"mem” for Maccabees and cruse of oil in background. Inscrip-
tion: "Rock of Ages, my Salvation."
6. Purim. Megillah scroll of Esther; in background, crown
and scepter. Inscription: "Peace to all his seed."
7. Passover. Four cups, embellished with grape leaves
representing the fourfold promise of redemption. In back-
ground, three matzohs. Inscription: "Let my people go that
they worship me."
8. Shavuot. Torah scroll in decorated cover with silver
ornaments. In background; stalks of wheat and grapevines
symbolic of Bikhurim, first fruits. Inscription: "Torah of
Truth."

Adult Education. Before the prominent festivals of the
year, the Sisterhood sponsors Institutes. Included are sessions
on the history and the meaning of the occasions and the tech-
ñiques for celebrating them in the home. This past year the Han-
ukah Institute and the Passover Institute are of special interest.
Photographs portraying the activities will be found on another
page of this volume. In addition to participating in the lecture
series offered by the Adult Education Committee of the Cen-
ter, Mrs. Harry Nachman this past year conducted classes in
Hebrew for Sisterhood members. This program is to be expand-
ed during the coming year.
Fellowship and Administration. A portion of our income
is devoted to strengthening the bond of friendship within the
Synagogue family. Through such programs as the Get-Acquaint-
ed Brunch for new members and Membership Tea, the purposes
and the meaning of Sisterhood were brought closer to the mem-
bership at large. Programs were put on at monthly meetings and
the membership advised of activities through the mail.
Special Projects. Practically every year needs arise in the
congregation for which the Sisterhood assumes the responsibilh
ty. Some year it may be a scholarship for one or two of our
young people to pursue studies in Hebrew. At another time it
may be providing furniture for class rooms. Kitchen equipment
has always been our responsibility. This year we have lent our
assistance with redecorating the Auditorium of the Activities
Building. A new set of drapes was needed and we supplement-
ed the appropriation for other decorations that will maintain the
beautiful Auditorium as a setting for High Holiday Services
and special family celebrations.
The annual Donors event for Beautification is the one oc-
casion during the year when members of the Sisterhood make a
special effort to acquire funds for the larger needs in carrying
out the objectives that the name implies. Synagogue furnishings,
as well as those just described for the Activities Building, are
the usual requirements. This coming year we look forward to
the completion of the school wing and additional classroom
furniture will be needed.
National Affiliation. The Sisterhood is affiliated with the
National Women’s League of the United Synagogue of America
from which we get guidance, program materials, information and
suggestions. We send delegates to the bi-annual National Con-
vention and to the regional conferences of the Southeast Branch,
which usually meets together with the Southeast Branch of the
United Synagogue. This past spring the latter was held at
Charleston, S. Car. and the next regional meeting is to be held
at St. Petersburg, Fla. Two years ago we were hosts to the re-
gion.

Home and Synagogue Partnership. One of the objectives
of Sisterhood is to strengthen Judaism and make it meaning-
ful in the lives of Sisterhood members and their families, both
in the Synagogue and in the home. Towards this end we have
established the Sisterhood Book and Gift Shop. The display of
religious ceremonial objects in the showcases found in the Lobby
of the Activities Building has already encouraged a great many
of our families to acquire beautiful pieces for their homes that
are religiously significant. The Gift Shop items are appropriate
for newly born, Bar Mitzvah, Bas Mitzvah, Confirmation and
Wedding gifts. From the small profits made, the Sisterhood
Book and Gift Shop Committee has been able to furnish much
needed equipment.
Many of the activities of the Sisterhood are portrayed on
other pages of this book. We look forward to another year of
increased interest in our continuing projects and initiation of new
ones. We aim to enrich Jewish living among all our members.

Jewish Welfare Society
The Jewish Welfare Society is the family and children’s casework agency of the Jew-
ish community. Affiliated with the Community Chest—United Fund, the local program
is almost entirely supported by our contributions to the Chest. One of its programs, the
re-settlement of New Americans, is financed by funds that you contribute through the
Jewish Community Council.
The agency began searching for a social worker with two years graduate training in
August, 1956. As of this writing, due to the great shortage, we have not as yet been
successful in locating one. Many of the future plans depend on how soon we can locate
one.
The program of the Jewish Welfare Society can be broken into six overlapping cat-
egories:
1 Family consultation and case work; 2. Child placement and work with unmarried
mothers; 3. Applications to River Garden Hebrew Home and follow up case work; 4.
New Americans, naturalization, immigration; 5. Transients; 6. Prison program.
The heart of the agency’s program is the family consultation and case work pro-
gram. About 9 out of 10 local cases were for counseling, planning and other services that
do not involve a direct outlay of funds. To help give the best service, the agency retains
the services of a psychiatrist, Dr. Merton Ekwall, whom the director consults when he
feels this would be to the best interest of the client.
During this year, two chlidren were placed for adoption and the agency had tempo-
rary custody of another.
Aside from the refugees from Hungary, the agency helped a number of families in
their efforts to bring over their relatives from Egypt, Morocco, Israel and other coun-
tries. Many families were helped with their restitution claims for Nazi persecution.
The agency also continued its work with transients and, under the leadership of Rab-
bi Israel L. Kaplan, its limited work with the prisoners at Raiford.

The A. B. Weil Lodge of B’nai B’rith continues to assist the National B’nai B’rith, an
organization of 400,000 members, in its program of Jewish philanthropy and good neighbor policies.
Our anti-defamation committee, due to efforts by A1 Schneider and Sam Siegel, executive
board members of the state Council, is diligently attending to problems of anti-semitic nature.
Our public relations committee is constaantly studying such problems as bible teaching in our
public schools, which is contrary to our American policy of separating church from the state, and
the revival of the Ku Klux Klan.
Lionel L. Gaines
In the past year we have continued our services to the Lake City Veterans Hospital and to the March of Dimes drive. We have taken under-
privileged children to local baseball games. The Hillel Foundation at Gainesville has received our support, the Hillel Players appearing in a play
at one of our meetings. The B nai B’rith also sponsors B.B.Y.O. activities, headed by Norman Friedman. We give aid to Israel in immigration, bond
sales and forest planting.
At present a program is being formulated io increase funds for our Scholarship Loan Fund. These funds are held in a special trust account
and are for local students who need assistance in furthering their college education.
The Lodge sponsors a Luncheon Club with Ben Cohen, President; Hyman Selber, Treasurer; Sidney Brown, Secretary. Members meet on the
second and fourth Thursdays at the George Washington Hotel. Civic leaders as well as outstanding leaders in Jewish life appear as speakers. The
only cost is the luncheon and a B’nai B’rith membership.

With the 1956-57 season a new post was established in our congregation and
Mr. David I. Weinstein assumed his duties as Administrative Director and Hebrew
instructor.
Mr. and Mrs. Weinstein and their two children came here from Denver, Colo,
where Mr. Weinstein was associated with one of the congregations. Mr. Wein-
stein holds a B.A. degree from the University of Denver, Colo, in Sociology and
has taken graduate studies in the field of Psychology. His Jewish training he
received from his father, Rabbi Reuben Weinstein of Buffalo, N. Y. and at the
Yeshivah in Cleveland, Ohio.
The various departments and officers of the Jacksonville Jewish Center are
serviced by the following secretaries: Mrs. Bernard Stegman, Mrs. Nathan Robin,
Mrs. Esther Fishier and Mrs. A1 Zadoff.

Education (Registration figures are as of the past year; regis-

Daily Kindergarten: Monday to Friday, 9 to 12 noon.
Four teachers and assistants.
Week-day Hebrew School:
1. Primary Grades: "Play way to Hebrew,” for
pupils 6 to 8 years of age, meets Monday and
Wednesday, 2:45 to 4 o’clock. 37 pupils.
2. Elementary Grades: Boys and girls above age 8
are taught on alternate days of the week, 3:30
to 5:30 or 4 to 6. 184 pupils.
3. Graduate class meets one afternoon and one eve-
ning a week. 26 pupils.
Sunday Religious School:
Sunday mornings, 10 to 12; Pre-Kindergarten
through 10th Grade, Confirmation Class, 24 teachers,
591 pupils.
Junior Congregation:
Sabbath and Festival mornings at 10 o’clock.
Youth Services:
Sabbath and Festival mornings at 10 o’clock for
pupils above the 8th Grade. The worship is held in
the Synagogue.
Minyan Club:
Sunday mornings at 8:45 for boys of Bar Mitzvah
age. Breakfast served at conclusion of the service by
the Center Men’s Club.
Adult Courses:
Institutes on specific subjects are arranged in conven-
ient units of study. In past years we offered "The
Story of Conservative Judaism” and the "Fiving Book
Series.” Special classes and discussion groups are ar-
ranged as called for.
Affiliated Organizations and Activities
(see the section devoted to each in this Year Book)
Sisterhood, Men’s Club, Couples Club, Amity Club,
Mr. and Mrs. Club, Young People’s Feague—young
adults, above age 18, U.S.Y. (Center Youth League
and Charles Palot Club—teen-agers.)
Scouting : Boy Scouts and Cub Pack, Girls Scouts and
Brownies.
Athletics: Programs for youth and adults, men and
women.
Social Events: Scheduled by the various organizations
on special occasions or in connection with regular
worship and meetings.
Community Service: The Center’s facilities are available
not only for the celebration of personal events, such as
weddings, and Bar or Bas Mitzvahs, but are also used
by youth and adult organization, civic groups and
inter-faith groups.
Center Bulletin: The "Center Bulletin” appears weekly
during the active season and is mailed to our mem-
bership and to interested persons whose names and
addresses are submitted to the office.
Center Year Book: For a fuller description of the Cen-
ter program and activities, the Center Year Book
published annually on the eve of Rosh Hashanah
offers a bird’s eye view of events for the preceding
12 month period, vital statistics of the membership,
Hebrew Religious Calendar for the coming year,
directory of community organizations and other de-
partments.

Jewish War Veterans
This is a national organization dedicated to Community and
National service. This Post meets every 2nd Thursday of the
month at 8:00 P.M. Its activities are varied in the pursuance of
ideals of this great fraternal order, which enjoys a world wide mem-
bership. Its founding dates back to the year 79 A.D.
This year our Post celebrated in proper manner the solemn and
festive holidays of our country and our faith. We participated in
all patriotic affairs, visited the hospitalized veterans and donated
blood to the stricken. We partook with great success in both the
United Appeal and the Israeli Bond drives. We were instrumental
in helping to find jobs and homes for our Hungarian Refugee
friends, aiding them to adjust to their new freedom. We sent a
delegate to the state convention. The 62nd National Convention
was held the week of August 13th at Boston, Mass.
LEONARD M. SILVER

Young People’s League
The Young People’s League, or Y.P.L., functions under the auspices of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center. In attempting to fulfill the purpose of our organization,
to furnish a program of service and congeniality for the single adults of the com-
munity, we have sponsored many and varied activities. We held one of the most
successful Post-Yom Kippur dances ever attempted. On Thanksgiving we had a
square dance and donated the entire proceeds to Muscular Dystrophy. Also worthy
of mention are: a Valentine dance, a visit to Hope Haven Hospital, a successfully
carried out program of home hospitality for Passover, the servicemen attending the
second night congregational Seder, and many other social functions. Our plans for
next year have already been launched. Instead of discontinuing our program for the
summer, we are carrying on monthly affairs. In the fall Y.P.L. hopes to become
even bigger and better than ever before, and we are looking forward to a highly
successful organization which will be a credit to our synagogue and our community.

Chairman of the Board
As Treasurer of your Building Fund for the past ten years,
I have collected and distributed hundreds of thousands of dollars
contributed by our loyal and generous members. During this
period we have erected our Activities Building, completely re-
novated our Synagogue building equipped with elevator, and
above all our Holy Ark and pulpit with suitable appointments
were installed. All this was provided by contributions, symbolic
of your good will and generosity.
Our most important undertaking for the coming year is the
erection of a School Building to provide adequate facilities for
our constantly growing community. We inaugurated a campaign
for funds to cover this project early in the year and have raised
approximately 50% of amount required to award contract from
a comparatively small segment of our membership.
Harry Gendzier, Chairman of our Building Committee, and
I thoroughly discussed this situation. In our opinion the funds
required would be forthcoming providing our entire membership
were fully aware of the crying need for completing the School
Building as quickly as possible.
Realizing the gravity of the situation, I asked to be relieved
of all other responsibilities in order to reorganize and revitalize
our campaign. With the assistance of loyal co-workers we shall
proceed to contact the balance of our membership as quickly as
possible.
Based on past experiences we feel confident you will respond
to our appeal and provide the funds still needed to proceed with
our plans.

HARRY GENDZIER
The Community Council is a democratic organization made
up of representatives of all Jewish groups which in turn elect
the officers, set policies and the framework within which the
Council operates. It is affiliated with the National Council of
Jewish Federations and Welfare Funds.
The Council is an incorporated body under the state laws of
Florida, its charter dating back to 1939. Since that date, it has
served the Jewry of Jacksonville in many ways. Its functions:
1. To help maintain the dignity and integrity of Jewish
life.
2. To develop an articulate, intelligent and effective pub-
lie opinion on community problems and interests.
3. To foster cooperation among all elements in the com-
munity.
4. To raise, receive, collect, hold and distribute funds for
Jewish causes, either directly or through presently existing or
subsequently established agencies.
5. To enable the Jewish community of Jacksonville,
through coordination of fund-raising activities to meet its obli-
gations to local, national and overseas causes in an intelligent
and effective manner.
6. To cooperate with other agencies for better mutual
understanding; and
7. In general, to plan for the welfare and development of
the community as a whole, engaging in activities as will contrib-
ute to that end.
To carry out these purposes, the council has created various
structures. The most important has to do with fund-raising.
Each year an annual Campaign is conducted to provide funds
for agencies, some 40 in number. These include aid for over-
seas needs through U.J.A., Hadassah and other groups; combat-
ting anti-Semitism; providing hospital facilities for the Tuber
cular and others who are ill; developing cultural facilities;
maintaining religious institutions and meeting needs of our own
local institutions, such as the River Garden Home.
To carry on its many other activities, the Council has es-
tablished various committees. The Refugee Committee in past
years has done a most important job in the acceptance, settle-
ment and integration of over 30 refugee families, including set-
tlement of 5 Hungarian families this year. Costs of housing,
medical care, education, in cooperation with the Jewish Welfare
Society, was provided.
Men and women in the Armed Forces are cared for through
the Armed Services Committee. Home hospitality during the re-
ligious holidays is provided and other needs of service person-
nel as the demands arise.
The Public Relations Committee quietly and effectively
stands ready to combat anti-Semitism and promotes a positive
program within the general community.
Calendars listing all of the holidays, Jewish and national,
were distributed to every teacher in the school system. Confer-
enees were held with the School Superintendent and others to
discuss and act upon questions of religion in the schools.
The COMMENTATOR, a monthly publication of the
Community Council, has been given national awards as a com-
munity newspaper. The Community Calendar is another service
of the Council and it has already demonstrated its value by re-
ducing conflicts in dates between organizations. All organiza-
tions now clear dates with the Council.
In addition to these functions, the Council maintains an
up-to-the minute list of the entire Jewish community; brings
the presidents of different organizations together regularly to
discuss mutual problems and, in general, functions as the Ser-
vice Organization for the Jewish Community.

Progressive Credit Union
The Progressive Credit Union, sponsored by the Jewish Progressive Society, has
continued to grow in its 16 years of operation. It is one of more than 10,000 Credit
Unions in the United States, having more than 5 million members and more than one
billion dollars in assests.
From a very modest beginning of 20 members and $500.00 in assets, we have
grown to over 350 members and more than $200,000.00 in assets. During our existence
we have made over 1600 loans to members, amounting to over $2,000,000.00 without
a single loss. We are organized to encourage thrift and provide a safe, convenient
medium whereby members can invest and accumulate savings. We are chartered by the
State of Florida and regularly examined by the Comptroller of the State, and the sav-
ings of members are safeguarded in several ways.
Life Insurance equal to a member’s savings up to $1,000.00 and Loan Protection
Insurance is paid by the Progressive Credit Union without any cost to the individual
member. The climax of each year’s activities comes with the annual meeting held in
the Jacksonville Jewish Center auditorium. Last year the Board declared a 2% dividend
on savings and 15% rebate of interest to borrowing members.

Jacksonville Chapter ; Senior
Hadassah
The Jacksonville Chapter of Senior Hadassah is composed of approximately
800 Jewish women who meet the second Tuesday of each month to serve together
in the ranks of the American Zionist movement. The evening group, the Hannah
Senesch Group, meet the second Monday of each month. Hadassah’s members join
in efforts to affect the national Hadassah program of health, vocational and educa-
tion services to Israel and the dedication of our local women to active participation
in the life and expression of American community affairs.
With more than 310,000 members in 1250 chapters and units in the United
States, Alaska and Puerto Rico, Hadassah today is the world’s largest Zionist
organization. While its pioneering efforts in medicine and social welfare have
permeated every facet of Israeli life, it has conducted significant activities on the
American scene to foster creative and intelligent living through education pro-
grams stimulating understanding of the Jewish cultural heritage. At the same
time, Hadassah participates in efforts to safeguard democracy and support of the
United Nations.
Since its founding, Hadassah has raised more than $115,000,000.00 in behalf
of the Jews of Palestine and Israel. This is a tremendous contribution—one that
was made possible only through the selfless devotion of Hadassah’s dedicated mem-
bers. Together with Z.O.A. Hadassah supports the Jewish National Fund for the
rehabilitation of the land of Israel and Zionist youth work through Young Judaean
Clubs.
Outstanding events of the coming year will be the opening luncheon to be
held at the Beauclerc Club on October 8, highlighting Hadassah Medical Organ-
ization. The Membership Tea will be a January event featuring a fashion show
at the Jewish Center. March is the month for Hadassah Hospital supplies and
the gala Donors’ affair is scheduled for April to honor Hadassah Donors.
Hannah Senesch Group
The Hannah Senesch Evening Group of Hadassah as part of Jacksonville
Chapter of Hadassah has given those women who can attend only evening meet-
ings an opportunity to participate fully in National Hadassah’s diversified program.
Membership is open to all Jewish women, with meetings on the second Monday
of each month.

RABBI'S MESSAGE
Words and Pictures
A picture is worth a thousand words, is an accepted
truism. Some words, however, are so meaningful, that thou-
sands of pictures could not portray them adequately.
Torah is one of those words. Older translations of our
traditional prayers adopted the word Law from the standard
English version of the Bible. Recent editions leave Torah
untranslated. The entire Jewish religious tradition is con-
centrated in the term that also stands for the Five Books
of Moses, the Bible, Talmudic literature and other sacred
writings.
Torah is most expressive of what the Jew means by reli-
gion. The Sefer Torah, the Scroll used during worship is our
sacred symbol.
The Cover Design
This year’s edition of the Center Year Book features the
Shavuot panel from the eight Festival carvings that embellish
the Holy Ark of our Synagogue. The Jacksonville Jewish
Center has in the past several years concentrated on beauti-
fying its Sanctuary. Cover designs of our year books have
displayed the rebuilding of the Synagogue with its most
distinguishing feature, the Holy Ark, in various steps of
planning and construction. Thank God, that task is achieved.
Our Children s Heritage
I take the opportunity of this Message to remind you
how far reaching the word Torah is, and to point out that
the symbolic representation on the cover is an inadequate
attempt to portray our dedication to the Jewish heritage.
Surely, one picture is not enough. In a sense, most of the
pictures in our Year Books are focused on Torah ideal.
When you leaf through this book you will find the pages
vibrating with the zest for teaching Torah to our children.
Another picture emerges—the need for classrooms. Sever-
al officers of the congregation are bringing this matter to
DR. SANDERS A. TOFIELD
your attention on the pages that follow. If you are not
acquainted with the facts from personal experience, the pic-
tures of this Year Book will confirm them. We thank God
for the blessing of a large child and youth population. As
parents and as synagogue members we are responsible for
their upbringing with vital religious indoctrination. Proper
classrooms are indispensable in that process.
Our Building Fund must have the means for carrying
through the classroom wing project with all possible speed.
A new school year opens under makeshift arrangements for
many of our pupils. It is our devout hope that while the year
5718 is still young our plans will be carried through to
successful completion.
Let me ask you to keep a mental note of the cover
design. You can draw a picture of Torah more glorious and
splendid than any artist. Transmitting the Torah tradition
to our children in the proper environment will give us a
generation that will exemplify the meaning of the Sacred
Word in their lives.
The Almighty grant us a blessed New Year in 5718 and
many good years to come.

Zionist Organization of America
The Jacksonville Zionist District continued its activities of public service and
education. On November 19 the Jacksonville District in conjunction with Senior
Hadassah sponsored a public rally at the Jewish Center featuring Prof. Joseph
Weil, Dean of Engineering at the University of Fla., and Dr. John B. Crane, noted
economist and columnist of "Europe Day by Day.” The Annual Israel Tree Festi-
val Banquet was held on January 31 at the Center with Rabbi Yaakov G. Rosen-
berg of Congregation Beth David, Miami, as guest speaker.
CANTOR A. MARTON
The District and Hadassah sponsored several meetings jointly for non-Jewish

For the past year the local Zionist District has had the largest membership
since the creation of the State of Israel. The Zionist organization is gratified over
the part Zionism has played in the realization of the State of Israel which will soon

Jacksonville Young Judea has approximately 120 in both senior
and junior clubs. The senior clubs are the Estherettes, Zionist Youth
Girls and Yehuda Jollies. The junior clubs are the Junior Maccabbee,
Israel-ites and Zionettes.
Senior clubs meet every other Wednesday evening and Junior clubs
every other Sunday afternoon at the Center. Programming follows the
four point program of Young Judaea: Jewish Living, American Affairs,
Group Activities, Israel and Zionism. In February Parents night was
held for all Clubs except the Junior Maccabbees who held their own. In
March the Annual Israeli Fair was held at the River Garden Home for
the Aged. Funds raised went to the Tsofim, the Scout movement in
Israel.
The Estherette Club introduced the Avodah weekend of study and
work, with discussions led by graduates of Tel Yehudah. A Seminar was
held at the Jewish Temple in May, and another in August.
The closing affair of Young Judaea each year, the Bikkurim Festi-
val, was held May 5 at the Jewish Center. Linda Gruenberg and Roselyn
Portnoy were announced as candidates to Tel Yehudah, and the follow-
ing to Machonah Avodah: Karen Armel, Annette Gottlieb and Ad-
rianne Perlman.
Jacksonville Senior Young Judaea won the plaque for the fourth
time for the Dance Group at the Winter Convention of Southern Young
Judaea in Augusta in December.
Young Judaea is sponsored by Hadassah and the Zionist Organ-
ization of America under the direction of the Youth Commission. Mrs.
H. Klausner is Chairman and Mrs. I. Brukner, City Director. Mrs.
Norman Moss, has been selected as the incoming Chairman. Young
Judaea is open to all boys and girls over 10 years of age.

Some 20 months ago, upon taking office, I made the
statement that community work and endeavor is a source of
enjoyment for those who participate in that activity and that
it was my objective and hope to obtain a great deal of
"Simcha” and joy while serving in the distinguished position
of President of our congregation.
The pleasures and honors that I have derived during my
tenure of office have been numerous and gratifying, the most
satisfying of which was to participate in the happiness and
rejoicing manifest at the many celebrations of Bar Mitzvah
and Confirmation.
We are thankful that there has been a number of these
joyful and happy occasions. Each year, we note, there has
been a substantial increase in such events over the preceding
one.
Analyzing the situation a bit further, one readily sees how
we come by this great blessing. That the enrollment in our
religious schools is constantly growing, is gratifying, to say
the least. We are thankful and heartened that our member-
ship has recognized the importance of the religious educa-
tion of our children. It has been the primary factor towards
the strengthening and the developing of our Synagogue-
Center. Increased numbers in our families and increased
membership bring more occasions for the proper spiritual
services of the synagogue.
Our Rabbi and his capable staff, in close harmony with
your lay leaders, have instilled dignity and enlightenment
into our religious life. Through their efforts our curriculum
and programming for religious education has been constantly
improved. Our children have obtained better spiritual guid-
anee and have grown religiously. Your leaders are striving
to maintain this pace of development.
Through the constant loyalty of our membership we have
built and modernized our institutional facilities to take care
of our religious and social activities. We are now in the
midst of the program for providing adequate educational
facilities to satisfy the needs of our growing population of
youth. Our congregation is fully aware of the necessity of

The Girl Scouts and Brownies, under the sponsorship of the Center Sisterhood,
have had a very busy and successful year.
The troops under their able leaders participated in all phases of the Girl Scout
program. The Girl Scouts did volunteer work at the Public Library, Cumberland
Nursery, hospitals, the Jewish Community Council and help with the Sisterhood
Bazaar. They were given the honor of being Color Guards at the city-wide Fun Day
at the Gator Bowl. They served as members of the City Council of the Girl Scouts.
Rene Slott was in charge of the Senior Girls Banquet held at the Center and Bar-
bara Ann Stillman was elected Treasurer of the Senior Girls of the Gateway Coun-
cil. The senior girls attended the Senior Scout weekend held at the Girl Scout camp.
All of the Girl Scout and Brownie troops took part in Girl Scout Sabbath and
served as hostesses after services. Arrangements were made by Mrs. Irving Kramer,
who was formerly Girl Scout Chairman, and her committee.
The Brownies were active in all neighborhood events. They presented an Is-
raeli Day at the Arlington Methodist Church. They danced at the city-wide Fun
Day at the Gator Bowl and took part in the "Massing of the Colors." The Brownies
also had a program of First Aid and learning about Scouting. They had a grand
closing party at the Birthday House at Storyland.

Center Sisterhood
We review another year of Sisterhood activity in the hope
that our membership of approximately 600 will take full satis-
faction in their achievements and that the rest of the congrega-
tion will see at a glance the importance of supporting the pro-
grams and activities of this auxiliary of the Synagogue.
Where does Sisterhood derive its funds with which to carry
on the manifold program? In the past year the primary sources
of income have been from:
1. Dues; 2. Food Show and Bazaar; 3. Rummage Sale;
4. Luncheons; 5. World Book Encyclopedia sales; 6. Miscel-
laneous.
Our income is budgeted to take care of the following pro-
jects that are carried on from year to year and expenditures
that arise on occasions:
Youth Activities. The two chapters of United Synagogue
Youth, C. Y. L. and C. P. C., are subsidized with expenses to
national and regional conventions and for the Leadership
Training Institute that is held late in August every year.
We also subsidize traveling expenses of delegates to the
Young People’s League National Convention.
Religious Education. Sisterhood provides the largest share
of funds for Sunday Religious School staff fees. We also fur-
nish prizes and awards made on various occasions throughout
the year and especially at the closing of the school session. Ap-
propriate refreshments are served to pupils in connection with
Holiday and Festival celebrations.
Girl Scouts. The Girl Scout and Brownie troops active in
our congregation are sponsored directly by the Sisterhood.
When the occasion arises we furnish the religious Menorah
award and support the movement in every possible way.
