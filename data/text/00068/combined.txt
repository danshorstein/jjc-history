Membership Committee
The Membership Committee’s overall
mission is to identify and cultivate the
values and activities that attract new
members and retain existing members for
synagogue life in the 21 st Century. It has been
apparent that “meaning and community” are
the key components to a thriving synagogue
membership.
This year the Membership Committee
is pleased to announce significant growth
in new member families. Several were
newcomers to Jacksonville. However, in some ways it was
even more rewarding that many of our newest members
were previously unaffiliated in the community. There was a
Shalosh Seudot on January 24th honoring new members of
2008. While new members will help us grow as a community,
membership retention is a continuous focus for the committee
and the Center board. Engaging our members, new and
established, with innovative programming and
volunteer opportunities has proven to be the
key to the Membership Committee’s success.
One of our more successful programs
has been the New Member Host Program.
It has been well over a year since the
Membership Committee implemented the
program, where a new member is matched
with a Host family or individual. It has proven
to bolster the welcoming atmosphere we strive
for at the Center and we are always looking for
volunteers for this program.
Thank you to the entire congregation for choosing to be
a part of this special community and for participating in the
mitzvah of hachnasat orchim, the welcoming of guests.

Burial and Mourning
Center Cemetery
The Center owns and maintains two cemeteries adjacent to the Evergreen
Cemetery in North Jacksonville. Both are open from 8 am until 5 pm every day except
Shabbat and Jewish holidays. To reach the Old Cemetery, travel north on North Main
Street to Winona Street. Make a right on Winona Street and go until you reach Gate
5. The Old Cemetery is on the left. To reach the New Cemetery, travel north on North
Main Street to 43rd Street. Make a right on 43rd Street. The entrance is at the next right,
which is Liberty Street.
During this past year, the cemetery committee has made significant improvements
to the New Center Cemetery. Signs have been replaced at the front gate, duplicating
the original plaques that were mounted when the cemetery opened, and section signs
have been updated and added for all of the more recent sections. The most significant
improvement has been the clearing of the second section, the construction of a driveway
culvert and road over the creek between sections 1 and 2 and a paving of the existing
access road. All parking will now be directed into that section and eventually the entire
section will be plotted for future grave expansion.
Loved ones can be remembered with a memorial plaque, to be placed on the
memory wall located in the Rogozinski Family Promenade, and a tribute in the annual
Yizkor booklets. You can also learn more about purchasing individual and family
plots at the New Center Cemetery. Details are available in the Center office and on
the Center’s website. Additionally, contributions can be made to the Cemetery Fund in
honor or in memory of loved ones. All donations will be used to enhance and maintain
our cemeteries.

Hevra Kaddisha
During the course of a lifetime, no one can avoid an encounter with death;
yet, it is an experience for which no one is prepared. Judaism embraces all of
life, of which death is a natural part, and so ascribes rituals which dignify our
departure.
The care of the deceased immediately following death is the solemn
responsibility of the Hevra Kaddisha, the holy society, consisting of dedicated
laypersons from the community. Members are knowledgeable in the traditional
duties, and perform them with the utmost respect for the deceased. The process
begins with the Taharah, purification, a ritual cleansing and preparation of the
body for burial. In addition, a Shomer, guardian, will stay at the side of the
deceased reciting required prayers until the burial.
It is gratifying to have a completely independent Jewish organization, under
the sole leadership of lay people, maintain such a high level of commitment and
dedication to one of the most important traditions in Jewish life.
Members of the Jacksonville Jewish community can take comfort in
the knowledge that the services of the Hevra Kaddisha are offered to all Jews
regardless of financial means or synagogue affiliation. Please feel free to call
any of our officers or rabbis with concerns or questions. “Dedication” is our
motto.

Center Men’s Club
‘Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life99
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Men’s Club is an organization dedicated to
the enrichment of Center life and the Jacksonville Jewish community. The
Men’s Club provides an opportunity for our Center men to work together
supporting, sponsoring, and participating in programs that bring meaning to our
slogan, “Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life”.
Our Men’s Club is an active member of the Federation of Jewish Men’s
Clubs (FJMC), an international organization dedicated to the ideals, philosophy and
traditions of Conservative Judaism. Our level of activities and Center participation
has earned your Men’s Club the First Place and Honorable Mention Torch Awards
from FJMC in years past, and our Yad Shel Chai program is now being implemented
in Men’s Clubs across the nation. We participate in a wide range of FJMC programs
and activities such as distributing Yellow Holocaust Memorial candles so that every
Congregant can honor the memory of the Holocaust on Yom HaShoah. We sponsor
educational programs based on the “Hearing Men’s Voices” series on issues
involving family, religion, career and health for the Jewish man in today’s world.
The Center Men’s Club works closely with Youth and Education leaders to
support your children and the Center youth organizations. We are available to lend
a helping hand with local activities and regional USY and Kadima conventions.
We sponsor the Yad Shel Chai (Hand of Life) and Double Yad Shel Chai awards
that recognize and reward our youth for reading Torah in our synagogue services.
The Men’s Club Martin J. Gottlieb Brit Hinukh award is given each Spring to one
teacher in the Center schools who best represents the life commitment necessary
to bring a quality learning experience to Jewish children. We provide hand-crafted
Israeli Kiddush cup sets for the Confirmation students and a metal finished Kiddush
Cup for Bar and Bat Mitzvah students. We sponsor and raise funds for the Horty T.
Gendzier Summer Scholarship Fund, which provides the opportunity for children to
attend Camp Ramah and other Jewish enrichment programs who otherwise would
not have been able to do so.
The Men’s Club is also a great way to make friends, have fun, and meet
people from our synagogue as well as our regional Jewish community. Our members
regularly get together on Sunday mornings to play pick-up softball, touch football
games or perform community service activities. Over the years, we have gone on
deep sea fishing trips, provided opportunities to meet with local Jewish celebrities,
and have had family outings to the Jacksonville Suns baseball games and other
local community sporting events. In the fall, our annual Membership Kickoff Party
features fun, food, raffles and the opportunity to schmooze with friends while we
watch the Jacksonville Jaguars play an away game on the big screen TV.
We are visible friends at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Throughout the year,
Men’s Club members help with Sukkot, Purim and Lag B’Omer programs. We lead
and participate in Men’s Club Shabbat and Armed Forces Shabbat. You will find us
ushering at morning services on Shabbat and on Holidays. We are your children’s
teachers, your community volunteers and your spiritual lay leaders.
We invite you to join the Men’s Club if you are at least 18 years old, male,
and a member of the Jacksonville Jewish Center community. I firmly believe that
the Center Men’s Club offers something for everyone. So come on out and join us
for an activity this year. I think you’ll enjoy supporting our Jacksonville Jewish
community and spending quality time with other guys just like you!
Dr. Daniel Wohl

Adorn is continually evolving. The INTERNATIONAL RED
CROSS has accepted MDA as a full member and ARMDI added

the United States.
MDA responds to all medical emergencies, from
pregnancies, heart attacks, strokes and illness to traffic accidents,
work-related injuries and terror attacks - 24 hours a day, 7 days
a week, year after year. Within minutes, one of MDA’s rescue
units arrive on the scene. Trained volunteers and professional
medical responders administer immediate help or transport
the patient to a hospital. They continually train to upgrade and
maintain their skills and to keep abreast of the latest technology.

back of a puzzle piece donated to a local shelter while the funds

would not be possible.

A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
Got Mitzvah?
One of the best known stories in
midrash about the giving of the Torah
depicts the Eternal as an itinerant
peddler, who unsuccessfully attempts to confer
God’s sacred teachings upon one people after
another. The nations of the world prove to be
sophisticated consumers - they want to know
more about the product before signing on
the dotted line. Accordingly, the descendants
of Esau turn God down because Scripture
forbids murder; the children of Ammon and
Moab decline the Divine invitation to accept
Torah because of its injunction against sexual
immorality; the Ishmaelites demur because
of the prohibition of theft. At long last, God comes to Israel.
Without equivocation the Israelites respond, “We will do and
hearken!” (Sifre Deuteronomy §343).
It is hardly surprising that teachers love to share this
story with students. Even adults are likely to smile at a tale in
which Israel soothes God’s hurt feelings by freely accepting
the Torah without a moment’s hesitation. Beneath the surface
the subtext of this popular midrash also implies the voluntary
nature of Torah - a message that resonates well with the
cultural sensibilities of 21st century America. Religion is
optional, right?
The reality, however, is a lot more complicated. In theory
we know that we have the right to desist from the practice of
Judaism, to cancel our synagogue memberships, to stop giving
to Jewish causes. Yet beyond the realm of the purely voluntary,
there are forces that impel us to stay connected. Some observe
because they feel a responsibility to their forebears; others get
involved out of a sense of obligation to their children. There
are those who remain within Jewish life because they see it
as an expression of God’s will, and still others who define
their commitment in terms of communal norms. Those who
call themselves Conservative Jews may not always experience
obligation through the prism of halakhah, but still subscribe to
a belief in a God and a Torah that commands us to answer a
higher call.
By exploring the motivations that lead us to embrace
mitzvot, we can begin to grapple with one of the most
fundamental religious questions. Why, exactly, is Jewish
tradition so important to us? Why do we practice certain
observances, but not others? There are some religious precepts
that will never appear on our radar screens; others that we feel
guilty about not doing; still others
that we would never dream of
dispensing with. What delineates
the boundaries between each of
these categories? How do we define
commandment and obligation?
Two years ago, Chancellor
Arnold Eisen of the Jewish
Theological Seminary, introduced
the Mitzvah Initiative to a select
number of congregations as a pilot
program. Designed to facilitate
dialogue, reflection and study about
the role of mitzvah in the lives of Conservative
Jews today, the initiative seeks to engender
broad conversation across the country about
the meaning of commandment as it finds
expression in our daily lives. The goals of the
program include: raising consciousness about
how the performance of mitzvot relates to a
greater concept of holiness; the construction
of personal narratives that incorporate mitzvah
through the lens of Conservative Judaism; the
creation of shared vision and practice within
the synagogue.
I look forward to introducing the
Mitzvah Initiative to the Jacksonville Jewish
Center in the coming year as a means of starting a much-
needed discussion about the role of commandment and
commitment in a religious culture where affiliation remains
voluntary. Beyond dialogue with congregants of varied
backgrounds, the Mitzvah Initiative will help us explore
engagement around various religious and spiritual activities
of the synagogue, including minyan, social action, tzedakah,
Shabbat and hospitality. In addition to playing a role on
our roster of adult education offerings, this conversation
might take place in a variety of venues: break-out groups at
congregational forums, parlor meetings in people’s homes,
Synaplex™ programming, board and committee gatherings,
Beit Midrash classes, and faculty meetings in the Center
schools.
We face many of the same commitment-related issues
as scores of other congregations: As the average age of regular
attendees at daily minyan steadily grows older, will we be able
to sustain weekday services a decade from now? How can we
better meet the challenge of finding volunteers and imbuing
them with a sense of sacred mission? What must we do to
transform the fee-for-service culture of membership dues into
one based on shared religious obligation? How do we nurture
the next generation of leaders, one that will grow organically
into positions of leadership through a lifestyle of engagement,
rather than attempt the cultivation of responsibility through
appointment to office?
It is said that when Israel received the Torah at Sinai,
every person heard God’s voice in keeping with his capacity
(Exodus Rabbah 5:9). That all of us will come to the table
with different religious sensibilities is a source of strength.
May we listen to and learn from each other’s individual
spiritual journeys. Above all, may
our voluntary acceptance of Torah at
Sinai lead us to a Promised Land of
self-actualized commitment in which
the performance of mitzvah becomes
its own highest reward.
Susan, Avichai, Elior, Itamar
and I wish you health, happiness
and much sweetness in the days
ahead. May we all be inscribed for
meaningful life in the New Year.

Volunteer Recognition
The Jacksonville Jewish Center held its Annual Meeting
on Monday night, June 15th. While synagogue
President Mitch Levine took a brief moment to update
everyone on the state of the congregation, the real spotlight
was on the valuable volunteers who make our synagogue the
vibrant place that it is. Recognition was handed out in the
form of “Gold Medals” to many of our volunteers.
There were several categories in which the “Gold
Medal” recipients took their prizes. The first group of
volunteers helped enhance all of the prayer services offered
here at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. These people included
minyan captains, Torah and Haftorah readers, Kol Train
musicians, Gabbaim and the choir, to name a few.
The next two categories of volunteers made our
synagogue more sympathetic and welcoming. Those who
extended acts of compassion and kindness, such as the
members of the Hesed and Hevra Kaddishah committees
were recognized. Volunteers who made our congregation
more welcoming were acknowledged. Greeters, ushers
and those who extended home hospitality to others in the
community also took home well deserved medals.
Our youth are the future of Judaism and everyone
who supported our children in the activities at the various
schools and youth groups were recognized. Other members
were making our congregation more of a learned community.
Those who either taught or took classes and SYNAPLEX
volunteers all medaled at the event.
Artists who loaned (and hung) their art to the Yergin
Gallery were recognized. The Men’s Club and Sisterhood
volunteers and board members all received well deserved
appreciation. Gratitude and medals were given to members
of the Board, including executive board members, and those
serving on the numerous committees that make the JJC run
smoothly. The small group of volunteers who quietly come
into the Center on a regular basis to help the office staff
with mailings, phone calls and archive maintenance were
appreciated, as well.
After the gold medals, certificates and “gifts”
were given as the results of the congregational volunteer
“Superlative Survey” were unveiled. At the very end of the
evening Mitch Levine recognized this year’s Volunteer of the
Year, Andrea Mail. —Committee Members-

We’re Here For You.
As we all know change is never easy; and change
is inevitable. Over the past year JFCS has been actively
involved with strategic planning and I am happy to share
our results with you.
We have reorganized all JFCS programs and services
into 5 core areas. One new core area, the Department of
Jewish Services, is devoted to meeting the needs of the
Jewish community. This department has a direct phone
line (224-2048) and dedicated staff to quickly assess
needs and route clients to services.
Although we closed the Community Kosher Nutrition program at Mt. Carmel,
we have kept serving meals through Kosher Kart. In fact, this home delivered meals
program is now more convenient and far reaching. Working with a new vendor who
delivers kosher frozen meals directly to the client’s freezer, menus can be customized
to meet specific health or dietary needs.
Our Transportation program continues to provide subsidized rides to medical
appointments, food shopping and for lifestyle (trips to the JCA, for instance). As
always, synagogue rides for Shabbat and holiday services are at no cost and are
unlimited.
We are grateful for the support of our many donors and the Jacksonville
Jewish Federation. It is a very challenging time as the client need has increased in
every area of our organization. With the ongoing support of our Jewish community,
lay leaders and our staff we look forward to meeting these challenges and sharing
our successes with you.
Stay tuned as we continue to “Change lives for better tomorrows.”

to serve the Jewish community more completely and better each year. We strive to welcome

synagogues and agencies in conducting special educational programs and celebrations.

5769 Simhat Torah Honorees
The High Holiday season reaches its joyous climax on Simhat Torah, the last day of the festival of Sukkot.
Because of our perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to as Israel’s “bride”. On Simhat Torah, we bring to
a close the annual cycle of the reading of the Torah and immediately begin anew. An ancient tradition evolved of
honoring worthy men by calling them to the Torah for the last reading, Hatan Torah, and the beginning portion, Hatan Bereshit.
In our synagogue tradition, we also honor the contributions made by an Ayshet Hayil, “Woman of Valor”.

Jacksonville Jewish Center
Parent-Teacher Association
The JJC PTA works diligently to put together a full and enriching year for students of
all three Center schools, JJC Preschool, the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School, and the
Bernard and Alice Selevan Religious School. The partnership of all three Parent-Teacher
Associations enhances the children’s educational experiences by providing program/events,
equipment, technology and so much more.
In the 2008-2009 school year, the PTA invested $2,500 in the classrooms. In addition,
funds have been allocated to purchase ride-on toys for the playground, Shalom Baby and
P.E. equipment, and VPK science and math materials for the preschool. We provided a new
Accelerated Reader program, supplemental language arts materials, technology, Orff instruments
for music classes, and more for the Day School. The PTA has supported the Religious School
by providing funds for the annual Sukkah in the Hut, Hannukah dinner, and the printing of the
Passover Haggadahs.
New events to look forward to for the 2009-2010 school year include a “stay and play”
after meet and greet in August, grandfriends classroom visit in September, Father/Daughter
dance in December, Mother/Son bowling evening in March and Parent luncheon in May. We
will continue to provide programs and services including Red Ribbon Day, Vision Screening,
Shabbat boxes and the publication of our yearbook, “The Pride.” We are able to provide so much
for our schools with your support through dues, fundraising and donations. Please support our
annual Chai Family Fun Run, our largest fundraising event, along with our annual magazine/gift
wrap sale coming up in the Fall of 2009.
Your PTA invites you to play an active role in your child’s development by participating in
any of our activities throughout the year. Becoming active is really easy - and it really makes a
difference! Whatever your time schedule allows or interests you have, we encourage you to get
involved and support the future leaders of our Jewish community.
We are looking forward to a Phenomenal, Top notch, Amazing year!

Center Youth Department
A Message from the Youth Director
Growing up at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center provides our
children with many fine
opportunities to be productive members
of their community, both inside and
outside the building. We are proud to
contribute to that growth.
One of our main goals is to teach
the children the importance of giving
back. That is repeatedly accomplished
through the many projects and programs
the Center Youth Department offers.
They not only learn to put smiles on other people’s faces,
but we’ve seen plenty of smiles on our kids’ young faces as
well. The memories created by these moments are, as the
commercial says, priceless.
The interaction among the children is also a special
part of life in the Youth Department. They often don’t
realize that they are learning and growing as they have so
much fun. The friendships they make and the relationships
they mold with the leaders of the Center are, again, things
that they will carry with them forever.
Through our advisors, your children
learn to be responsible, resourceful, original
and creative. Those adults are committed
to youth groups rich in Jewish values and
traditions. They serve as true mentors,
instilling a sense of pride in all our young
people.
We, at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
Youth Department, strive to grow along with
our children. We must encourage them to

A Message from the President
Community and spirituality, have
become buzzwords over the past few
years. The words seem to permeate
newspapers, board meetings, magazine
articles, blogs and even casual conversation.
The context in which the words are used will
invariably frame the content of whatever you
are reading or speaking about. The challenge,
of course, is moving from a broad based
discussion of community and spirituality and
actually plugging in towards implementation.
Community and spirituality can and often times do
live separate lives. Taken individually (disparate from one
another), it is possible to establish a community, without
ever really building one. Too, we can participate in a worship
service or a programmed event and leave without ever having
elevated our souls. Community and spirituality do not have to
be other worldly phenomena traveling on linear, but parallel
tracts. If we are to become a sacred congregation, then it must
be our goal to bring the forces of community and spirituality
together and cause them to intersect. That intersection would
occur at the center of our lives and truly frame our journey as
we become a kehila kedosha.
We would be remiss if we think we don’t already
possess a number of the characteristics of a kehila kedosha.
Still, our congregation could work at it a long time and fail
to become a sacred community. Sacredness is more than
doing the right things - though this is no doubt important. A
kehila kedosha pulsates with learning about finding kedusha
in every moment. A kehila kedosha uses study of sacred text
from past and present to shine a light on our own behavior
and give us insight into who we can become and what it
means to have a relationship with our creator.
A kehila kedosha elevates key moments and
gives them deeper meaning and significance.
A kehila kedosha creates connections where
we lift up and treasure our own story and
each other’s story.
Relationships are the keystones of
a vital community and a defining piece
of kehila kedosha. How we connect with
each other in genuine ways; sharing joy and
sorrow, valuing the sacred in each person,
will go a long way in determining what kind of community
we can become. Can we integrate different generations and
populations in real ways - fusing adults and children together
in worship, in classrooms, with gemilut chasadim - loving
acts which care for those in need and help to repair our
tattered world? Are we each willing to play a role in building
this kehila kedosha?
Sacred community is about our stories. We each have
one to tell and it is within these stories, shared with each
other and with our children that we open ourselves and our
relationships towards real meaning. Whether shared on-line,
in the lobby or as part of services; sacred community is about
your story and using your story to elevate our community.
Will we become a kehila kedosha? Do we believe that
it really matters? Isn’t it why we joined the Jacksonville
Jewish Center in the first place?
Susan, Stefanie, Kara, Emily, Jenna and I look forward
towards realizing this goal with you. We offer best wishes for
health and abundant happiness to you and your loved ones.
L’ shanah Tovah u’Metukah.
—Dr. Mitchell Levine
Dr. Mitchell Levine

How do we continue to make this vision, these values and
this rich heritage relevant for our children of the twenty-
first century? Our children are part of an ever expanding
global society, with connections to all parts of the world just
a technological fingertip away. Our task is to prepare them
to creatively problem solve, to write and speak
well, to think in a multidisciplinary way, and
to evaluate the vast amount of information
available to them. Our ultimate goal is to
produce committed young Jewish men and
women who are lifelong learners. How do we
accomplish this task and reach our goal?
The answer lies within ourselves, those who
teach. In Pirkei Avot, Rabbi Yishmael said, “A
person who studies in order to teach is given
the opportunity both to study and to teach.”
As the world of learning changes, so must we.
How fortunate it is then that the Day School has received a
generous one-year grant, enabling us to engage a Twenty-first
Century Learning Specialist. Her role will be to facilitate the
creation of our own unique professional learning community,
where we create, collaborate, and reflect with one another.
With our tools of technology, and their infinite uses, we can
then reach out to make global connections with the world of
educators and learners. Whether we are teaching math or
Mishnah, we must all find a way to challenge ourselves to
learn as we expect our students to learn.
Wishing you and your families a good and sweet year,
—Carole Goldberg

5769 was an exciting year for Adult Education. The
year began with a fresh installment of “Introduction to
Judaism” under the direction of Rabbis Lubliner and
Weiner.

led by Janice Knack.
Beginning Hebrew was taught by Lenny Maiman and

L’Hadassah” was introduced by Sylvia Lubliner.
Our Center Rabbis offered an array of on-going classes
including Rabbi Lubliner’s Monday Morning Mishna, Coffee
& Torah on Shabbat mornings and weekly Talmud study series.
Rabbi Weiner led his weekly class of “Think like a Rabbi.” The
newest addition to our clergy, Hazzan Holzer, led classes filled
with learning and laughing. “Afternoon at the Yiddish Theater”
was an instant hit with large groups of all ages. “Cantors and
Cocoa” was another innovative class offered.

the Center welcomed
practicing psychologist,
Dr. Dorree Lynn, author
of “When the Man You
Love is III, Doing your
best for your partner
without losing yourself"
Adult Education
and Synaplex joined

and Modernization: From Mishna to
Microsoft.” It was a fascinating review

archive, study and preserve sacred texts.

received addition this year: “Expressions

Judaica Art Class led by artist Kim

went “Food Network” and taught two kosher gourmet cooking
classes to standing room only crowds.

Wolfson, author of The Spirituality of Welcoming. After a full
weekend of Shabbat programming
and record crowds, the patrons of
the weekend enjoyed a lovely social

Lynn Maiman. Sunday morning,

synagogue community.

A Message from Rabbi Cohen
What will your gift be to the world this year?
Within the Rosh Hashanah liturgy
there is a text that reminds us to
be responsible stewards to the
world around us. “Hayom harat olanT -
“This day the world was called into being”
- are the words that come before we say
that today is the day all of the creatures of
the universe stand in judgment before God.
As we present ourselves and are judged for
our actions these words remind us of our
responsibility to the world and to God. As
partners in creation we bear responsibility
of the direction of the environment and the world in which
we live. Now more than ever, as we prepare to stand in
judgment before the birthday of the world, all that can be
done rests on our shoulders. As we approach the yamim
noraim, we should consider how we engage with the
world around us. If today is the birthday of the world and
we have a new year ahead of us, what will your gift be to
the world this year?
In the traditional Jewish model of our partnership
with God we are empowered to work the land - being
its stewards and looking after the earth. In the Book of
Breishit, God commands Adam “/ ’ovdah u ’Ishomrah, to
till and tend the land.” The Midrash in Kohelet Rabbah
tells a story that expresses a surprisingly modem approach
to the environment and this partnership: “When God
created the first man God showed him all the trees in the
Garden of Eden and said, ‘see how beautiful and perfect
are My works! All that I have created, I have created for
you. Therefore, be ever mindful: Do not abuse or desolate
My world. For if you abuse or desolate it, there is no one
to repair it after you.” (Kohelet Rabbah 7:26)
This approach places us at the center of the world.
We use nature though we dominate the world. As humans,
we are ostensibly the most important beings. As stewards
and partners with God, we are liable for
our actions. We bear responsibility for the
consequences that follow. Over the past
couple of years I have become involved
with the organization Hazon, a Jewish
organization that works to create a healthier
and more sustainable Jewish community
as a step towards a healthier and more
sustainable world. Known for organizing
bike rides in the United States and Israel,
Hazon also convenes a program to assist
Jews in understanding kashrut from an
environmental perspective. For nearly 3,000 years kashmt
has been an integral part of our lives as we look at what is fit
- what is kosher for us to eat. The work of Hazon reminds
us that the food choices we make have consequences for
ourselves as individuals and for the global community.
Purchasing organic products or belonging to a
community supported agriculture program may not have
been hallmarks of traditional Kashrut or what the rabbis
had in mind in the Midrashic story of God speaking to
Adam, but perhaps it is time for us to adopt practices that
deepen our commitment to thinking seriously about how
and what we eat. As stewards of the earth, the birthday of
the world and the yamim noraim are ideal times for us to
consider how we will mark the occasion. If we understand
that we are created in God’s image - nivra adam btzelem
elohim - and that we are an extension of God’s presence
on earth, then we have a tremendous gift to give to the
world and to one another this year.
Michele and Benjamin join me in wishing the entire
community a shana tova u ’metuka, a good, sweet and
healthy new year.
—Rabbi Raft Cohen

Governor’s Gold Seal Award for Excellence in Long-Term Care
“Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long...” It was this
commandment, which inspired the founding of River Garden Hebrew Home
in 1946. It was this commandment, which nourished the love, and care that
for more than sixty years has made River Garden a nationally recognized
model of excellence in its field. It was this commandment that made it
necessary to create a Home that is welcoming to families and encourages the
use of it as an extension of family space. River Garden residents are part of
the community not apart from it.
River Garden Hebrew Home is the anchor of a growing system of elder
services including the Home, The Coves, The Therapy Center, Resident Care
Services Program and Home Health Agency. The success of River Garden
is based on the tradition of meeting 100 percent of local Jewish community
needs and of serving rich and poor alike with excellence. The River Garden
Foundation accepts donations to help River Garden continue to meet its
mission. This would not be possible without substantial charitable support
from the community and the work of The River Garden Foundation to
stimulate planned gift annuities, trusts and bequests.
River Garden’s programs of short-stay rehabilitation, respite care,
long-term residential care, Alzheimer’s/Dementia care, Adult Day Program,
and outpatient therapies serve hundreds of people a day. These programs are
guided by a devoted, professional full time staff of over 275. Staff includes
physicians, nurses, certified nursing assistants, social workers, pharmacists,
activities specialists, beauticians, physical, occupational and speech
therapists, dieticians, and other support staff. In addition, scores of dedicated
volunteers help create a special caring community.
River Garden’s 35-acre Mandarin campus containing lush Florida
landscape, private lake, and meandering walking paths is the perfect setting
for residents and visitors. River Garden is a community where people live,
love and care. It is a community of many lifestyles and of rich Jewish content,
and a community which every day strives to live up to the mandate of the
Fifth Commandment.
River Garden
foundation
The River Garden Foundation is a philanthropic organization, established
to provide resources for programs and facilities to enhance the quality of life for
the aged. Its primary purpose is to fund the not-for-profit River Garden system of
services.
The foundation is dedicated to helping the elderly remain in control of their
own lives by assuring services to people at all levels of need and financial ability.
The aim of the Foundation is to keep pace with the changing needs of the
aging through programs that meet a broad array of health care needs, as well as
provide a full spectrum of residential options.
This elegant yet affordable
retirement community is located on River

Sponsored by the not-for-profit

growth during the retirement years, in a

A Message from the Early Childhood Director
Each child who enters the
Jacksonville Jewish Center
Preschool becomes part of the
Center family. We view each child as
a unique and precious gift entrusted
into our care. Our teachers are carefully
chosen not only for their experience and
credentials, but for their warmth and
caring. Our entire faculty works closely
with each child to help develop their
self-esteem through problem solving,
encouraging self-expression and offering
positive reinforcement. We take delight in our children,
care for their individual needs, and work tirelessly to
provide them with the greatest gift of all, a love for
learning. Everyone at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
Preschool is committed to supporting our families in
raising healthy, happy, and secure children that will be
successful in school and in life.
We offer a unique Judaic program led by Morah
Rachel & Morah Miriam. They visit each classroom three
times a week and work with our wonderful classroom
teachers to integrate Jewish values, music, stories and
crafts into our everyday curriculum. Rabbi Lubliner,
Rabbi Cohen and Hazzan Holzer enrich our program
through sing-a-longs, cooking projects,
dramatic story telling, ceremonies, and
answering questions.
We welcome all our parents to join
our Preschool Parent Association. Each
month we meet to share our joys, current
preschool happenings, and to plan future
events. Our parents are an important
part of our school. With their help we
are able to offer creative programming,
baked snacks, wonderful story times and
so much more.
We believe that it takes a village to raise a child
and we are honored to be an important part of our
families’ lives. We look forward to a wonderful year
working with our families and watching our children
blossom.
On behalf of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
Preschool faculty and staff, I wish you a Shana Tova
U’Metukah, a Healthy and Happy New Year.

2009 - 2010 Center Choir
For over 59 years, the Center Choir has been enhancing
services on the High Holidays and Shavuot with moving
liturgical music. Over the years, they have given numerous
concerts featuring members of the choir, and nationally
acclaimed soloists. Many of the current members have
been singing with this group for twenty years or more. New
members are always welcome. Call Raymond Cohen 733-
6979.
Scouting
Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14 completed anther adventous year! The boys
worked on their merit badges and learned basic boy scout
skills. They attended summer camp in North Carolina at
Camp Boones Boro at Camp Daniel Boone. They worked on
their merit badges and scouting skills.
Senior Patrol Leader Joey Dranetz lead orienteering
class, while the patrol leaders, Charles Pitt and Zach Sandler,
worked hard educating the new scouts in scouting basics.
Bruce Goldring did a fine job as scout master and we thank
him for all his efforts.. We welcome the new scout master of
Troop 14 - Jeffrey Sandler. A lifelong scout, he is bringing
new ideas and to the troop such as the first Court of Honor,
Family Night and the Spring camp out, held at Jekyll Island.
2010 will be the 100th Anniversary of scouting in
America. Troop 14, founded in 1919, is one of the oldest
troops in Florida.
Boys, ages 11 to 18 are encouraged to become part of
the proud tradition of scouting at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. Attend our meetings on Thursday evenings at 7 PM
in the Center’s Neal F. Portnoy Youth Lounge.
— Dayan Sandler
Cub Scout Pack 14
Cub Scout Pack 14 has been a part of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center youth program for 69 years and is the oldest
pack in northeast Florida with a continuous charter. Scouting
continues to create global leaders with strong abilities while
enjoying wholesome outdoor activities with friends and
family.
