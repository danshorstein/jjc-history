Art, the University and the Center . . . .
Those of us who are identified with the synagogue,
Judasim, and the Jewish people are under constant
obligation to ask ourselves the question of purpose.
There is a continuous need on our part to define and
redefine objectives and the reasons we have banded
together as a congregation and as a community. We
are not proud. We seek enlightenment from every pos¬
sible source.
On my summer reading list was a book by Ben
Shahn, the painter, entitled “The Shape of Content”.
A few years ago Mr. Shahn was invited to deliver a
series of lectures at Harvard University and what he
said, I think, can be applied to our search for defini¬
tion. Mr. Shahn was bemoaning the fact that an artist
on the faculty of the university does not usually pro¬
duce memorable works. Nor does the university itself
pursue the goals of art with great seriousness, for it
has other more pressing concerns, the study of liter¬
ature, science, philosophy, language, etc. He makes a
number of pointed observations.
First, “Individuals and institutions in the prac¬
tical world customarily look to the university for the
most advanced work and opinion obtainable.”
Second, ”Ideas and leadership flow out oi the
university and into general currency.”
Third, “Individuals who teach and work under
the university aegis are actually working in the center
of their field and not on its fringes.”
Fourth, and this is Mr. Shahn’s lament, “The
student of art in a college is almost required to guard
himself against becoming involved or too serious about
his art. He will dabble . . . and must not . . . make
of art a field of major interest.”
I suppose it is almost automatic with me, beyond
my control. As I read Ben Shahn, I immediately trans¬
posed into my own Jewish key. From art I moved
instinctively into the synagogue and what is expected
of it. Perhaps it was Shahn’s constant reference to the
university as center which put me on. An easy thing,
therefore, for me to link this Center, this Synagogue,
Judasim, the whole enterprise in which we are en¬
gaged, and which serves as one of the purposes of this
Year Book, edition 5727, to ask ourselves a number of
questions.
First, Do people have a right, I thought, to look
to us for the most advanced work and opinion obtain¬
able in the realm of Jewish values and human, ethical
and moral aspirations?
RABBI JEROME LIPNICK
Second, Should not ideas and leadership for the
transformation of our society flow out of the synagogue
into general currency?
Third, Ought not those who belong to our fellow¬
ship and who find themselves under our aegis actually
work at the center of the field of problems that con¬
front us and our world, not its fringes. Each Jew, each
family, to the best of its ability, artist?
So many of us are so preoccupied with our
customary pursuits and we guard ourselves against
becoming too seriously involved. We dabble, and do
not make Judaism and its serious demands upon us
our major field of major interest.
Our search for a purpose and definition, it seems
to me, must express itself along the lines which Shahn,
and I think our heritage, have indicated. We must ex¬
press ourselves in terms of the most advanced work
and the most advanced opinion, fully involved, not
dabbling; at the vortex, not at the periphery of the
social, political and religious concerns, shaping our¬
selves and the content of our time, and our history.
As we begin another year of our common quest,
I wish to salute and congratulate Cantor Abraham
Marton on the completion of his twenty years of serv¬
ice and devotion to our congregation and to the com¬
munity. My hopes for him, his wife and his family
are for many years of continued service.
RABBI JEROME LIPNICK

President’s Message
At the approach of the New Year, I want to
express my gratitude and appreciation to the large
number of our membership, who have so obligingly and
graciously cooperated with the leadership of the Cen¬
ter throughout the past year. Because of this combined
effort, we were able to record a year of financial prog¬
ress, a year of unity and good will.
Due to our Rabbi’s effective planning, the adult
educational program was a richly rewarding one. Ev¬
eryone who attended and participated in it certainly
derived a wealth of information and gained knowledge
on a wide variety of very interesting subjects. There
was a deep sense of gratification felt in the educa¬
tional phase and a pleasurable experience on the social
hours that followed each lecture and discussion. We
are indebted indeed to Rabbi Lipnick for the excellent
manner in which he prepared and presented those lec¬
tures to us.
We truly are delighted and proud of the products
of our Religious School. It is heart warming and en¬
couraging to see the great number of our pupils, who
participate and conduct every facet of the Sabbath
Services. They are well poised and perform with abil¬
ity and ease, both in the chanting of the Service and
in the Torah reading. It is almost a weekly source of
joy to all of us.
JACK BECKER, President
Many thinks are due to all those who have given
so much of their time and energy for the welfare of
the Center. The more we give, whether it is of our
substance or of our time, the greater is the joy of life.
The Officers and Board Members join me in best
wishes to all for a year of good health, happiness and
Peace. Le Shanah Tovah Tikatevn!
It gives me pleasure to welcome Mr. W. Z. Gold¬
berg as our new executive director. Mr. Goldberg has
been a member of the Center Board and an ardent
worker in our Synagogue for many years. We have
great expectations and look forward to a year of fruit¬
ful service. He has the support and cooperation of
the entire Congregation. We wish him and his family,
good health and success in his new endeavor.
Let us become more cognizant of the important
role the Center occupies in our respective lives, and
support every phase of its activities to the best of our
ability. The religoius and educational programs de¬
mand our financial support and our personal partici¬
pation.
BURTON I. GROSSMAN
Chairman of the Board

If you were the wisest man in the world and could have but one wish
granted you, what would you pray for?
FOR PEACE?
FOR POWER?
FOR LONG LIFE?
FOR WEALTH?
FOR LEARNING?
The question is not hypothetical.
It was asked of King Solomon in his day.
He answered none of the above; instead — if there were only one thing
he could have — he asked to be able to tell the difference between good
and evil.
He asked for an understanding heart.
Why?
Today is the birthday of the world.
Whatever was our yesterday, today as every day we start anew.
Will we — as individuals, as families, as nations—
meet problems today we have not yet solved?
Of course we wi
Must we, today, face evil as well as good?
Of course we must.
But can this evil defeat the good?
It cannot.
For the good is stronger than evil.
We can, therefore, without despair, without discouragement,
set ourselves to any problem we face today and tomorrow.
We need only realize that ours is not to complete the task,
yet neither can we neglect it.

This book is respectfully and affectionately dedicated to
Cantor Abraham Marton and his family, for twenty years
of devoted service to the congregation and the com¬

If members of the congregation continue addressing
Cantor Marton in sentences of twenty words, credit the
idea to his colleague Hazzan Max Wohlberg of New York,
not Western Union. It was Cantor Wohlberg who called
our attention to the fact that two of our favorite blessings
of the Amidah consisted of exactly twenty words, the num¬
ber of years the Cantor has been at the Center. “Sound the
Shofar proclaiming our freedom” and “Cause the dynasty
of David to flourish” were expressive of Cantor Marton’s
attachment to Zionism and his love for Israel.
The twenty word proposal was not aimed at brevity or
succinctness, for no one Sunday night wanted to limit his
effusive expression of regard and affection. But art is con¬
cerned with form and this was the way an artist chose to
give form and symbolism to an occasion that evoked so many
warm memories for another artist.
As a touching climax to his “Torah of Twenty”, Cantor
Wohlberg chanted a prayer in Hebrew that began “Bless
us, oh our father, all of us together, with the light of our
presence and ended with the “Yevarechecha”. (count the
words — twenty).

impressive and expressed the art of the interpreter of Scrip¬
ture. Cantor Marton was described as an eminent Hazzan

A Man, A Melody, A Congregation, A Tradition . . .
and musician and choral conductor of talent and excellence
in his own personal right; then, as a man of vision and
insight whose love of Zion has been expressed daily in teach¬
ing and in song and in Zionist political and cultural activity;
and third, the overseer, the leader, the man of responsibility,
dedicated to the transmission of his zeal and skill by train¬
ing and inspiring others.
The evening began with a service in the sanctuary, and
for me it will remain as one of the most memorable in which
I have participated. Cantor Marton, clutching to the Amud,
the Choir seated in the balcony, this time not out of sight
in the choir loft, and the Cantor’s family proudly seated in
the front row, everyone tense with excitement and expecta¬
tions as they reflected on what these two decades of prayer
and melody have meant.
At the banquet my eyes were riveted to the table at
which sat the three brothers Marton and their families and
the Cantor’s two daughters, Barbara and Shelley. I watched
the joy that illuminated their faces with each address and
tribute. For some unexplicable reasons this was the first
time that two of his brothers, Benjamin and Joseph had
seen each other in 40 years. Amazing that the Cantor, over¬
whelmed and moved as he was, had the presence of mind
in his own response to quote the verse in Genesis when Joseph
the Viceroy, made himself known to his brothers, and beheld
Benjamin.
It came as no surprise when our President rose to offer
his tribute of appreciation in behalf of the congregation, but
we were all deeply moved when he concluded his remarks in
the Hebrew language. His thesis: each occasion that helps
to perpetuate our people must provide room for the tongue,
that all our generations have revered and held sacred. To
have spent twenty years of the four millenia of the Jewish
people’s search for the true and the good in one congrega¬
tion is an achievement of no small consequence.
It was an evening of warmth and strong sentiments that
made me think of a passage in Peretz that only the power
of music, the power of man, and the power of a mighty trad¬
ition could evoke.
4‘Sunday evening was a memorable evening for the
Mar tons, the Jacksonville Jewish Center, and for Jewish
Life, may they increase!” Count the words — twenty. And
whether or not you say it in as many words, it is true.

The Choral Society at the Annual Concert
On Sunday, May 7 the Center Choral Society and
Glee Club presented the 19th Annual Concert of
Jewish Music and featured Hazzan Isaac Goodfriend
of Ahahath Achim Congregation, Atlanta, Georgia as
guest artist. The theme for the Concert was “Contri¬
bution of East European Jewry to Jewish Music.”
The Choral Society is the parent organization of
the Center Choir that serves on Friday nights and an
augmented Choir for the High Holidays.
The Glee Club is composed of members of our
Religious School and performs particularly at various
Holidays in the year.
The entire musical program is under the direction
of Cantor A. Marton. The music program of the Cen¬
ter was organized in order to serve our congregation
and to stimulate an appreciation for Jewish music and
culture.

Each member and prospective member will be contacted

to the majority of the circle. The circle will contain a Chair¬

to contribute for a specific meeting.

Create a warmer atmosphere with more personal
contact.
Create a source of supply for every facet of Sister¬

Increase attendance at ALL Sisterhood functions,
by creating greater interest of overall program.
Discover and develop potential leadership,
to achieve better understanding of aims, objectives,
purposes and policies of Sisterhood.
Now let’s all work together as a really active Sisterhood—
so that the meaning of Sisterhood is really “Sister-Friend”

Jewish education has always been a primary con¬
sideration of any individual Jewish home following the
Biblical instruction “teach them diligently unto thy
children. ”
Should Jewish education end with the Bar Mitz-
vah ceremony? This aspect was made a major consid¬
eration in the school’s policy for Bar and Bat Mitzvah
preparation. Emphasis has been placed on natural use
of skills at regular services. To a great extent pupils
are not trained for the personal ceremony during the
last months before Bar Mitzavh. The policy has been
that students must show they know how to lead the
basic portion of each service for Sabbath and holiday
occasions. This policy is being continued with greater
emphasis for the coming year.
In respect to further division of the school, both
Junior Congregations and the Sunday morning Minyan
Club have been divided into intensive and extensive
sections.
With the limitation of Foundation School to three
grades, the Hebrew School had to accommodate these
advanced students on their own level. The curriculum
is designed to meet the needs of older students on the
same level as the younger graduates from Foundation
School, who are being kept together to maintain their
high spirits.
In the Sunday Religious School, primary assemblies
have been re-instituted with the help of teachers of this
department. Advantage was taken of the local Israeli
group which includes a number of skilled young people
who could add their knowledge and Hebrew skills to
our program. For the first time the prayer courses have
reached a regular analysis of the High Holiday services
so that now a special study is conducted of the prayer-
books used throughout the year. An innovation in
Sunday School is to conduct oral tests of individual
students in the School Office. With our duplicating
machine, we are now able to produce detailed study
sheets for each lesson. The school is developing its
own workbooks to accompany the courses.
Special thanks are due to Jack Shorstein, Chairman
of the Education Committee, and its sub-Chairman,
Sheldon Gendzier.

In so many ways, C.Y.L.’ers are proclaiming “this
was the year that was.” We worked and studied to¬
gether in a unity that was to bring rich rewards at
the end of the year. Anti-Semitism and Comparative
Religion were but two of the main topics of study for
the year. Requests for copies of our program in honor
of Israel’s Independence Day poured in from all over
Ha Negev Region. We also took time out for fun —
the trip to Cape Kennedy in November when we met
the Satellite Beach chapter and toured the space com¬
plex together; and a highly successful progressive din¬
ner in March.
Conventions gave us the opportunity to expand
our spheres of knowledge and friends. St. Louis was
the scene for National Convention. There our eight
delegates swelled with pride as Southeast Region, for
the fifth consecutive year, won a Regional Award. Na¬
tional awards to six of our members were also received
there. In March a happy busload left for W. Palm
Beach and Regional Convention and an even happier
group returned home after the election of Eliot Safer
as Regional U.S.Y. President. The Tri-City Kinnus
hosted here gave us another chance to grow and learn.
Successful conventions are the result of hard work and
Morry Edwards and Mayer Becker, co-chairmen cer¬
tainly proved that. The Tri-City elections were almost
a clean sweep for us as Debbie Morris was elected
President and Martin Berman, East Coast Vice-Presi¬
dent. The Kinnus was topped off with our own Annual
Installation Banquet, which was one of the most suc¬
cessful in recent years. New officers were installed by
Mr. Louis Safer and awards were presented to the
year’s most outstanding C.Y.L.’ers. The boy’s award
was won by Eliot Safer and the girl’s award by Hilda
and Gilda Goldman.
The summer saw another successful L.T.I. at
Camp Blue Star in Hendersonville, N. C. There our
chapter received an award for outstanding achievement
in study, prayer and program. We also carried home
the honor of winning the Region’s highest award —
The Regional Programming Award.
Under the able leadership of this year’s president,
Eliot Safer and his excellent officers and board, we
have concluded a most significant year. Special thanks
to Hilda and Gilda Goldman for their outstanding pro¬
gramming. Also many thanks to our advisors, Rita
Weinstein and Sheila Kramer, for their guidance and
support past, present and future.
Now we look forward to the continuation of our
efforts under our newly-elected officers headed by
Martin Berman. May we attain the goals we have set
for ourselves.

The Jewish Single Adults Club of Jacksonville,
sponsored by the Jewish Community Council, has
been active in Jacksonville for the past year. The pur¬
pose of the Club is to bring together single Jewish
adults who wish to mix socially with other single
people. The Club also brings together new people
who have just come into the city.
Jacksonville has a definite need for such a club.
There are a great number of single adults in the city
who would ordinarily have no other way of meeting
Jewish single men and women.

barbeques. An event is held about once a month with
invitations going as far as Atlanta and Tampa. People
come from Tallahassee, Gainesville, Daytona and many
other cities around the northern part of Florida and
southern part of Georgia.

new people and renew old friendships!

ATID has now completed its third year of pro¬
gramming for our congregation’s college students.
During the December vacation, the group gathered
for a Friday evening reunion to share the experiences
of the students on various campuses.
This past summr ATID continued its program of
weekly discussions at the home of the members. The
first series of discussions dealt with “Civil Rights and
OUR Changing Community”. The first session, con¬
ducted by Jacksonville’s former mayor, Louis Ritter,
focused upon the Negro in local politics and upon the
deplorable condition of low income housing. The sec¬
ond session related to the economic and sociological
aspects of the problem and to the vicious cycle of
poverty. The guest speakers wer Gordon Bunch, Exe¬
cutive Director of Greater Jacksonville Economic Op¬
portunity, and Rod Freeman, a staff worker with
G. J. E. O. The final session in this series examined
the position of the local Jewish community in the
matter.
Meeting held in Gainesville, Florida
The second series of discussion dealt with topics
of contemporary interest to the students. Among these
were the concept of prayer, Jewish theology, the new
sexual morality, and the war in Vietnam.
The last series of discussions focused upon the
theme, “American Judasim — A Problem for Sur¬
vival”. The aspects of the problem discussed included
the approaches of the various movements within Juda¬
ism to the challenge, the threat posed by intermar¬
riage, and the roles of existing institutions, especially
those of the college campus.
ATID also worked in cooperation with G. J. E. O.
to sponsor Sunday afternoon outings for underprivi¬
leged Negro children of East Jacksonville. Among these
were trips to the Mayport Carrier Basin, the Castillo
de San Marcos in St. Augustine, the Jacksonville Zoo,
Meadowbrook Dairy, Fort Caroline, Fire Station No. 1,
a boat ride on the St. Johns River and a swimming
party at Seminole Beach.

20th ANNIVERSARY WITH THE JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER — 1967
Written by Mrs. Rose Sloat
Look back twenty years . . .
You were invited by the Center
And engaged in Jacksonville as a Cantor.
You were young and honeymooning with your
charming wife
A committee came to meet you,
And with respect they greeted you.
They placed you in a lovely hotel with luxurious
lobbies,
But I had the honor to have you as guests, for your
first Shabbos.
On an occasion some time later,
I had the pleasure of having you for a Seder.
Pearl sent me a thank you card.
You not only came to my house, but into my heart.
The first Kabbalos Shabbos and his beautiful
Kiddush
He understood the Payrush Hamilos.
After you conducted your first Shabbos service
With your correct Ivra and your sweet voice,
The Center made a very fine choice.
Your duties are more than just a Cantor,
You also teach Bar and Bas Mitzvah students who
come to the Center.
We love your Choir with their voices ringing,
And also the Choral Society when they are singing.
You formed a Sunday morning club in your opinion
For the boys to lay tefillin and da ven with their
own Minyan.
You established a Glee Club for the young children
To cultivate their voice, which one is the best in
your choice.
With your encouragement and effort
You inspired some young boys from the Center
Who some day will make fine Cantors.
In joy and sadness the Cantor is near.
When he makes an Almen rachmim, you can’t help
but shed a tear.
For each Simcha, the Cantor is there,
With his sweet voice adding happiness everywhere.
Once a year he makes a Concert for our amuse¬
ment,
The proceeds he sends to the Cantor’s Institute,
the whole amount.
The Center duties take a great deal of his life,
Many times he has to neglect his children and his
wife,
But Pearl is sweet and understanding,
Adds encouragement and is not demanding.
Pearl also does her share,
She helps the Sisterhood when work is there.
How they teach the children
Is a credit to the whole staff of the Center.
Reading the Torah, the Maftir, the Musaf,
On Purim the Megillah and Yom Kippur Kol
Nidre,
Musaf and Neila with their own K’heila,
But the melodies and music belong to the Cantor.
In the Zionist organization and many other places
He is so active and handy.
Let’s hope for another banquet in the next twenty.
In the twenty years, with God’s help
You’ve made a success.
Loved by your Congregation
And the fine family you possess.
In conclusion, I wish you and Pearl, Shelley and
Barbara
Good health, long life, Nachas from the children
And your voice should be with you all your life.
That is the Brocha
From me and my Mishpocha.
Your Friend, Rose Sloat
