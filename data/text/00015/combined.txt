Rabbi s Message
MEANING OF THE HOLY ARK
(Excerpts from acknowledgment address at
Dedication Service)
It is gratifying to see that our synagogue's shrine has
something to say to the wider community. As time goes on
it shall, and properly should have, more meaning to our
fellow citizens who seek the Lord in sincerity and truth.
Newspapers of our city showed great interest in our
new sanctuary. They caught the spirit of the symbolism
written large by expert handcraft in the design of our Holy
Ark and pulpit. But I must say, without slight to their
competence, that they did not exhaust the inspiring message.
The Florida Times-Union and Jacksonville Journal would
have required special supplements to approximate such an
aim.
To you, our honored guest, Dr. Simon Greenberg, Vice-
Chancellor of the Jewish Theological Seminary of America,
these representations speak in an articulate idiom.
Prominently emblazened on this lectern is the emblem
of the Burning Bush, inspired by seal of our beloved Semi¬
nary.
The curved panels flanking the doors of the Ark indicate
more profoundly how we aim to transmit the guidance of
the fountainhead of Conservative Judaism in America to
those whom we serve religiously. These two panels rise as
pillars of faith in support of the placque of the Ten Com¬
mandments. With artistic subtlety these ceremonial objects
spell out the sacred cycle of holy days on which our tradition
revolves.
The message is Jewish in content, but all-human in in¬
tent. They proclaim solace and confidence to a humanity
that cries for meaning in life. Can it be denied or over¬
looked that the world is nagged by the cruel realities of a
war of nerves! Conflicting interests on the domestic scene
give the shudders to sensitive souls. Most of us grope for
sufficient spirit to meet the tensions of daily routine.
The sequence of sacred occasions remind us of historic
realities that transcend the turbulence of the moment. Ar¬
ranged in columnar fashion they invite us to lean upon the
sense of holiness in life. God’s grace, love, and protection
are advanced here as our source of strength. For oppressive
thought there is reliance. Countering an aggressive impulse
stands humility. Above the futility of many of life’s mo¬
ments hovers divine affection.
We pray God’s help in bringing these lessons to our
membership, to American Jewry, to Americans of all faith,
races and cultures. The Almighty uphold us in this resolve.
RABBI SANDERS A. TOFIELD

RALPH S. MIZRAHI
President
OUR COMBINED MESSAGE
The message on this page in itself portrays a harmonious
partnership that exists in our congregation. Your President and
Chairman of the Board combine their thinking in addressing their
fellow members on some important facts and the obligations that
issue from them.
The past decade has witnessed great strides in our synagogue.
The congregational administrative structure, headed by our esteemed
Rabbi, has been responsive to the changing needs of the community
and at the same time achieve stability. The lay leaders have de¬
veloped more and more experience and are able to give advice and
counsel to the new leadership—a leadership guided by the congre¬
gation, democratic in its approach, endeavoring to breach the gap
between youth and elders, approaching every problem with the view
of providing the congregation with the best.
The physical facilities developed over the years are obvious. We
have a spacious, modern structure, welcoming and awaiting our
members to avail themselves, spiritually and socially.
Occasionally one wonders—“What is lacking? Why is it difficult
to get active participation of so many of our members ?” Is it the
lack of understanding or the lack of information? We feel that the
understanding lies within the hearts of all our members. Maybe
through information, we can bring out that essential touch which
has perpetuated our existence over the centuries.
Through education we build leadership. The school system of
the synagogue is the keynote of our faith. Our membership and
prospective members should be interested in the following facts and
information regarding our educational program, described in full
in a special section of this Year Book:
Sunday Religious School 500 pupils 18 teachers,
5 assistants
Hebrew School 200 pupils 6 teachers, full
or part time
Daily Kindergarten-Nursery 65 pupils 5 teachers
Three buses plus private cars furnish transportation for our
Hebrew School and Kindergarten to and from all sections of the city.
The past year, we had 26 Bar Mitzvahs, 2 Bas Mitzvahs, and
a Confirmation class of 23. Each year the number increases and in
each category there are always found a goodly number of out¬
standing youngsters who have acquired the knowledge, inspiration
and desire to participate as leaders or devoted workers in our youth
organizations.
The nominal fees charged in these various branches of our
educational systems are far less than the cost and must be subsi¬
dized from dues by our membership. The more this program de^
velops, the more subsidizing is necessary and more dues are required.
We must depend on the generosity of our congregation to continue
providing our children with the best in Jewish education.
The dedicated lay leadership are sacrificing of themselves and
are working with enthusiasm and drive to build this institution to
even greater heights. The congregation has its obligation to ease
this task of cooperating to the fullest through both, your active and
financial support. The work of the Board is open to those who are
interested. Members who care to participate are welcome. We are
looking for “new blood’’ to bring new ideas, new enthusiasm and
new goals towards attaining a better understanding of our heritage.
In the name of the Officers and members of the Boards, we
extend our heartiest greetings to the congregation:
“Le Shanah Tovah Tiketevu!’’ A Happy and Good New Year!

Dedication of Holy Ark
DESCRIPTION OF ARK
Wednesday, May 2, 1956 may be recorded as the most
distinguished occasion of the past year and a great event,
one of the greatest events in the history of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. Marking the completion of renovations of
the Center Synagogue, the Holy Ark was dedicated with
Dr. Simon Greenberg, Vice-Chancellor of the Jewish Theo¬
logical Seminary, delivering the address of the occasion.
Local newspapers carried photographs of the Ark with
explanations of the symbolic significance portrayed in carv¬
ings and architectural design. The Florida Times-Union,
issue of April 28th, stated as follows: “Together with other
pulpit appointments, the Holy Ark, historically associated
with the Ark of the Covenant, symbolizes God’s revelation
of the Law on Mt. Sinai. The desired effect is created by
the mounting rise in the height of the furnishings. The Ark
culminates about 25 feet above the synagogue floor in the
crown-shaped Eternal Light of polished, satin-finish bronze,
fitted with a crimson shade.
Immediately below the Sanctuary Lamp (Ner Tamid),
but set slightly back, is a placque of the Tablets of the Law.
From a concave surface of unpolished walnut emerge deeply-
cut Hebrew letters with gilded facing. These spell out the
first two words of the Ten Commandments.
Shadows playing on V-shaped rays extending from the
Tablets suggest the banks of clouds that Moses pierced on
the mountain of revelation.
Throughout the Ark's construction, symbolism is heavily
accented through the use of light and dark woods, delicate
carvings, panels and other devices.
Most significant, perhaps, are the two panels of walnut
flanking the doors. Delicately framed here is a set of eight
carvings depicting ceremonial articles used in Holy Day
observances.
Through the grilled doors may be seen a curtain of blue
velvet. The Ark shell is lined with white and arranged in
two tiers are Torah scrolls mantled in blue with silver em¬
broidery. Many of the scrolls are adorned with embossed
and hammered silver ornaments.
Funds for the new Holy Ark were raised by popular
subscription two years ago at one of the High Holiday serv¬
ices. Members of the Center Sisterhood, through their beau¬
tification fund, purchased carpeting, seats and other items.’’

picting eight sacred occasions of the Jewish year. The sym¬
bols are sculptured in walnut and are embellished by Hebrew
lettering of an appropriate quotation.
1. The Sabbath. Two candlesticks and the kiddush cup;
a loaf of twisted Hallah bread in the background. The
inscription: “Sabbath holiness.”
2. Rosh Hashanah. A shofar, a set of scales symbolic
of divine judgment in the background. Inscription: “Happy
New Year” and the word “Repentance” which together with
the two Hebrew words in the next panel occur in the Une
Tañe Tokef prayer.
3. Yom Kippur. A prayer book with the verse “On this
day shall Atonement be made for you” inscribed on its open
pages and a Charity plate, with a tall taper candle alongside.
Inscription: “Prayer and Charity.” The full quotation is
“Repentance, Prayer and Charity lead to Grace and Salva¬
tion”.
4. Siikkot. Lulav (palm branch) set into a holder with
myrtle and willow branches, a etrog (citron) lies alongside.
Inscription: “Thou shalt rejoice in thy feast.”
5. Hanukah. Menorah with candles modeled after an
antique piece found in the Jewish Museum. In the back¬
ground are a small shield with the letter “mem” for Macca¬
bees and a cruse of oil. Inscription: “Rock of Ages, my
Salvation.”
6. Purim. Megillah scroll of Esther coming out of a
silver case, inscribed are the first three words of the Book of
Esther and the names Mordecai and Esther. In the back¬
ground are a crown and scepter. Inscription: “Peace to all
his seed”, the three closing words of the Book.
7. Passover. Four cups embellished with grape leaves
and the Hebrew word Pesah which represent the four-fold
promise of redemption. In the background are three mat-
zohs used at the seder. Inscription: “Let my people go that
they worship me.”
8. Shavuot. Torah scroll in decorated cover with silver
ornaments. In the background are stalks of wheat and grape¬
vines symbolic of the Bikhurim, first fruits, theme of the
festival. Inscription: “Torah of Truth”.
Note: A fuller description of the significance of the sacred
occasions symbolized here will be found in the explanation
of the Hebrew Calendar printed in another section.

grown; where it has failed, the synagogue has lost ground.

Now, what can the Men’s Club do for the synagogue. First, the club cannot he considered as an independent fraternal or philan¬
thropic organization. The Men's Club is part of a religious institution and a religious movement. Whatever it engages in must always
express Jewish dignity and self respect as well as be in harmony with Jewish tradition and discipline. They must help by means of their
activities to contribute to the advancement of Jewish religious values.

The Jewish Community Council is the one organization
which speaks for and represents all the Jews in Jacksonville.
It is one of hundreds of Jewish Community Councils
throughout the country, each of which is a member of the
parent organization — the National Council of Jewish Fed¬
erations and Welfare Funds.
The Community Council is a democratic organization
made up of representatives of all Jewish groups which in
turn elect the officers, set the policies and the framework
within which the Council operates. Any activity which
concerns the entire Jewish community is the direct concern
of the Council.
The Council s charter dates back to 1935. The By-Laws
best describe its functions:
1. To help maintain the dignity and integrity of Jewish
life;
2. To develop an articulate, intelligent and effective
public opinion on community problems and interests
3. To foster cooperation among all elements in the com¬
munity of Jacksonville
4. To raise, receive, collect, hold and distribute funds
for Jewish causes, either directly or through presently
existing or subsequently established agencies
5. To enable the Jewish community of Jacksonville,
through coordination of fund-raising activities to meet
its obligations to local, national and overseas causes
in an intelligent and effective manner.
6. To cooperate with other agencies for better mutual
understanding; and
7. In general, to plan for the welfare and development
of the community as a whole, engaging in activities
as will contribute to that end.
To carry out these purposes, the Council has created
various structures. The most important has to do with fund¬
raising. Each year an annual Campaign is conducted to
provide funds for agencies, some 40 in number. These in¬
clude aid for overseas needs through U. J. A., Hadassah and
other groups; combatting anti-Semitism; providing hospital
facilities for the Tubercular and others who are ill; develop¬
ing cultural facilities; maintaining religious institutions and,
of course, meeting needs of our own local institutions such
as the River Garden Home. Almost 2 million dollars have
been raised for these several purposes since the inception
of your Community Council.
To carry on its many other activities, the Council has
established various committees. The Refugee Committee in
past years has done a most important job in the acceptance,
settlement and integration of over 24 refugee families.
Men and women in the Armed Forces are cared for
through the Armed Services Committee. Home hospitality
during the religious holiday is provided in cooperation with
local synagogues.
The Public Relations Committee quietly and effectively
not only stands ready to combat anti-Semitism, but also in
promoting a positive program within the general community.
Recently, as a prime example of the high calibre of
Council work, a census was taken. The results of that self
study were so well received that requests for the Study were
received by many national organizations, public libraries, in¬
cluding the New York Public Library and the Study itself
was given a national award as the “best of its kind” at a
National Convention.

This year marks the end of a decade of service to the aged and infirm
Jewish people of Florida. Starting in 1946 with a remodeled mansion housing
12 people, the Home has added to its facilities whereby it now serves 50
residents. During this period of time, over 150 persons have found a haven
at River Garden. This gave their families the security that their loved ones
were well taken care of, in a comfortable setting which emphasized the
positive aspects of Jewish living.
The outstanding feature of the past year was the beginning of the new
addition to the Home in November, 1955. The two story modern structure
will be completed in the Fall of this year. It will be a most welcome im¬
provement to the Home because it not only will relieve overcrowded conditions
but will be able to accommodate many people waiting to enter.
The new building will especially answer a most pressing need for spec¬
ialized health service to an increasing number of infirm aged. Adequate
nursing facilities are provided for in the new Health Center as well as the
most modern infirmary equipment.
Thus the services and program of the Home will become more adequate
and fruitful in a setting especially designed to meet the health problems of
an aging population.
The fear and dread of living in a Home for the Aged, so common a
few years ago, have largely been dispelled by the demonstrated services of
River Garden. It has been adequately proven that life in the latter years can
be a happy and satisfyiing one in a Home designed to meet the needs of each
individual.

The Jewish Welfare Society is the family and children’s case
work agency of the Jewish community. Affiliated with the Com¬
munity Chest-United Fund, the local program is almost entirely
supported by your contributions to the Chest. One of its programs,
the re-settlement of New Americans, is financed by funds that you
contribute through the Jewish Community Council.
Its program can be broken into 6 overlapping categories:
1. Family Consultation and Case Work
2. Child Placement and Work With Unmarried Mothers
3. Applications to River Garden Hebrew Home and Follow Up
Case Work
4. New Americans, Naturalization, Immigration
5. Transients
6. Prison Program
The heart of the agency’s program is the family consultation
and case work program. Two outstanding aspects of this program in
the past year were the retainment of Dr. Merton Ekwall as psychi¬
atric consultant to the director and a marked increase in the num¬
ber of local people seeking family counseling services. In 1954,
there were 87 new local requests. In 1955, it jumped to 129, with
the trend continuing up.
Nine out of ten local requests were for counseling, planning
and other services that do not involve direct outlay of funds. This
percentage remained about the same as in previous years. They
involved problems of marital difficulty, conflicts between parents and
children, mental illness, physical illness or handicapped, educational
difficulties, economic problems, problems that sometimes develop
with the aged and many other varied and complex problems.
LAWRENCE RACKOW
For its counseling service, the agency has a sliding fee schedule, according to one’s ability to pay. As a community program, this
service is available to all Jewish residents and no one is turned away because of their inability to pay.
In 1955, the agency worked with three unmarried mothers and placed two children for adoption. It also received temporary
custody of and placed one child in a treatment center. It is difficult to predict how active this program will be but apparently the child
placement service is definitely established.
Although it has been necessary to cut back its services to the residents of the River Garden Hebrew Home to a referral basis, the
Society maintained its close collaboration with the Home, interviewing the applicants to help them determine the best plans for them
and to help work out financial arrangements with the Home. The Society looks forward to working with the Home on a day care pro¬
gram which hopefully will begin after the building program is completed.
The New American program, which is financed by the Jewish Community Council, re-settled two families in 1955. Almost all of the
families brought over are now citizens. Some New and Old Americans are being assisted in helping their relatives work out sound
migration plans. About 20 families were helped to fill in the forms, questionnaires and affidavits in their restitution claims for Nazi
persecution.
The agency also continued the work with the transients and the Jewish prisoners at Raiford.
The agency re-instituted its quarterly educational meetings, focusing its interest on mental health and parent-child relations. It
continued cooperating with various schools on their undergraduate program and with the Florida State University in supervising the field
placement of a graduate student.

The Jacksonville Chapter of Senior Hadassah is com¬
posed of approximately 750 Jewish women who meet
monthly to serve together in the ranks of the American
Zionist movement. Meeting in the afternoon on the second
Tuesday of each month, or in the evening with the Hannah
Senesch Group on the second Monday, Hadassah’s members
join in efforts to affect the National Hadassah program of
health and vocational education services to Israel: and the
dedication of our local Jewish women to active participation
in the life and expression of community and American
affairs.
The story of Hadassah is the tale of a group of women
who were instrumental in sending two American trained
nurses to Jerusalem in 1913 to ease the high mortality rate,
and who remained to develop a great health program, not
only to save the mother and the newborn child from pain¬
ful death, but also to build education and health facilities;
to rear the infant into healthy childhood and productive
adulthood.
Jacksonville's Hadassah membership joins with 300,000
Jewish women throughout the country to maintain seven
hospitals, a school of nursing, the Hebrew University-
Hadassah Medical School, the New Hadassah-Hebrew Uni¬
versity Medical Center, Youth Aliyah training farms, and
general youth medical services emanating from the Jerusalem
child health centers, together with a network of vocational
education facilities which range from printing to hotel
management and fashion design.
Today Hadassah’s experiment is preventive and com¬
munity medicine at Bet Mazmil encompasses the entire
family unit, and promises to serve as a pattern for health
programs for the world’s progressive governments. The
Hadassah woman in Jacksonville is aware that through her
active participation in her chapter activities she is sharing
in this historic movement.
The Hadassah motto, “The Healing of My People,”
serves to remind that the healer is made well even as the
bedridden and diseased are restored. By working through
established civic organizations, and through the Hadassah
education program, the Hadassah woman joins in molding
a healthy community here.
Together with Z.O.A. Hadassah supports the Jewish
National Fund for the rehabilitation of the land of Israel
and Zionist Youth Work through Young Judaean Clubs.
The projects are carried out through individual con¬
tributions, and through fund earning events. Highlights
of the coming year will be the opening Brunch to be held
in the Jewish Center on October 9, the Membership Tea,
an event of November 1 3 and the annual Hadassah Show
on February 16th. The year will also include a “Dollars for
Linen” shower for Hadassah-Hospital Supplies; and the
Fund-earners Luncheon in the early spring which will honor
Hadassah Donors.
Organization membership is open to all Jewish women.

THE JEWISH RELIGIOUS CALENDAR
EXPLANATORY NOTES
The Jewish Religious Calendar is the result of many stages of
development. In ancient times the unit of time was the month,
determined by the phases of the moon. The Hebrew Calendar
was thus lunar. However, since religious Festivals are observed
annually, the calendar soon became related also to the solar
system. The lunar month consists of 29Vl days. In seven of a 19
year cycle an extra month is added so that the religious occa¬
sions that are observed on the same date of the lunar month will
occur in proper season, which is determined by the solar calendar.
The year 5717 in the Jewish Religious Calendar is a leap year.
The Hebrew months listed below include an Adar I and Adar II,
giving the year 13 months. In this manner the objective of
keeping the lunar and solar months in the proper season as men¬
tioned above is realized.

GENERAL NOTES
In this year’s edition of the Year Book the months run ac¬
cording to the Civil Calendar with the Hebrew date appearing in
the lower left corner.
On Fridays and on the eve of Festivals, as well as on the
first day of Festivals, there appears the symbol, L. C. "Light
Candles." The time that follows is for lighting the candles, 18
minutes prior to sunset, derived from information furnished by
the United States Weather Bureau for Jacksonville.
Rosh Hodesh, Beginning of the Month, is observed with special
prayers in the service for that day and with the Blessing of the
New Month on the Sabbath previous. When the new moon appears
in the middle of the day the outgoing month consists of 30 days
and the last day is considered the first day Rosh Hodesh for the
coming month.
The Sidrah, the portion of the Torah read on Saturday, morn¬
ing, is indicated. Special Sabbaths will be explained below under
the month in which they occur.
TISHRI
Rosh Hashanah, Jewish Religious New Year.
Fast of Gedalia, commemorates the assassination of the gov¬
ernor of Judea who attempted to restore Jewish independence after
the destruction of the First Temple by the Babylonians.
Shabhat Shuva, Sabbath of Repentance, derives its name from
the Prophetic reading, "Shuva Yisrael,” "Return, O Israel."
Yom Kippur, Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and prayer,
a day for confession of sin and supplication for forgiveness. "For
transgressions between man and God, repentance on Yom Kippur
brings atonement. For transgressions between man and man, Yom
Kippur brings no atonement until amends are made."
Sukkot, Tabnernacles, commemorates the period when our fore¬
fathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings through
the wilderness of Sinai.
Hoi Hamoed, Intermediate Days of the Holiday, are observed
as semi-festivals.
Hashannah Rabba, the day on which prayers with the refrain
Hosanah ("O Save Us") is recited.
Shemini Atzeret, Eighth Day of Assembly, marks the conclu¬
sion of the fall festivals.
Simbat Torah, Torah Festival, is the second day of Shemini
Atzeret and celebrates the completion of the annual reading cycle
from the Five Books of Moses and the beginning of readings for
the New Year.
KISLEV
Hanukah, Feast of Dedication, commemorates the rededication
of the Temple by the Maccabees in 165 B.C.E. The Maccabean
triumph over Syrian forces was the first victory for a people’s right
to the freedom of worship. Lights are kindled in the synagogue
and in the home on every one of the eight evenings.
TEBET
Asar ah BeTebet, Fast of the 10th of Tebet, commemorates the
beginning of the siege of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in 586
B.C.E. which led to the destruction of the first Temple.
SHEBAT
Hamisha Asar BeShebat, also known as Tu BeShebat, 15th of
Shebat, is Holy Land Arbor Day or New Year of the Trees.
Shabbat Shekalim, the Sabbath when every adult Jew in the
days of the Temple was reminded to pay his poll tax of a half a
shekel on the first day of Adar.
ADAR
Shabbat Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of Purim
a special portion of the Torah is read beginning with the word
"Zakhor"—"Remember Amaelk." Haman is presumed to have
been an Amalekite.
Purim, commemorates the day when, through the intervention
of Mordecai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly escaped
being victims of Haman’s plot to exterminate them.
Shabbat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning the
ashes of the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual purifica¬
tion by our ancestors in preparing for the Passover Festival.
Shabbat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read, em¬
phasizing the significance of the month of Nisan in which Israel’s
great deliverance occurred.
NISAN
Shabbat Hagadol, the Sabbath which precedes the great Festi¬
val of Passover, derives its name from the Haftorah which con¬
tains a reference to the "Great Day of the Lord.”
Pesah, Passover, celebrates Israel’s deliverance from Egyptian
bondage. The festival lasts eight days during which matzot are
eaten in the place of leavened bread. The celebration of the Pass-
over with the beautiful ceremony of the Seder on the first and
second evenings has fostered by Jewish people the ideals of liberty
and freedom.
IYAR
Yom Ha’atzma’ut. The new State of Israel declared its in¬
dependence on May 14, 1948, which was the 5th of Iyar accord¬
ing to the Hebrew calendar.
Lag B' Omer, the date of a victory by Bar Kokhba in the final
Jewish struggle against the Romans. He was supported by the
sage Rabbi Akiba and his disciples. It is known as "The Scholars
Festival" and marked by field day outings.
SIVAN
Shavuot, Feast of Weeks, completes 7 weeks from the second
day of Passover on which the Omer (a measure) of the new barley
was brought to the Temple. It was also the day when Bikkurim
(first fruits) were presented. Tradition assigned to Shavuot com¬
memoration of the Covenant established at Sinai between God
and Israel and the giving of the Ten Commandments.
TAMMUZ
Shiv ah Asar BeTamuz, Fast of the 17 th of Tammuz, that com¬
memorates the breach in the walls of Jerusalem by the Babylonians
in the Year 586 B.C.E.
Ab
Shabbat Hazon, precedes Tisha B’Ab derives its name from
the first words of the Haftorah "Hazon”—the vision of Isaiah,
which foretells the destruction of Jerusalem.
Tisha B’Ab, the Ninth Day of Ab, is a fast day which marks
the destruction of the First Temple in Jerusalem by the Baby¬
lonians (585 B.C.E. and also of the Second Temple by the Ro¬
mans (70 C.E.)
Shabbat Nahamu, Sabbath of Consolation; the first word of the
Haftorah is "Nahamu, nahamu, Comfort ye, My People."
ELUL
Selihot, Penitential Prayers, recited at midnight in preparation
for the holiday season, "The Great Days of Awe."
