emard
Selevan was one
of six children
raise in Brooklyn.
He and his
siblings were
educated at the
Bensonhurst
Yeshiva, founded
by his father, and
attended synagogue regularly. In an
observant Jewish home, the Selevan
children learned strong ethical and family
values, the virtue of hard work, concern
for others and the importance of education
and charity.
Our Hatan Torah remembers how
important the school and synagogue were
to his father. When the family moved to
Miami in 1932, his father was instrumental
in starting an orthodox synagogue, Knesset
Israel, in a house on 14th and Euclid,
which later became Temple Emanuel. The
Selevan children painted the house and
made it presentable for services. His
father was one of the founders of the
Hebrew Academy in Miami. Bemie and
Alice’s son Jack was one of the first
students.
Bernie believes that giving is a
responsibility owed to the community.
“The key to Jewish survival is Jewish
Education, “ he says. His children, Jack
and Russell, were educated in the Center
schools; his grandchildren, Marc and
Andrew, graduated from Solomon
Schechter, and now Jackie attends
Schechter and Hope is in our pre-school.
The family financially supported the
school by dedicating the Selevan Early
Childhood and Learning Center.
Bernie received the Ben Gurion
Humanitarian Award from Israel Bonds,
one of one hundred in the world to receive
it. It was given on what would have been
the 100th birthday of Ben Gurion. The
Selevan family tradition continues as the
third generation, Jack and Russell, show
the same commitment to Jewish education
and continuity by their involvement in the
Center’s Building Fund campaigns and
educational programs.
A devoted husband, father and grandfather,
our Hatan Torah shares this special honor
with his wife Alice, and his sons Jack and
Russell, and his grandchildren, Marc,
Andrew, Jackie and Hope.

Renee Gordon
Woman of Valor
Jewish
involvement is
nothing new to
Renee Gordon. It
has been woven
into her life ever
since she was a
young girl in
Charleston, S. C.
Naturally it was
expected that she would become involved
in Jewish life when she came to
Jacksonville as Alan Gordon’s bride in
1973. Almost immediately she became
active in Center Sisterhood first as Vice
President of Ways and Means, then as
Sisterhood President. It was Renee who
instituted the Sisterhood High Holiday
Flowers, which has been one of the groups
most financially successful endeavors.
As fund raisers go, Renee is hard to beat,
or for that matter, hard to refuse. This
year’s raffle exceeded our goal by $5,000
and most of the credit goes to Renee’s
tenacity and dedication and “nudging” as
she calls it. As Financial Secretary of the
Congregation, Renee oversees the Raffle
and Yearbook. She diligently watches the
expenses and “pushes” for the most rev¬
enue possible.
Renee is proud to be Jewish. She feels
there is “a natural bond” between Jews
which needs to be preserved. To her, it is
the synagogue which provides the glue for
this bonding; that is why her commitment
to the Center is so strong. Her children,
Michael, Ronnie and Faye, attended the
Solomon Schechter Day School. Renee’s
greatest wish is that her children will feel
the same commitment to Judaism as she
does.
Renee’s community activities include: Life
Member of Hadassah, National Council
of Jewish Women, River Garden
Auxilliary, Jewish Community Alliance,
Volunteer for the Leukemia Society and
American Cancer Society.
Sharing this honor with Renee is her hus¬
band Alan, her daughter Faye, her sons
Michael and Ronnie, her father Nat Zinn„
her sisters Wanda Mirkis and Sydelle Zinn.

hen Ron

A sports enthusiast, Ron began his volun¬
teer activities as a member, and then
chairman, of the Center’s Athletic Com¬
mittee, which created the Sunday
Basketball and Recreational Programs.
He founded, and then chaired the
Alexander Brest Tennis Tournaments for
several years.
Ron chaired the Center Blood Drive for
three years, sold raffle tickets, helped
with holiday setups and decorations, and
chaperoned whitewater rafting trips. In
addition, he called newcomers and he and
Marsha hosted many newcomer socials.
When his wife, Marsha, became the first
female president of the Center, he was
affectionately called “The First Man.”
Marsha was always an extremely active
volunteer, and Ron was always support¬
ive. His three children, Joy, Stacy and
Lee, attended the Solomon Schechter Day
School and were very active in the Center
youth groups.
When the Center moved from Third and
Silver in 1976, Norman Moss asked Ron
if he would take over as the High Holiday
Usher Chair. Ron accepted and has chaired
the committee for 17 years, serving as a
diligent and exemplary chairman.
As Grantland Rice said, ...“And when
that One Great Scorer comes / To Mark
against your name, / He writes not that
you won or lost -/ But how you played the
game.” Ron has always been a team
player and supporter of the Center. He is
a high scorer in our book.
Our Hatan Bereshit shares this honor with
his wife Marsha, his son Lee, his daugh¬
ters Joy Nebel and Stacy Fialkow, his
mother Miriam Pollock, his grandson,
Matthew Nebel and his father-in-law
Morris Leibo.
