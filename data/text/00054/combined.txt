Energy, Tenacity and Love
Her ever-supportive husband Jack explains
affectionately, “Sylvia had that rare ability to
recognize a need and to organize a successful
solution with intelligence and good humor.
Her personal motto and driving force for
herself and her family was ‘Just do it!’ ” Not
only did Sylvia encourage everyone else to
‘Just do it!’, she lived by her own motto.
Sylvia’s appetite for history swept her into
the excitement of research for the Jewish
Museum of Florida, MOSAIC, where she
was an officer and a member of the Board
of Directors. Museum president Norman M.
Giller recognized in Sylvia her integral role
in documenting the Jacksonville’s traveling
Jewish History exhibit. Executive Director
Marcia Zerivitz stated at Sylvia’s death that
“her memory will live on at the Jewish
Museum of Florida because so much of our
collection is a result of her energy, intellect,
tenacity and love.” In conjunction with her
work on MOSAIC, Sylvia began organizing
a tremendous ongoing project, the Center
Archives.
Passion for Life
Sylvia’s unconditional love of life, her
passion for each day’s existence and the joy
she received from her family helped her to
emerge victorious in the face of ailing health.
Victorious, because she never allowed the
conditions of her health to overcome her. Her
character and dignity were never altered by
pain or self-pity. Sylvia lived life to the
fullest, giving continuously of herself and
seldom willing to accept accolades or fanfare
for her life’s work, which was to Sylvia, just
plain living. A woman of tremendous energy,
her hobbies were really a series of meaning¬
ful jobs, serious commitments, impacting
everything from music and art, to children
and adults, the rich as well as the needy.
The Shorstein Family has established The
Sylvia Shorstein Scholarship Endowment
Fund to honor her memory by providing
financial aid to deserving students at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center.

You pass those street murals on city buildings — portraits, surrounded by
images and symbols and a person's name. Know what they are? They're
memorials to young people killed in the streets. People paint them to
reassure their dead friend and themselves: You won't be forgotten.
Who doesn't feel the need to be remembered by someone? Even God says,
"Never forget Me."*
To be forgotten is devastating.
To forget one's world is equally devastating. That's what makes Alzheimer's
such a fearful disease; it corrodes memory, and memory is the power to
make connections. It is memory that enables us to be responsive and
responsible — to learn, to plan, to choose, to create, and to love. When you
lose the ability to remember, you lose your very self, and you are alone.
Who is more alone than someone who has forgotten everyone?
Some people can't help forgetting. But some choose to. And some simply let
it happen.
When individuals forget, lives are ravaged.
What happens when a whole society forgets?
When it doesn't recognize the faces of its own family members and sees
them as strangers?
We're not strangers. Remember? We're inextricably connected, from way
back. It is vital to our wellbeing that we remember our connection to each
other — and, remembering it, that we take responsibility for one another
as individuals, as families, and as a nation.
If we forget that, how will we be remembered?

FROM
Rabbi Gaffney
The Ever Living People
T
JL he "buzz word" in the American
Jewish community over these past
5 years has been the subject of
"Jewish Continuity." We assume that
it is only this generation of Jews
that is anxious as to whether they
will raise Jew grandchildren. This
is clearly not the case. Sometime
ago I had a chance to re-read an
essay written by Professor Simon
Rawidowicz. He proposed that
throughout Jewish history there was
hardly a generation of Jews that did
not consider itself the final link in
Israel's chain. He cited these
examples:
The great Maimonides shortly after
he finished his Guide For The
Perplexed, wrote to a small group
of scholars and friends and said, "...in
our difficult times there are none
left among the people of Israel who
care for Torah and Talmud except
for you. Fortify yourselves, for the
survival of Israel and the Torah
depends on you alone."
Maimonides was unaware that at the
very moment he was predicting the
end of Torah, new schools and
communities dedicated to increasing
the study and observance of our
tradition, were being established in
other parts of Europe.
In more recent times, at the turn of
this century, the Hebrew writer,
Joseph Chaim Brenner, described
himself as, "the last Jew, pouring
out his soul, writing in the language
of the last Jews - writing for and to
those last survivors who would soon
disappear without leaving a trace."
Brenner was a great writer but a poor
prophet. Rawidowicz's essay had a
positive ending. He says that "Israel's
constant vision and commitment to
Rabbi David & Inge Gaffney
that vision in the end helped it to
overcome every crisis and to emerge
from every threatening end as a
living unit." If we are," he continued,
"the last [Jews], let us be the last
as our forefathers were. Let us
prepare the ground for the last Jews
who will come after us and for the
last Jews who will rise after them."
The ongoing discussion of Jewish
continuity reminded me of
Rawidowicz's essay. It was as if I
wanted the assurance that our
generation's struggle will have the
same conclusion as the ever dying
Jews before us. Despite his words,
however, I am often overcome by
the anxiety that somehow conditions
are different today. Might we become
over confident with our people's
knack for survival. While it is an
inspiration to record the resiliency
of previous generations, it is also
essential to remember that the
conditions that saved Judaism in the
past may prove to be ineffective for
our time. The saga of our survival
does not guarantee that the present
generation has the necessary
determination to endure l'dor va'dor,
from generation to generation.
This brings me to the crux of my
remarks. I have long been of the
opinion that only the revival of the
central vision of our people will be
sufficient to arouse us to a response,
passionate enough to save one more
generation. When God gave the
people of Israel the Torah, the
people, hearing God's charge said -
"All that God hath spoken we will
do." With this response as
encouragement God gave Israel the
Torah. We are a people, someone
said, only by virtue of the Torah.
There is only one Jewish communal
institution - the synagogue, that has
the urgent goal of the transmission
of our religious heritage as its raison
de etra, its reason for being. It is
the only place that Jews meet qua
Jews... as a community commited
to fulfill this vision first articulated
at Sinai. That institution is the
synagogue.
On Rosh Hashana and on Yom
Kippur most of us return to the
synagogue. Deep within us there is
a longing for faith, a yearning for
the presence of God. The Psalmist
states - Elohim nitzav ba adat el ...
"God's presence is found among the
multitudes who worship him." I hope
that this is the case for us over Rosh
Hashana and Yom Kippur as it will
hopefully be the case each Shabbat
and whenever we gather to affirm
that "Am Yisrael Chai" - the people
of Israel lives.
Because of the seriousness of this
moment in Jewish life, questions are
being asked of our synagogue. Are
our programs, services, education
and outreach adequate to meet our
responsibilities to the future? Is there
anything more we can do to revive
a sometimes dying people? The
coming years will demand new and
creative efforts. I hope that every
member at the Jewish Center will
reaffirm this commitment to our
sacred covenant with God and join
in the effort to meet this challenge.
Your personal support, the
thoughtfulness of each person, the
dedication and loyalty we are able
to elicit among you, our congregants,
will be our generation's response to
God when we say - "All that you
have spoken we will do."
Inge and I extend heartfelt wishes
for happy, healthy and productive.
L'Shana Tova

“An ignorant person cannot be
pious; one who is bashful cannot
learn (afraid or ashamed to ask);
he who is irritable and impatient
cannot teach” (Avot 2)

W-7 tudy and learning have always been the
cornerstone of Judaism. When the Children
of Israel accepted the Torah at Sinai, it was
given to them only when they called out,
Na’aseh v’nishma, “we shall do and we shall
understand.” Since that momentous event,
knowing and understanding have been cen¬
tral to Jewish living.
Beginning with that “book”, the Torah, the
history of the Jews can be traced through
the written word - the Bible, the Talmud,
the prayer book (Siddur), the commentar¬
ies of Maimonides and Rashi, the Shulchan
Aruch and the Zohar, to name just a few.
In order to understand the relevance of Ju¬
daism, it is important to study the available
literature all through our lives. The sages
understood this as evidenced when they said
“He who does not increase his knowledge,
decreases it” (Avot 1).
The Supplemental Schools of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center represent the
principles and ideology of the Conservative
Movement. Conservative Judaism accepts
the importance of the classical ideals of
Torah and Mitzvot. Although much attention
is paid to teaching tradition, the need for
our students to make a place for themselves
in the modern world is not overlooked.
Equipping our children to come to terms
with this tension between tradition and
change is the major challenge facing our
schools.
As we approach the new year, we are about
to dedicate the various hallways, rooms,
and gardens of our synagogue building to
those who made it possible for us to have
this beautiful facility. Jewish people have
always valued their synagogues as houses
of learning as well as worship and fellow¬
ship. Let us dedicate ourselves to filling
our synagogue with the sounds of children
studying Torah and the voices of parents
with their children coming together to pray
and socialize together. May this year bring
a renewal of our commitment to dedicate
our lives to Torah, Synagogue and Family.
For our part, we have hired the finest staff
and will provide them with the most up-to-
date curriculum material so that our students
will be offered the most challenging courses
and activities. We strive to create a well-
rounded individual who is comfortable and
proud of his/her Jewishness.
With this in mind, we would like to en¬
courage every parent of Afternoon Hebrew,
Hebrew High or Sunday School aged chil¬
dren to take part in our educational and
youth programs. Call or come by the office
if you would like additional information.
We challenge the family unit as a whole
by offering many activities in which the
whole family must participate, bringing the
spirit of Judaism into the home and involv¬
ing all our synagogue families in the
education experience.
The Torah tells us: “He who toils in Torah
needs no sacrifice to atone for his sins, for
Torah is more valuable than sacrifice.”
Zohar, iii, 35a.
On behalf of Alina and myself, let me wish
you and your family a very Happy New
Year!

X arents and
Schools - what
is it that we re¬
ally need from
each other?
Pick up a
newspaper or
magazine; click
on a news pro¬
gram; listen to a
conversation
among parents
of school age
children. The
chances are
great that you
will read or hear something related to rearing
children in today’s climate.
Parenting is more difficult today. Parents are
feeling more stress from more sources than
ever before, and are having to combat an in¬
creasingly violent culture. We worry about our
children’s physical safety, but also about the
onslaught to their minds and hearts.
Ellen Goodman, the columnist, recently wrote
an article about today’s counter culture. Not the
kind you may imagine; not destroying values,
but building them. What she stated was that
parents today must be successful in “counter¬
ing the culture.” In her words, “Parents see
Solomon
SCHECHTER
Day School
themselves in a struggle for the hearts and
minds of their children. It is not that they can’t
say no; it is that there is so much more to say no
to. Parents are expected to protect their chil¬
dren from an increasingly hostile environment.”
So, what do parents need? They need our sup¬
port, a safe environment, confidence, respect
and trust.
Schooling is more difficult today for some of
the same reasons. Change is always disquiet¬
ing, it is the pace of change that is different
today. There is less time to absorb, evaluate, or
adjust to the changes rapidly taking place. Com¬
munication is instantaneous, technology races
ahead hour by hour. Yet, the call is to provide
an education which will prepare each child to
be thoughtful, well-informed, life-long learn¬
ers with inquiring minds who know the value of
service and of respect.
Your child’s teacher is also a member of the
“counter culture,” striving daily to provide posi¬
tive values, to nurture intellectual curiosity, to
plan a balanced academic program which will
provide strength in all the necessary skills for
educational growth.
We, your school’s staff, must also counter
negative messages often endorsed by society.
Accepting responsibility for one’s own behav¬
ior is not a popular theme these days. Belittling
others as a way of enhancing oneself is too
often the model. Avoiding hard work, accept¬
ing “good enough”, and using appropriate
language are other examples of current societal
messages which we join parents in trying to
counter and rebut. So, what does our school
need? We need your confidence, support, re¬
spect and trust.
We each have a passion for our children. We
each call upon the respect and trust of the other.
Solomon Schechter parents and school form a
partnership enabling students to grow, learn,
excel. The school has the power to have an
impact on students in many positive ways. We
also have an obligation and responsibility to
continue to strengthen our role in education.
Our families and friends commit their time,
energy and resources in support of our school.
They speak with a positive voice about the
school, at home and in the community. This is
a powerful endorsement of what we offer. We
need this support.
We each share a passion - your child. We need
and must have each others trust.

The annual Blue & Gold Banquet enabled the scouts to join Cub
Pack 14 for an evening of fun, entertainment and recognition.
Boys and girls from all the scout groups sponsored by the Center
led services on Scout Sabbath.
The boys remained true to the ideal of service to others. The Troop
served residents of Mt. Carmel a Thanksgiving Day meal, and
enjoyed visiting the senior residents. The boys helped kosher the
Center kitchen for Passover. The scouts collected food for the
needy, and performed various other service projects.
The outdoor program included weekend campouts to: Fort Clinch
State Park for the rigorous 15-mile historic trail hike; Osceola
National Forest for the lifelike reenactment of the Battle of
Olustee, the major Civil War encounter in Florida; Hanna Park
to camp beside the Atlantic Ocean; Scout World with thou¬
sands of other area scouts; and local scout camps, Echockotee
and Shands.
Personal advancement in scouting was a major emphasis in 1994-
95. All scouts made significant strides toward the Eagle rank. Leading
the way were Eric Moemke and Aaron Finck who earned an as¬
tounding 60 merit badges between them last year.
The Troop’s adult leadership is in the best condition in many years.
Randy Silverberg is in his 17th year as scoutmaster. He is ably
supported by experienced assistants, Steve Solomon and Bill
Wilensky. The addition of Kathy Moemke and Stella Finck pro¬
vided a tremendous boost. Bob Fleet and Jim Richman are ex¬
pected to be valuable new leaders. Dan Wilensky chairs the Troop
Committee of Debbie Jackrel, Charles Trager, Harry Peltz, Ann
DePaul and Leon Yergin.
For the first time in Troop history, our scouts attended scout
summer camp in 5 different states, Nebraska, Utah, New Jer¬
sey, North Carolina and Florida. Activities included whitewater
rafting, mountain climbing, boating, fishing and archery. In
Utah, scouts Eric Moemke, Aaron Finck, and C. J. Dulberger
camped amidst snow at elevations of 10,300 feet. The 10 scouts
who attended Camp Daniel Boone in North Carolina learned
why a gas mask is useful at camp. The scouts enjoyed their
summer experiences, and earned a booty of advancement badges.

Our goal is simple, to “Strengthen the

The Women’s Zionist
Organization of
America, Inc.
For the Jewish people, the dream of re¬
turning to Zion is almost as old as the faith
itself. Theodor Herzl believed that Ameri¬
can Jewish women would play a major role
in the unfolding of the Zionist movement.
He encouraged their activism and suggested
that groups of women meet to study and
teach the ideals that motivate Jewish life.
Women responded by forming study groups
called Daughters of Zion and on February
24, 1912 just before Purim, 38 of its mem¬
bers met to found a national organization.
Henrietta Szold, an early Zionist was cho¬
sen as chairman. The name was eventually
changed to Hadassah, the Hebrew name of
the courageous Queen Esther. From 1913,
when it sent two nurses to Palestine until
the present Hadassah has tried to emulate
the selflessness of the heroine of Purim.
The efforts of a handful of thoughtful, en¬
ergetic women have been transformed into
a movement of more than 300,000 mem¬
bers of all ages.

Jacksonville Hadassah proudly celebrated its
80th anniversary in 1995. From the signing
of our chapter charter by Henrietta Szold
herself, the women of Jacksonville Hadassah
have dedicated themselves to support for

cal, educational and youth institutions and
land reclamation projects. Recognizing that
women are confronting the demands of mul¬
tiple roles and responsibilities in the family,
in the workplace and in society at large,
Hadassah offers each member an opportu¬
nity to realize fully her potential as a
powerful and positive force within the Jew¬
ish community and in the United States.
The Jacksonville Chapter is divided into four
groups - Tikva, Shir Li, Hannah Senesch
