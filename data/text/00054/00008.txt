You pass those street murals on city buildings — portraits, surrounded by
images and symbols and a person's name. Know what they are? They're
memorials to young people killed in the streets. People paint them to
reassure their dead friend and themselves: You won't be forgotten.
Who doesn't feel the need to be remembered by someone? Even God says,
"Never forget Me."*
To be forgotten is devastating.
To forget one's world is equally devastating. That's what makes Alzheimer's
such a fearful disease; it corrodes memory, and memory is the power to
make connections. It is memory that enables us to be responsive and
responsible — to learn, to plan, to choose, to create, and to love. When you
lose the ability to remember, you lose your very self, and you are alone.
Who is more alone than someone who has forgotten everyone?
Some people can't help forgetting. But some choose to. And some simply let
it happen.
When individuals forget, lives are ravaged.
What happens when a whole society forgets?
When it doesn't recognize the faces of its own family members and sees
them as strangers?
We're not strangers. Remember? We're inextricably connected, from way
back. It is vital to our wellbeing that we remember our connection to each
other — and, remembering it, that we take responsibility for one another
as individuals, as families, and as a nation.
If we forget that, how will we be remembered?
