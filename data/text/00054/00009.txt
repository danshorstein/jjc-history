Rabbi Gaffney
The Ever Living People
T
JL he "buzz word" in the American
Jewish community over these past
5 years has been the subject of
"Jewish Continuity." We assume that
it is only this generation of Jews
that is anxious as to whether they
will raise Jew grandchildren. This
is clearly not the case. Sometime
ago I had a chance to re-read an
essay written by Professor Simon
Rawidowicz. He proposed that
throughout Jewish history there was
hardly a generation of Jews that did
not consider itself the final link in
Israel's chain. He cited these
examples:
The great Maimonides shortly after
he finished his Guide For The
Perplexed, wrote to a small group
of scholars and friends and said, "...in
our difficult times there are none
left among the people of Israel who
care for Torah and Talmud except
for you. Fortify yourselves, for the
survival of Israel and the Torah
depends on you alone."
Maimonides was unaware that at the
very moment he was predicting the
end of Torah, new schools and
communities dedicated to increasing
the study and observance of our
tradition, were being established in
other parts of Europe.
In more recent times, at the turn of
this century, the Hebrew writer,
Joseph Chaim Brenner, described
himself as, "the last Jew, pouring
out his soul, writing in the language
of the last Jews - writing for and to
those last survivors who would soon
disappear without leaving a trace."
Brenner was a great writer but a poor
prophet. Rawidowicz's essay had a
positive ending. He says that "Israel's
constant vision and commitment to
Rabbi David & Inge Gaffney
that vision in the end helped it to
overcome every crisis and to emerge
from every threatening end as a
living unit." If we are," he continued,
"the last [Jews], let us be the last
as our forefathers were. Let us
prepare the ground for the last Jews
who will come after us and for the
last Jews who will rise after them."
The ongoing discussion of Jewish
continuity reminded me of
Rawidowicz's essay. It was as if I
wanted the assurance that our
generation's struggle will have the
same conclusion as the ever dying
Jews before us. Despite his words,
however, I am often overcome by
the anxiety that somehow conditions
are different today. Might we become
over confident with our people's
knack for survival. While it is an
inspiration to record the resiliency
of previous generations, it is also
essential to remember that the
conditions that saved Judaism in the
past may prove to be ineffective for
our time. The saga of our survival
does not guarantee that the present
generation has the necessary
determination to endure l'dor va'dor,
from generation to generation.
This brings me to the crux of my
remarks. I have long been of the
opinion that only the revival of the
central vision of our people will be
sufficient to arouse us to a response,
passionate enough to save one more
generation. When God gave the
people of Israel the Torah, the
people, hearing God's charge said -
"All that God hath spoken we will
do." With this response as
encouragement God gave Israel the
Torah. We are a people, someone
said, only by virtue of the Torah.
There is only one Jewish communal
institution - the synagogue, that has
the urgent goal of the transmission
of our religious heritage as its raison
de etra, its reason for being. It is
the only place that Jews meet qua
Jews... as a community commited
to fulfill this vision first articulated
at Sinai. That institution is the
synagogue.
On Rosh Hashana and on Yom
Kippur most of us return to the
synagogue. Deep within us there is
a longing for faith, a yearning for
the presence of God. The Psalmist
states - Elohim nitzav ba adat el ...
"God's presence is found among the
multitudes who worship him." I hope
that this is the case for us over Rosh
Hashana and Yom Kippur as it will
hopefully be the case each Shabbat
and whenever we gather to affirm
that "Am Yisrael Chai" - the people
of Israel lives.
Because of the seriousness of this
moment in Jewish life, questions are
being asked of our synagogue. Are
our programs, services, education
and outreach adequate to meet our
responsibilities to the future? Is there
anything more we can do to revive
a sometimes dying people? The
coming years will demand new and
creative efforts. I hope that every
member at the Jewish Center will
reaffirm this commitment to our
sacred covenant with God and join
in the effort to meet this challenge.
Your personal support, the
thoughtfulness of each person, the
dedication and loyalty we are able
to elicit among you, our congregants,
will be our generation's response to
God when we say - "All that you
have spoken we will do."
Inge and I extend heartfelt wishes
for happy, healthy and productive.
L'Shana Tova
