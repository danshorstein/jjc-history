Committees & Organizations
HESED COMMUNITY 5773
The Hesed committee’s mission is to offer help and comfort to our Center members and families during
challenging times. Hesed Committee members help Center members who are ill or homebound, or who have lost
a loved one, by sending get-well cards and condolence cards, making phone calls, making visits or sending home-
cooked meals. Transportation is provided on occasion, as well.
Our newest initiative is the Care and Comfort Box. This is a package of assorted items that is delivered in person
to a newly bereaved congregant, within the first month or two of his or her loss. Our committee has enthusiastically
embraced this project, suggested to us by Andrea Olitzky. In addition, the committee continues to support the
delivery of meals to parents of newborns. Many thanks to our hardworking members.

¥-A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
CONVERSATION STARTERS
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
It is nowhere near Passover, but I have four questions to ask. Like those posed by the
youngest child at the seder, they need not receive a direct answer. On the other hand, they are
not rhetorical questions, either. Like the queries of the seder, they are intended as conversation
starters. So here goes ... Should we require middle school students to enroll and attend classes
in one of our schools as a prerequisite for b nai mitzvah? Should we obligate our youth to attend
Shabbat services? What are our expectations of synagogue board members? Beyond dues,
should we ask anything of our members?
In some areas of congregational life we expect individuals to adhere to certain norms. In
other parts of synagogue experience, we may articulate something as a requirement, but turn
a blind eye when the obligation goes unmet. In most spheres of communal life, however, we
neither ask nor expect very much. The bell curve of expectation in all areas save fundraising
dramatically diminishes with age; we ask more of our twelve-year-olds than we do of our adults.
We are in great company because this holds true of the vast majority of synagogues in North
America.
For the past five years we have worked hard to be a more hospitable congregation, and
have become “seeker friendly” in ways that would have been unfathomable years ago. We are
more open than ever to intermarried families, same-sex couples, non-Jews interested in exploring Jewish identity for themselves.
In sum, we’ve gotten far better at the empathetic transcendence needed to understand what the shul landscape looks like through
the eyes of a newcomer to the Center. This is all good, very good... and we are not done yet with becoming ever more welcoming
and inviting.
I recently read about a church where the pastor was holding an orientation session for new members of the parish. The
newcomers had numerous questions: What does this church expect from my family? What am I supposed to believe? How
much of my time should I volunteer to help the community? Now that I am member of this congregation, how should my life be
different? The pastor wrote their questions on a page of newsprint, and instead of responding to them, ushered the group down the
hall to a board meeting in progress. “These newcomers,” he said, “want to understand what the church expects of them. I hope
we’ll take some time to understand their questions and begin to think about our answers.”
If we were to respond to those questions, our answer would be that it’s mostly up to you. We’ll offer you opportunities and
give you abstract guidance, but you need to choose for yourself what, if anything, you want to do.
Here is the $64,000 question: Can we be welcoming while simultaneously raising the level of expectation? I believe we
can; more to the point, to thrive we have to. Dan Hotchkiss, a senior consultant at the Alban Institute, a not-for-profit think
tank for houses of worship, writes, “Raising mutual expectation requires letting go of the idea that to be free, people have to be
uncommitted. In its place, we need to dust off older language about promises and covenants . . . covenant is not the opposite of
liberty, but a time-tested method of extending it.”
Conventional wisdom maintains that in our self-directed, consumer-driven society, expectations are a turn-off. But the
concept doesn’t have to connote a top-down authoritarian model filled with “thou shalt nots” and finger wagging. Our expectations
may be no more than asking you to engage in having this conversation, to challenge you to challenge us. The fact is the less we
expect of our members the more we subtly communicate a message that we have little to offer. Those afflicted with low self¬
esteem expect less from the world around them . . . precisely because they expect nothing of themselves. There are those who
hunger for commitment and meaning; they want to be pushed to do more, but may not yet know how or what to ask.
In the year ahead we need to have these conversations as leaders, members and synagogue professionals. The Torah teaches,
“And now, O Israel, what does the Lord your God demand of you? Only this: to revere the Lord your God, to walk only in God’s
paths, to love God, and to serve the Lord your God with all your heart and soul” (Deuteronomy 10:12). A tall order or a definition
of meaningful Jewish identity? Nice words or an invitation to be more? In 2013
how does this verse translate into the ethics, rituals and celebrations of synagogue
life? Ensuring a meaningful and vibrant synagogue for the generations to come
requires that we lower the threshold to entry AND raise the bar of expectation for
ourselves as individuals and as a community. As the sage Hillel once asked, “If not
now, when?”
Susan and the boys join me in wishing you and your loved ones a year of
health, blessing and spiritual growth. May we all be inscribed and sealed in the
Book of Life for 5774.
Shanah Tovah,
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner

In the summer of 2012, nine members of our congregation joined Hazzan Holzer and 70 other cantors and
some 200 congregants from around the globe in a special Cantors Assembly mission to Germany. The itinerary

including our very own Hazzan Holzer. Judaism is alive and well in Germany. Center participants included:
Mike and Marilyn Mass, Dan and Cindy Wohl, Ed and Judy Mizrahi, Lenny, Lynn and Elana Maiman.

HEVRA KADDISHA
During the course of a lifetime, no one can avoid an encounter with death;
yet, it is an experience for which no one is prepared. Judaism embraces all of
life, of which death is a natural part, and so ascribes rituals which dignify our
departure.
The care of the deceased immediately following death is the solemn
responsibility of the Hevra Kaddisha, the holy society, consisting of dedicated
laypersons from the community. Members are knowledgeable in the traditional
duties, and perform them with the utmost respect for the deceased. The process
begins with the Taharah, purification, a ritual cleansing and preparation of the
body for burial. In addition, a Shomer (guardian) will stay at the side of the
deceased reciting required prayers until the burial.
It is gratifying to have a completely independent Jewish organization, under
the sole leadership of lay people, maintain such a high level of commitment and
dedication to one of the most important traditions in Jewish life.
Members of the Jacksonville Jewish community can take comfort in the
knowledge that the services of the Hevra Kaddisha are offered to all Jews
regardless of financial means or synagogue affiliation. Please feel free to call any
of our officers or rabbis with concerns or questions. “Dedication” is our motto.

A YEAR OF ONE LAST TIME
2013, a year of memories. A year of “one last time.” Every event, every
convention, every trip, every bus I ordered I knew would be “my last one” or “one
last time”. After 27 years, it was a lot of memories. It was a lot of remembering.
Camp 1986, 10 staff, no lunches, no art teacher, sleepovers in the park and lots
and lots of fun. Camp 1999, 35 staff, 20 CIT’s, lock-ins in the building, field
trips, specialty camps, an NBA basketball player, helicopters landing in the park
and again lots and lots of fun. Over a decade of memories were made, with
the children of the JJC. Strong, independent, wonderful, excitable children who
wanted to do everything offered at camp and more.
SS A (Sunday Sports Association) Basketball - 12 weeks every Sunday from
9am -9:30pm. Hundreds of children, parents and guests shouting encouragement,
shouting directions in both Hebrew and English. “Ponytail plays” meant, throw
the ball to a girl! Everyone played, everyone was part of their team. Practices
every night, competition was fierce. And again, fun was had by all!
And then it changed, an opportunity opened. It was time to grow again. As Youth Director, I have tried
throughout my years to teach our children to lead with confidence, integrity, respect and dignity. To be
imaginative, to work together. To show love in every decision. To speak with wisdom. To show strength of
character. To keep an open mind that there is always a way to succeed. And in the mix, always, always to have
fun. I believe I have accomplished my goal.
While it would be impossible for me to thank everyone who has been a part of my professional career,
I would be remiss if I did not mention a few. The Clergy here is the very best. Thank you for sharing my
career. To the fabulous administrative staff, our wonderful maintenance staff, and to Margo’s Catering, you
made my job fun and most importantly, possible. Thank you for your help, love and support. To the volunteer
lay people. You are out of this world. There has never been stronger or better youth commission chairs or
Youth VPs anywhere in the Southeast region! Thank you for your guidance and your leadership. To all of my
assistant Camp directors, camp staff, and youth group advisors, the job could never have been done without
you. Thank you for lending your love, time and skills to our youth. To my Center parents, and to “my”
children, there would have been no youth program without you. Thank you for joining me in our adventures.
It was with great appreciation and pleasure that I accepted the 2013 Rabbi David Gaffney Leadership in
Education Award. To the Jacksonville Jewish Center community and the Martin J Gottlieb Day School, thank
you for this special and significant moment in my life. 1 am honored and proud to have joined this elite group
of outstanding educators.
To the children of the Jacksonville Jewish Center, remember to use your strength of character, devotion
and imagination to create the most wonderful youth groups ever. With pride and care you can create
friendships that will last forever. Show commitment to your Shul and Judaism. Commitment to enrich your
own lives and the lives of your friends with strong, vibrant youth groups.
“Every great dream begins with a dreamer. Always remember, you have within you the strength, the
patience, and the passion to reach for the stars to change the world.” Harriet Tubman

CENTER MEN’S CLUB - 5774
Harry Peltz
Center Men’s Club President
“Involving Jewish
Men in Jewish Life”
It is my honor to serve
as the President of the
Men’s Club. My family
has been a member
of the Jacksonville
Jewish community for
one hundred years! The
Jacksonville Jewish
Center Men’s Club is an
organization dedicated to
the enrichment of Center
life and the Jacksonville
Jewish community. The
Men’s Club provides an opportunity for our Center men
to work together supporting, sponsoring, and participating
in programs that bring meaning to our slogan, “Involving
Jewish Men in Jewish Life”.
Our Men’s Club is an active member of the Federation
of Jewish Men’s Clubs (FJMC), an international organization
dedicated to the ideals, philosophy and traditions of
Conservative Judaism. Our level of activities and Center
participation have earned your Men’s Club the First Place and
Honorable Mention Torch Awards from FJMC in years past,
and our Yad Shel Chai program is now being implemented in
Men’s Clubs across the nation. We participate in a wide range
of FJMC programs and activities such as distributing Yellow
Holocaust Memorial candles so that every congregant can
honor the memory of the Holocaust on Yom HaShoah and
World Wide Wrap which brings together congregants, clergy
and Center youth on Super Bowl Sunday to participate in the
mitzvah of wrapping tefillin.
The Center Men’s Club works closely with Youth and
Education leaders to support your children and the Center
youth organizations. We are available to lend a helping
hand with local activities and regional USY and Kadima
conventions. We sponsor the Yad Shel Chai (Hand of Life)
and Double Yad Shel Chai awards that recognize our youth
for reading Torah in our synagogue services and building on
the success of the Yad Shel Chai program. The Men’s Club
Martin J. Gottlieb Brit Hinukh award is given each Spring to
one Teacher in the Center schools who best represents the life
commitment necessary to bring a quality learning experience
to Jewish children. We provide a metal finished Kiddush
Cup for Bar and Bat Mitzvah students and gifts for our 12th
graders that complete the Siyyum program. We sponsor and
raise funds for the Horty T. Gendzier Summer Scholarship
Fund, which provides the opportunity for children to attend
Camp Ramah and other Jewish enrichment programs who
otherwise would not have been able to do so.
The Men’s Club is also a great way to make friends,
have fun, and meet people from our synagogue as well as
our regional Jewish community. Our members regularly get
together on Sunday mornings to play pick-up softball, touch
football games or perform community service activities. One
of our highlights is the opportunity to prepare meals at The
I.M. Sulzbacher Center for the Homeless. This past year, we
have gone on kayaking, hiking, and co-sponsored a comedy
improv night with Sisterhood. We have had opportunities
to meet with local Jewish celebrities and have had family
outings to the Jacksonville Suns baseball games and other
local community sporting events. In the Fall, our annual
Membership Kickoff Party features fun, food, raffles and the
opportunity to schmooze with friends while we watch the
Jacksonville Jaguars play an away game on the big screen
TV. We are visible friends at the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Throughout the year, Men’s Club members help with Sukkot,
Purim and Lag B’Omer programs. We lead and participate
in Men’s Club Shabbat and the Armed Forces Shabbat. We
are your children’s teachers, your community volunteers and
your spiritual lay leaders.
We invite you to join the Men’s Club if you are at least
18 years old, male, and a member of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center community. I firmly believe that the Center Men’s
Club offers something for
everyone. So come on out and
join us for an activity this year. I
think you’ll enjoy supporting our
Jacksonville Jewish community
and spending quality time with
other guys just like you!

SEASON OF RENEWAL
“When you were born, you were, for however brief an amount of time, the
youngest person on the planet.”
Sometimes, a passage, however simply stated, can move you to tears. This
quote was one of the first sentences I ever uttered to our daughter Rena. For
a new child, everything is brand new. Judaism teaches a powerful message in
recognition of new life. Midrash teaches:
With each child, the world begins anew.
We have other opportunities to begin anew. A change in scenery, a new
person in our lives, or even a new skill acquired. All of these events bring with
them the act of renewal for all. We become refreshed and revitalized; our motors
are reignited and reenergized. This is the power of renewal.
With each person, the world begins anew each year.
We each have the capacity to change not only ourselves, but our community at large. This is not a “chance”
to change every year, it’s our obligation to revisit ourselves from within. As Martin Buber wrote,
“Every person born into this world represents something new, something that never existed before,
something original and unique. It is the duty of every person in Israel to know and consider that he is unique
in the world in his particular character, and that there has never been someone like him before. For if there had
been someone like him before, there would be no need for him to be in the world. Every single person is a new
thing in the world and is called upon to fulfill his particularity in the world.”
Each Friday night we recite the following words during our Kabbalat Shabbat service.

Let the rivers clap their hands, the mountains sing joyously together. (Psalms 98:8)
We join in song, hoping to create something so different and awe inspiring that it causes the earth to not
only tremble, but to stay on the beat! We take this journey together.
I invite you to take this opportunity in this season of renewal to try something “new”- attend a service
you have never attended, refresh your Jewish memory by taking on a Jewish ritual long forgotten. Join our
community as we liven up old melodies and create new ones. If
the rivers can clap and the mountains can sing, certainly we can
as well!
Leora, Rena and I thank you for the outpouring of love on
the birth of Rena Meirav. We are so blessed to be a part of this
sacred Jacksonville Jewish Center community. Shanah Tovah
u’Metukah, A Happy, Healthy year of renewal!

SOCIAL ACTION
The Social Action Community at the Jacksonville Jewish Center has been very busy the past year. Our support of
Operation Isaiah helps to fill the shelves of the food pantry at JFCS. Our thanks to Mimi and Marty Kaufman for seeing
that this get done and to the many people who help to fill the baskets in the Center’s lobby. Our donate the weight this past
year during the holidays was again a huge success. Members of our congregation continue to volunteer at Sulzbacher Center
preparing and serving dinner there. We have made a commitment to be there every other month on the second Sunday of
the month and need volunteers to help us with this project.
Kesher Shabbat found many of our congregants gathering in the DuBow Lobby to make sandwiches and put together
bags of fruit and snacks that were delivered to Sulzbacher Center to help feed Jacksonville’s homeless population. There
were close to 400 bags made and delivered.
A new collaboration this year took place between the Center and the Temple as we took on Christmas Day Meals On
Wheels. The food was purchased and then prepared at the Temple by volunteers from both congregations and then delivered
by more volunteers. We worked with Aging True, a local agency that oversees the Meals on Wheels program and provided
the food and the names of those who needed the food.
A new project this past year was the ConKerr Cancer Miles of Smiles pillowcase project. Many of our congregants
who like to sew made pillowcases that were then donated and will end up under the heads of sick children in the community.
Many donated either money for fabric or the fabric itself to be made into fun pillowcases. Our Kadimanicks helped with
this project when they met here for their convention and tie-dyed, sprayed and decorated pillowcases with appliques. Our
estimated number of donated pillowcases is over 200.
After months of planning, March brought Mitzvah Hero Danny Siegel to our synagogue for the Setzer Levit Scholar-
In-Residence Mitzvah Shabbaton. This began with a Friday night Shabbat dinner and program, continued with services on
Saturday with Danny Siegel and culminated on Sunday with a breakfast and congregants going out into the community at
large to volunteer. We cleaned park grounds, helped at the Teacher’s Supply Depot, went to help at the Community Hospice
Thrift Store, Presbyterian Ministries Thrift Store, served lunch at Cathedral Towers and went to Daniel Memorial for a day
spent with the children who live there. We created Play Dough in our pre-school and made bracelets for the children at the
Children’s Home Society. We had a blood drive and tested for bone marrow donors through the Gift of Life organization.
This year our Social Justice group got involved with ICARE and our aim is to become an active and involved part of
that local non-profit hoping to make Jacksonville a better place to live. We have ongoing collections in the Center’s office
of books and magazines that are donated to a number of local organization who will recycle and use them.
Trying to stay green, we are collecting a variety of items that the Teacher’s Supply Depot gives to our local teachers. It
is amazing what can be made from plastic jugs and styrofoam trays and we are hoping to collect items rather than have them
end up in our local landfills. Magnifiers have been made available for those who may need them to read our siddurs.
We look forward to continuing to do Mitzvot and need YOUR help.
SAC CHAIR STAFF LIAISON
Rhoda Goldstein Rabbi Jesse Olitzky

Makom
HEBREW HIGH SCHOOL
MAKOM, the place to be for Jewish teens,
had another fantastic year. We began our year with
a great kickoff event by seeing the Jewish reggae
star Matisyahu live in concert. It was an energized
beginning to a very successful year. Another
highlight was the work of our Siyyum class. At the
Jacksonville Jewish Center, Siyyum is the name
of our twelfth grade program, which serves as a
capstone to enrollment in the MAKOM Hebrew
High School of the Galinsky Academy. The high school seniors who participate
in the program research and design a project of social action to address needs
within the larger Jacksonville community. Through informal study and dialogue,
Siyyum fosters leadership skills among young adults and strengthens their Jewish
identity as well as their connection with the ethical imperatives of our tradition.
This year’s Siyyum class voted to learn more about the challenges faced by
refugees granted political asylum in the United States. To that end we partnered
with the Jacksonville affiliate of World Relief. Here’s a little information about
our local World Relief chapter and the work they do for our community.
World Relief Jacksonville: World Relief Jacksonville’s mission is to
empower local houses of worship to serve the most vulnerable. Jacksonville received 440 refugees from
Burma, Bhutan, Cuba, Iraq and other parts of the world in 2011, and 430 in 2012. World Relief helps refugees
by providing for their initial housing needs, offering English classes, arranging initial medical care and
immunizations, partnering with community volunteers and houses of worship in assisting them with orientation
to American culture.

THE MAGIC IS IN THE JOURNEY
Early childhood is a magical time in a family’s
life and we are honored to be a part of it. We strive to
work with parents to provide a loving home away from
home for their children. The mission of our preschool
for over 69 years has been to offer a supportive,
creative, safe, enriching, and developmentally
appropriate environment for the youngest members
of our community. Challenging our students to
stretch their unique potential, we’ve created a place
to grow towards maturity and independence. We also
believe that Jewish early childhood education lays the
foundation for the development of a strong Jewish
identity upon which lifelong learning and commitment to the Jewish community is
based.
The DuBow Preschool offers learning and interactive activities that are meaningful
and designed to promote independence, social development and intellectual growth.
We embrace the philosophy that children learn best by doing. Each day our students are artists, scientists, bakers,
teachers, musicians, builders, and explorers. Utilizing a program that is inspired by Jewish values and traditions,
we stimulate each child’s social, emotional, physical, and cognitive development. We celebrate Jewish holidays
in a meaningful and developmentally appropriate way. Our families are invited to share in our weekly Shabbat
celebrations and in our many wonderful programs which include a Hannukah program, model seders and more.
2012-2013 was a year to remember! Our school was renamed the DuBow Preschool. We are truly thankful to the
DuBow family for their generosity and faith in our school. We have a beautiful new look, state-of-the art equipment,
furniture, a new indoor/outdoor Discovery Studio and 21st Century technology. We created an integrated science
exploration curriculum with a focus on the highest quality science and technology instruction. We hired a coordinator
to help develop the programming, train teachers, and lead weekly instruction in the Discovery Studio and organic
garden.
As part of the Galinsky Academy, we launched a new program called “Creating a Community of Kindness”.
The ongoing goal of the program is to ensure that our schools are positive, kind places, where teachers, parents and
students work together to make sure that everyone is included and treated in a kind, respectful manner. This year
we offered parent workshops, teacher training, and classroom activities in order to guarantee our families a safe and
healthy environment for their children. We incorporated the teachings into our Jewish Values curriculum. Each
month we focus on a Jewish value with the overall desire of helping families raise little mensches.
We look forward to another exciting year in 2013. On behalf of my faculty, and my daughters Cayla and Julia,
I wish you a Shana Tovah Umetuka, a good and sweet new year.

KOLTRAIN
Koltrain has made a permanent stop at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Hazzan Jesse Holzer, inspired by
a desire within the congregation to bring new forms of music to the synagogue ritual experience, created a
synagogue band 4 years ago in honor of his installation. In what could’ve been a one shot deal, the congregation
embraced the new sound that led up to the Friday night liturgy. Given the name Koltrain (Kol in Hebrew
means voice), the group adds their voices and instruments to our Sunday morning Shaharit LIVE! Service,
Friday night services, holiday dinners and community programs. Over the past few months, Koltrain has
experimented with different musical styles ranging from fast paced Chassidic melodies to the more mellow
acoustic vibe. If you are interested in hearing more about and from Koltrain, please contact Hazzan Holzer.
Current Koltrain Members include: Scott Zimmerman- bass, Todd Morse and Bruce Horovitz- guitar, Dennis
Mizrahi Helen Hill and Max Blumenfeld- drums, Jaime Kaufman- trumpet, Leslie Blumenfeld- saxophone,
Cindy Wohl and Hazzan Holzer- vocals
The JJC Chorale Society was created a few years back to re-engage our congregation with choral music
throughout the Jewish calendar year. Choral singers who had sung in our choir for years were given an
opportunity to explore new musical styles, while new singers were able to lend their voices. Beyond our
retooled High Holy Day Chorale, our semi-professional group Harmonía contributes to our High Holidays
as well as Hallel services throughout the year. We look forward to adding innovative song to our community,
working this coming year on a blended Friday evening service with our Koltrain instrumentalists. If you love
to sing, if you are looking to become engaged or re-engage yourself in our worship services, contact Hazzan
Holzer! Current Members include: Michael Kuhr and Leonardo Maiman- Bass, Beth Shorstein, Beth Fleet
and Michelle Burns- Sopranos, Cindy Wohl and Minda Gordon- Altos, Hazzan Holzer- tenor.

^-A Message from Rabbi Olitzky
ARE YOU THERE GOD? IT’S ME!
There is a frequently told story by an unknown author about a man that asks for proof
that God exists:
A man whispered' “God speak to me, ” and a meadowlark sang, but the man did not
hear So the man yelled “God speak to me!” The thunder rolled across the sky. But the
man did not listen. The man looked around and said, “God let me see you! ” A star shone
brightly in the sky, but the man did not notice. The man shouted, “God show me a miracle ”
and a new life was born. So, the man cried out in despair. “Touch me God and let me know
that you are here!” God immediately reached down and touched the man.
But the man brushed the butterfly away and walked on.
Rabbi Jesse M. Olitzky
How often do we read the text of our Torah and expect God to talk to us through a
burning bush? How often do we look up to the Heavens waiting for God’s “strong hand and outstretched arm”? How often
do we look at the world around us and await God’s miracles like the splitting of the sea? How often do we give up on
finding that God or seeing those signs from God, and stop looking altogether?
We may come to synagogue for the sake of our children. We may come to synagogue for community. Maybe we come
to learn or come to socialize. But how often do we come to find God? How often do we even look for God in this sacred
space?
We are not supposed to truly understand God. We are not supposed to have God all figured out. Doubt is normal. Rabbi
Neil Gillman teaches that to question God at times is to truly believe in God. Our relationship with God is reflective of the
events in our lives. The high’s and the low’s, the moments of joy and sorrow, may impact our relationship with God. In fact,
Judaism teaches that we must struggle with our relationship with God. As Jews, we are referred to in Torah as Children of
Israel, B ’nai Yisrael. While Abraham was the first Jew, we do not call ourselves the Children of Abraham. Rather, we are
the Children of Israel, the name given to Jacob after he wrestles with a Divine being, an Angel, a Messenger of God. For
Yisrael means ‘he who wrestles with God.’ Thus, as B ’nai Yisrael, we, the Jewish people, are God’s children who are also
supposed to wrestle with the Divine.
Wrestling with God involves active participation. Wrestling with God involves YOU. Wrestling with God involves
taking the blinders off to see the beauty of God in the world around us: in the cloudy sky and in the sun glistening on the
river’s waters, in the faces of our children and our parents. Wrestling with God includes using the liturgy of the siddur as
a jumping off point for our own conversations, making time for communal and personal prayer.
Talking to God is not easy. Sometimes, God responds, but like the man in the story mentioned above, we don’t
necessarily open our eyes and our ears to embrace God’s presence. I am not asking us to talk to God. I am not inviting us
to talk to God. Rather, I am requesting that you wrestle with God. Struggle with God, but make time for God. Continue
to make the Jacksonville Jewish Center your home for learning and socializing. Continue to make the Center the place
where your children go to school and grow, the place to see extended family members and old friends. Continue to make
the Center your second home. In doing so, I invite you in this new year to
also make the Center a place where you will wrestle with God. Don’t just
find your social community at the Center. Find your spiritual community
as well.
Andrea and Cayla join me in wishing you a Shanah Tovah u ’Metukah,
a Happy and Sweet New Year filled with many opportunities to appreciate
God’s presence in all of our lives.
Best,
Rabbi Jesse M. Olitzky

¡5a— A Message from the Executive Director —4
LET’S BEGIN WITH PEOPLE
Donald A. Kriss
Executive Director
Shalom All...
Here we are at another High Holy Day Season. The year has flown by so
quickly... filled with programs, ample opportunities to attend any number of
adult education sessions, lots of activities for seniors, empty nesters, young
families and youth activities!
We had great events; we had snappy names: Latke Palooza, Sukkot Fest,
Purim Unmasked, Empty Nesters’ Shabbat, Laugh-Out-Loud Shabbat, etc...
We had great looking marketing materials for each and every one of these
well-planned activities. HOWEVER, I regularly wonder what have we done
as a congregation to build community, how have we engaged in building
relationships?
Ron Wolfson in his new book “Relational Judaism” suggests that it is a
Face-to-Face community of relationships that offers meaning and purpose, belonging and blessings. He
goes on to suggest that to create such a community, we need to turn our engagement model upside down.
Rather than spending the vast majority of our time planning events and hoping people show up, let’s begin
with people. I could not agree more! He furthers his claim that being welcoming, hearing our members
stories, identifying our member’s talents and passions, caring about all of our members is the first priority.
Only then can we craft programs that engage our members with the Jewish experience.
Moving in this direction will require great strides in how we interact with member’s day in and day out.
We have re-initiated a great service that will help new families affiliate. An Ambassador Buddy System
has been established that will help guide new families - one on one. We have re-established our Havurah
building initiative, designed to form smaller groups of members to build bonds. I recently attended a Bat
Mitzvah service where family members were invited to do any number of ritual roles at the service. Then
Rabbi Lubliner invited the Bat Mitzvah’s Havurah family to come to the Bimah... this group of unrelated
people - 30 plus strong came to the pulpit, loving each other for over 30 years; the impact on me was awe¬
inspiring. Our programs are good; in the end it is our relationships that last!
So as we fill out those calendars for next year, let’s embrace a new goal: to engage every member
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center in a deeper relationship with Judaism, with the Jewish experience and
with each other. Let’s begin by putting people before
programs. Let’s learn who we are before trying to
figure out what we want.
Janet and I send our wishes for a healthy New Year!
Don Kriss

A MESSAGE FROM THE DIRECTOR OF EXPERIENTIAL EDUCATION
I am honored - as an Eagle Scout, former USYer, and a Ramahnik with a Master of
Arts in Education, and over 10 years of experience - to be chosen as the individual to help
take our youth programming and experiential educational programming to the next level.
I was very lucky to start my career in teaching as a camp counselor. In that
role I saw the transformational power that Jewish community can have on improving
people’s lives. Since that time it has been my obsession to bring that powerful sense
of community that can develop in a camp setting into everyday life. Whether we
are praying, learning, singing, doing tikkun olam, or socializing; we will seamlessly
transition between different programs with a furrowed brow of intense concentration,
a smile, and a laugh. The gift of experiential education is that it makes learning feel
natural, exciting, and desirable. By building strong peer and mentor relationships,
students are set free to take risks, grow, and work with others to bring their vision of
reality into being.
As the Jacksonville Jewish Center’s first Director of Experiential Education, I will be
overseeing two already incredible programs: the Youth Department and Camp Ki Tov.
With three active youth groups (Chalutzim, Kadima, and USY) serving students
in fourth through twelfth grade, and a ton of USY leaders on the chapter, sub-regional, and regional level, we have
a lot to be excited about. No matter who you are, what you are looking for, or who you want to be; the Center
youth groups are where our youth are creating and experiencing what it means to be the Jewish men and women of
tomorrow.
In addition, as the DEE, I have the pleasure of collaborating with the other members of the Education staff to
promote and ease participation between programs for all our youth without regard to age or their entry point into the JJC.
Improving upon the incredible community that the members and staff of the Center has cultivated for years is
not a simple task. However, with the help and support of all of us - from the youngest to the wisest - we are building
the kind of community that will be the envy of the Jewish world. Wishing you a meaningful and transformative High
Holy Day experience.
Shanah Tovah,
Ezra Flom

OUR MISSION
The purpose of the Youth Programs of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center is to bridge the invisible gap between the home, the school
and the synagogue. All of our youth activities combined offer a
unique opportunity to stimulate educational, spiritual and social
growth and development within a Judaic environment. To oversee
these activities, the Youth Commission and Youth Director, sets
the standards and policies for these programs.
The Youth Commission oversees all youth
organizations and programs sponsored by the
Jacksonville Jewish Center including Chalutzim,
Kadima, USY, Scouts and the Center Day Camp. The
committee is made up of youth group advisors, presidents
of our clubs and caring Center members who believe
that the future of Conservative Judaism can be secured
through participation in innovative programs designed to
develop upcoming Jewish leaders.
In addition to membership in Center Youth Groups,
the Youth Commission encourages our youngsters to attend United Synagogue summer programs, such as Camp Ramah,
USY - on - Wheels, Ramah Seminar in Israel and USY Poland/Israel Pilgrimage. These provide excellent opportunities
for our children to share wholesome fun and uniquely Jewish experiences with their peers from all over the United States.
The Youth Commission assists applicants with a variety of scholarships.
Through our College Outreach program, our college undergraduates stay connected to the synagogue and community.
Four times a year, at Rosh Hashanah, Hanukkah, Purim and Passover, care packages are sent that bring warm wishes and
promote holiday observance. Help us to stay in touch with your children by keeping us updated on their school mailing
and email addresses.

READING THE TORAH
Scott Zimmerman
Ritual Director
On each Shabbos we read a Parsha, a portion, of the Torah. We start each yearly cycle
on the Shabbos following the holiday of Simchas Torah, with the very first Parsha, that of
Bereshis. However, come the holidays, there are special portions of the Torah read. These
portions deviate from the yearly cycle. Instead, the subject of the portion read relates to the
particular holiday in some fashion. Rav Moshe Chayim Luzzato, in his Derech Hashem (4:8)
explains further the concept of reading the Torah.
He writes “the reason for (the reading of the Torah) is that the Torah consists of something
that was given to us by God to read. It was furthermore designated so that His holy light should
be transmitted to us through such reading. On certain special days, it is also appropriate that
special portions be read, relating to the concepts of those days. In this way, the special holy
light of these days is strengthened through the power of the Torah, which is the strongest power
that we have.”
The Torah was given to us to read, to study, and to explore. So that we maximize our
spiritual benefit from this reading, it was ordained that we read the Torah every week and on
special days. On these special days, our spiritual benefit is increased: we receive not only the holiness that comes from the reading
of the Torah, but also that from the holiday itself.
I would like to thank the many members of our congregation who have added their voices to the process that is Torah.
Through their dedication and devotion, we have maintained a high level of participation in reading Torah. The commitment and
encouragement of our synagogue organizations and their leaders will ensure that this quality standard will be continued for many
generations to come. I would like to thank all of our teachers for their continued support and cooperation.
On behalf of Suzanne, Casie, Aron and myself we wish the entire congregation a heartfelt Shanah Tovah.
Shanah Tovah - Have a healthy and happy new year!
Scott Zimmerman

Mauri Witten Mizrahi
Aishet Hayil
Mauri is a Jacksonville native and 4th generation Jacksonville Jewish Center member.
Mauri has recently completed her term as the Vice President of Education for the
Jacksonville Jewish Center Board. During her tenure, she guided the formation of the
Galinsky Academy, one of her crowning achievements. Mauri is also a past co-chair of the
congregation’s L’Dor V’Dor Society. She has been active as a Chalutzim and USY Advisor,
as well as serving on school committees, working on countless Shabbat dinners and school
fundraisers. Mauri is a member of the Jacksonville Jewish Center Sisterhood and has
chaired the always popular Hamentaschen baking project. One of Mauri’s highlights while
growing up at the Center was being on Rabbi Kentof’s 1st white water rafting camping trips. Among her other
many other achievements, Mauri is the recipient of the llene Selevan Young Leadership award. She also chaired
the Young Leadership Division and is a past Board member of the Jewish Lederation of Jacksonville. Mauri is
married to Alan Mizrahi who she first met at the Solomon Schecter Day School in 5th grade. Her children are
Ryan, Max, and Zach Mizrahi who currently attend the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School. Her parents are Edward
and Bunny Witten and her in-laws are Edward and Judy Mizrahi. Mauri is a Physical Therapist at River Garden.
Harry Shmunes
Hatan Bereshit
Growing up in Jacksonville defines Harry as a seasoned Jewish Southerner. His memories
of the Jacksonville Jewish Center take him back to services at 3rd and Silver where he
has fond memories of his parents, aunts, uncles, cousins and friends. Harry has served the
Center well as a member of the Board of Directors and as a member of the Education and
Finance Communities. Harry is a current member of the Religious Life Community. You
would be hard pressed not to find Harry at the Center at Shabbat Services every week; he
is a regular Minyan attendee and serves as the Monday Captain. Harry is a founding family
member of his Havaruh and proud of his longtime friendships that have been formed.
Harry worked for 23 years in numerous positions for Pic N’ Save and the National Merchandise Company. He
was recruited by Sears Outlet Stores in Skokie, Illinois, as senior buyer and was able to return to Jacksonville and
work as GM/COO for Dixie Sales Company. Harry created the Fenicks Sales Company. Harry is married to Joni
and they have three children, Adam (married to Kim, daughter Talia), liana, and Ross.
Margo Ilyne Pilton Ruby
Kallat Torah
Where do you begin to tell the story of Margo? She has been active at both 3rd and
Silver and at the current Jacksonville Jewish Center locations. Margo’s hands-on nature
has placed her front and center of the Sisterhood. She has run the Sisterhood Gift Shop
at the JJC downtown location, is co-author of the Try It, You’ll Like It Cookbook, was
Chairperson for Sisterhood luncheons and dinners and she worked in the Sisterhood
kitchen alongside Annie May. She is a past Vice President of the Sisterhood Ways and
Means committee. Her catering services have nourished generations of celebrations under
the supervision of the Rabbis of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Her official catering to the
Jewish Community began in 1973. Since then, she has owned Margo’s Kountry Kitchen, Margo’s Restaurant and
now Margo’s Catering. Twelve years ago, under her leadership, her business returned to its roots by becoming
the exclusive kosher caterer of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. As Margo would say, “She came home where her
family legacy started.” Margo is a graduate of Florida State College of Jacksonville and St. Augustine Vocational
Tech with a degree in Culinary Arts. She is the first Caterer in Jacksonville to be recognized as a Certified Catering
Professional through Purdue University and The International Technological Exchange in Las Vegas. Married
to Stanley Ruby for 55 years and the proud mother of 4 children (Andrew, married to Catrina; Holly, married
to Gordon Silva; Allen, and Paula Warner), 5 Grandchildren (Whitney Baker, David Baker, Jessica Ruby, Jacob
Ruby, and Sophie Warner) and 1 Great Grandchild (Mason Baker).
