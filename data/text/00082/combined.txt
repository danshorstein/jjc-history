Rendered to the Jacksonville Jewish
Center for the past seven years, this—the
thirty-fourth annual edition of the Center
Year Book and Directory—is sincerely dedi¬
cated to Mr. Benjamin Baker, whose constant
and indefatigable work for the institution
will be remembered as long as the organiza¬
tion itself exists. Important to the perpetua¬
tion of any organization is the presence of
strong and efficient leaders to. guide and
direct its policies; but just as indispensible to
the proper functioning of the group is the
willingness and ability of its members to do
the enormous work involved in discharging
the tasks facing the organization every day.
To find a person possessed of both
qualities—a leader and a worker—is indeed
a blessing to any institution. And especially
did this prove to be true of the Center, when
in the depths of the depression and when
onerous financial problems were bearing heav¬
ily upon the officers, Mr. Benjamin Baker was
MR. BENJAMIN BAKER
elected to the Board of Directors and assigned immediately to the all-important position, of chairman of the
finance committee. From the time of his appointment until the present day, the financial status of the Cen¬
ter has constantly been on the up-grade—chiefly through his personal efforts both in bolstering the income of
the Center and in wisely appropriating its funds.
Although Mr. Baker has been a loyal member of the Center and its predecessor organization, the Con¬
gregation B'Nai Israel, for the past thirty-three years since his arrival in Jacksonville on June 15, 1904; it
has only been with in the past seven years that he has been a member of the Board of Directors, being
elected upon the death of Mr. Paul Newman in June 1930. Two yea¡¿ later in May 1932 Mr. Baker was
elevated to the position of second vice-president, which position he has held until the present time.
Hardly a day passes which doesn't find Mr. Baker away from his business attending to the financial
affairs of the Center in the nature of "good will visits" among the members collecting their dues pay¬
ments. Mr. Baker's personal doctrine of correct financing—"To have the funds first and then to worry
about the expenditures"—has proven to be the guide by which the Center has attained its sound financial
status at the present time. Mr. Baker's entire life seems to be wrapped up in the activities of the Center,
for it seems that he misses no opportunity to make collections, to enlist new members or to aid the Center
in any way, be it during his business hours or his leisure time.
In addition to his position as Vice-President and Chairman of the Finance Committee, Mr. Baker
is a member of the Chevra Kadisha, (Center Cemetery Committee), and the Religious and Kashrus Com¬
mittees. Mr. Baker is a partner of the B. and B. Shoe Co-., wholesale shoe dealers.
For the invaluable service which Mr. Baker is rendering daily for our institution, he deserves the' ad¬
miration and cooperation of every member and friend of the Center. May God grant him and his
family long life, good health and happiness, and courage to carry on his noble work and to exert his
beneficent influence upon our community.

As the year 5697 rapidly draws to a close and with the
Eve of Rosh Hashonah already upon us ushering in another
year, the Jacksonville Jewish Center presents to the local com¬
munity its Thirty-fourth Annual Year Book and Directory. This
annual publication quite fittingly assumes a two-fold perspec¬
tive, of looking back in retrospect to the past events and of look¬
ing forward to the future and what it might have in store.
It will be noted that the size of this year's publication has
been increased to eighty-four pages. It contains the regular
features, such as the calendar, directory of the several organiza¬
tions, messages, New Year's Greetings of the members, etc.; but
there have been included in addition such new features as the
increased coverage of the various affiliated organizations, photo¬
graphs of the officers of the various groups, a complete resume
of the past year's Center activities, a table of contents and a
directory of advertisers.
It is appropriate at this time to express our sincere and
grateful appreciation to our advertisers for their willingness to
cooperate in every way in making this publication possible.
The tremendous success of this year's publication is entirely due to the various merchants and concerns,
both local and national, who have so graciously inserted their advertising in our book. It is our duty
as members of the Center to concretely demonstrate to these people our gratitude. Those who have
so materially aided our institution certainly deserve in return the patronage of the members of our
organization and its affiliated groups.
The staff has expended increased efforts in making this year's calendar and directory both
accurate and complete, and urge our members to examine the contents of this book carefully and to
avail themselves of the valuable information included within its covers. This book should occupy a most
prominent position in every Jewish home.
The staff wishes bofh its advertisers and members to understand that the proceeds from this
publication go directly to the Center, enabling it to maintain its educational, religious and social activi¬
ties; and that this institution is in no way connected with any other publications, excepting the pro¬
gram published each year in connection with the Daughters of Israel Annual Cabaret.
In conclusion, the staff wishes to extend its appreciation to the various organizations who have
so willingly cooperated in the task of assembling the photographs and data necessary in the editing
of this book.
PHILIP SELBER

The physical home of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center, which was erected in 1927 under the
leadership of Rabbi Samuel Benjamin and Mr.
Harry Finkelstein, its first president, not only
houses the activities and institutions directly
sponsored by the organization but has played a
major role in the existence and development of
practically every Jewish group in the community.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center includes a
Synagogue, maintains the only Hebrew School in
this vicinity of the state in addition to its Sunday
School, provides the Jewish community with a
spacious Auditorium, supports a Daily Minyon
where services are held twice each day, and
directly sponsors such clubs and groups as Boy
and Girl Scout Troops, Young Judean Clubs, a
Junior Congregation, and others. In addition the
Center has always aided and cooperated, in any
way that it could, with every other Jewish organ¬
ization in Jacksonville.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is affiliated
with the United Synagogues of America and
conducts its Saturday and holiday services ac¬
cording to the Conservative system of prayer,
using the traditional prayer book, but supple¬
menting the Hebrew versions with English
prayers, congregational singing, and sermons de¬
livered in English, and also allowing mixed pews.
However, on the High Holidays an overflow
service is held in the Auditorium, with the men
and women. separated according to orthodox re¬
quirements. The Center usually calls its spiritual
leaders from among the graduates of The Jewish
Theological Seminary of America.
The building housing the activities of the
organization is a three-story brick structure on
the corner of Silver and West Third Streets
facing south and being directly opposite the beau¬
tiful Springfield Park. The building contains
three chief sections: the Auditorium located on
the ground floor, the Synagogue on the second
floor, and the rear section of the building, which
contains class rooms, meeting rooms, the Rabbi’s
study, a daily prayer room (Beth Hamidrash),
and a library.
The large Auditorium has a capacity of over
five hundred persons, being equipped with com¬
fortable heavy chairs, and in addition possesses
one of the finest dance floors in the city, and is
often used for dances, banquets, and receptions of
various sorts. In the front of the hall is found
a well designed stage for the production of plays
and the presentation of concerts. Adjoining the
Auditorium on one side is a modern and adequate¬
ly furnished kitchen, while opening from the
other side is the office. Reproductions of biblical
paintings decorate the walls, and rust colored
drapes adorn the large windows.
The beautifully decorated main synagogue,
which is directly above the Auditorium, is capable
of seating over six hundred and fifty worshippers,
the main floor being provided with five hundred
individual cushioned seats of light walnut con¬
struction with over one hundred and fifty similar
seats contained in the balcony. Large windows
of frosted panes, with a stained glass insertion in
each, are located on both sides of the synagogue.
The platform, pulpit, and Ark are made of dark
walnut and are adorned with gold decorations.
The class rooms in the rear of the building are
all well ventilated and equipped. The daily prayer
room is capable of seating over forty persons
comfortably and contains several book cases of
Biblical and Talmudical volumes. On the second
floor the Center maintains a library of English
Judaica and juvenile literature.

The year 5697 marks the end of one and the be¬
ginning of another epoch in Jewish history. The last
forty years of Zionist endeavor were fittingly compared
at the recent World Zionist Congress, to the forty years
spent by our ancestors in the Sinai desert before reach¬
ing the borders of the Promised Land. If was, indeed,
a period which brought not only untold suffering to our
people in the form of pogroms, poverty and humiliation,
but was marked by confusion and aimlessness. Even dur¬
ing the last ten years when Jewish colonization in Pales¬
tine, despite periodic riots, met with tremendous success,
Jews nevertheless faced the future with uncertainty and
perplexity. Even Zionist leaders who in their hearts knew
well the ends set by Dr. Theodore Herzl for the Zionist
Movement, lacked the courage to speak candidly of
these aims before the Royal Commission in defining the
Jewish National Home.
Whether one agrees or does not agree with the
partition plan proposed by the Royal Commission one
cannot deny that its report is of great historical im¬
portance to the Jewish people. Great Britian definitely
recognized that the Jewish National Home means a
Jewish commonwealth, an independent Jewish state; and
what is of still greater significance, the English government
conceded that Zionist achievements in Palestine have
made Jews ready immediately to assume the task of
forming a sovereign state. The report of the British
Royal Commission, terminates a period of hesitation and
ambiguity, a period of "Wandering in the Wilderness"
and begins an era of "entering the Promised Land," an
era which begins the fulfillment of our Zionist and Mes¬
sianic hopes.
On this Rosh Hashonah let us enter the House of
God in a spirit of thankfulness for the great historic
opportunity that was offered our generation. Let the
shofar rouse within us a spirit of greater Jewish loyalty
and self sacrifice so that we may be able to fulfill the
great tasks that lie before us.
May the New Year mark the end of Jewish misery,
suffering and division of heart and be the beginning of
bountiful blessings, and of unity of heart and action.
May the year 5698 herald the advent of an era of peace
and prosperity for our people and all mankind. Amen.

As I look back upon the year 5697 which is just
coming to a close, I realize that during the past year,
more than at any other time, I have felt the great power
and influence of religion. In an hour of bereavement
one becomes more attuned to the impulses of love, char¬
ity, and of working for the common good, which are
the very essence of our religion.
My work with the Jacksonville Jewish Center especi¬
ally was a great source of consolation. Witnessing the
growth and development of our fine Hebrew School and
Sunday School, being present at Bar Mitzvah ceremonies,
participating in the very impressive confirmation service,
which so ably demonstrated the accomplishments of our
children, seeing our boys and girls celebrate the various
Jewish holidays and festivals in a real Jewish spirit—all
of these things have brought me many happy moments.
I feel, as you should all feel that the Center has
made great strides. We are meeting all our obligations
and we are definitely on the way to financial stability.
This is due to the fact that our membership, which is
steadily growing in number, has exhibited remarkable co¬
operation with the Center leadership. About 60 per¬
cent of our members have willingly increased their dues
so that we might be able to meet the rising cost of opera¬
tion due to post-depression conditions.
Our membership has also taken a more active part
in communal affairs. They have increased the ranks of
the outstanding Jewish organizations in the city; have
contributed generously to the campaigns of the Jewish
Community Council; and have responded magnificently
to a special appeal for the Youth Aliyah program.
All these things are an indication of a deep sense
of Jewish loyality that abides in the hearts of our mem¬
bers and an awareness of a great sense of responsibility
towards the local, national and international Jewish prob¬
lems. With such splendid cooperation on the part of
our members, and the spiritual guidance of our honored
Rabbi, I feel confident that the leadership of the Jack¬
sonville Jewish Center may look forward to a year of
great progress and new achievements.
May the Almighty God inscribe us all In the Book
of life, happiness and prosperity. Amen.

The past year has proven most gratifying to the
Jacksonville Jewish Center and has in more ways than
one given the officers and directors of the institution
reason to look back with considerable pride upon the
accomplishments recorded during the preceding twelve
months.
Whatever the field might be, whether it be religious,
educational, cultural, social, charitable, financial, athletic,
or otherwise, great progress has been noted in each
line, evidencing the continued rise to prominence of this
institution as a true "Center" of Jewish activity.
EDUCATION. Facilities for the education of both
children and adults were greatly increased during 5697,
with an increased enrollment being noted both in the
Center Sunday School and Hebrew School. In addition
to the regular curriculum studies, the children were pre¬
sented in an excellent Hanukah program, a highly success¬
ful Purim Carnival, and the annual Children's Sedar.
Hebrew classes for adults were conducted each Fri¬
day afternoon by Mrs. M. D. Margolis, and advanced
Talmudical studies were conducted each Saturday after¬
noons by Rev. G. Finn.
During the year eight boys, who were given their
required educational background by the Center, entered
the^ ranks' of our people through the traditional Bar
Mitzvah service. On Shabouth, eleven boys and girls
of the Sunday School were confirmed at a very im¬
pressive service.
RELIGIOUS. The High Holidays and Festivals were
fittingly observed in the beautiful Center Synagogue
with inspiring services conducted by the Rabbi and choir.
Friday night services, which were conducted each Friday
evening from Sucofh through Shabouth, were well at¬
tended, with capacity crowds being present on special
Sabbaths. Under the capable supervision of the Center
Sexton, Rev. J. Robin, daily services were held each morn¬
ing and evening throughout the year.
CULTURAL. The past year witnessed an improve¬
ment in the. calibre of artists and lecturers brought to the
Center in various concerts and lectures. Outstanding
musicians, artists, writers, poets, editors, and lecturers
were featured in the Center sponsored programs, staged
from time to time. A successful start was made in
establishing a Center Open Forum, three interesting
speakers addressing the audiences composed of both
Jews and Gentiles on current topics, which talks were
followed by periods of open discussion.
FINANCIAL. Th rough the cooperation of the
members in raising their dues and through an increase in
membership, the Center for the first time found itself
in a sound, financial position, capable of taking care of its
current budget expenditures, and allowing the officers
to plan for increased facilifies for the coming year.
However, the increased budget of the institution for the
coming year will necessitate the continued cooperation
of the members.
SOCIAL. Th roughout the year the Center proved
to be the true center of Jewish social activity, with the
various groups staging plays, concerts, dances, banquets,
bridge parties, luncheons, minstrels, and various other
entertainment features.
CLUBS. The Center cooperated with and spon¬
sored a number of boys and girls clubs, including the Boy
and Girl Scouts, and the several Young Judean Clubs.
In addition, the Center helped in every was the activities
of the adult groups, such as the Senior and Junior
Hadassah, Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society, Jewish
Progressive Society, A. Z. A., F. T. E., B'Nai Brith, and
others.
ATHLETICS. Another successful season was record¬
ed in the Jewish Center Diamond Ball League, with more
Han sixty Jewish athletes taking part in the games played
each Sunday morning at the Forest and Palm streets
diamond. The Center Boy Scout troop won the city¬

of the sixth year that Rev. Joseph
Robin has filled the position of Sex¬

ingly to these young people on their

odd tasks which .he performs, such

and the recitation of Memorial Pray¬

Keeping pace with the general progress made by
the Center itself, the Daughters of Israel, who serve in the
capacity of a sisterhood to the main organization, re¬

stantly aiding the congregation in its task of maintaining
the various Center activities.
Although the Daughters of Israel have projected
themselves into many fields of activity, their chief line
of endeavor is concerned with the maintenance of the
daily Hebrew School and Sunday School, through which
they have been able to provide every Jewish child
with the opportunity to receive a Jewish religious educa¬
tion.
In carrying out this educational work, the society
sponsored the entertainments and celebrations of the
Talmud Torah such as Simchas Torah, Chanukah, Purim,
and Pesach. All of the teachers of the Sunday School
were presented gifts by the Daughters of Israel at the
conclusion of the year's work, and the Confirmands were
similarly honored with gifts, consisting of Bibles and
prayer books.

The Jacksonville Jewish Center maintains both
a modern well equipped Talmud Torah, which ranks
with the finest in the Southeast, and a Sunday School,
which last year boasted a total enrollment of over 190.
The curriculum of the Talmud Torah provides for
a complete six year course which gives its pupils a
thorough training in reading and writing of modern
Hebrew, and the Hebrew Bible, thus preparing them
to participate intelligently in the traditional religious
services, and imbuing them with loyalty to the aspira¬
tions and ideals of the Jewish people, and to Palestine
as the symbol of the regeneration of our race and its
culture. Yiddish is also taught to the older pupils in
whose homes Yiddish is still spoken.
The curriculum of the Sunday School provides for
an intensive nine year course in Jewish history, cus¬
toms and ceremonies, beginning with the kindergarten
class and terminating with the Post-Confirmation Class.
It must be stressed, however, that Sunday School at
its best, offers the Jewish child a very inadequate
preparation for active and intelligent participation in
Jewish life and for an understanding of its heritage.
Since it is at most only effective as a supplement to
the Hebrew School education, members of the upper
grades who could not read Hebrew were last year
made to attend special classes to acquire a reading
knowledge of the Hebrew Prayer Book.
The number of children attending the Talmud
Torah last year was about 85, being divided into six
classes, a beginner's class for young children between
the ages of 6 and 8 years; a beginner's class for
children between 8 and 10 years; one elementary
Hebrew class; one advanced Hebrew class; one
Chumosh class; and a Bar Mitzvah class. The Center
furnished free tuition to 40% of the children last year
because of the inability of their parents to pay.
Transportation was provided to those of the students
residing in the Riverside section.
During the year the children of both the Hebrew
and Sunday Schools sponsored many interesting events
on the occasions of the major and minor festivals. The
Chanukah entertainment and the Purim Carnival were
notable successes. The Simchas Torah celebration was

On February 28, 1937, Jacksonville’s first chapter of the A.Z.A., Junior
Order of B’nai B’rith, came into existence. On that day Rabbi Labovitz, adviser
of the Savannah Chapter No. 206, and a group of Alephs from Savannah and
Charleston officially inititiated the local chapter into the order, immediately
following which the installation of officers took place.
A.Z.A. is a Jewish Youth Organization with an approximate membership
of 5000 boys in 300 chapters throughout the world. Every Jewish boy of good
character between the ages of fifteen and twenty-one is eligible for member¬
ship. When a boy reaches the age of twenty-one he is considered a life member
and then becomes a B’Nai Brith.
The executive body of the order is known as the Supreme Advisory Council,
which is composed of an executive secretary, an assistant executive secretary,
and members who are elected by a majority vote of the delegates to the Inter¬
national Convention held each year. A.Z.A. also has its own uniform consti¬
tution and by-laws which is subject to amendment by the delegates at the
convention. This year the local chapter sent their secretary, Meyer Leibovitz,
to Port Jervis, N. Y., where he wTas recognized as the official delegate of the
Jacksonville chapter.
“Five Fold and Full” is the terminology which designated the A.Z.A.
program and which found its origin in the fertile mind of the late Dr. Boris
D. Bogen, International Secretary of B’Nai Brith. Its activities consist of the
following lines of endeavor: Religious, Social Service, Cultural, Social, and
Athletic. The creed of the A.Z.A. is composed of seven cardinal virtues which
are as follows: Patriotism, Judaism, Filial Love, Charity, Conduct, Purity,
and Fraternity.
The local chapter, although very young, has accomplished a great deal
in the religious, social, and athletic fields, and in the. future they expect to
become very active upon the five-fold program of A.Z.A. At the present time
the local Alephs are striving for the development of a closer relationship among
the Jewish Youth of Jacksonville, and among the neighboring communities
throughout the Southeast. An outstanding program has been outlined for the
coming season.

As the local chapter of the international order of B’Nai Brith takes
stock of its activities of the past year, it is with a feeling of justification that
it looks forward to the future with sincere expectations for a continuation of its
splendid accomplishments of the preceding year,
B’Nai Brith, which was founded in 1843, has grown in nine decades to a
network of more than 400 local lodges throughout the United States and Canada.
In serving both Jewry and humanity in general, B’Nai Brith has long been
known for its work in such fields as philanthropy, defense of Jewish rights,
civic cooperation, social service, the spreading of good will, Americanization,
emergency relief, Hillel Foundations at Universities, and the formation of the
Anti-Defamation League.
The highlight of the year’s activities was the banquet tendered in the
Roosevelt Hotel on January 24th in honor of the Hon. Alfred M. Cohen, president
of the Order. Mr. Cohen addressed the gathering on the history of the organiza¬
tion, stressing the importance of the work that B’Nai Brith is today doing,
A number of B’Nai Brith members from St. Augustine, Daytona, Gainesville, and
Orlando attended the meeting.
Other highlights of the year were the visit of Mr. Hyman S. Jacobs and
Mr. Maier Triest, president and secretary respectively of District Five; the
monthly luncheons which were addressed by Mr. Joseph Wilensky, Nathan
Roberts, and Harold Cohn; and the installation of a local A. Z. A. chapter.
Three members of the local group, Mr. Abe Rothstein, Mr. Max Moss,
and the late A. B. Weil, attended a state-wide conference in Orlando, at which
time plans were formulated for the founding of a Hillel Foundation at the
University of Florida.
The chapter was very unfortunate during the past year in losing two of
its most active members through death: Adolph B. Weil, its secretary at the
time, and Joe Jacobson. In recognition of the invaluable services rendered by
Mr. Weil to the order and as a lasting tribute to his memory, the name of the
local chapter was changed to the Adolph B. Weil Lodge No. 800.

This committee known as the Chevra Kadishah, though long considered by
the community as a mere honorary group and though it lay dormant for a long
time, has effected a change in policy, and during the past two years has com¬
pleted what is no doubt one of the finest projects that local Jewry has ever
undertaken.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Cemetery Committee, which consists of
eight men elected for life, supervises the managment of the cemetery grounds
and in addition directs all burials. At the present time the by-laws of the
committee are being revised and a new code of regulations will soon become
effective.
The first move in the two-year program of the committee was the purchase
of the Cemetery from the Evergreen Cemetery Association, giving to the Center
the sole ownership of the grounds, For the completion of the second phase of
the work, that of beautifying the grounds of the much neglected property, the
committee and the Jewish community as a whole were extremely fortunate that
Mr. Simon Selber undertook to perform this most difficult task.
The work that has been done, such as the resodding and replanting of new
grass, cutting of walks, laying of pipe, planting of shrubs, improvement of the
roads, etc., is entirely due to Mr. Selber’s work.
The attention of the members of the Center is being called to the new
regulations which will shortly go into effect in an attempt to establish a
uniform and adequate method by which tne cemetery may be maintained in
its present splendid condition. There will be a continuation of the assessment
upon each lot owner of the nominal sum of ten dollars per year for the upkeep
of the individual lots, and it is hoped that the payments of such assessments
in the future will be more prompt, allowing the committee to function even more
successfully than in the past.
The committee has also formulated a definite policy as to regulations and
limitations upon the type of monuments to be erected, and solicits the co-
operation of the Center membership in carrying out these rules. The chairman
of the committee, Mr. Max Rubin wishes the members to understand that, in
time of emergency, they may communicate directly with him or Mr. Dave
Moscovitz or any other member of the committee.

The Jewish Progressive Society was organized
under the Charter laws of Florida, on the first
of January 1932, with 26 members. Mr. I. M.
Lieberman was the founder of this society, which
was built upon the remnants of the disbanded
B’rith Sholom Lodge. During the first four years
of its existence, the organization had only fra¬
ternal and charitable aims. During the year
1936-37 the Progressive Society took a decisive
step by affiliating itself with the Zionist Organ¬
ization of America. Two dollars of the five dollars
dues per member is sent to the Z. 0. A. and the
remainder is used for local and national charities.
Through the wise leadership of Mr. Lieberman,
the organization has made remarkable progress
during the past year, and now has a total of 156
paid up members.
The society has taken an active part in the
1937 campaign of the Jacksonville Jewish Com¬
munity Council Welfare Fund, Mr. Lieberman
being co-chairman of the campaign, with Mr.
Max Moss.
This being a Congress year the Jewish Pro¬
gressive Society conducted a vigorous Shekel
Campaign so that about 300 Jews voted on elec¬
tion day. This fine record should be credited to
the splendid work of the Shekel Committee, headT
ed by Mr. J. Robin, chairman, Mr. Murray B.
Glickstein, Mr. H. Kline, Mr. Henry Herzenberg,
Mr. J. Lapinsky and Mr. Beno Becker.
The Jewish Progressive Society also partici¬
pated in the Community Chest Campaign and
contributed to the Non-Sectarian Anti Nazi
League. It also extended help to members in need
and assisted a considerable number of unemployed
in obtaining work.
A fine educational program was also sponsored
during the year, under the guidance of Mr. Harry
Sachs, education chairman. Speakers at these
programs were Mr. L. H. Cohen, who spoke on
the Social Security Act; Mr. Sachs who delivered
a talk on the Florida Recovery Act, followed by
a discussion on the subject; Mr. A. H. Rothstein,
Mr. A. P. Carnot, Mr. Joe Goldstein and Mr. M. B.
Glickstein who debated the merits of the proposed
changes in the Supreme Court. Rabbi M. D.
Margolis delivered a lecture before the Society
on the “The Bible In Its Historical Background.”
The outstanding educational event was the lecture
delivered by Pierre van Paasen, distinguished
journalist, who was brought to Jacksonville by
the Society with the cooperation of the Senior
Hadassah. Mr. Sachs presided and Rabbi Mar¬
golis introduced the speaker. A large audience
of Jews and Gentiles was greatly impressed by
Van Paasen’s splendid address on Jewish condi¬
tions in Europe and Palestine.
Through the efforts of Mr. Lieberman, Mr.
Rothstein and Mr. Sachs a Zionist Group was
organized in St. Augustine. Mr. I. M. Lieberman
was a delegate to the Zionist Convention, and gave
a glowing report at the last meeting of the Society
in June. While in New York Mr. Lieberman laid
plans for the organization of a Southeastern Zion¬
ist Region. The Jewish Progressive Society views
with pride its achievements during 1936-37 and
is looking forward to a new year of progress and
good work.

During the past year the Jewish Welfare Society rend¬
ered a wide constructive service to the needy Jewish people
in the community and to stranded and sick strangers. Al¬
ways the Jewish Welfare Society is standing by alert to step
in and help with either funds or sympathetic and construc¬
tive advice. Throughout the wide range of its service the
society strives always towards the rehabilitation both of the
individual and the family and the cohesion of the home.
Last year 349 cases, representing about 900 individuals
presenting a variety of problems, were given financial aid,
medical, optical, dental and hospital care, loans, legal aid,
institutionl care and transportation to legal residence of
families.
Funds for carrying on the good work of the Jewish
Welfare Society is provided by the Community Chest Asso¬
ciation of Jacksonville.
The following standing and special committees served
efficiently during the year: Employment Committee, Mr. L
M. Lieberman, Chairman; Social Welfare Volunteer, Mrs.
Albert Somner, Chairman; Big Brother and Sister, Mrs.
M. D. Margolis, Chairman; and Special Community Chest,
Rabbi I. L. Kaplan, Chairman.
The Jewish Welfare Society takes this opportunity to
wish all its members and friends a very happy New Year
and to thank the Officers, Board Members, professional men,
merchants, organizations, institutions and individuals who
made it possible to carry on successfully the work of the
organization. Special appreciation is given the Ladies
Hebrew Sheltering and Aid Society for their splendid
co-operation.

Since the beginning of the Jewish Community in Jack¬
sonville, the Young Men’s Hebrew Association has played an
important role in the development of the local Jewish ele¬
ment, and only in the past few years has it been forced to
abandon temporarily its activities.
Its building located at 712 West Duval St., long the
scene of all Jewish activitiy, has some time ago lost its
usefulness because of the shifting of the Jewish population,
and is today leased to another organization. However, the
proceeds from this rental together with the equity owned in
the building forms a start with which the members hope to
erect new quarters in a more suitable location in the future.
The task of rebuilding and reawakening the Y. M. H. A.
is an opportunity for the youth of this city to accomplish
something which would not only fill a present need, but
which would prove of lasting worth to the Jewish population
of_J acksonville.
Temporarily, arrangements are being made to sponsor
several athletic teams during the coming year, utilizing
the Public School gymnasiums and city athletic fields.

ship consists of women, who have either come directly from Pushalot or who

Hadassah, the Women's Zionist Organization of
America, is a national organization now in its twenty-
fifth year, having chapters in 251 cities in the United
States. Through its chapter meetings, study circles,
Hebrew classes, choral and dramatic groups, lectures
and other activities, every Jewish woman in America
is given an opportunity to express herself Jewishly—to
bring a renewed appreciation of Jewish values into her
home, at the same time contributing her share toward the
rebuilding of the Jewish National Homeland.
From the tiny settlement estalished by its first two
nurses in 1913 was developed a modern complete med¬
ical and public health system that has won praise of
eminent medical authorities. Today, crowning the years
of splendid achievement, Hadassah with the cooperation
of the American Jewish Physicians' Committee, is erect¬
ing a medical center on Mount Scopus that will correlate
the curative, preventive and teaching branches of medi¬
cine a twentieth century edifice to science. Its alliance
with the Jewish National Fund and its new 'project, the
Youth Aliyah for the settlement of German and Polish
children in Palestine has given Hadassah still greater
opportunity for service to Palestine.
No less remarkable than the steady and progressive
development of the Hadassah work in Palestine that has
so profoundly influenced the well-being of the entire
country, is the growth of the Hadassah organization in
this country and the administrative ability of its leader¬
ship. With a program of Jewish education in this coun¬
try that is attuned to the times and a field of work in
Palestine, that has a potent appeal, it is little wonder that
Hadassah has been able to'rally more than 55,000 women
to its membership rolls.
Mrs. Nat Cohen, as president of the local unit of
Senior Hadassah for 1936-37, enjoyed a year of unusually
successful endeavor, devoting much of her time and
effort to the work of the organization, and her distinctive
success as a leader and ardent Zionist, is manifested by
the accomplishments of the chapter here during the
past year.

visit to Jacksonville by Frau Yetta Levy-Stein of Berlin,
who came here in the interest of Youth Aliyah. She was
guest of honor at the annual Membership Tea, and also
addressed a combined meeting of the Jewish Temple
and the Jewish Center, in the Center building. FHer
earnest appeal for financial aid for the Youth Aliyah
movement met with overwhelimng success and inspired
many to a new interest in the work of Hadassah.

The Ladies’ Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society and Home for the Aged, organ¬
ized for the purpose of giving shelter and aid to transients, supplying them with
Kosher Food and providing lodging in Jewish environment; relieving the needs
of local unfortunates; establishing a Free Loan Fund, the Gmieles Chesed,
whereby many families are enabled to help themselves over a financial crisis;
and giving immigrant aid wherever possible, avoiding the necessity of deporta¬
tion for many; and with the goal of establishing a Home for the Aged, a Moshev
Zehkenim, has to its credit the continual progress of all the former objects
through these years and an actual sound accomplishment towards the achieve¬
ment of the latter object, working diligently, though slowly and surely.
During the past several months representative members of the Ladies’
Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society and Home for the Aged and the Jewish Pro¬
gressive Society have selected the following officers to lead the Joint Home
Committee: Mr. Joseph Wilensky, Chairman; Mrs. M. Stein, Co-Chairman;
Mr. J. Lapinsky, Vice-Chairman; Mrs. J. Shapiro, Vice-Chairman; Mr. Morris
Wolfson, Treasurer; Mrs. PI. Rosenzvaig, Secretary; Mrs. H. Talisman, Ass’t.
Secretary.
The first step of this Joint Committee has been to enter into negotiations
for the purchase of a most desirable property for the building of a Home for
the Aged, where those who will be fortunate enough to spend their reclining
years, will have a haven of comfort and peace, in a clean, beautiful and quiet
location.
The annual reports given at the Installation of Officers during the early
part of the year have shown most unusual progress, stressing the methods
used by the Ladies’ Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society in providing the needy with
food, clothing, fuel, medicines, etc., without any embarrassment, heartache,
or publicity, following the ancient method of the Jews of giving where and when
needed, without the mention of names, thereby saving the self-respect of the
recipient.

The second annual Welfare Fund Campaign conducted by the Jacksonville
Jewish Community Council established the work of this organization as one
of the most successful undertakings of the local Jewish community.
The Council has for its aim the uniting of all fund raising campaigns by
sponsoring one joint welfare fund, which eliminates the many independent
solicitations and separate campaigns, and permits these funds to be equitably
distributed according to the merits and budgets of the respective beneficiary
institutions.
The 1937 Campaign for funds, conducted during the early months of the
year, was launched at a luncheon and meeting with an address by Dr. Ben
Zion Mossinsohn, one of the foremost leaders in the World Zionist movement.
Mr. Max Moss served as chairman of the drive and Mr. I. M. Lieberman acted
as co-chairman.
Harry Finkelstein, Robert L. Seitner, and B. S. Levy were the Honorary
Chairmen; Harry W. Reinstine, Max Rubin, Ben Stein, and Joseph Glickstein
were the Vice-Chairmen. A special gift committee included Halle Cohen,
Morton R. Hirschberg, Dave Moscovitz, and the late Adolph Weil. Rabbi Morris
D. Margolis and Rabbi Israel L. Kaplan served as the secretaries of the cam¬
paign, while Jac Rosenberg performed as treasurer.
Committees from the Jewish Progressive Society, the Temple Ahaveth
Chesed, the Jacksonville Jewish Center, the Junior and Senior Hadassahs, the
Temple Sisterhood, the Daughters of Israel, the Ladies’ Hebrew Sheltering Aid
Society and Home for the Aged, the B’Nai Brith, the Workmen’s Circle, and
the Y. M. H. A. all worked diligently to insure the success of the Campaign.
Mr. Simon J. Levine, representative of the United Palestine Appeal worked
tirelessly with the Campaign Organization.
The Budget Committee allocated the funds to many worthy American
and overseas educational and charitable institutions. The chief beneficiaries
were the Distribution Committee, the United Palestine Appeal, and Jewish na¬
tional institutions, such as the Denver Hospital, the Atlanta Orphan Home, the
B’Nai Brith Wider Scope, and others.
Three hundred donors pledged $6,000.00 of which $4,600.00 has already been
collected, the balance of $1,400.00 to be paid on pledges. Disbursements to date
total $4,515.00.
The various charitable institutions and hospitals who receive allocations
from these funds and who are constantly giving assistance and care to thou¬
sands of needy and ill men, women, and children, have expressed their apprecia¬
tion for the cooperation they have received from the Jewish community of
Jacksonville through the United Welfare Fund.
The officers of the Council urge all the members of our Jewish community
to give their wholehearted support to this organization, so that local Jewry
may do its share in alleviating the suffering and widespread disaster of our
fellow Jews overseas, and in helping our less fortunate brothers in America.
The officers and members of the organization for the United Welfare Fund
Campaign for 1937 express their thanks to all the Division Leaders, Team
Captains, volunteer committees from cooperating organizations for their un¬
tiring efforts, and to the management of the Roosevelt Hotel for their assistance
in arranging the workers’ luncheons and meetings held there throughout the
Campaign.

upon successive pages, and en¬

the book as a complete calendar

dar contains the Hebrew names
of the festivals, holidays, and

cular Sabbath by the name

val candles (L. C.) appears op¬
posite the proper days.

dar contains the English names

a calendar of the important

found to be very valuable, giv¬
ing each organization an oppor¬
tunity to plan for their respec¬
tive undertakings without con¬
flicting with the other organiza¬

ing them ample time to prepare
for these events, assuring their
success.

brief explanations of the holi¬
days and special Sabbaths, and
various other explanatory notes
concerning special occasions and
events.

1) Rosh Hashonah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and lasts
two days. It is also the beginning of the 10 days of Penitence, which end with Yom
Kippur. Sincere prayer and meditation during these days help us to experience
the reality of God to dedicate ourselves anew to the establishment of His Kingdom
on earth—the reign of freedom, justice and peace.
2) Shabbat Shuba derives its name from the Haftorah of that day. Hosea
xiv, 2-10 which begins with the word ShubahAReturn’, i.e., Repent.
3) Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when we
strive to reawaken the latent spiritual power in our souls so that it may become
an effective and directing force in our daily conduct.
4) Sukkos (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of thanksgiving after
all the products of the field have been gathered in. The Sukkah symbolizes the
wanderings of our ancestors in the wilderness of Sinai.
5) Simchath Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of Shemini
Azereth. Then the reading of the Pentateuch is completed and begun anew for the
coming year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos—procession with the scrolls
of the Torah to the accompaniment of songs and dancing.
6) Rosh Chodesh is the term for the beginning of a new month. As the
Jewish year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coin¬
cides with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered the
first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodesh of the month following.
