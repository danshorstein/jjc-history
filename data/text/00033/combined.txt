Rabbi David Gaffney
in his study
The last words of the verse read . . so that I
may dwell in their midst.” We must become a com-
munity in which, as it were, God could dwell with
pride. A synagogue which elicits among its congre-
gants, a microcosm of the Jewish people, love, sensiti-
vity, concern and compassion; a synagogue which
exudes friendliness and warmth, which elicits among
its members a concern for the plight of those less
fortunate than we, for all of mankind, for the Jew
in the Soviet Union and for the security and vitality
of the State of Israel, is a place in which the spirit of
God may dwell.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is at the threshold
of a historic beginning. Through the efforts of our
Building Development Fund Committee and the guid-
anee of our Architects Committee we are at a point
in which a move to our site on State Road 13 is
imminent. As I write these words construction on the
new Sanctuary-Center has begun and with God’s help
we will be worshipping together at the next High
Holidays in our new home. I pray ihat we remain
conscious of the challenge and sensitive to the pur-
pose for which synagogues are fashioned so that we
may fulfill the historic demand expressed by the
words “let them make Me a Sanctuary so that I may
dwell in their midst.”
With every best wish to our members and friends,
from Inge, myself, Asher and Shalva, for a year of
blessing, health, joy, contentment, fulfillment and
peace. Amen
״Let them make me a sanctuary so that I may dwell
in their midst”
Exodus 25:8
This verse, taken from the book of Exodus, is the
traditional Biblical source for the enterprise in which
hundreds of generations of Jewish communities have
been engaged—the building of Houses of Worship and
the fashioning of Jewish communities around the
synagogue as the center of its collective life. The
verse, beginning as it does with the words “Let them
make me . . .” as well as subsequent verses in the
text suggest that support for the sanctuary was often
the free will and voluntary offering of the heart.
And so it is. Judaism is a voluntary enterprise.
One can choose to become sensitive to Jewish
literature and the patterns of Jewish life ... or discard
and ignore them. This does not mean that a decision
can be dismissed cavalierly in a “you can take it or
leave it” attitude. On the contrary, the most import-
ant things in life are “voluntary.” Love, the blessing
of family, the quest for wisdom and understanding;
each represent a “voluntary” enterprise which can en-
rich us. We are impoverished not so much by what we
cannot take from life but by what we choose not to
give to life. Synagogue affiliation alone, however, in
and of itself, does not enrich us. It is important to
belong. But affiliation without affirmation is, to auote
an old Yiddish song, like a body without a soul. Syna-
gogues exist so that through prayer, study and fellow-
ship; through the modes and patterns of Jewish life,
a common ground is created for families, for parents,
children and a community to realize the best that is
within themselves and the traditions to which they
are heirs.
The second phrase of the verse from Exodus.
“Mikdash,” has as its root the Hebrew word of “holy.”
,This is not the place to engage in a study of langu-
age or theology, but the word “holy” at the very
least indicates that a synagogue has a purpose and
goal; an end towards which it directs itself . . . the
common striving of a group of Jews to establish a
conscious relationship between themselves and God,
mankind, their history, rich and life-affirming tradi-
tions and their destiny. Only the synagogue has so
comprehensive a goal for the Jewish community. As
significant and important as are the concerns of other
Jewish affiliations and organizations, without the on-
going and historic inspiration which comes from the
synagogue, we would long since have disappeared as a
vital people, religious and culture.

President’s Message
This past year has given us many memorable
events for the Jacksonville Jewish Center; the Ground-
breaking Ceremony, Rabbi Gaffney’s Congregational
Trip to Israel, “Simcha 13”, and most notably the
start of construction of our new Synagogue-Center on
San Jose Blvd.
In retrospect, the success of each activity was
the result of enthusiastic, committed members whose
devoted and loyal efforts brought about beautiful mo-
ments for all of us to enjoy.
Two words especially, enthusiasm and commit-
ment, have left an indelible impression on my mind
ever since I assumed the responsibility of President.
How good it would be if in the New Year all of
us would devote some of our energies to the better-
ment of the Jewish Center.
This is my personal hope and prayer for 5735.
“L’SHANAH TOVAH.”
CHARLES J. MIZRAHI

Do people think of you as being rich or poor?
And how do they measure this?
It is easy for us to become confused about the meaninq of wealth and poverty.
We think of wealth as the piling up of money, or the things money can buy; we
measure our wealth by whether we have more or less of these things than others do.
Yet, isn't it strange
The more things we own, the more likely we are to want more and more of them;
THE MORE LIKELY WE ARE TO BE ALWAYS IN WANT.
We seek possession of things in order to live well, and yet, we often see that
such possessions tend to splinter a man's life and separate him from others.
How then can we enrich ourselves?
WE CAN ARRIVE AT ENDURING ACHIEVEMENT AND OUR OWN FULFILL-
MENT ONLY THROUGH THE WEALTH OF LIFE ITSELF.
For these choices are always open to us,
To choose things . . . which limit, divide and consume us,
To choose LIFE — which enlarges us, involves us, and continues us,
To choose LIFE means:
—gaining possession of knowledge of ourselves, our fellow men, and the universe.
—experiencing the beauty of Creation; a tree, the sky; a song.
—building our lives on the foundations of wisdom — of the past and present.
For these enlarge us.
It means
—giving of our thoughts, our feelings, our time . . . GIVING OF OURSELVES
... to our children, wife, husband, neighbor or friends.
—meeting our deepest responsibilities in the community, in our country, our
people, wherever they may be.
—sharing the pain and suffering of others, knowing well that "THEY WHO
SOW IN TEARS SHALL REAP IN JOY." (Psalms 126:5)
For these involve us.
It means
—fighting an evil
—nurturing an ideal
—fashioning a character
All of which extend our impact and meaning in the world beyond our lives,
joining us with the generations of men, as with our Maker.
For these continue us.
Having chosen life . . .
Having chosen to enlarge, involve and continue ourselves . . .
We discover the endless creativity of life, its enduring capacity for good is this:
AS WE SHARE WITH OTHERS THE MEASURE OF LIVING, WE INCREASE
THEM FOR OURSELVES.

In the course of time the overall structure of this
Council was generally broadened. While the main
purpose is still fund raising, it also attempts to be a
unifying force of Jacksonville Jewry. Now, our united
voice is heard on matters of importance to Jews all
over the world. Israel, Soviet Jewry, etc., are among
our primary objectives of grave concern and concerted
actions.
Last, but not least, the aim and goal of the
Council is to extend the horizon of Jewish identity
and to intensify a desire for Jewish education to a
more significant depth on a community wide basis.
We endeavor to eliminate duplication, fragmantation
and its consequences. A united effort on this particular
field benefits all segments of the community.
We all have so much in common. Let us strive
together for the causes that are dear to all of us.
The Jacksonville Jewish Community Council was
organized in the middle 193IO’s by Rabbi Israel L.
Kaplan and Rabbi M. D. Margohs, then Rabbi of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center, and a few others.
The original purpose of the founders was to eli-
minate the large number of solicitors, ‘meshoolachim’,
who visited the city regularly. We realized that a
considerable percentage of the money collected was
absorbed by the various solicitors’ expenses. Hence,
the Council was formed to eliminate the unnecessary
high cost of collection and give the institutions a
greater share of the funds raised collectively by local
volunteer workers.
During the Second World War this organization
was expanded considerably, as the need for relief of
European Jewry increased enormously.

The uniqueness of River Garden is that its em-
phasis is on “wellness,” not “illness,” despite the fact
that it is a medical facility. The stress is on the capa-
cities, not the disabilities, which modem medicine can
not reverse, but only alleviate. Therefore, the focus is
on what can still be done. Over 20 activity programs
have been designed to fit individual preference and
capability. Participating in chapel services, arts and
crafts, River Garden Players, Tea and Talk, Men’s
Only, the Yiddish or Classical Music Hour, or more
sedentary activities is only a medium for developing
new relationships to substitute for those which are
now gone by loss or separation from loved ones.
Another interesting fact is that the Home does
not limit its admissions to those over 65 only, but
does waive the age limitation for younger people who
are in need of our special services. Twenty residents
in the younger category are now being served by the
Home.
Mental impairment in the aged is not unusual
and all too often committment to a State Hospital
occurs. River Garden is particularly proud of its record
in this area because so few elderly Jewish patients
are now in the State Hospital' System. As soon as the
acute phases of the illness is over, the referral is made
to the Home.
The Home, which serves Florida, excepting the
Greater Miami area, appreciates the support and co-
operation of the Jewish Communities of Florida. Thanks
to these communities, no one is ever denied admission
because of limited financial resources.

CHEVRA KADISHA OF JACKSONVILLE
The care and consideration and respect that
are bestowed upon the living must be accorded the
dead as they are attended, prepared and escorted
to their final abode on earth. To assist in the prepa-
ration and burial of the dead is one of the greatest
mitzvot in our faith.
The association that is organized to perform
this service is appropriately named Chevra Kadisha,
the Holy Society. It was one of the first associations
to be established in the traditional Jewish com-
munity of the past. Membership in the Chevra
Kadisha has always been accounted a unique privi-
lege. The members must be observant Jews, of high
moral character, who meet together from time to
time to review the requisite rituals and practices
and the problems that are encountered when the
Chevra Kadisha officiates.
The local Chevra Kadisha was organized this
past January as an organization completely inde-
pendent of, but working closely with the local
synagogues and rabbis, to prepare and bury the
dead with honor and dignity according to Jewish
law and customs. President of the local group is
Joel Bressler who may be contacted for further
information.
