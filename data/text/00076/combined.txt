BUILD-A-SUKKAH COMPETITION
In 2014 we competed to build the best sukkah on the grounds of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center. This year, we took the competition on the
road to showcase the sukkot in your homes! Prizes were awarded for
Most Creative, Most Energy Conscious and Best Display of Judaic
Content. Thank you to all of our entries: The Dubow Preschool,
The Clayman Family, The Jaffa Family, The Goldstein Family, The
Horovitz Family, The Fubliner Family, The Millman Family, The
Morris Family, the Wolpoff Family, and The Zaifert Family. Here’s
the description of our fan favorite, the Clayman Family Sukkah:
For anyone who has ever experienced this season called “Fall,”
the Clayman sukkah brought this season to life. A cornucopia was
on display with gourds and other fruits. This lattice structure was
accented by three aspects- a creative sign that says, “Clayman Family Sukkah”, a fall-friendly (and rain
friendly) tablecloth, and a “welcome crow” that welcomed all visitors into the sukkah.
For amazing descriptions of our other entries please visit https://buildasukkah.wordpress.com/.

GALINSKY
ACADEMY
Brent Trager
VP of Education, Galinsky Academy
Shalom and Shanah Tovah! As the
Jacksonville Jewish Center’s VP
of Education, I feel humbled and
honored to serve Jacksonville’s Jewish
community. Over the past few years
I have been privileged to work with
and learn from Debby Kaye, whose
service to the schools and community
has been remarkable. I am inspired
by the dedication and enthusiasm that
the Galinsky Academy’s leaders, teachers, and staff bring to their
work, and am thrilled to help offer and deliver a high-quality secular
and Jewish Education to our children. I strongly believe that Jewish
education is the best bridge from our history to our future.
The Galinsky Academy provides Jewish children of all ages, from infancy through high school, the highest
quality education. Comprised of DuBow Preschool, Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School, Martin J.
Gottlieb Day School, and Setzer Youth Education, the Galinsky Academy embodies the commitment that all our
children - regardless of the path their parents choose - benefit from the finest teachers, an engaged clergy, and
the best curriculum and technology available.
Our schools and programs teach respect for the diversity of Jewish practice. The Galinsky Academy is a
place where Jewish children, regardless of background, come together around a shared heritage. The Galinsky
Academy not only provides innovative educational programs, it also engages a diverse student and parent
community. We are truly a family of families, a community of learners, representing a wide range of Jewish
practices, commitments, and beliefs. Our children and youth are the thought leaders, doctors, Israel advocates,
community volunteers, philanthropists, inventors, artists, entrepreneurs, teachers, lawyers, and engaged Jewish
adults of the future.
With your help, Jewish Jacksonville will continue to produce a wellspring of young leaders. The Jacksonville
Jewish community shows how much it values our efforts to educate our children through the annual L’Dor V’Dor
giving campaign. This annual campaign is critical to ensure our vision for Jewish Education becomes a reality.
If you regularly donate to this campaign, I thank you for your generous support. If you have not been a recent
supporter, I encourage you to give generously to this cause. Raising the next generation of Jewish children
requires the support of the entire community. Please show that you find the cause of Jacksonville’s Jewish
Education to be worthy of a spot at the top of your philanthropic initiatives. We need everyone’s support to
ensure Jewish life in Jacksonville can do more than just survive. Together, we can thrive!
It is truly a humbling experience to serve this community as your VP of Education for the Jacksonville
Jewish Center. With the support of our community and our staff, the children and their families we serve will
learn the skills necessary to excel in the world of tomorrow, while honoring and respecting our historical Jewish
heritage. May our efforts to serve our community go from strength to strength, from generation to generation.
Sincerely,
Brent Trager
Galinsky Academy, VP of Education

SOCIAL JUSTICE
Our Social Justice Committee, known as Rodet Tzedek (pursuer of justice), had a banner year working with
our interfaith affiliate ICARE (Interfaith Coalition for Action, Reconciliation and Empowerment), to promote
positive change for our community. As the only synagogue among its 40 members, the Jacksonville Jewish
Center’s team, led by Kim Glasgal, attended community problem meetings as well as the great Nehemiah
Assembly. Our members fought for the rights of everyone in Jacksonville, dealing with issues of crime, support
for our mental health services, among others. Among ICARE’s achievements in the past year:
1. Signed the Memorandum of Understanding that expands civil citations and restorative justice to all first-time
nonviolent youth. It will ensure that at least 90% of eligible youth receive civil citations in Jacksonville. This
saves the city millions in arrest fees and saves the lives of our youth.
2. Two other counties, Clay and Nassau, signed on to make civil citations the default. Our work is making
waves outside of the county.
3. Restorative Justice is now seen as the best alternative to jail in our city for nonviolent youth. We have
worked on this campaign since 2011 and it only happened because we are persistent and continue to build
power. Sheriff Williams committed to issue civil citations to 90% of eligible youth by March 27th, 2018.
He also committed to working with Teen Court and State Attorney Nelson to find funding for three more
neighborhood accountability boards.
4. $900,000 has been allocated to the Jacksonville Re-entry Center and six new staff persons have been hired
and more people are being served because of it. Darian Jones spoke eloquently about his experience with the
Re-entry Center.
5. State Attorney Nelson committed to divert youth post arrest to restorative justice programs and work with
judges on this as well. She also agreed to work towards funding.
This work is based upon “people power.” We need more of our congregants to take an active role to ensure
Jacksonville is a safe place for all of its citizens. If you’d like to get more involved in social justice work for
Jacksonville and the greater community, please contact us!

HESED COMMUNITY
The Hesed Committee at the Jacksonville Jewish Center is comprised of caring members of our own
congregational family who are available to perform acts of loving-kindness, such as:
Making a phone call • Providing a friendly visit • Sending a card • Delivering a meal
How many times have you made a phone call to someone who was ill, visited someone in the hospital or
comforted someone who was grieving? Chances are you have performed countless acts of loving-kindness
to someone going through difficult times. What about when the tables are turned? Most of us have no qualms
about helping someone else, yet we resist letting someone else help us. Why is it so much easier to give than
to receive?
Maybe it would be easier if we took a moment to remember that the mitzvah is not only in our providing
the kindness, it is also being able to receive a kindness. When we accept the kindness of others, we are giving
someone else the opportunity to practice the mitzvot of “g’milut hasadim” just as we have done in the past,
and just as we will continue to do in the future.
Please call or email Danielle Berke at (904) 292-1000, dberke@jaxjewishcenter.org if you or someone
you know would benefit from receiving any of these simple gifts of kindness. Let us all work together to keep
these sacred traditions with each other and strengthen our caring community.

I«-A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
BIRDS OF A FEATHER FLOCK TOGETHER
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
Jack F. Shorstein Senior
Rabbinic Chair
Florida is a bird watcher’s paradise, though I make no claim to knowing much
about the species that fly through our neighborhood. Yet even someone as
ignorant of ornithology as I was amazed by the chance to see the thousands of
Purple Martins passing through Jacksonville this summer. Neither the name of a
cocktail nor a rock band, the Purple Martin is North America’s largest swallow, a
swift and skilled flyer who eats, drinks, and even bathes on the wing. Migratory
birds that live in the U.S. and Canada, Purple Martins winter as far south as
Brazil.
For some unfathomable reason known only to the birds themselves, a flock
of thousands - enough to show up on Doppler radar - chose the trees along
Flogan Street, just west of the Jacksonville Landing, as their home-away-from-
home. From late June through much of July around dusk, one could watch from
the North Bank Riverwalk as countless birds swirled through the darkening
skies in moving patterns that constantly changed shape. As the various hues of
blue deepened, the Purple Martins dove and wheeled ever closer, finally coming
to roost in a copse of small trees next door to the Landing. As they settled down
for the night and crammed each and every branch, the chittering noise they made sounded like a rather loud
waterfall. It was an incredible sight to see and hear. In my amazement at these sights and sounds, a verse from
Scripture passed through my thoughts: “How many are the things You have made, O Lord, You have made
them all with wisdom; the earth is full of Your creations” (Psalm 104:24).
Watching countless thousands of birds wing their way across the sky in an unpredictable wave, one could
only marvel at how they managed to fly in sync without even a single collision. Scientists call this behavior
“flocking.” Each bird’s change of direction is a response to the movements of its neighbors. Every flyer
benefits from the updraft created by the flapping of the wings of the birds in front - so much so that when
any bird falls out of formation, it will notice the extra air resistance it encounters and immediately get back
in line. When the flock changes direction, new leaders will take the helm; as they grow tired, they will rotate
back into formation while others then take the lead. Should a sick or wounded bird drop out of formation,
another bird will follow it until it recovers or dies.
Flocking may be for the birds, but avian behavior could teach us a thing or two about how to better
connect as individuals to the synagogue and vice versa. There is a beautiful fluidity in the way the behavior of
the flock shapes the behavior of the individual. Yet being part of a community doesn’t cost the individual his
or her own identity. On the contrary being a part of the collective enhances the experience of the individual;
sometimes it may even save a person’s life.
In the year 2017 we are caught between a radically self-absorbed culture defined by the secular ethos of
the selfie and the myriad religious fanatics who dismiss the value of individual lives. One extreme is vapid
and selfish; the other is warped and deadly. Judaism rejects both extremes. At the Jacksonville Jewish Center
we believe in Hillel’s dictum, “Do not separate yourself from the community” (Mishnah Avot 2:4), but also
recognize that a community’s ultimate worth is measured by the ways in which
it makes a difference in the lives of its individual members. In the year ahead let
us help you forge a stronger connection to your synagogue family. Joy shared is
multiplied; burdens carried together are lightened. But hey, don’t take my word
for it; just look up to the sky.
Susan, Avichai, Elior, and Itamar join me in wishing you a happy and healthy
New Year 5778, one of health, blessing, and contentment in community. May we
all be inscribed in the book of life.
Shanah Tovah Tikatevu u 'Tehatemu,
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER FUTURES COMMITTEE
Two years ago, under the leadership of Mike Mass, the Jacksonville Jewish Center launched an amazing
program called the JJC Futures. The Center has over 100 years of rich and beautiful history that is made possible
by two important components; incredible loyal congregants and the devotion of strong leadership. The JJC
Futures Committee was formed to ensure the continuation of education about our history and governance
process of current and future leaders within our community. It was decided that an infusion of knowledge for
our membership would be greatly beneficial to potential incoming leaders.
Since its formation, two groups of JJC members have gone through our “leadership” course. As we went
along, the committee itself went through a learning process demonstrated by the evolution of the course between
the first and second class offerings. Regular feedback from the members indicates that there has been great
satisfaction with the overall experience. The course will be offered at regular intervals to ensure that our present
and future leaders will always have a basic knowledge about the functions of the JJC. It is also intended to
teach and exercise various leadership skills. This course and its governing committee are to be supported by an
endowment for governance at the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
The JJC Futures committee will meet on a regular basis to continue the development and presentation of our
leadership course. We also hope to ensure the evolution of this course to explore new ideas as we all move into
our collective future in Conservative Judaism.
If you are interested in participating in the class or on the committee or have further questions, please
contact Mike Mass, (904) 737-6773, massjax@comcast.net.

DAY SCHOOL FUSES SCIENCE & ART FOR INNOVATIVE LEARNING
Inspired by the national conversation taking place regarding integrating creativity and science, Martin J. Gottlieb
Day School has begun implementing a STEAM approach to teaching. An acronym for Science, Technology,
Engineering, Art, and Math, STEAM seeks to bridge the logical and creative sides of the brain to the benefit of
both, with the goal being to encourage innovative thinking. Proponents of STEAM methodology believe it is
vital to competitiveness on the world stage in areas of technology innovation, medical research, and clean energy.
Second graders learned about white light and prisms in science class, while learning about color theory in
art. Art teacher Shana Gutterman explains, “We are following in the footsteps of DaVinci and Rube Goldberg.
Scientists and artists both observe and use the world around them.” “By integrating the knowledge learned in
science with the creativity fostered through art, we aim to enhance our students’ understanding,” adds science
teacher Karianne Jaffa.
With an initial donation from the Galinsky Academy PTA, our STEAM teachers were able to purchase
sets of ‘littleBits,’ which are modular snapping building blocks that have a variety of functions and which put
creativity and ingenuity right into the hands of our students. They offer an outlet for structured projects, and
also an excellent opportunity for independent invention and maker-station work. Classes throughout our lower
and middle schools have already had chances to experiment and build with the littleBits sets. For example, our
Third Grade’s unit on the rainforest became a cross-curricula project. During language arts they researched
information about rainforests, and in science class made terrariums based on their research. In P.E. they even
played a game about the rainforest! Art class taught how to paint toucan birds and also how rainsticks are
made. The students then constructed a robotic arm from the littleBits sets to rotate the rainstick!
A day school family was so impressed by the lessons that they committed $15,000 to take the STEAM
program to the next level. Want to see how STEAM learning works in action? Please contact the day school
office at 268-4200 to schedule a tour!

BAR MITZVAH CELEBRATION
On the morning of January 28,2017, the Jacksonville
Jewish Center community came together to honor
its Jack F. Shorstein Senior Rabbinic Chair, Rabbi
Jonathan Lubliner, for reaching the milestone of 13
years of service to the congregation. The heimish
service involved the participation of family and
special friends who shared personal stories of the
impact that Rabbi Lubliner has had on their lives.
Colleagues and congregants shared their stories in a
special memory book for the Lubliner family. In a
special tribute, ten representatives (a prayer quorum)
of the community gave thanks for the different facets
of Rabbi Lubliner’s pulpit, thanking him for being
our rabbi, our mentor, our teacher and our friend. A big kudos to the whole committee who worked on everything
from decorating tables to assembling the memory book. Rabbi Lubliner shared his own favorite 13 memories
from these past 13 years, the most poignant being the last one:
Of course, I will always remember how our community pulled together when Scott Zimmerman and Don
Kriss passed away. I’ve tried to stay away from referring to the sorrows and celebrations of individuals and their
families because they are too many to mention and I don’t want anyone to make invidious comparisons. Suffice
it to say that the opportunity to be a part of SO many people’s lives at critical junctions, some sweet and others
sad, has meant so much to me. I estimate that in the past 12 years, 6 months, I’ve officiated at 70 to 75 baby
namings (and britot milah), 300 b’nai mitzvah, 90 weddings, and nearly 400 funerals...plus 7 duckling namings.
What is most wonderful is to be present at a naming or bris and then officiate at that child’s bar/bat mitzvah, or
having participated in a child’s Jewish coming-of-age to see him/her go on to college. You truly feel like you are
a part of someone’s life when you share multiple liminal moments in that person’s journey through time.
Mazel tov to the entire Lubliner family!

ISRAELI AFFAIRS
As American Jews, we care very deeply
about Israel. Even though many of us may
have never visited Israel, we are deeply
committed to our Jewish homeland. There
is much to learn about and many different
ways to support Israel. The Israeli Affairs
Committee attempts to raise awareness
about Israel through educational and
cultural activities, inviting synagogue
members to participate in community
events at our synagogue and with the
Jacksonville Jewish community at large.
This past year we were fortunate to
have many opportunities for involvement.
Rabbi Matthew L. Berkowitz,the Director
of Israel Programs for The Jewish
Theological Seminary of America, visited
us from Jerusalem. He presented a short
film “Barriers,” a winner of First Prize
for Short Drama at the Jerusalem Film
Festival 2011 and Best Film Intercultural
Dialogue at the International Munich
Film Festival, amongst other awards. We
had a great turn out for this event, which
involved breaking into discussion groups
about this thought provoking and moving
short film.
We also hosted an AIPAC inspired
meeting for JJC high school students as
well as participants from The Temple and
Etz Chaim. AIPAC brought in their Early Affairs Director Josh Losner who works with high school and
college age students to engage them in becoming aware of and advocating for the support of Israel. The
students were attentive, inquisitive, and interactive with Mr. Fosner. Many of them expressed an interest in
attending AIPAC’s national meeting in Washington, DC.
Later in the year, a speaker from ZOA joined us and facilitated a meaningful discussion on the topic
“The Boycott Divestment and Sanctions Movement Against Israel and the Reality of Anti-Semitism.” The
discussion was followed by a wonderful meal.
For Yom Ha’Atzmaut,The Israeli Affairs Committee proudly participated in bringing the first community
wide celebration in many years to the entire Jacksonville community. Over 600 people attended the event,
showing a huge outpouring of support for Israel. We provided an opportunity to learn about the Israeli Defense
Forces complete with army band music, real IDF uniforms to try on, and a photo backdrop for picture taking.
There was also an informative movie about The Lone Soldier program and an opportunity to write letters to
the soldiers to show our support for them.
Many thanks to our clergy and staff at the JJC for their help in making these events happen. If you would
like to get involved or have an interesting idea or suggestion for Israeli Affairs, please let us know!

Rena was not more than a week old when I compiled a list of “Best Songs About
Daughters” on my iPhone. Trying to put down into words what this new title of
“daddy” really meant to me, I knew that music and great storytelling could help
me put it all into perspective. Over the past 4+ years, I’ve taken a peek back at
the list every so often to get a little nostalgic and a little verklempt. One song,
Billy Dean’s “Let Them Be Little,” ended up as the soundtrack on both of our
daughters’ one year old video compilations:
Let them be little, cause they’re only that way for a while.
Give them hope, give them praise, give them love, everyday.
Let ‘em cry, let ’em giggle, let them sleep in the middle.
Oh just let them be little.
I hadn’t touched the list since March of 2013, but I began adding to it after
chaperoning a mitzvah trip for our Martin J. Gottlieb Day School this past
November. Looking for a radio station that actually plays music, our ride moved
between 99.9 Gator Country and 99.1 WQIK. No talk, just great country music and even greater story telling.
I’ve been hooked ever since. Between Tim McGraw’s “My Little Girl,” Darius Rucker’s “It Won’t Be Like
This for Long,” and Zac Brown Band’s “My Old Man,” I can’t get through a car ride without getting a little
emotional. A new love for country music and songs with a simple, yet powerful message about parental
relationships will do that to you. In essence, all of the songs mentioned deal with the passage of time - how
time slows down or speeds up and how that affects our relationships with our loved ones.
What is the soundtrack of your life? What’s on your life’s playlist? How often do you take the time to
listen in? How accessible do you allow it to be? Life throws us curveballs and we undoubtedly fall out of
routine. My message to you this 5778 is to make sure that playlist isn’t lost in the back of an archive folder.
Those reminders that touch us, the words and music that we connect with, deserve a place in our daily mix -
for their sake as much as for our own.
The synagogue is a home for such connection. We’re continuing to develop our Friday night instrumental
services with the help of our own talented musicians that breathe life and heart into our prayer. Our holidays
are enriched by Harmonía and our High Holiday Chorale. Our kids choir brings joy to every performance.
Our musical series continues this year with a growing partnership between the Jacksonville Jewish Center
and other local centers of arts and culture such as Douglas Anderson School of the Arts, the Civic Orchestra,
and the Jacksonville Symphony. We’ll be host to a
springtime “Divas on the Bima” concert featuring a
number of my cantorial friends from across the country.
I hope the music and programs of our synagogue will
be a part of your playlist. Be it a Shabbat service or a
concert series, there is the pretext to create powerful
memories and moments that may even leave you teary-
eyed. May we continue to appreciate each and every day.
May we find moments for nostalgia and the creation of
new nostalgia for years to come.
The entire Holzer household wishes you all a Shana
Tova - a happy, healthy, nostalgic and new New Year!
Hazzan Jesse, Leora, Rena and Dafna Holzer

SETZER
YOUTH EDUCATION
Gabrielle Magid
Youth Director
Setzer Youth Education is the department of the Galinsky
Academy that is home to our youth group programming.
We engage with students from 4th - 12th grade, giving
them experiences designed to build friendships and create
memories with their peers. The beauty of our experiential
program is that it provides a safe, fun environment that
allows our youth to develop and strengthen their Jewish
identities as well as their commitment to the Jewish
community here in Jacksonville and around the world-
all while having fun!
In Chalutzim (4th and 5th grade) the primary focus is to build bridges and
foster friendships that extend beyond the walls of the Day School and Religious
School. This builds so that in Kadima (6th-8th grade) students take more
initiative and are taught about the leadership it requires to run their chapter. By
the time they reach USY (9th-12th grade), the students take over, putting their leadership skills into practice.
The department had some big wins this year. We engaged with 111 different kids, doubling membership
in Kadima and seeing improvement in attendance at all youth group activities. We employed six advisors,
providing young professionals as role models. And, we even hosted a Kadima convention and sent 63 kids to
Disney Day!
Being Jewish should be fun, and it’s our goal to prove that to our youth —after all, they are the next
generation of Jewish leaders! We need them engaged in and fulfilled by Jewish life now, so that it’s something
they effortlessly carry with them throughout all stages of life. Whether you find joy in Judaism by ice skating,
searching for Rabbi’s missing lulav on a Sukkot Scavenger Hunt, going to conventions, dancing at an Ugly
Hanukkah Sweater Party at the Coves, etc...there’s never a dull moment in the Youth Department! We hope
you’ll join us and get in on the fun this year. ShanaTovah u’Metukah from your Youth Director and Advisors!
CENTER YOUTH COMMISSION
The Youth Commission oversees all youth programs
sponsored by the Jacksonville Jewish Center including
Chalutzim, Kadima, USY, and Scouts. The committee
includes the USY president(s), congregants, the youth
director, and second rabbi. The Youth commission’s
vision is to inspire youth to embrace being Jewish and
joyfully engage with the community. The Youth
commission’s mission is to provide opportunities for
youth to deepen their experience of Judaism, grow lasting
friendships, and learn menschlichkeit (kindness, integrity,
honor, compassion) through community service, prayer,
sports, arts, leadership training, and nature.
In addition to membership in Center Youth Groups,
the Youth Commission encourages our youngsters to
attend United Synagogue summer programs, such as
Camp Ramah, USY - on - Wheels, Ramah Seminar in
Israel and USY Pilgrimage. These provide excellent
opportunities for our children to share wholesome fun
and uniquely Jewish experiences with their peers from all
over the United States and Canada. The Youth
Commission assists applicants with a variety of
scholarships.

SOCIAL ACTION
Social Action is alive and well at the Jacksonville Jewish Center! The consistent participation of our community
leads to our success in helping others that are less fortunate.
Thanks to our many generous members, more than 300 new bed pillows were donated to our local Ronald
McDonald House (RMH) during the past year. These pillows went into new suites at the RMH. Having a new, good
pillow to sleep on is vital for a restful night’s sleep and the families who are here in Jacksonville with a sick or injured
child need rest and relaxation.
RMH also depends on aluminum can tabs to help pay their bills. We have a container at the JJC where we
collect them and encourage our members to do the same at home and bring them to us for donation. They do make
a difference!
We participated again in Dignity U Wear’s Drop Your Drawers drive and although we didn’t donate as much this
year as last, our contribution was significant. We also donated warm hats, gloves and scarves, many handmade this
year. We helped to keep many children and adults warm in the colder months!
For the fourth year, our joint Mitzvah Day with the Temple provided holiday meals for many here in Jacksonville.
On December 25th, we cooked, packaged and delivered more than 300 meals for Meals On Wheels recipients. We
cooked and served dinner at Sulzbacher Center to hundreds and did the same at The Veteran’s Center and RMH.
This year, we made a Hanukkah dinner at Gabriel House and gave those in residence a tasty selection of latkes and
traditional sides and donuts. Residents were happy to share in our holiday celebration and loved the variety of foods
that were prepared by our volunteers.
Once again, our volunteer bakers provided thousands of cookies that were packaged and delivered to our local
First Responders on Christmas Day. This is always a treat for the children in our community and often includes a
tour of the fire stations and trucks.
We continue to keep our carts full of food donations for our JFCS Food Pantry and help to feed many in our
community on an ongoing basis. What a blessing to be able to provide nourishment for those in need!
Our recycling of magazines continues as we bring them to hospitals and local senior centers. Other items are
recycled by donating them to our Teacher Supply Depot. Local teachers rely on these items for a variety of projects
throughout the school year.
We are grateful to our groups of loyal volunteers who go to the Sulzbacher Center on the third Sunday of every
other month to prepare and serve dinner. Many in our community depend on these meals for sustenance.
We are always looking for additional committee members and supporters for our projects. If you are interested
in helping, please contact Rhoda at rhodagoldstein@hotmail.com.
We are looking forward to another wonderful and generous year of giving and helping!

Adult Education experienced another busy and
insightful year in 2016-2017. Currently, over 125
adults are regular attendees at weekly or monthly
sessions at the Jacksonville Jewish Center, and our
lectures attract a wide variety of community members.

informative classes and special events at all levels
of learning for our congregants and the Jacksonville
Jewish community in general. We thank the Adult
Education committee members who, along with our teachers and clergy, provide many hours of hard work
and dedication to deliver these educational opportunities.

and Torah. Our Adult Library is being used daily, providing a resource for all who wish to do research,

we repeated the very successful “Planning Your Jewish Funeral” workshop, sponsored by Hardage-Giddens.

CENTER CEMETERIES
In 1905, lands were purchased for the purpose of creating proper Jewish
burial grounds for our congregation. The Center now manages three cemetery
locations in and adjacent to Evergreen Cemetery in North Jacksonville.
To reach the New Cemetery, also known as Jacksonville Jewish Center
Memorial Park, travel north on Main Street to 43rd Street and make a right turn.
The entrance is at the next right, which is Liberty Street. The “Old Cemetery” and
the Beth Shalom cemetery section are both located within Evergreen Cemetery.
To reach the Old Cemetery, travel north on Main Street to Winona Street. Make
a right on Winona Street and follow the road until you reach Gate 5. The Old
Cemetery is on the left. To reach the Beth Shalom section, enter through Gate 5
and take the red brick road to the left (Durkee Drive). Follow and turn left onto
Cummer Drive and continue until the road ends (0.4 miles). The Beth Shalom
Cemetery will be on your right.
Our committee is dedicated to the preservation and expansion of our
cemeteries. During the past year, we successfully removed 20 dead or dying trees
and trimmed back 10 others, reducing the risk of trees falling in future storms

HEVRA KADDISHA
During the course of a lifetime, no one can avoid an encounter with death; yet,
it is an experience for which no one is prepared. Judaism embraces all of life, of
which death is a natural part, and so ascribes rituals which dignify our departure.
The care of the deceased immediately following death is the solemn
responsibility of the Hevra Kaddisha, the holy society, consisting of dedicated
laypersons from the community. Members are knowledgeable in the traditional
duties, and perform them with the utmost respect for the deceased. The process
begins with the Taharah, purification, a ritual cleansing and preparation of the
body for burial. In addition, a Shomer (guardian) will stay at the side of the
deceased reciting required prayers until the burial.
It is gratifying to have a completely independent Jewish organization, under
the sole leadership of lay people, maintain such a high level of commitment and
dedication to one of the most important traditions in Jewish life.
Members of the Jacksonville Jewish community can take comfort in the
knowledge that the services of the Hevra Kaddisha are offered to all Jews
regardless of financial means or synagogue affiliation. Please feel free to call any
of our officers or rabbis with concerns or questions. “Dedication” is our motto.

THE DIFFERENCE WE WILL MAKE TOGETHER
There is a Hasidic story about a young boy who travels to a nearby town and hears the
sound of a bugle. One of the townspeople explains that the bugle sounds whenever
there is a fire and it is quickly put out. The boy is amazed! He decides to bring this
miraculous custom back to his small village. On his way home, he buys a bugle and
when he arrives in his village, he calls all of his neighbors outside. He then sets fire
to one of the nearby houses and blows the bugle with all his might. The boy assures
his fellow villagers not to worry because the fire will soon be extinguished.
As the fire spreads to the next rooftop and then the next, the villagers scold the
boy for his foolishness. They immediately begin working together to bring water
from the well and put out the fire. The villagers explain that the bugle is merely an
alarm intended to rally the people to put out the fire. Just as blowing the bugle is not
enough to extinguish the fire on its own, we blow the shofar to remind us to take
stock of our actions and repent. The sound of the shofar has the power to awaken us from our complacency, and
encourage us to commit to a life of mitzvot guided by our values.
For me, the most striking thing about this story is how natural it is for us to act like the young boy instead of
the wise villagers. It is very tempting to seek out easy shortcuts like blowing a bugle to extinguish a raging fire.
We could go about our lives hoping that our actions will suffice and everything will be alright, but Jewish tradition
¡teaches that we need to put in the effort to achieve the desired results.
The High Holidays are a time of personal contemplation and self-reflection, when each of us has the
opportunity to turn our scrutiny inwards and pledge to improve our behavior in the year ahead. This season is also
a time of togetherness, of individuals and families joining in community. In the story, it is really the bugle, not the
boy, that brings the villagers together to save their homes. Only through collaboration are they able to demonstrate
i
the true power of the bugle as their communal wake-up call.
In the coming weeks, I invite you to pay attention to your internal bugle. Spend time considering the changes
you hope to make for yourself, and finding ways to achieve your goals, this year and in the future. If we all strive
to make the necessary alterations in ourselves, we have a chance to better the entire Jacksonville Jewish Center
community as well. I cannot wait to see the difference we will make together!

IMPORTANT CONNECTIONS
I’ve lost my connection!!!
How many times have you heard that comment regarding your Wi-Fi,
your cell phone or any device connected to today’s electronic world?
But, have you really lost your connection... to you as a person? Because
of your busy day-to-day life, have you lost the connection to what is really
important?
Have you lost your personal connection with your parents, your children,
your grandparents or even the one special person in your life?
Have you lost your connection to what is important to you?
Have you lost your connection to God and to your faith, to your synagogue,
to Israel, to our Jewish community here in Jacksonville and Jews around the
world?
With our Jewish New Year 5778 upon us, I would like to invite you to take
this special time of year to reconnect with what is really important.
Please reconnect with your Wi-Fi, I know that comes first, but after you
complete your connection...
Reconnect with your parents. Even if they are no longer with us you can still reconnect by visiting
their graves during the Memorial Service between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur. Remembering them
by attending services for Yizkor and their Yahrzeit. If you are lucky enough to have them with you, tell
them you love them and get your family’s living history before it is too late.
Reconnect with your children and grandchildren. Take the time to really know who they are as people.
We lose sight of many wonderful moments in today’s busy world. On Friday night, get together and
have Shabbat dinner, with candles, kiddush and hamotzei. Sit and talk. Try it and you will find that this
connection will create wonderful moments of discussion and well-spent time together.

MINYANNAIRES SHABBAT
Minyan has been a major component of synagogue life for
our congregation. For over 115 years, the Jacksonville
Jewish Center has held a regularly scheduled minyan
in the morning and evening. Those who attend on a
regular basis are known as Minyannaires and are
valued by those that attend in need of a minyan to say
kaddish. These Minyannaires are also members of a
special community. On Shabbat morning, February 18,
the congregation celebrated all of the members of this
community on our third annual Minyannaire Shabbat
and proudly honored Evan Yegelwel as our Scott P.
Zimmerman Minyannaire of the Year.
Minyannaires Shabbat is an opportunity to shine
a light on our congregational minyan for which there
are many reasons to be proud. The Center has a minyan
for Shacharit (in the morning) and Mincha/Maariv (in
the evening) services every day which allows mourners
and people observing a yahrzeit to say kaddish for their
loved ones. Each and every day our members come
together for prayer, camaraderie and to perform the
mitzvah of participating in the daily minyan.
Our honoree, Evan Yegelwel, sat on the Center’s
Executive Committee and the Board of Directors
for over two decades, having served as congregation
President and Chairman of the Board in the late 1990’s. He also served as chairman of the building fund at
a key moment in the Center’s history. However, his involvement with our congregation began with minyan,
serving on the Minaynnaires Club committee in the early 1980’s and scheduling speakers for the Sunday
morning post minyan breakfast.
In the late 1980’s, Evan served as a Shabbat morning usher, and in 1996, he began an almost twenty year
run as Sunday evening minyan captain.
Evan is a leader in the Jacksonville legal community in addition to the Jewish community. He is a
partner in the law firm of Terrell Hogan Yegelwel, PA. and has been a member of the firm for over 32 years.
He is a Florida Board Certified Civil Trial Lawyer, has focused his personal practice in the field of personal
injury, products liability and mass torts.
He served as the first President (1992-1995) of the Foundation for River Garden Hebrew Home for the
Aged, served on the Board of Directors of the Jacksonville Jewish Federation for 16 years, on the National
Executive Committee for AIPAC and was AIPAC Northeast Florida
Chair for over 25 years.
As part of this event, we bestow the Minyannaire of the Year award
to one member for his or her activities in minyan throughout the years.
The award has been named in memory of our beloved Ritual Director,
Scott P. Zimmerman, who passed away in 2014 and honors someone
who stands out as a minyan participant who: attends frequently; shows
initiative; developed davening skills; experienced religious growth; taken
on a leadership role and has shown the ability to bring others along on
their own spiritual journey.

A passion of mine has always been to recruit and retain members that
regularly participate in our minyan. Sadly, over the last few months, we have
experienced a bit of difficulty obtaining a minyan. In a congregation of our
size, I am confident that each member can make a small commitment to ensure
that a minyan is established at all prayer times.
It is stated that God never rejects the prayers of any congregant when they are part of a minyan.
Even if a person’s kavanah (concentration, intention) is imperfect, if one prays with a congregation, their
prayers will be heard. Nowadays, as we all typically do not have perfect concentration when we pray, it
is all the more important that we pray with a minyan. In addition, when praying with a minyan, one is
able to say and/or hear many prayers that are only recited with a minyan such as Kaddish, the repetition
I of the Amidah, the Torah reading, etc. If you are not yet a minyan attendee, please consider making a
commitment to one day a week, one service a day or more. Your dedication will be greatly appreciated by
all participants!

Our Men’s Club is an active member of the Federation
of Jewish Men’s Clubs (FJMC), an international
organization dedicated to the ideals, philosophy and
traditions of Conservative Judaism. Our level of activities
and Center participation have earned your Men’s Club the
First Place and Honorable Mention Torch Awards from
FJMC in years past, and our Yad Shel Chai program is
now being implemented in Men’s Clubs across the nation.
We participate in a wide range of FJMC programs and
activities such as distributing Yellow Holocaust Memorial
candles so that every congregant can honor the memory
of the Holocaust on Yom HaShoah and World Wide Wrap
which brings together congregants, clergy and Center
youth on Super Bowl Sunday to participate in the mitzvah
of wrapping tefillin.
The Center Men’s Club works closely with Youth and
Education leaders to support your children and the Center
youth organizations. We are available to lend a helping
hand with local activities and regional USY and Kadima
conventions. We sponsor the Yad Shel Chai (Hand of
Life) and Double Yad Shel Chai awards that recognize
our youth for reading Torah in our synagogue services
and building on the success of the Yad Shel Chai program.
The Men’s Club Martin J. Gottlieb Brit Hinukh award is
given each spring to one Teacher in the Center schools
who best represents the life commitment necessary to
bring a quality learning experience to Jewish children.
We provide a metal finished Kiddush cup for Bar and
Bat Mitzvah students and gifts for our 12th graders that
complete the Siyyum program. We sponsor and raise
funds for the Horty T. Gendzier Summer Scholarship
Fund, which provides the opportunity for children to attend
Camp Ramah and other Jewish enrichment programs who
otherwise would not have been able to do so.
The Men’s Club is also a great way to make friends,
have fun, and meet people from our synagogue as well as
our regional Jewish community. Our members regularly
get together on Sunday mornings to play pick-up softball,
touch football games or perform community service
activities. One of our highl ights is the opportunity to prepare
meals at the I.M. Sulzbacher Center for the Homeless. This
past year, we co-sponsored a campout with the Galinsky
PTA. In the fall, our annual Membership Kickoff Party
features fun, food, raffles and the opportunity to schmooze
with friends while we watch the Jacksonville Jaguars
play an away game on a big screen TV. We are visible
friends at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. Throughout the
year, Men’s Club members help with Sukkot and Purim
programs. We lead and participate in Men’s Club Shabbat
and the Armed Forces Shabbat. We are your children’s
teachers, your community volunteers and your spiritual
lay leaders.
We invite you to join the Men’s Club if you are at
least 18 years old, male, and a member of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center community. I firmly believe that the Men’s
Club offers something for every man. So come on out and
join us for an activity this year.
I think you’ll enjoy
supporting our Jacksonville
Jewish community and
spending quality time with
other guys just like you!

Bernard & Alice
SELEVAN
RELIGIOUS SCHOOL
The Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School
continues to bring the joy of Jewish culture and Hebrew
prayer to our families with an exciting, experiential
curriculum. Our program includes Kindergarten
and 1st grade Sunday school classes, where loving,
dedicated teachers introduce our youngest students to
the Torah, Hebrew, and holidays. As they grow, we add
one weekday as we continue to expand the extremely
successful “Judaism Through the Arts” program for
grades 2 through 5, using a rotation of crafts, literature,
technology and music to nourish a love of Jewish culture. In our middle school,
we challenge our 6th and 7th graders, regardless of their level of formal Jewish
education and observance, as they discover the relevance of Jewish learning and
culture in their own young adult lives. Inclusive family programming, allowing
for parental involvement and instruction, round out this exciting new concept in
Jewish education.
We pride ourselves on our healthy blend of traditional and modern methods.
The school looks forward to the involvement of the new assistant rabbi, Rabbi Shira Rosenblum, as well as
all of our professional clergy and staff, as we blend our Religious School family with the Jacksonville Jewish
Center community.
Wishing you a sweet and healthy new year: L’shana Tovah Umetuka!

A busy year in the
‘Hood7 I am honored
to be the president of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center
Sisterhood. I can honestly
say that our programming
and our fundraising this year
has been phenomenal and
I thank everyone who has

and generous donations. I
always strive for women to
feel welcome and connected
at our events. My dynamic
board has worked hard
to change with the times as our Sisterhood has evolved to adapt
to women’s busy schedules. Nothing gives me, along with my
Sisterhood Sistas, more pleasure than making a difference in our
synagogue community. We are the backbone of the Center and I
am proud to announce that Sisterhood generously donated $23,500
so all children within our synagogue will be able to benefit from
the incredible educational and youth programming offered at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center and Galinsky Academy! Included in that
donation was a specific $10,000 donation to L’Dor V’Dor to help
fund day school, preschool, and professional development in the area
of special education. I am so proud that we were able to purchase a
defibrillator for the day school, which is critical to the well-being of
our youth. Thank you to all who have donated their time or funds
to make our generous 2017 donation possible. My main goal as
president is to help women make connections within our synagogue
community. By providing relevant and timely programming, I hope
to create an opportunity to learn, be stimulated, and to socialize. I am
proud that the Jacksonville Jewish Center Sisterhood is the recipient
of the 2017 Jewels in the Crown Award, which is the prestigious
Women’s League Award for excellence in programming. Some of
our highlights were a Conversation with Anita Zucker, Sisterhood
Shabbat highlighting Woman’s League International President
Carol Simon, Painting with a Twist and Comedy Zone evenings
in collaboration with the Galinsky Academy PTA, Shalom Yoga,
and Hot Topics with relevant topics. Whether you purchased gifts
from the Judaica Shop, sent our beautiful Rosh Hashanah flowers
and Leagrams, or simply just supported Sisterhood by paying
dues, I thank you on behalf of Sisterhood for making our year not
only productive, but also creating a warm community of women
within our synagogue community. Please go to our website www.
jycsisterhood.com or follow us on Facebook (JJC Sisterhood) for
more information.
