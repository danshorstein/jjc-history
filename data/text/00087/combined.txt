The Center Men’s Club is one arm of the Center, a part
of the whole, a part strong* in its endeavors, continually ex¬
panding and striving in every way to aid and assist the
Center in accomplishing its social, cultural and religious
goals.
We feel that to base our future actions on past activity
is using a standard that is continually too low. We are forever
eyeing and striving for greater projects, and to enhance
those values which the Center is constituted to provide.
Close affiliation with the Men’s Club aids in directing the
energies, skill and talents of its membership toward a richer
self, at the same time strengthening and developing Jewish
life in our community.
Of the three phases mentioned above, we have developed
an annual project in each category. Socially, we begin the
fall season with a dance just prior to the High Holidays.
Here the opportunity is two-fold. We give our friends a
chance to renew friendships after the summer interval and at
the same time a healthy financial contribution is made to
the Center. Plans for the coming social year include a paid-up
Membership Affair, a Barn Dance and two other events. It
might be appropriate at this time to mention the highly
successful Fashion Show presented last year.
In the cultural field, our high spot was the Center
Choral Society Concert sponsored by the Center Men’s Club,
Although only a nominal charge was made, local citizens were
given the opportunity to hear the well known Cantor David
J. Putterman of New York. The enthusiasm of those at¬
tending attested to the popularity of the program, which
consisted of Jewish and Hebrew selections. The Choral
Society is directed by Cantor Abraham Marton. The entire
proceeds of the event were presented to the Cantorial
School sponsored by the Jewish Theological Seminary of
America. The success of this annual concert promises to
be greater than ever this coming year.
The sponsorship of the Minyan Club is the religious ac¬
tivity from which we derive the greatest satisfaction. This
group is composed of boys about to be or who have already
been Bar Mitzvah. The Men’s Club arranges transportation
for the boys every Sunday morning and after the service
acts as hosts at a breakfast. The Men’s Club also assists, in
cooperation with the Center Sisterhood, in the celebration of
various Holidays and Festivals and Sunday School projects.
Athletics have always been one of the Men’s Club major
fields of activities. During the past year the Club spon¬
sored teams in basketball, volleyball and tennis, also aiding
materially in the support of the Center basketball Team.
Expanded plans have been made for the coming year.
The men who have given of their time and effort in
carrying out the program of the Men’s Club are too numerous
to mention. However, they are rewarded by the knowledge of
a job well done. They have helped themselves by helping
others. In a service organization such as ours, there is no
better goal. There is a place in the Center Men’s Club
for every Center member who desires to avail himself of
the joys and opportunities inherent therein.

In furtherance of the expanded scope and program of
Center activities, the past year saw the formation of the
Couples Club, the purpose of which was to increase social
fellowship and companionship among its members and to
present an opportunity for and encourage its members to
indulge in entertaining self-education.
Opening its membership to all young Jewish couples, the
Club quickly attained its membership limit of 75 couples, and
because of the great interest shown by the community, the
Club has now expanded its membership to 100 couples.

To promote the purposes desired, the Club sponsors one
social affair each month and one cultural affair. Among
the social events have been dances, swimming parties and
theater parties. Included in the cultural affairs were a
child psychology program, a panel discussion on the Passover
holiday, and a marriage clinic.

As an auxiliary of the Synagogue, Sisterhood works
toward the preservation of Synagogue values. Those
women who sincerely align themselves with Sister¬
hood activities are playing a major role in the streng¬
thening and perpetuation of Jewish life in this coun¬
try.
Sisterhood strives by the sponsoring of Jewish edu¬
cation, to instill within our youth a deep understand¬
ing of their religion and a sense of pride in the glori-
ousjheritage of their people.
Sisterhood is a complex organization, bringing its
strength and vitality to bear on the many facets which
go toward pointing a well knit complete union, divert¬
ing its energies toward the successful culmination of
a varied program.
To mention but a few of our varied projects:
1. The Book and Gift Shop, Mrs. Isadore Gartner,
Chairman, does a most outstanding job in acquainting
our membership with traditional Jewish art and cere¬
monial objects, making them more aware of the beau¬
ty and culture to be found in the Jewish way of life.
2. Participation in the religious- and educational
phases of the Center’s activities and subsidizing Sun¬
day School teacher’s salaries.
3. Sponsorship of the Center Year Book under the
chairmanship of Mrs. Joseph Krestul, Mrs. Hyman
Selber and their many co-workers, who solicited the
advertisements, funds from which became the most
productive source of income for the Beautification
Fund.
4. The Beautification Committee, headed by Mrs.
Shelby Trager and her many sub-committees, helped
to raise funds for the furnishing of the Senior and
Junior Lounges, as well as the Junior Game Room on
the second floor of our Youth Activities building.
5. With the completion and dedication of our sec¬
ond floor, Sisterhood has added still another project,
to be known as “Funds for Youth,” under the able
chairmanship of Mrs. Sam Siegel. Monies raised
through this media will be used to sponsor a well
planned and professionally directed Youth Program
so that our young people of all age levels may fully
enjoy the facilities we have available to them.
Close affiliation with Sisterhood work enriches the
lives of those women active in its consecrated activi¬
ties. There is a place for every Center woman in this
work and. an opportunity for rendering vital service
to others.

Tuesday night is Y. P. L. night! This is becoming the
slogan of more and more of the young people in the com¬
munity. The completion of the wonderful facilities of the
Center’s new Activities Building has enabled Y. P. L. to
institute a fuller and more active program.
During the summer months, Y. P. L. has not suspended
activities, rather, it has held a series of weekly open
house affairs. Special events, such as ping pong tournaments,
card parties, and cultural discussions on timely subjects
have highlighted these evenings. Many service men stationed
in the area have taken part in the organization’s program.
Some features of the previous year have been the group’s
participation in the annual Y. P. L. Sabbath service, discus¬
sion groups held with the Rabbi following several Friday
night services and a number of successful social affairs.
In the coming year Y. P. L. hopes to be able to make
itself an active and worthwhile group, both in aiding the
future of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and as an important
factor in the lives of the youth of the community.

TED FREEDMAN
Socrates, the Grecian Teacher said “Be interested in
the development of your sons for they will inherit the
fruits of your efforts.” Scouting-, through the program of
moral, mental and physical training provides a broadening
character experience for boys.
Scouting this year at the Center experienced a re¬
organization of leader personnel, and suffered a shrinkage
in membership. However, those who participated actively
did excellent work. The Troop received an “A” rating at
the Camporee at Crandall on the St. Marys River, earning
them the privilege of participating in the Council Camporee
at Silver Springs where the Troop earned a “B” rating. The
troop also enjoyed several outdoor overnight camping trips.
During summer camp at Camp Echockotee all the boys
received feathers for achievement in field events; several
received awards of the Camp Patch to be worn on the uni¬
form. Merit Badges in Life Saving and First Aid were
earned by Barney Chepenik and Arnold Lasris.
During the year advancement in rank was made by
Larry Cohen, Arthur Goldstein, Stephen Zann and Arnold
Lasris. Merit Badges were received by Martin Rothstein and
Barney Chepenik. George Glossman, Jerry Rothstein and
Sammy Sutton were inducted into the Troop in the Tender¬
foot Rank.
Through the generosity of the Board of Directors of
the Center, the facilities of the Center are used for Mohawk
District Court of Honor of the North Florida Council, on the
third Thursday of every month. At the Court of Honor,
awards for achievement and badges for advancement are
presented to the Scouts who have earned them during the
previous month. The Troops participating in the Court are
sponsored by a great many Churches and Civic Organi¬
zations who have expressed their appreciation for this
courtesy extended to a large segment of Jacksonville.
The fathers of the Scouts who formed the Committee and
took an active part are deserving of special appreciation.
They are Manny Goldstein, Committee Chairman, Bernard
Glossman, Irvin Rothstein, Ben Lasris, Hyman Selber,
Committeemen and Ted Freedman, Scoutmaster. Bernard
Glossman acted as Chairman of the Board of Review and
formed an Explorer Unit in the Troop. Irvin Rothstein
served on the Board of Review, supervised the boys at
Scout Camp, assisting the Scoutmaster greatly, and has been
appointed to serve as Institutional Representative. Ben
Lasris and Hyman Selber filled many jobs, including trans¬
portation, mailing notices and also serving on the Board
of Review. Mr. Selber came out to Scout Camp and with
fathers of the Committtee conducted services on Friday
night.
Ted Freedman, Scoutmaster, did an outstanding job in re¬
organizing the Troop and in building a strong nucleus for
the bigger and better Troop that he anticipates in the
coming year. His knowledge and interest in the Boy Scout
movement inspired the members of the Troop to strive
diligently for advancement. He was honored by the Com¬
mittee who proposed him for membership in the Order of the
Arrow, the Honor Society of Scouting. He took the Mem¬
bership Ordeal and induction at Scout Camp.
The Troop has openings for interested boys who wish to
avail themselves of the many exciting experiences in Scout¬
ing.

The Lodge was chartered in 1950 and has a present mem¬
bership of 80, entirely Jewish. David E. Freedman was the
first Noble Grand of the Lodge and in addition to his position
as Grand Instructor for the State of Florida, has presently
been honored by being commissioned District Deputy Grand
Master.
The IOOF holds to no creed, but all who become members
must believe in the existence of a Supreme Being, the Creator
and Preserver of the Universe. While the membership is
largely in the United States and Canada, there are Lodges
throughout the world. The history of the IOOF can be traced
back about two centuries. The first United States Lodge
dates back to 1819.
Teaching forgiveness and tolerance, the Order promotes
benevolance and charity and has established Homes for the
Aged and Indigent, the Widows and Orphans.
The beautiful auditorium of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center has been the scene of several social affairs, including
the presentation of the initiatory degree and drill given by
the famous Delaware Degree Team during the Jacksonville
convention, of the Grand Lodge of the State of Florida. For
this affair, Israel Lodge No. 11 acted as hosts to hundreds
of local and visiting Odd Fellows and Rebekahs
Among its other local activities this past year, the Lodge
held its annual dance at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
auditorium, gave a Hanukah party for the residents of the
River Garden Home for the Aged, and sent a high school
student to the United Nations Assembly Convention. Other
activities in local affairs and youth movements are planned
by the Lodge for the future.
The Lodge led the entire State Subordinate Lodges in
membership gained during the preceding year.
The Lodge meets at the Jacksonville Jewish Center every
Wednesday night.

For 17 years, since 1935, Jewry of this city has had its
Jewish Community Council to represent it in an ever-
widening field of interests. For all Jacksonville Jewry, the
Council carries on the centuries'-old tradition of communal
living, of democratic discussion and of united action for
common purposes.
For a good part of the Council's life time, much of
world Jewry has been in dire need of financial aid to help
it remain alive, to rebuild itself after the terrible scourge
of Hitlerism had subsided. Through their Council, local
Jews have responded magnificently to the calls of their less
fortunate brothers.
In the years of its being, the Council, through its annu¬
al “United Campaign," has raised approximately $1,900,000
which has been disbursed according to need among more than
40 overseas, national and local Council beneficiaries.
While fund-raising necessarily has been and continues to
be the major function of the Council, it by no means is its
only activity. All Jews of the community, as individuals
and as members of some 25 different organizations, have
problems and programs in which they are interested. The
Council, as their instrument and voice, acts for them in
many ways.
Day to day, through the year, the Council's Public
Relations Committee, whose members represent all facets
of the community, works tirelessly in many directions.
It sponsors programs to improve relations between Jews
and non-Jews. It adds its weight to all democratic forces of
the community. It speaks and acts quickly when the com¬
munity or any of its members are attacked by anti-Semites.
In the field of group relations—for that essentially is its
field, the Public Relations Committee is the conscientious and
skilled servant of the community.
Since 1943, the Council has published the Commentator,
which several times has received national recognition for
its excellence as a community-sponsored newspaper.
The recent past saw the Council, for the community,
accept some 60 refugees into our midst, and with the
assistance of the Jewish Welfare Society, provide all the
care and services required by them until they became self-
supporting “new Americans."
Since warfare in Korea burst into flame, the Council's
Armed Services Committee has been reestablished to provide
every type of service to the Jewish men of the armed forces
stationed in and around Jacksonville.
In many other ways the Council serves. Its downtown
offices are open to groups for meetings. Its office equip¬
ment is freely offered all organizations. It maintains a
community calendar for the benefit of all. Through its
Presidents' Council it channels the interest and strength of
all organizations behind various community and city-wide
projects.
The Community Council is, indeed, the voice, the arm,
the heart of our city's Jewry.

Young Judaean Clubs are under the direction of the
Zionist Youth Commission and sponsored by Hadassah and
the Zionist Organization of America.
Our Four-Fold Program: 1. Group activities 2. Jewish
Living 3. Zionism and Israel 4. American Affairs, provides
a well-rounded program of club activities dedicated to the
service ideal of the Zionist movement and a creative Jewish
life.
The senior clubs began the year with a Senior Judaean
Conclave at Camp Immokalee in October. Herman Popkin,
Southern Region Zionist Youth Director and the Regional
Executive Board of Senior Judaea attended the Conclave and
provided a stimulating experience for Jacksonville.
A new newspaper, The Jax Judaean, edited by Larry
Siegel was published and distributed to the members during
the year.
Ten senior members were awarded scholarships to Tel
Yehudah, National Young Judaea Camp, established for the
purpose of developing leadership within Young Judaea, they
were Gail Rubin Chermack, Eunice Price, Harriet Witten,
Arlene Moscovitz, Elvina Mack, Ruth Shmunes, Dorothy
Newman, Byrna Lee Datz, Terry Klausner and Stephen Puldy.
Highlighting the junior club activities was the first Pre-
Senior Conclave held in Daytona Beach under the direction
of the first Florida Region Zionist Youth Director, Sy
Friedman with Rose Lynn Levin assisting from Jackson¬
ville. Club members returned to complete a successful year
for the projects and purposes of our organization. A County
Fair with Mrs. Rajmiond Winterfield as chairman, held at the
River Garden Home concluded the year, at which time funds
were raised for the S. O. S. (Supplies On Schedule) to equip
the Hava Training Center in Israel.
Local clubs were under the direction of Mrs. Maury Gold¬
stein, Young Judaean Chairman, Mrs. Wm. Goldman, Youth
Commission Chairman, and the following club leaders and
co-leaders, Eleanore Cohen, Ann Margolin, Herbert Smullian,
Jean Hoffman, Doris Garten, Minnie Schieber, Myra Thier,
Teddy Moscovitz, Dorothy Krantz and Rose Lynn Levin.

In Jacksonville, Council's program of service includes
MEDICAL LOAN SHELF which is well known to the local
welfare agencies. Hospital beds, wheel chairs, crutches, bed-
pans etc., are available to those indigent and ill who cannot
afford to buy or rent. COUNCIL'S SEWING GROUP meets
weekly to make lovely layettes which are made available to
needy infants. Each baby born to a New American family is
presented a complete layette. These ladies also make scuff
slippers for the needy children in the Pediatrics Ward of the
Duval Medical Center. COUNCIL'S VETERANS COMMIT¬
TEE has brought much cheer to the service men in the
Naval Air Station hospital wards where Council parties are
given monthly. THE GOLDEN AGE CLUB sponsored by
Council, is well known to the residents at River Garden
Home Oldsters who are physically sound but in need of
recreation are served by this national project. Square danc¬
ing, games and refreshments are part of the pleasure of¬
fered, plus the opportunity to associate with those of similar
need and interest. SERVICE TO FOREIGN BORN is Coun¬
cil's national project and is well known on the local level.
Many new American families have been met at the station
by Council women, taken to their first American home,
assisted with shopping and other personal matters. Classes
in English were first started by volunteer Council members
who were later instrumental in getting a paid instructor
from the Board of Public Education. SHIP-A-BOX program.
Children in the resettlement centers of Israel and in the
underprivileged communities of Morocco receive packages
of food, clothing, pencil, paper, kindergarten materials and
toys monthly. Council's overseas services include the School
of Education of the Hebrew University in Jerusalem. The
SCHOLARSHIP program offers training to women overseas
who are trained in welfare work in American universities.
After their courses here are completed, they may return to
their native country and help to better conditions abroad.
Maintenance of a home in Paris for unattached Jewish girls
has served as a temporary haven since 1947.
In its Service and Education Program, as in the Overseas
program, Council is concerned with all the problems of the
community, the nation and the world and offers its services
wherever the need is greatest.

The A. Z. A. Chapter, No. 272 of Jacksonville has been a
very active order this past year.
The Chapter was host to the Annual Florida Regional
Convention held here in December. Most of the activities
of the Convention were held in the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. Without the use of the Center and its wonderful fa¬
cilities, it would not have been the most successful convention
in the history of the Florida Region of the A. Z. A. Sollie
Safer was elected Regional Aleph Gizbor and Carol Kantor,
our Chapter Sweetheart was elected Sweetheart of the Florida
Region. The next Regional Convention will be held in Miami.
Another outstanding event sponsored by Chapter 272
was the Annual Alumni Banquet held on March 1st. Mr.
Joe Eisenberg of Brunswick, Ga., a member of the Executive
Committee of the Fifth District of B’nai B’rith was the
principle speaker.
As part of its religious program, the Chapter held its
Annual Sabbath Service at the Center on May 2nd. Many of
the boys had parts in the service and others assisted with the
ushering.
The closing affair of the year was the Annual A. Z. A.
Minstrel, which was successfully produced by the boys of the
Chapter and the members of the newly formed B. B. G.
Chapter.
During the month of June a District 5 Convention was
held at Camp Blue Star. Sollie Safer and Joel Selber were
delegates from Jacksonville. Sollie Safer was elected Aleph
Sopher.
The athletic, community service, cultural, religious and
social phases of A. Z. A. (The Five Fold Program) have been
well filled this year by our Chapter. This program is the
backbone of A. Z. A. Through the fulfillment of the Five
Fold Program A. Z. A. achieves its purpose in the community^
The Jacksonville Chapter is looking forward to an even more
successful program in the coming year.
In closing the Chapter wishes to express its sincere thanks
to its able and hard working advisors, Mr. Morris Chonin, Mr.
Ally Goldstein and Mr. Hyman Selber, who have spared no
efforts in assisting with every phase of its program, contrib¬
uting in great measure to its success. We hope they will
continue to guide us. We also want to thank Charles Levy,
who served as president during the past year, and who re¬
signed his post upon leaving the city. The balance of the
term is being filled by Neil Chonin.

Rabbi's
Message
Address delivered on presentation of National United Synagogue Solomon
Schechter Award, field of youth work on May 19, 1952.
WE stand before the Almighty in profound
gratitute and deepest humility. To our fel¬
low citizens of Jacksonville, fellow laborers in the
vineyard of the Lord on behalf of youth, go our
heartfelt appreciations for their felicitations and
words of commendation. I want to reciprocate their
kind sentiments by sharing with them and with the
leaders among our young people — the real cele¬
brants of this occasion — a thought on the place of
youth service at the present junction in human
affairs.
I am especially pleased that the leaders among
our young people are here to witness a significant
demonstration. Everyone who has spoken is engaged
in some phase of a common adventure, whether the
endeavor be Christian, Jewish, inter-denominational
or non-denomination. Whether it be called character
building or spiritual guidance, the ultimate aim is
to render more meaningful the span of years that
form the gatewa yto maturity. Our objective is to
help youth attain life abundant on a spiritual plane.
The fact that all young people share desires and
aspirations leads me to a further observation. Pro¬
gressive leaders have come to realize that lack in
spiritual commitment noticed at times among young-
people is not entirely of their own making. Some¬
where along the line the adult world had failed.
Secularized society with its inevitable cynicism is
really responsible for weakening moral fiber.
On the other hand, we hear today frantic appeals
for a return to religion in order to save the world
from moral chaos. Finally, I am suspicious of most
of those exhortations. The term religion as employed
in many of these cries of desperation is just another
magic cure. Repeating sacred words and perform¬
ance of sanctified ritual in themselves will not do.
Only a reawakening to the truths inherent in religion
will avail us of its magic grace.
The urgent need for spiritual values today, to my
mind, is comparable to the need for a moral and just
form of government in the days when America
came into being. For inspiration our founding-
fathers went to the Hebrew scriptures. This past
Sabbath incidentally, the lesson read in synagogues
the world over was the final chapter in Leviticus
RABBI SANDERS A. TOFIELD
which included the words inscribed on the Liberty
Bell, “Proclaim Liberty throughout the land, unto
all the inhabitants thereof.” Divinely inspired was
the system of human rights with freedom of worship
among them for which this verse became the sturdy
foundation. Since 1776 secular philosophies took
over. Almost forgotten is the specific and the all
embracing ideals which those words taught.
All of you recall that this injunction proclaimed
the Jubilee Year observance. Freedom was the aim.
There was to be no landless farmer, no subjected
humanity—no man was to lord over his neighbor.
With the blessing of liberty went an even greater
postulate, “For they are My servants.” Humanity
is meant to be free, but also meant to be responsible
to divine authority.
It is my implicit belief that young people under¬
stand this message. They resent being squelched
They cherish opportunities for displaying initiative.
Their example of zeal in the direction of the inherent
dignity of man gives me faith in their ability to take
society out of its moral doldrums. Provided, of
course, youth is properly guided.
Work with youth stimulates the application of
fresh insight. All of us cherish the blessings of a free
society. Freedom is meaningless however, unless re¬
lated to the sacred premise. “For they are My ser¬
vants.” External tyrannies we shall not tolerate. We
shall live by one compulsion, one compulsion only—
the command of God. Youth service under the banner
of faith is based on faith in the integrity of young
people. They appreciate moral imperatives when
taught along with the virture of responsibility. They
appreciate freedom that stems from moral control. I
am confident that they shall bring us all to the con¬
viction, “We are servants of God and deserve to be
free.”

U. S. Y. SOUTHEAST REGIONAL CONFERENCE
Youth of Conservative Synagogues Elect Officers, Adopt Plans
When the U.S.Y. first Southeast Regional
Conference came to a close at a luncheon held
on Sunday, March 30th, in the Center audi¬
torium, delegates from 12 congregations re¬
peated regrets that the time for dismissal had
arrived. The newly elected Executive Commit¬
tee met in the Library to map procedures man¬
dated in resolutions adopted at the sessions.
Officers Elected
Following lively discussion of the Nominat¬
ing Committee report, submitted by Larry Sie¬
gel of Jacksonville, the following were elected
to head the organization for one year: Presi¬
dent, Charles Levy, Jacksonville; 1st Vice
President, David Shear, Tampa; 2nd Vice Presi¬
dent, Arnold Tenenbaum, Savannah; Treasur¬
er, Paul Weiss, Miami Beach; Corresponding
Secretary, Bryna Lee Datz, Jacksonville; Re¬
cording Secretary, Carlene Brown, St. Peters¬
burg ; Executive Board, Moshe Shapiro, Miami,
Lynn Cooper, Daytona Beach, Roberta Gins-
burg, Lakeland, Marilyn Dingfelder, Orlando,
Sonny Goldstein, Columbus. Five additional
members at large were to be selected by the
Executive Committee. Mrs. Leo Moskovitz was
consultant to the Nominating Committee.
Regional Administration
Carefully considered plans for the organiza¬
tion of individual U.S.Y. chapters in communi¬
ties were placed for consideration of the Body
by the Chairman of the Organization Commit¬
tee, Paul Weiss. Significant among these was
cooperation in programming. Mr. Philip Selber
was consultant to the Organization Commit¬
tee.
Resolutions on matters pertaining to the pur¬
pose and function of U.S.Y. became the unani¬
mous decision of the body as reported on by
Arnold Tenenbaum. Miss Rosalyn Abramovitz
of Tampa was consultant to this committee.
Seminars
The heart of the Conference was the four
Seminars conducted on the following subjects:
“A Program for U.S.Y., Leaders, Philip Selber,
Jacksonville, Abe Tenenbaum, Savannah ;
“Need for Synagogue Youth Leagues, Leaders,
Mrs. Henry Wernick, Tampa, Mr. Ted Gross,
Jacksonville, “Jewish Youth on the American
Scene”, Leaders, Mr. Jerome Gordon, Miami,
Dr. Ben Glasser, Orlando, “What We Mean by
Conservative Judaism,” Leaders, Mrs. Sanders
A. Tofield, Jacksonville, Mr. Paul Freedman,
National U.S.Y. President. Delegates continued
discussing the subject matter of the study ses¬
sions for the remainder of the Conference.
Social Events
Along with educational sessions and worship
hours, visitors and Jacksonville youth enjoyed
fellowship at the Oneg Shabbat held on Friday,
March 28th, Saturday, March 29th, the out¬
standing banquet and dance held on Saturday
night and the Minyan Club Breakfast, Sunday,
March 30th. Mr. and Mrs. David Harris contri¬
buted substantially by joining as hosts in honor
of their son’s Bar Mitzvah. The Dating Com¬
mittee, assisted by Mrs. Sanders A. Tofield,
Mrs. Leo Moskovitz and Mrs. Esther Rosen¬
berg, received a vote of thanks.
Athletics
In the girl’s Ping-Pong Tournament, Judy
Barnett of Savannah defeated Elaine Simovitz
of Tampa, 21-13, 21-15. In the boy’s division,
Paul Newman of Jacksonville defeated Sidney
Setzer of Jacksonville, 21-16, 21-19.
In the girl’s Volley Ball tournament, the
Jacksonville team defeated a combined team of
Tampa and St. Petersburg, 15-10. The local
team was composed of Harriett Witten, Judy
Barnett, Carol Wolfson, Pat Yales, Maxine
Horwitz, Linda Lovitz, Jean Portnoy, Elka
Miriam Block and Lois Sachs. Jacksonville’s
boy’s Volley Ball team defeated Miami 9-11,11-
2, 11-2. The local team was composed of Harry
Katz, Bernie Fraden, Charles Levy, Ray Blas-
enstein, Byron Selber and David Baker.
The universal satisfaction evidenced with
this Conference, speaks well for the future of
U.S.Y. in the entire region.

national Veterans Councils and committees. Because of its
relationship with leadership in veterans circles, J. W. V. is
able to effectively serve Jewish interests in important areas.

In its third year, the Center Youth League, (C. Y. L.)
established itself as an integral organization of the Jack¬
sonville Jewish Center. It has advanced in all of its pur¬
poses, namely, to bring Jewish youth closer to the syna¬
gogue and to provide wholesome recreation and opportunities
for self-development among Jewish teen-agers. Recognition
for the achievements of the C. Y. L. is shared by the
officers listed who were elected at a meeting held on
April 13th and by the previous administration.
C. Y. L. sent a delegate to the charter convention
of National United Synagogue Youth (U. S. Y.) that was
held in the Jewish Theological Seminary of America in
New York in December of 1951. The delegate was Charles
Palot, who was president at the time. By affiliating for¬
mally with the newly launched national synagogue youth
movement, the Club took a far reaching step in furthering its
program.
At the end of March, C. Y. L. was host to the South¬
eastern Regional Conference of U. S. Y., when over 125
boys and girls attended from Conservative congregations in
Georgia and Florida. South Carolina and Alabama are also

Six years, as a measure of time, is not so very long, but
the measure of accomplishment which has taken place during
the past six years, since the inception of River Garden, is
nothing short of amazing.
It was not too long ago that our Home was but a dream
in the minds and hearts of a few women, who united together
under the name of The Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society.
They had as their guiding star—Mrs. Rosa Stein—of blessed
memory, whose vision, devotion, courage and sheer determina¬
tion served not only as an inspiration for the ladies of the
organization, but made itself felt by all with whom she came
in contact. She and this small group of inspired women,
were able to make others cognizant of the community’s
need for a Home for the Aged, and they gave tirelessly of
themselves and worked zealously toward raising funds so
that a Home might one day become a reality.
From this dream of a few there came into being a ser¬
vice for many, and here in our community stands the symbol
of thirty years of unswerving devotion to an ideal,—The
River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged.
As the Home has grown, so too have grown its needs,
and we, The Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society are ever
ready to fulfill these needs, whether they be for funds to
defray the cost of an elevator, a sprinkler system, whether
it be the replenishing of linens, sponsoring the work of the
Hobby Shop, personal services for comfort, happiness, and
assistance of the residents, or merely to help burn a mortgage,
we are dedicated to wholeheartedly rendering service when¬
ever and however it is needed.
As the Woman’s Auxiliary of the River Garden Home,
we further dedicate ourselves to service, and pray that we
may ever be a blessing unto the Home, as the Home is a
blessing unto us.

hygiene, school lunches for 150,000 children,
recreation program and vocational education.

maintain and educate nearly 60,000 children.
Hadassah works with the Jewish National
Fund in purchasing and reclaiming land for
colonization.

June, ground was broken for the new $20,-
000,000 Medical Center in Jerusalem.

in local and national affairs and keep abreast
of the best in progressive thought and action.

live a positive Jewish and American life.

Since 1946, when River Garden opened its doors to de¬
monstrate whether or not a Jewish Home for the Aged was
needed in the State of Florida, each succeeding year has
shown an increase in the resident population and a growing
waiting list.
Today the demands for the services of the Home have
increased to a point where the present 43 available beds are
not sufficient to give immediate occupancy to persons making
application for entrance.
The trend during the past year also indicated a greater
demand for our infirmary facilities, where our 14 bed capa¬
city are for the aged who show marked physical, emotional,
and mental deterioration.
Idle minds and idle hands, among our well aged group,
have indicated the need for servies beyond that of routine
care. Elderly people like any other group, must have recrea¬
tional and leisure time activities. During the past year we
inaugurated such activities as monthly forum series, instruc¬
tion in ballroom and square dancing, and shuffleboard games.
We have also continued with our Hobby Shop program, the
publication of the resident’s quarterly newspaper, and the
showing of motion pictures.
Our auxiliary, the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society,
ever mindful of our needs, not only helped us to pay off the
existing mortgage, but constructed a Summer pavilion at
the riverfront where the residents could enjoy the scenic
beauty and cool breezes.
The Home’s future plans are to extend its services to
the aged Jewish people of our community who reside in
their own homes. It is believed that many of these elderly
people are isolated from their own age group, and lack the
opportunity to participate in organized activities. If brought
together with others, they could enjoy an active social life
and their days would be more meaningful. This day care
program for elderly people is in the planning stage, and we
hope to inaugurate the . program shortly.
We anticipate the fact that more and more of the aged,
particularly in the infirm, chronically ill, and mentally dis¬
turbed groups, will need our services. In order to meet these
anticipated needs, we must look forward to and plan
for a modern infirmary in order that we can truly say, "we
take care of ALL our own.”
In planning to meet the challenges of future goals,
River Garden will continue, as in the past, to provide the
finest care and the highest standards of medical and recrea¬
tional services to the aged of this community.

One of the most important functions of the Jack¬

community in providing appropriate and dignified

have been adjusted in keeping with this analysis and
the splendid cooperation of the membership in accept¬

YOUTH CENTER DEDICATION BANQUET
Before a gathering of 350 Center members,
and the youth of the congregation, the second
story of the Activities Building, known as the
“Youth Center” was dedicated on Sunday,
April 20th. The prevailing spirit was of wor¬
shipful thanksgiving for the completion of the
structure that will make possible the finest
youth program in this part of the country. At
the same time there was a solemn resolve to
achieve the expansion program launched sever¬
al years ago, that will culminate with the re¬
modeling of the synagogue building.
Rabbi Morris B. Chapman of Congregation
B’nai Israel St. Petersburg, delivered the ad¬
dress, “Give Youth A Chance.” The event was
sponsored by the Building Fund Committee and
reservations were by subscription towards a
$12,000.00 fund for the completion of the con¬
tract.
Prior to the dinner, served in the beautiful¬
ly decorated auditorium, from 6 to 7 o’clock,
a reception was held in the library and lounge.
Mr. and Mrs. Burton Grossman took charge.
Arrangements of the evening were in the hands
of a committee headed by Joseph Hackel and
the following ladies took care of the table dec¬
orations: Mesdames Lou Feigenbaum, Henry
Kramer, Joe Krestul, Hyman Selber and Louis
Slott.
Harry Gendzier chairman of the Building
Fund Committee acted as toastmaster and pre¬
sented Center president, Philip Bork and Sis¬
terhood president, Mrs. Ralph Mizrahi, who
made formal presentations of the Youth Center
and its furnishings to representatives of the
Center sponsored youth organizations. Ac¬
knowledgments were offered by Charles Palot
president of C.Y.L.-U.S.Y.; Miss Horty Thier,
Y.P.L. president; Charles Levy, president of
Southeast Region, U.S.Y., and Larry Siegel
president elect of C.Y.L.-U.S.Y.
The inaugurations and opening of the Youth
Center followed. Rabbi Tofield called on C.Y.L.
representatives, Dorothy Newman, Ina Jean
Yoffee, Carol Kantor, Sidney Setzer, Bryna
Lee Datz and Byron Selber to draw the rib¬
bons extending from the auditorium through
the opening to the second floor. In the apprecia¬
tions to the congregation and to the Sisterhood,
the services of the committee responsible for
equipping the Youth Center was gratefully ac¬
knowledged. It consisted of Mesdames, Maurice
Palot, Herman Klausner and Henry Kramer.
After a vocal solo by Cantor Abraham Mar-
ton, with Mrs. Harry Brownstein at the piano,
the audience heard a description of the propos¬
ed renovations for the synagogue building and
school rooms given by Philip Selber, chairman
of the Architects Committee. Considerable en¬
thusiasm was displayed by the dinner guests
for the numerous projected improvements and
beautification of the sanctuary.
Seated at the head table in addition to those
participating in the program were Center of¬
ficers and their wives: Mr. and Mrs. Robert J.
Gordon, Mr. and Mrs. Burton Grossman, Mr.
and Mrs. Joe Hackel, Mr. and Mrs. Ralph Miz¬
rahi and Mr. and Mrs. Nathan Newman.

Presidents
Message
PHILIP BORK
11 NTO the ages past another year is cast! For the
Jacksonville Jewish Center it has been a year of
rededication of our Youth—and increasing spiritual
values for our entire membership. With the dedica¬
tion of the second floor Youth Activities Building the
character and scope of activities of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center has become completely rounded. To¬
day we are not only a basic religious institution; we
are building character, body and mind. Our program
includes culture, sociability, recreation and athletics.
During the past years, slowly but surely our hopes
have taken form. Our dreams of yesterday are near¬
ly realized. There is only one step left toward the
completion of our expansion program—the remodel¬
ing, air-conditioning and beautification of our Syn¬
agogue and classrooms. When completed we shall
have one of the outstanding Synagogue Centers in
America. But no matter how beautiful an edifice,
unless it has a good foundation of positive achieve¬
ment it can crumble to dust.
Neither the old-fashioned Cheder nor Sunday
Religious School once a week, can develop boys and
girls who will have the proper background and un¬
derstanding of their Jewish heritage to be well in¬
tegrated into the American scene.
My primary objective during my administration
was to balance the current fiscal budget. The task
is not too difficult. All that is required is that the
membership pay a fair share in dues to meet their
responsibility. Over ninety percent of the members
so far contacted have increased their dues. Increas¬
ed dues plus new membership is our solution. Our
expenditures, in view of services rendered, are below
Synagogue-Center average.
At this time I wish to give thanks to those officers
and members of the Board who gave willingly, not
only of their money but their services, counsel and
energies. We can be proud of our lay leadership.
My appreciation also for the loyalty and cooperation
of our membership.
Personally, I am very grateful for the guidance
and vision given the Jewish Center and myself by
our worthy Rabbi Sanders A. Tofield in helping us
reach this stage in the realization of many of the
Center’s aspirations. May I also express my appre¬
ciation to Cantor Abraham Marton for his fine work
and to the Center’s loyal and faithful staff?
A closing thought — the Synagogue is the major
organization in Jewish communal life and should
be the core of Jewish life in America. Conservative
Judaism, American in origin, offers positive values
and is the potent force based on the ethics and ideals
of our Torah. So let us resolve to increase our labors
in the vineyards of our faith. To all I extend my sin-
cerest and warmest greetings for the New Year.
May it bring peace, health and happiness.

The Progressive Credit Union, sponsored by the Jewish
Progressive Society, has continued to grow in its 12th year of
operation. Our Credit Union is one of more than 10,000 Credit
Unions in existence in the United States, having almost 5
million members and more than 1 billion dollars in assets.
While the Credit Union movement in the United States has
grown phenominally, the Progressive Credit Union in our own
community has also grown.
We look with pride upon our accomplishments. Prom
a very modest beginning of 20 members and $500.00 in assets,
we now have over 300 members, and more than $165,000 in
assets. We are especially proud of the fact that during our
existence, we have made over 1200 loans amounting to ap¬
proximately $1,500,000 without a single loss. This has been
accomplished by the membership striving to make evident, by
practice, the benefits of cooperation.. We are organized to
encourage thrift and provide a safe, convenient medium
whereby members can invest and accumulate savings. We
use the members’ surplus to assist another member when
circumstances require it. To eliminate usury and increase
the purchasing power of our membership by enabling them to
borrow for provident, productive and other beneficial purposes.
We sponsor the Blue Cross Hospitalization Plan under group
rates for those members who desire it. We carry Life Savings
Insurance equal to a member’s savings up to $1000.00, and
Loan Protection Insurance. This coverage is paid by the
Progressive Credit Union without any cost to the individual
member.
The climax of last year’s activities came with the annual
meeting and banquet held at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
Auditorium. At that time our Board declared a 2% dividend
on savings and a 25% rebate of interest to borrowing mem¬
bers. Our Credit Union is one of the few that make it a
practice to return to our borrower’s a percentage of the
interest they pay on loans. For the past year we show a
steady growth in all departments and look forward to even
greater growth in the coming year.

FRIENDS OF THE SEMINARY
THE SEMINARY'S ROLE
IN THE AMERICAN JEWISH COMMUNITY
The Seminary’s diversified program directly affects the
American Jewish Community, as well as millions of all faiths
throughout the globe,
. . . More than 2,000 students are being trained for the rabbi¬
nate, the teaching profession and careers in Jewish leader¬
ship at the Seminary and its West Coast branch.
, . . Nearly 500 Seminary-trained rabbis are providing spiritual
leadership in congregations throughout North America, and
to Jews in the Armed Forces.
. . . Millions of all faiths listen each week to The Eternal Light,
prize-winning radio series, and to the "Frontiers of Faith”
TV shows, which have won critical and public acclaim.
. . . The Library of the Seminary, the world’s largest collec¬
tion of Judaica and Hebraica, is a research center for scholars
of all creeds.
Rabbi Joel S. Geffen (center), Director, Department of Field
Activities and Community Service of the Seminary, and local
leaders examine one of the exhibits at the Miami branch of the
Seminary’s Jewish Museum.
. . . The priceless treasures of The Jewish Museum in New York
and its branches at the Miami Beach Jewish Center and Los
Angeles are seen annually by thousands of visitors.
. . . Nearly 1,000 religious leaders—Catholic, Protestant and
Jewish—gather for graduate study at The Institute for Re¬
ligious and Social Studies in New York, New England and
the Midwest in an effort to explore their different traditions
within the American democratic framework.
. . . Thousands of young people learn the mean¬
ing and practices of Judaism through the Lead¬
ers Training Fellowship and at the two Camps
Ramah.
. . . The Seminary Israel Institute strengthens the
spiritual and cultural bonds beteween the State
of Israel and America.
. . . The United Synagogue of America and The
Rabbinical Assembly of America provide planned
programs of religious activities for men, women
and children in congregations thoughout the
nation.
JACKSONVILLE'S PARTICIPATION
IN THE SEMINARY PROGRAM
The Jacksonville Jewish Center maintains strong ties with
the Jewish Theological Seminary of America.
Its Rabbi, Sanders A. Tofield, is a graduate of the Semi¬
nary’s Rabbinical School, and a member of its alumni or¬
ganization, The Rabbinical Assembly of America, serving on
its important Committee on Jewish Laws and Standards.
The Center is affiliated with the United Synagogue of Amer¬
ica and with all of its branches—The National Federation of
Jewish Men’s Clubs, The Women’s League, The Young People’s
Leagues, United Synagogue Youth.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center looks to the Seminary for
guidance and for inspiration. It is proud of the leading role
the Seminary has taken in advancing Jewish scholarship.
World-famous scholars, such as Dr. Louis Ginzberg, Dr. Alex¬
ander Marx, Dr. Saul Lieberman and Dr. Mordecai M. Kaplan,
Reading: Americans receive the Seminary’s honorary degree.
Shown here at the Seminary’s last Thanksgiving: Convocation
(left to right) are: Dr. James B. Oonant, President of Harvard
University; Arthur Hays Sulzberger, Publisher of The New
York Times; Dr. A. Whitney Griswold, President of Yale Uni¬
versity; Dr. Louis Finkelstein, Chancellor of the Seminary; and
Lewis L. Strauss, formerly a member of the U. S. Atomic
Energy Commission.
are members of the Seminary Faculty. The Seminary’s Chan¬
cellor, Dr. Louis Finkelstein, is internationally recognized, and
its Vice-Chancellors, Dr. Simon Greenberg and Dr. Max Arzt,
are distinguished among American Jewish leadership. The
Seminary itself, located at Broadway and 122nd Street in New
York City, is a cordial neighbor of such famous academic
centers as Columbia University, Union Theological Seminary,
the Julliard School of Music and International House.
Through the Friends of the Seminary annual
membership contributions the Center helps main¬
tain the ever increasing service that the Semin¬
ary and its coordinating bodies offer to Jewry
all over America.
In recent years the Friends of the Seminary
Dinner has become one of the distinguished
events on the Center’s calendar. Chairmen of
the project were Joe Hackel, Jacob Becker, Joe
Becker and Philip Bork, respectively. This year’s
Seminary Dinner will be remembered for its
historic connection with the Schechter Award
won by the Center for its Youth Work. The
chairman and toastmaster was Sam Siegel.

The Jewish Welfare Society has served the needy in
Jacksonville for many years. It is a member agency of the
Jacksonville Community Chest and the Jewish Community
Council. The agency’s functions are divided into various cate¬
gories.
The Society works with the National Council of Jewish
Women and the United Service for New Americans in helping
New Americans adjust themselves in our community. It ad¬
ministers the funds furnished by the Jewish Community
Council which, make their resettlement possible.
Local families receive financial assistance and receive
casework help to enable them to strengthen themselves and
find a more satisfying way of life.
Transients are counseled and helped wherever possible,
either with food, lodging, employment or contact with their
families.
The Welfare Society works in close relationship with
River Garden in processing applications for the Home and
furnishing a casework service to the residents of the Home
to make their initial adjustment to group living easier,
and their continuous residence richer.
The Society is the only Jewish licensed child placing
agency in the State of Florida and maintains a Foster Care
Program as well as an Adoption Program.
The Society offers a limited Social Service Program with
Jewish inmates at the Florida State Prison at Raiford.
With the financial help of the Levy Fund, the Jewish
Welfare Society has inaugurated, to a limited extent, the
operation of the Family Consultation Center. It serves
families where there are problems due to parent-child rela¬
tionships, as well as families experiencing martial discord.
Under the auspices of the Jewish Welfare Society, the
Family Consultation Center is non-sectarian. The Center has
completed 2 years of highly successful operation gaining
wide community and professional recognition.
Mr. Clifford J. Bodarky is Executive Director of the
Jewish Welfare Society and Director of its Family Consul¬
tation Center. Mr. A. H. Rothstein is President of the Jew¬
ish Welfare Society, and Dr. S. Halpern serves as Chairman of
the Administrative Committee of the Family Consultation
Center.
The Society is a member of the Florida Conference of
Social Welfare and the National Conference of Jewish Social
Welfare.
