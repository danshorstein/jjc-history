The Synagogue is one of the
finest examples of enduring and
progressive human institutions. It
has a history which reaches back
2500 years, it has been the pat¬
tern for the Christian Church and
the Mohammedan Mosque, and is
still today the most important in¬
stitution in Jewish life.
The Synagogue has endured,
and will continue to endure, be¬
cause it embodies the past and
present experiences and tradi¬
tions of the Jewish people, be¬
cause it is able to adjust itself
to the every-changing experiences
of new generations. The Syna¬
gogue was not imposed upon the
Jewish people, it was not the
‘‘brain child” of one or more
“planners” who were going to
give the people something good—
the Synagogue in its present form
is the result of 25 centuries of
growth, the Synagogue came from
the people, it is the creation of
the Jewish masses.
Historically it is difficult to
determine when the first Syna¬
gogue made its appearance. It
is known, however, that it had its
beginnings in Babylonia about
2500 years ago. The First Temple
was destroyed, the Jews were
exiled from their land, and their
traditions and their observances
were badly uprooted. The Baby¬
lonian Jews sought companion¬
ship, they wanted to study the
Holy Books, they longed for the
rituals and observances associated
with the Temple. In response to
these needs the Synagogue
emerged. Jews would gather in
the homes of their leaders to
worship together, to chant pray¬
ers and hymns, to listen to ^a
learned Jew reading a chapter or
two from the Bible and explain¬
ing it; they gathered to share
their joys, their sorrows, their
laughter and their tears.
And even during the Second
Temple this democratic institu¬
tion continued to grow and to
expand. True, worship through
sacrifices and offerings were con¬
centrated in the Temple proper.
The Synagogue, however, became
the Beth Ham-Midrash, the House
of Study.
