During the middle ages the
Synagogue once again demon¬
strated that it was the concrete
embodiment of the past and
present experiences and traditions
of the Jewish people, that it was
able to adjust its functions to the
new needs of new generations,
that it was a democratic institu¬
tion created by the people and for
the people. During this dreary
period of Jewish life, the Syna¬
gogue became the Beth Ha-
Keneseth, the House of As¬
sembly. Thus the Synagogue was
at this time not alone a place
for worship or study, it was the
meeting place for all Jews. Of
course these three functions of
the Synagogue did not develop as
rapidly or as simply as this brief
outline may indicate, it reached
these expressions only after cen¬
turies of continued existence.
Today the Synagogue is faced
with the necessity of making an¬
other adjustment. It can no
longer serve only religious needs,
it can no longer serve only as a
House of Study, nor can it serve
as a House of Assembly in the
narrow sense of the term. The
Synagogue today must become
the expression of the entire
Jewish community, it must pro¬
vide fellowship, religion and
study for all kinds of Jews and
for all age levels. Arid many
Synagogues have successfully
made this adjustment. The Syna¬
gogue Center provides educa¬
tional and1 recreational activities
for all of its meinbers and for the
community at large; community
Forums, athletic activities, plays,
concerts, art exhibits, men’s clubs,
women’s organizations, clubs, li¬
braries, social activities, etc. — all
these are the new services of the
Synagogue Center and only the
synagogues which will respond
and adjust to these needs will
flourish. And only by such ad¬
justments will Judaism continue
to be the most important element
in Jewish life.
