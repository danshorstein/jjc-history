Yom Kippur
One of the prime functions of
Yom Kippur is to break down the
natural resentment on the part of
human beings to admit that they
are wrong. The solemn Kol Nidre
service is opened with a petition to
God that he may permit us to pray
in the company of the transgres¬
sors, a term which may be applic¬
able to every member in the con¬
gregation. The public recitation of
the long catalogue of sins, among
which we are sure to find some that
apply to ourselves, the request for
forgiveness, which fill the prayer-
book, the custom of asking forgive¬
ness from those whom we know
that we have wronged, all of these
are calculated to bring about a
mood in which the worshipper ac¬
knowledges his errors and sins. This
is the first step necessary in making
any improvements, for without
acknowledging error there can be
no correction of error.
Yom Kippur also serves to dem¬
onstrate the supremacy of the spirit
over the flesh. Twenty four hours of
fasting is not only a denial of the
natural hungers of the body but
also a test of a man’s willpower to
withstand the temptations to which
he may be exposed in the year to
come.
