1 he practice of lighting candles in the Jewish home
upon ushering in the Sabbath every Friday evening and the
Festivals whenever they occur in the cycle of the Hebrew
Religious Calendar, is one of the most beautiful rituals in
Judaism. I radition has conferred this charming and edify¬
ing spiritual touch to the sanctity of the home upon the lady
of the house.
Jewish women, the wives and mothers of many genera¬
tions, made it a practice to add a personal prayer to the
required benediction. The impromptu prayer was usually
given in the language most familiar to the lady. It included
requests for every day blessings such as physical and spirit¬
ual welfare for the home and the dear ones it represents.
Accordingly the prayer varied with the mood, the occasion
and the need of the hour. For this reason one o f the fre¬
quently observed Jewish rituals has very few prayers hand¬
ed down from tradition. The primary significance of
kindling the Sabbath and Festival candles is the merging,
of the light from the soul with the Light of the Universe
at the time light is spread in the family abode.
With hesitancy we suggest a prayer found in the book,
“Sabbath, the Day of Delight” by Abraham E. Millgram.
Let it serve as a model of a type prayer that each woman
may find it in her heart to utter and not become a perfunc¬
tory stereotype.
Judaism has associated its fondest ideals with the sym¬
bol of a light.
“For good deeds are as a candle, and the Torah
is light .”
“For with Thee is the foundation of life, in Thy
light do we see light.”
1 hope that including the text o f the benedictions for
the lighting of the candles on various occasions and the
suggested prayer in our Year Book will help the women
of our congregation practice the observance with faith¬
ful ness. On the Hebrew Calendar we have shown
the time of lighting candles of the year next to the symbol
L. C which stands for “lighting of candles.”
