The year of construction has also been a cost con¬
structive year in all phases of Center activity. True, our
best energies and thought in the year 5710 that we are
summarizing in the 1950-51 edition of the Year Book were
devoted to the erection of our Activity Building and re¬
modeling portions of the present building for educational
purposes. Closer study of our program during the same
period, however, will reveal that we kept apace with the
line of progress our synagogue has shown in recent years.
To be more specific. Included this year among branches
of the Center family is a new affiliate, the C. Y. L. During
this past year the Center Youth League was formed and
made a place for itself in the community as will be shown
further on. Registrations in the various departments of our
Religious School showed increases in proportion greater
than mere increase in population. We are improving om
services constantly. Gratifying indeed were the projects
undertaken and successfully accomplished by our Sister¬
hood, Men’s Club and Y. P. L. We look forward to
ever more impressive records once our enlarged and more
beautiful accomodations are ready for occupancy, with the
help of the Almighty, within a matter of months.
For a full appreciation of what the Center means in
the life of its members a broader view is necessary. We
have to place ourselves against the backdrop of history
as well as against the present outlook on the Jewish scene.
How does the Center compare with the congregation that
it was in 1927 when the present location was occupied?
There is almost no basis for comparison with the modest
beginnings in B’nai Israel which was our original syna¬
gogue formed at the turn of the century. We have ad¬
vanced from stage to stage on the impetus of Conservative
Judaism in America.
Center leaders have understood for some time that
the Jewish Theological Seminary of America, the United
Synagogue and the Rabbinical Assembly form a fountain¬
head for vibrant and vital Jewish life on the American
Continent. That awareness is spreading to an ever wider
circle within our membership. Increased numbers of
Friends of the Seminary year by year is a sure indication.
On the national level and in the region,
officers of the Center are well represented. The strength
that we add to the Conservative movements whether by
strengthening the arm of the Seminary, United Synagogue,
Rabbi Sanders A. Tofield
or Woman’s League reflects directly upon our own ef¬
fectiveness in bringing the message of our faith closer to
the hearts of our own people.
With a deep sense of gratitude to the Almighty I
contemplate the opportunity I had as your Rabbi to share
in two of the most basic issues confronting traditional Jew¬
ish living in America today. It was my prvilege to serve
on commissions of the Rabbinical Assembly deliberating
on the most effectve rapprochement between the new Israel
emerging in the Lloly Land and the greatest settlemet of
diaspora Jewry that we represent in America. Secondly
revitalization of the Sabbath for our people through a
better understanding of our traditional law called the
Halakhah became a major concern. The concepts and pro¬
cedures clarified at the Rabbinical Assembly Jubilee Con¬
vention may well prove to be the light guiding us to Zion
and blissful realization of worthy life in America. The
year 5711 that beckons with divine promise as we pass
through its gateway on Rosh Hashanah coming calls upon
us for renewed commitment. Pray that our synagogue
shall respond nobly to the challenge.
Years of construction are before us. They can be
years of reconstruction in the souls and hearts of all who
thrill to the lofty tradition that has been our heritage and
to which we are eternally bound. May the year 5711 bring
happiness and joy, well being and blessing to the entire
Center family.

In meeting the day to day problems of the Center,
certain distinct conclusions make themselves apparent. Our
greatest need today is a better evaluation of the Center
program by its members. One thought above all else must
be hammered home/’ The work of the Center is not a
charity nor is it concerned with the providing of luxuries.
It is solely directed to making our devotion to God meaning¬
ful and in making our lives as American Jews balanced and
normal.
Few Center members can visualize the rearing of a
family without the assistance of the Center. Can one imagine
having a son grow into manhood, without bene fit o f Heb-
rew religious education, Bar Mitzvah, confirmation, and
without the opportunity for meeting socially with other
young Jewish people? Certainly, the Center is part of our
living. Normal Jewish life would be impossible in Jackson¬
ville without it. Why then do we experience such difficulty
in getting our members to consider their obligations to the
Center as they do their other every day living expenses?
Again, the Center is not a charity, nor is it a luxury.
Too often synagogue leadership is criticised for concern¬
ing itself with monetary matters. This is not the role that
such leadership would prefer, but rather it is the role which
has been reduced by congregation membership, for those
burdened with responsibility have become convinced that
what a congregation achieves depends to a great degree upon
the amount of funds it can secure. Especially is this true of
the Conservative Synagogue.
The Conservative Synagogue is primarily interested in
developing Jews who are loyal to their traditions and at the
same time well integrated as Americans of the twentieth
century. This program is not easy nor is it inexpensive. The
requirements are considerable. The old fashioned Chedar
• or Hebrew School will not do it. Neither will the once a
week Sunday religious school alone be successful. It is the
nursery-kindergarten, the daily Hebrew School, the Sun¬
day religious school, the day camp, the well trained staffs,
meeting in modern facilities and served by adequate bus
systems, that are today's requirements.
Schools alone are not enough. The recreational and
social activities of the children must also be provided in a
sympathetic Jewish enviroment. Religious services must be
traditional and yet attuned to the needs of the modern adult,
thus requiring progressive spirtual leadership, the services
of Cantor and choir, all with the greatest degree of talent
and training. Modern congregational members demand the
pastoral services of a Rabbi and staff and the sonstant avail¬
ability for performance of Jewish rituals.
Philip Selber
We certainly cannot sell our way of Jewish life on
the basis of price or convenience. We can only secure the
loyalty of our members by convincing them of the destiny
of conservative Judaism and its philosophy of American
Jewish life. Our members must have a keen sense of loyalty
to the Center as the local expression of this movement. It
is good for them as it is for the Center.
The evident conclusion is that congregations cannot
be maintained on the basis of each person paying according
to what he directly receives. Some members who are on
fixed salaries or who are in small businessess cannot pos¬
sibly pay the cost of the direct services which they receive.
Similarly, at certain periods the demands o f the individual
on the institution are at a peak, while at other times his
requirements are at a minimum below his actual contrib¬
ution. We must therefore realize the merit o f the now accep¬
ted princip le that our support must be proportionate with
our financial ability. Above all, we must keep in mind that
if we are interested in success of American Jewish life,
we should not transfer the financial responsibi lity com¬
pletely to the parents of the children who are the medium
by which Judaism in America will survive.
At this time I call on you to stand aside from day to
day activities long enough to analyze the 1 ong term trends
and fundumental meaning of your Center-Synagogue. Such
examinations become imperative if the organization is to
experience sound growth.
Sincerest and warmest greetings to each and everyone
of you for a Happy New Year.

Jewish Music today plays a unique and unparrelled
part in molding the character of American Judaism; wheth¬
er it be making services reverent and meaningful, or wheth¬
er it be in transmitting the cultural value of Israel life to
America, or whether it be in perpetuating the sacred chants
of our forefathers.
Cantor Abraham Marton
CENTER CHORAL SOCIETY AND CHOIR
In this realm, the Jacksonville Jewish Center is most
happy and gratified to comment on the remarkable progress
of the musical program in the past year, under the direction
of Cantor Abraham Marton. Our volunteer choir served
nobly at High Holiday services, at Friday evening Family
Sabbath S ervices and on all appropriate occasions. Candi¬
dates for Bar Mitzvah are being trained in the traditional
Hazanut chant. Planned for the future is a Junior Choral
Society.
Outstanding among the musical activities at the Center
is the annual concert of the Center Choral Society, given
in connection with the observance of Jewish Music Month.
A recital of Jewish Music was presented at the Uni¬
versity of Florida under the auspices of the Department
of Music of the University on February 26, 1950. 1 n ad¬
dition to that the group participated in several programs at
various events.

THE TASK AHEAD
Construction is going ahead on schedule and when
completed the building will be one of the finest of its type
in the South.
Among the many conveniences included in the plans
of the bui Iding is equipment for an air conditioning system,
and it is planned that this will be installed in the near
future.
Many projects will have to be undertaken in order to
complete the building and its furnishings. The young
peoples section on the second floor will be complete
enough to be used for the various activities; however, the
furnishings for the different rooms will have to be pro¬
vided. It is hoped that these will be furnished through
projects undertaken by the affiliated youth organizations,
included in the many furnishings needed are those for the
lounge, arts and crafts room, and other meetings and rec¬
reation rooms.
A committee of women from the Sisterhood have or¬
ganized the Beautification Committee which will undertake
the task of furnishing the main lounge, kitchen, and Li¬
brary. T hrough the enthusiastic cooperation of committees
such as these, it is hoped that the best equipment will be
provided to fill the needs.
The present building is now undergoing the first step
of a remodeling program. From the auditorium, spacious
rooms are being provided for classroom purposes and for
a large daily prayer room. I here will be additional class-’
rooms at the rear of the present building when the present
faci Iities are removed to the new building. This will]
greatly enhance the present classroom facilities until such
time as the plans can be carried out to build a modern
new school building on the Sil ver Street side o f the pro¬
perty. It is anticipated that at that time the Synagogue
will be completely renovated and the seating capacity en¬
larged utilizing the present classroom space in the rear of
the building.
1 he task has been a long and arduous one in
order to reach the present point of progress in our building
expansion program. I he Building Expansion Committee,
under the capable leadership of Harry Gendzier, has spent
many hours pondering over the plans and solving all the
important problems of financing this huge program. It is
urged that each and everyone give them every cooperation
possible in the near future, whether it be through meeting
pledges at once or helping with the many projects being
set up for furnishing and beautification of the building.
With the help of the Almighty, the dream of this
generation will have been turned into reality for themselves
and their children.

Festivals whenever they occur in the cycle of the Hebrew
Religious Calendar, is one of the most beautiful rituals in
Judaism. I radition has conferred this charming and edify¬
ing spiritual touch to the sanctity of the home upon the lady
of the house.
Jewish women, the wives and mothers of many genera¬
tions, made it a practice to add a personal prayer to the
required benediction. The impromptu prayer was usually
given in the language most familiar to the lady. It included
requests for every day blessings such as physical and spirit¬
ual welfare for the home and the dear ones it represents.
Accordingly the prayer varied with the mood, the occasion
and the need of the hour. For this reason one o f the fre¬
quently observed Jewish rituals has very few prayers hand¬
ed down from tradition. The primary significance of
kindling the Sabbath and Festival candles is the merging,
of the light from the soul with the Light of the Universe
at the time light is spread in the family abode.
With hesitancy we suggest a prayer found in the book,
“Sabbath, the Day of Delight” by Abraham E. Millgram.
Let it serve as a model of a type prayer that each woman
may find it in her heart to utter and not become a perfunc¬
tory stereotype.
Judaism has associated its fondest ideals with the sym¬
bol of a light.
“For good deeds are as a candle, and the Torah
is light .”
“For with Thee is the foundation of life, in Thy
light do we see light.”
1 hope that including the text o f the benedictions for
the lighting of the candles on various occasions and the
suggested prayer in our Year Book will help the women
of our congregation practice the observance with faith¬
ful ness. On the Hebrew Calendar we have shown
the time of lighting candles of the year next to the symbol
L. C which stands for “lighting of candles.”

Rosh Hashanah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year
and lasts two days.
Shabbat Shuva the Sabbath occuring between Rosh Hashanah
and Yom Kippur, takes its name from the first words of the
Haftorah, “Shuvah, Yisrael—Return, 0 Israel.”
Yom Kippur the Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting
and prayer, a day for confession of sin and supplication for
forgiveness. “For transgressions between man and God, re¬
pentance on Yom Kippur brings atonement. For transgres¬
sions between man and man, Yom Kippur brings no atone¬
ment until amends are made.”
Sukkot the Feast of Tabernacles commemorates the period
when our forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their
wanderings through the wilderness of Sinai after their Egypt¬
ian bondage.
Simhat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) celebrates the com¬
pletion of the reading of the five books of Moses. On this
day, the reading is begun anew for the coming year.

The Jewish Community Council is well named. It
is Jewish, definitely concerned with all Jewish activity
on a broad community basis. It is the community, since
it is made up of all Jewish organizations, and this is a
Council to which organizations elect their own people to
represent them in a governing and policy-making board.
The Constitution of the Council describes its purposes:
1.—To he I p maintain the dignity and integrity of
Jewish life.
2.—To develop an articulate, intelligent and effective
public opinion on community problems and interests.
5.—To foster cooperation among all elements in the
community of Jacksonville.
4.—To raise, receive, collect, hold and distribute funds
for Jewish causes, either directly or through presently ex¬
isting or subsequently established agencies.
5.-To enable the Jewish community of Jacksonville
through coordination of fund-raising activities to meet its
obligations to local, national and overseas causes in an
intelligent and effective manner.
6.—To cooperate with other agencies for better mu¬
tual understanding and
7.—In general, to plan for the welfare and develop¬
ment of the community as a whole, engaging in activities
as will contribute to that end.
Now, just how does the Council carry out these re-
sponsibi Iities?
Naturally its most important job is the annual United
Jewish Campaign. In 1949 we raised about two hun¬
dred thousand dollars, In 1948 we raised about three
hundred and forty thousand, and in 1947 about the same
amount. Budgeting and allocation of these sums is an
important job, one which helps maintain such local or¬
ganizations as our family agency and the Old Folks Home.
To raise these sums a complete and up to date file of the
entire community is maintained.
Another function of increasing importance is the
Community Calendar. As the city develops and organ¬
izational activities increase, there is great need for a
central clearance service.
The developmet of a Presidents Council, made up of
the presidents of all organizations, both to serve as a
sounding board in the discussion of mutual problems and
to exchange ideas, is another service of the Community
Council.
A major activity is the Public Relations Committee.
The Committee exem plif ies an effort by a representative
cross-section of our community to deal with anti-semitism
and other problems which affect the well-being of local
Jewry. Eli Fink is chairman of this important committee.
Still another function is our care of refugees. The
planning for refugee reception, their integration into the
community, the financing of the entire program, is carried
out by the Council. In this regard the Jewish Welfare
Society acts as the service arm of the Council. Abe
Rothstein is chairman of the Council’s Refugee Committee.
On the recreational end the Council originated and
sponsors two major bowling leagues — one for men and
the other for women.
To acquaint the Jacksonville community with what
is going on, the Council publishes the Commentator.

Since River Garden opened its doors in March, 1946,
it has continued to add facilities and services for the aged
Jewish people of Florida. During the past year our origin¬
al building was remodeled into an infirmary, to care for
the chronically ill, senile, and handicapped aged. This
infirmary has a bed capacity of fourteen.
Through the generous support of the Ladies Hebrew
Sheltering Aid Society an elevator was installed to afford
better service. I his infirmary service required an expanded
nursing staff, as well as psychiatric consultation and treat¬
ment. A part time physiotherapist also has been added to
our staff, and comes daily to give massages, heat treat¬
ments, and aids the handicapped in relearning to walk. A
preventative, as well as rehabilitative program of medicine,
has resulted in maximum health standards for all residents.
River Garden serves as a regional home for all the Jew¬
ish communities of Florida, with the exception of Dade
County, and derives its support proportionally from each
of these communities. Capital funds for building and
renovations are derived through gifts and legacies from
friends of River Garden, and through the auxiliary fund
Sidney Entman
raising of the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society.
River Garden affords a fuller life for its residents, by
social activities and programs designed to stimulate their
interest. The Hobby Shop is one of the most popular
programs. An annual Fair is held at which time all articles
made in the Hobby Shop are displayed for sale.
The Golden Age Club, a program of the National
Council of Jewish Women, has continued its activities
at the Home. Other women and youth organizations
sponsor parties for holidays and special events. Movies are
shown regularly. A social hour of games, music, and
visitors every evening bring many pleasant hours into the
lives of our residents.
River Garden keeps looking to the future, and how to
meet the increased problems and needs of the aged. With
the Home filled to its capacity of 42 residents, and an
increasing waiting list, a further need for expansion is evi¬
dent. River Garden is constantly striving to meet the goals
set by its founders, to provide a home and a haven for
all the Jewish aged and infirm in need of their care.

The Jewish Welfare Society has served the needy
in Jacksonville for many years. It is a member agency of
the Jacksonville Community Chest and the Jewish Com¬
munity Council. The agency’s functions are divided into
five categories.
The society works with the National Council of Jew¬
ish Women and the United Service for New Americans in
helping New Americans to adjust themselves to the com¬
munity life in Jacksonville.
Local Relief cases are seen on a regular basis and
assisted accordingly.
j ransient cases are counseled and helped wherever
possible either with food, lodging, employment, or contact
with their families.
The Welfare Society works in close relationship with
River Garden in processing applications for the Home and
also aiding the residents to adjust to institutional life.
With the financial help of the Levy Fund, the Jewish
Welfare Society has inaugurated, to a limited extent, the
operation of the Family Consultation Center. It serves
families where there are problems due to parent-child re¬
lationships. Under the auspices of the Jewish Welfare
Society, the Family Consultation Center is non-sectarian
and inter-racial.
This summer, Mr. Bodarky was appointed to direct
the professional operation of the Family Consultation Cen¬
ter . Mr. Bodarky is formerly of New York and records
show a broad knowledge in dealing with child behavior
problems.
