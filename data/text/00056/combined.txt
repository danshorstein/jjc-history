Happy 50th Birthday Israel
50 years seen through the eyes of our congregation
a time line researched by Wendy Honigman
1947: In Israel, Dov Kentof and his family crowded around the radio listening to the United Nations vote on the
creation of the State of Israel; it was quiet in his little town until the announcement - then dancing in the streets.
1948: 7 January: Harry & Lilo Frisch were married in Petach Tikva. William
“Bill” Katz joined the Israeli Air Force; became Commanding Officer of B-17 Flying
Fortress bomber squadron ’’Hammer”. 9 May, Mother’s Day: the anticipated creation of
the State of Israel is announced at Rhoda & Norman Kagan’s wedding. 14 May: David
Ben-Gurion, standing beneath a portrait of Theodor Herzl, proclaimed the creation of the
State of Israel. Harry Frisch drove in the Independence Day Parade. 15 May: the British
lowered their flag and left Palestine; the first Egyptian warplane was shot down over Tel
Aviv; the Arabs cut off Jerusalem’s water supply. Rabbi Tofield and Jack Becker were in
Chicago for a United Synagogue Convention in Rabbi Solomon Goldman’s (president of
United Synagogue) shul when Ben Gurion announced the State of Israel. Rabbi Dov
Kentof recalls friends and family leaving to fight in the War of Independence. Nili Zimlich
(Grinberg) was among those evacuated from Ramat Rachel to Jerusalem, by tank; she
remembers hiding under the bed during bombings because there were no shelters. 26
November: Ben Frisch born in Petach Tikva.
1949: 14 February: first elected Knesset opened. Armistice agreements with Arabs signed. Defense Service Law
mandated the army to teach conscripts Hebrew, Bible, Israeli and general history, geography, and science. May: Ella &
Jacob Rogozinski, as honeymooners, arrived from a DP camp in Germany. On his first trip to Israel, Jack Becker bought
land north of Tel Aviv, near the airport, for $2300. 1 May: Perry Mibab, his brother, mother and sister came from DP
camp in Germany to a live in a tent. November: Groundbreaking for Youth Building at 3rd & Silver synagogue.
1950: 27 February: Chaim Rogozinski was born in Ramat Chen. Knesset passed Law of Return. “Operation
Magic Carpet”. Jaffa became Tel Aviv-Yafo. May: Mibab family moved to a flat near Tel Aviv in an former orange grove.
ZOA program featured Ben Forman a member of the crew of the ship Exodus. Bill Katz joined El Al.
1951: ZOA program featured Dr. Carl Herman Voss, who talked about his
non-Jewish impressions of Israel. Young Judea clubs raised funds for Hava, a training
center that was to be built near Jerusalem. Horty Thier (Gendzier) was president of
Young People’s League at Center.
1952: 13 April: Masada Club sponsored a Variety Show for the Benefit of
Medical Supplies for the State of Israel; $760 was raised toward the purchase of an
electro-cardiograph machine for the new Hadassah Medical Center at Ein Kerem.
14 June: Morrie Osterer born in Yafo; lived in a shack on the beach. 60th Anniversary
of Jacksonville Jewish Center.
1953: Abba Eban elected a vice-president of the United Nations General
Assembly. Soviet Union broke off diplomatic relations. 8 December: ZOA sponsored a Chaim Weizmann memorial
service for the 1st president of the State of Israel and more than $2000 worth of JNF trees were purchased.
1954: 18 May: Abe Rogozinski was born in Ramat Chen. Ellen Grossman (Freedman) became Bat Mitzvah. Harry
Gendzier was president of River Garden.
1955: ZOA/JNF program had Mayor Burns as guest speaker. Center Youth League affiliated with United Synagogue
Youth of America. December: Center’s Children’s Choir performed a Hanukkah program on local television.
1956: 1 February: Hadassah and other Zionist groups presented an Israeli
Fashion Show. March: Perry Mibab comes to Jacksonville for a visit. 20 April:
ZOA observed 8th Anniversary of State by honoring Max Rubin with an
inscription in the Golden Book of JNF. June: Demri family immigrated to
Israel from Tunisia. 29 October: Sinai Campaign, Operation Kadesh. Hungarian
Revolution refugees arrived. New Aron Kodesh (Holy Ark) dedicated in main
sancturary at 3rd & Silver; it was refitted years later for the chapel of present
synagogue.
Pollie and Philip Copans's country club in
Johannesburg, South Africa, raised funds to build
a bowling green and club house in Ramat, Israel.

Simhat Torah Honorees
The High Holiday season reaches its joyous climax on Simhat Torah, the last day of the festival
of Sukkot. Because of our perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to as Israel’s “bride”. On
Simhat Torah, we bring to a close the annual cycle of the reading of the Torah and immediately begin
anew. An ancient tradition evolved of honoring worthy men by calling them to the Torah for the last
reading, Hatan Torah, and the new beginning portion, Hatan Bereshit. In our synagogue tradition,
we also honor the contributions made by a “Woman of Valor”.

Shabbat Torah Readers
Ted Gross, Coordinator
Every Bar and Bat Mitzvah
knows the dalet line of the Ashrei
prayer, beginning “Dor L’Dor..”. It
emphasizes the continuing tradition
of synagogue worship. Like most
other prayers, its origin is in the
Psalms, chapter 145. The rabbis
considered this psalm to be so
important that it is the only one
repeated in the morning service. For
us, the emphasis on dor Vdor stresses
the centrality of reading the Torah at
every official service. We are,
therefore, especially thankful to those
candidates who joined our team of
readers this past year. The names of
on-going readers are repeated every
other year.

Center Youth Groups
Chalutzim - Grades 4 and 5
Research has proven that the survival of Judaism depends on
the exposure of our youth to Jewish activities, friends and experiences
that commit them to perpetuating that feeling of Jewishness and Jewish
life.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center sponsors three outstanding youth
groups for youngsters in grades 4-12. Each group meets regularly
and enjoys programs of a religious, cultural and social nature.
Through active participation in these groups,
a sense of pride and an identification with Judaism
are instilled. Jewish values, attitude and behavior
patterns are discussed and carried out through
Mitzvah programs and study.
Conventions, regional activities and camps give
our youth the opportunity to meet other Jewish youth
from all over the United States.
The youth programs are coordinated by Rabbi
Dov Kentof, and are supervised by the Youth
Commission. All groups are affiliated with the
Southeast Region and United Synagogue of America.

Solomon Schechter Day School
This fall, Solomon Schechter Day School opened its doors with
the second highest enrollment increase since its beginning 36 years ago.
One reason for the growing numbers is the marked improvement
Schechter has shown in retaining students. At the same time, the
enrollment base has continued to grow by adding an additional
kindergarten class for Fall 1997.
Willard M. Kennedy
Principal
Quality of education is our hallmark. I am pleased to note an
overall confidence in the mission of the school and in its ability to make
that mission a reality.
After much evaluation and deliberation, over the last few years, our Judaica faculty endorsed the adoption
of a new Hebrew curriculum which begins with the TaL Am program. This curriculum is a scoped and sequenced
language program. In preparation for this undertaking, members of our staff have received intensive in-service
education from the Jewish Education Council of Montreal.
We have planted the seeds of family education at Schechter with the Family University program. The roots
are growing and the plant is blooming, but it is not a full-fledged tree yet.
Another area of successful parent participation is our hard-working PTA. The school is truly enriched by all
the parent volunteers who provide many support services during the school year. A special thanks is due the PTA
for its generous gift which enabled us to computerize the Henry Kramer Library and
Resource Center and expand its services to our student population.
It is my hope that people will continue to feel free to come and visit me, to share
with me their concerns, hopes and dreams for their children. I look back on a year of
successes for individuals and for the school as a whole. I look ahead to a year that
will be equally successful, and at the same time filled with challenges.
School Secretary

Center Cemetery
The Center owns and operates two cemeteries, both adjacent
to the Evergreen Cemetery in North Jacksonville. Both are beautiful
pieces of property with large shade trees and mature landscaping.
The original cemetery is referred to as the “Old Cemetery.” The
newer cemetery is known as the Center Memorial Park. A new section
has been opened. The Center
provides complete maintenance
of these grounds. Both
cemeteries are open daylight
hours every day except
Shabbat and Jewish holidays.
A Memorial Service is
conducted each year on the Sunday between Rosh Hashanah and Yom
Kippur at each of the cemeteries. To reach the Old Cemetery, travel north
on North Main Street to Winona. Make a right on Winona and go until
you reach Gate 5. The Old Cemetery is on the left. To reach the Center
Memorial Park, travel north on North Main Street to 43rd Street, which
is beyond Winona. Make a right on 43rd Street and the entrance is on the
next right, which is Liberty Street.
Just as with other life cycle events at the Center, burial and mourning
rites are reserved for Center members. Therefore, only full members may
purchase a plot in one of the Center cemeteries. Associate members are
not eligible to purchase a plot. For more information regarding the purchase
of cemetery plots, please call the Center administrative office.

Message from the Director
Early Childhood Department
Located in the Selevan Pre-School
The Jacksonville Jewish Center’s Early Childhood Department is
proud of our affiliation and accreditation with The Florida Kindergarten
Council. This organization is comprised of independent schools dedicated
to maintaining high standards in early childhood programs throughout
Florida. The Florida Kindergarten Council is recognized as an official
evaluative and accrediting organization for non-public, pre-primary schools
of the State of Florida.
An accredited early childhood school encompasses goals which
provide for the total development of young children as its paramount
consideration. There is hardly any subject that parents feel more passionate
about than their children’s education. A healthy, happy and challenging
environment where children are encouraged to grow and develop to the
best of their abilities is created and maintained. Our curriculum is planned to nurture the social, emotional,
intellectual and physical development of each child.
The child’s early learning experiences form the foundation for educational success. An effective curriculum
provides many opportunities for creative and cognitive learning situations which are suited to the maturation
levels of young children.
The staff of the Early Childhood Department and the Florida Kindergarten Council
members recognize the great importance of the early years of a child’s life. A good beginning
enhances a child’s joy of learning, which in turn results in positive and enthusiastic attitudes
toward academic study in the future.

Jewish Community Alliance
. 8505 San Jose Blvd. • Jacksonville, FL 32217
Phone: (904) 730-2100 • Fax: (904) 730-2444
Over the past 9 years, the JCA has grown and become an important part of
Jacksonville’s Jewish community, serving people of all ages — from 6 week old
babies to senior adults. Through our programs, services, and facilities we have
created a “hamisha” neighborhood.
Historically, Jewish Community Centers helped to improve living conditions,
educational opportunities, and helped fulfill various other needs of Jewish
immigrants. Today, many of these objectives are still part of the Center movement,
revised to meet the needs of today’s Jewish community. Centers, like the JCA, are
viewed as significant community resources created to provide positive Jewish
experiences, which are important for Jewish continuity.
The mission of the JCA is to (1) contribute to the continuation of Jewish life,
culture, and community, (2) provide a common meeting ground for the Jewish
community, (3) enhance the quality of life for all segments of the community, and
(4) strengthen family life. The JCA fulfills its mission through an integrated range
of high quality programs for people of all ages including: child care and family
programs; fitness and recreational facilities and activities; Jewish education, culture
and arts programs; and by providing a place for family, “neighborhood,” and
community gatherings.
It is with great pride that we are able to work with the Jacksonville Jewish
Center and other Jacksonville synagogues and agencies in conducting special
educational programs and holiday celebrations. The JCA serves the Jewish
community by the free use of JCA facilities for all synagogues, the Federation, and
other Jewish organizations.
We have made changes and are continuing to make changes to better serve
the Jewish community and enhance the quality of life. A sliding fee scale is available to
make membership affordable to all. The doors are always open in our neighborhood -
please come in and join our JCA family - Shalom!

of the River Garden Auxiliary since it’s beginning in 1922 as the Ladies
Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society. Together with a combination of love,
dedication and hard work, our goal is to help enhance the lives of the residents
of River Garden Hebrew Home.
In order to accomplish our goal, we need the help of each and every
member of the Auxiliary. We also need many new members, not only to
support us financially, but also to find more willing hands to come and share

have come to realize that in our elders we have a precious and useful asset.

to receive the rewards of caring. Our “Helping Hands” make a real difference.

year olds, to Jacksonville.
•Spearheading advocacy efforts at the local, state and national levels
for programs and policies which address the six priority areas which include
women’s issues, children and youth, health and aging, constitutional rights,

Scouting
Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14, founded in 1919, carried on the scouting tradition at the Jacksonville Jewish Center in fine fashion,
enjoying a year of outdoor fun, adventure and activities in 1996-97.
This was a year of change for the leadership of Troop 14. Randy Silverberg,
who had been Scoutmaster since 1979, stepped down. He was recognized for his
many years of service with a special presentation made at the annual Blue and Gold
Banquet. Bob Fleet is the new Scoutmaster. Youth leadership was provided by Eli
Fleet, Senior Patrol Leader, and Justin Jackrel, Assistant Senior Patrol Leader.
Outdoor activities included campouts on the Tomoka and Suwanee Rivers,
where the scouts enjoyed canoeing and kayaking. At Ft. Clinch State Park, the scouts
hiked the 16 mile Ft. Clinch Historic Trail. On the campout at Blue Springs State
Park, the scouts enjoyed the manatees, took a winter swim in the spring, and had a
great time even though it rained. In the spring, the scouts camped at Hanna Park.
Troop 14 continues to be involved in the community. They served as ushers for Shimon Peres’s speech. They also
attended the Boy Scouts’ Distinguished Citizen Banquet, as the guests of Stanley Kantor.
Meeting programs included a tour of the Federal Courthouse, a presentation by two Navy S.E.A.L.s, working
with Rabbi Kentof on scout skills, and working on merit badges.
Two scouts, Justin Jackrel and Jared Fleet, were elected and inducted into the Order of the Arrow, an honor and
service organization of the Boy Scouts of America.
This summer, the scouts attended camp at Ten Mile River Scout Reservation in New York. This camp is the only
kosher Boy Scout Camp in the country. The scouts enjoyed mountain biking, rock climbing, rafting, and being at a
scout camp with other Jewish scouts from all over the country.

Rabbi Kentof’s Message
In the days of Moses, God commanded our forefathers,
“And thou shall teach them diligently unto thy children.
Dear Friends,
Inspired by that sacred charge,
every generation of Jewish parents
has sought to implant in the hearts of
their children, a love for knowledge,
enlightenment and tmth. At a time when
people about them were enslaved by
ignorance and superstition, our
forebears were accorded the noble title
Am Hasefer, “People of the Book. In
an era when learning and knowledge
were the guarded monopoly of the
privileged few, the descendants of the prophets and scribes
made access to education the inalienable right of all
the people — young and old, wealthy and poor, alike.
In periods when powerful nations worshipped brute
force and idolized physical might, our people remained
steadfast in their conviction that the Book must triumph
over the Sword.
In the spirit of continuing the
tradition of VDor V’Dor, “from
generation to generation”, we in the
Jacksonville Jewish Center
Supplemental Schools & Youth
Department are committed to
providing the best Jewish curriculum
and programming available. Our goal
is to inspire and teach your children,
leading them into a life of being
Jewish by choice, as well as by birth.
By utilizing creative program¬
ming and opening their eyes to new experiences, we hope
to create a wonderful memory bank that our students
will possess throughout their lives and, hopefully, pass
on to their own children and grandchildren.
The new educational year will bring many new
opportunities to reach you, your children and your
family — touching your lives and bringing you closer
together. We hope that you will have a renewed
commitment toward Jewish education and synagogue
involvement.
Rabbi Dov Kentof
Director of Education & Youth
Alina and I wish you and yours a Happy and
Sweet New Year.

JFCS is Here for You
Iris T. Young Executive Director
For 80 years, Jewish Family & Community Services has been here to
help Jacksonville’s Jewish families, because our community is not immune to
the world’s problems. We have poverty and hunger, social discord and divorce,
abusive spouses, unwed mothers, and a host of other frustrations.
We also have the ability to create families through adoption; heal families
through counseling; protect our seniors with outreach services; guide our young
toward meaningful lives; and wrap these efforts in the warmth of Jewish
understanding.
JFCS professionals can help clients deal with a variety of concerns, among
them:
• Personal Issues, ranging from abuse to panic disorders, co-dependency
to depression
• Marital Problems, for individuals or partners
• Divorce, helping clients sort through the emotional, financial, legal
and moral issues. Divorce Mediation and Divorce Custody Home
Studies are also offered.
• Family/Step-Family/Blended Family Issues, which take into
account the feelings of all family members. JFCS is a licensed child
placing (adoption) agency, licensed in Florida, New York and
Connecticut
• Parent-Child Conflict, resolving difficulties between generations
• Teen Behavior, addressing issues such as poor self-esteem, peer
pressure, respect for authority and self-awareness
• Children’s Behavior, working with the child who bites, the child
who says “no,” the child who throws tantrums
• Abuse/Neglect; Bereavement/Coping with Loss or Change
JFCS also operates the only two community kosher nutrition sites in
Jacksonville, at Mt. Carmel Gardens (Monday-Friday) and Etz Chaim
Synagogue (Wednesdays). Transportation to and from meal sites is available.
Services are offered on a sliding fee scale, and some evening appointments
are available. Emergency financial assistance and non-perishable food is also
provided to those in need. Client confidentiality is strictly maintained and protected
at all times. For more information about any JFCS service, call 448-1933.

Community Agencies
River Garden
HEBREW HOME/WOLFSON HEALTH & AGING CENTER
“Honor thy father and mother that thy days may be long... ”
It was this commandment which inspired the founding of River Garden
Hebrew Home in 1946. It was this commandment which nourished the love
and care that for more than fifty years has made River Garden a nationally
recognized model of excellence in its field.
The success of River Garden is based on the tradition of meeting 100
percent of local Jewish community needs and of serving rich and poor alike
with excellence. To be served, all you need do is call. The River Garden
Foundation accepts donations to help River Garden continue to meet its
mission.
River Garden’s programs of short-term rehabilitative/respite care,
long-term residential care, Alzheimers’ and Adult Day Care, serve over
180 people on any given day. These programs are guided by a devoted,
professional full time staff of over 200. Staff includes physicians and
beauticians; nurses, social workers, pharmacists, activities specialists;
physical occupational and speech therapists; dieticians, etc. In addition,
scores of dedicated volunteers help create a special caring community.
River Garden is located on a 33 acre Mandarin campus containing
lush Florida oak and pine trees, a private lake, meandering walking paths,
the perfect setting for residents and visitors. River Garden is a community
where people live, love and care; a community of many lifestyles and of
rich Jewish content, and a community which every day strives to live up to
the mandate of the Fifth Commandment.
River Garden
FOUNDATION
The River Garden Foundation is a philanthropic organization,
established to provide resources for programs and facilities to enhance the quality
of life for the aged. Its primary purpose is to fund the not-for-profit River Garden
system of services.
The foundation is dedicated to helping the elderly remain in control of
their own lives by assuring services to people at all levels of need and financial
ability.
The aim of the Foundation is to keep pace with the changing needs of the
aging through programs that meet a broad array of health care needs, as well as
provide a full spectrum of residential options.

Adorn has expanded its responsiblities to become the State
of Israel’s emergency medical, disaster, ambulance and
healthcare network. We built and equipped the MDA

throughout the world.

administer immediate help and then transport the patient
to the hospital.

Committees
Adult Education
“Adult education...it’s not just kid stuff!”
Expanding one’s knowledge in Judaism is more
important than almost any other of the 613
commandments. The Jacksonville Jewish Center is
a valuable source of adult education in the
community. Our members are challenged to broaden
their perspectives on many issues that face them each
day. Anyone who has attended one of Rabbi
Gaffney’s courses on Jewish ethics or Talmud study
is aware of how much satisfaction learning in a group
setting can provide.
To answer the diverse educational needs of our congregation, the Adult Education Committee coordinated
an ambitious program with a wide variety of courses and speakers throughout the year. Hebrew literacy, Talmud,
synagogue and holiday workshops, Jewish Kaleidoscope, Firing Line and other discussion groups formed the
basis of a comprehensive, on-going curriculum. The Setzer-Levit Memorial Lecture, Shabbaton, Missionary
Impossible and community-based engagements brought prominent speakers of national and international acclaim.
For many of us, these were once-in-a-lifetime opportunities to see and hear Jewish celebrities. Exciting plans to
enrich your spiritual life in the coming year are underway.
Shabbaton Weekend, underwritten by the Marilyn and Louis Safer Shabbaton
Fund, featured the dynamic Rabbi Lawrence Kushner, who spoke throughout the
weekend on “Where Heaven and Earth Meet: A Journey of Jewish Spiritual Renewal”.
Rabbi Kushner’s warm, animated and humorous style endeared him to those in
attendance. His views on modern spirituality were thought provoking, as well as
educational.
Malcolm Hoenlein, the executive vice president of the
Conference of Presidents of Major Jewish Organizations,
informed us about the “State of World Jewry” at two sessions
presented by the Setzer-Levit Memorial Lecture. ^
Workshops to help the Jewish community understand the dynamics of the Christian
campaign to convert Jews, and how to respond to these pressures, were presented by
Julius Ciss, the executive director of Jews for Judaism. He addressed students and
parents from Center schools and Beit Midrash in three informative sessions called
Missionary Impossible.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center was privileged to host an evening with the
former Prime Minister of the State of Israel, Shimon Peres. We are indebted to the
Jacksonville Jewish Federation, Mr. Jay Stein and Mr. Doron Nevo for bringing this
important world leader to our community. It was exciting for our youth, as well as
adults, to ask questions directly of Mr. Peres. The experience is one that will be
recorded in everyone’s memory book.

President’s Message
A longtime basketball coach, who
quit after 15 seasons, stated, “My job was
not to build the building, but it was to
repair the foundation.”
I felt privileged last year when I
was installed as president of the
synagogue because we have such a fine
physical plant in such a beautiful setting.
Unlike former presidents, the burden was
not on my shoulders to build the
synagogue; however, there were some
cracks in the foundation that needed immediate repair.
Looking back over this last year, I feel that we have
accomplished many of our goals. We implemented a
successful Patrons Campaign in which more than 65
families pledged $1,000 each to offset our deficit. We
had the most successful raffle in the Center’s history, the
proceeds of which benefit youth education. Our summer
camp program completed its most successful season. Life-
cycle events were celebrated here almost every weekend.
And this school year, we have the largest enrollment,
ever, in our day school grades K-6.
On Rosh Hashanah two years ago, when I spoke to
the congregation as Building Fund chairman, I discussed
how critical it was to the future of the congregation that
we reduce our mortgage. Just a few months ago, three
loyal families, the DuBows, the Selevans and the Setzers,
once again offered their support. Through their generosity
in buying the Center’s northside property, our mortgage
has been reduced to an amount that can be covered by
existing pledges that are due to be paid over the next three
years. At last, a debt-free congregation as we approach
our second 100 years!
Confronting the future challenges
of our congregation, I am mindful that
“One who is concerned with the needs of
others: preserves the past, creates a
present, and prepares the way for the
future.”
I am grateful for the dedication of
the Center’s officers and board,
professionals and administrators. Our
congregation is also blessed with many
members who do contribute time and
money, without prodding. This year we will again be
calling on you, as we embark on a million dollar campaign
for major physical improvements and security needed for
the building. When you are contacted, I hope you will
respond, “Hinneni- “Here I am.” Sisterhood will be
calling for your support with our new merchandise scrip
program, the V.I.R Club. When contacted, I hope you
will respond, “Hinneni” Our vital committees and
organizations will be asking you to join and volunteer. I
hope you will respond, “Hinneni”. The children of our
schools and youth groups will be asking for your help
and involvement. I hope you will respond, “Hinneni” -
“Here I am.”
As it is written, “It is not our job to complete the
task, but neither are we free to refrain from it.” I am not
asking anyone to give until it hurts. I am asking you to
give until it feels good. None of us can do it all, but all of
us can do it together.
On behalf on Arlene, Esther, Tania , I want to wish
you all a Happy and Healthy New Year! L’Shanah Tovah.

Scouting
Junior Girl Scout Troop 5
Junior Girl Scout Troop 5 had a fun and exciting
year. With money earned from cookie sales last spring, we
purchased 12 tents, and camped with Cadette Troop 969
at Ft. Wilderness. Besides increasing our outdoor skills,
patrols (teams of 6-8 girls) planned menus on a budget,
purchased food and prepared each meal for 30+ campers,
with enthusiasm and creativity!
The goal of our badge work was the “Sign of the
Satellite”. The girls interviewed women, performed
services for the Jacksonville Jewish Center and earned the
“Celebrating People”, “Skysearch” and “Ready for
Tomorrow” badges.
Laney Cohen and Rebecca Simon were selected for
the Council’s 6th grade event based on St. Augustine in
the 1700’s. They wore authentic outfits, which they helped
to sew and embroider, as they camped and served as tour
guides in the historic district.
Ten scouts earned the Bat Or, the Jewish religious
award for Junior Girl Scouts, thanks to mentors Beth
Shorstein, Wendy Brezin, Lauren Hochrad and Jill
Tager.
Thanks also to Amy Cohen — Badge Mom, and to
Helen Shacter — Cookie Mom and Assistant Leader.
Did you know there are over 100 girls involved in
scouting at the Center? Get involved — it’s great! For
information on a lot more than cookies and camping, call
Jan Lipsky, Leader, 268-7225.
Cadette Girl Scout Troop 969
This was the first year of Cadette Troop 969. We are
making history! Cadettes are in the 7th through 9th grades,
and work towards the second highest award in Girl
Scouting, the Silver Award. Five girls are off to a good
start, as they earned the “Dreams to Reality” patch —
investigating careers, completing job applications and
resumes.
The troop organized games and skits, and assumed
leadership roles when they camped with Junior Troop 5 at
Ft. Wilderness. Then they camped with other Cadette troops
in a Victorian era weekend at Camp Chowenwaw.
In partial completion of the Emergency Preparedness
interest patch, the scouts, with their parents, learned CPR.
They also toured a critical care unit at St. Vincent’s Medical
Center, and Fire Station #551.
The girls are working on the Menorah Award, the
religious award for Jewish Cadette Girl Scouts. Thank you
Robin Mtorris for being their mentor in this effort.
Additionally, the girls thank Cathy Cohen, Cookie
Mom, for organizing the cookie sale, and the Jacksonville
Jewish Center for giving us a great place to hold our
meetings.
For information on the Cadette level of Girl Scouting,
call Karen Southall, Leader, 292-9269, or Jan Lipsky,
Assistant Leader, 268-7225.

A funfilled evening with hostess Talie Bielski.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center provides
opportunities for Jewish singles from 21 to 35ish to
gather together for dinner and socializing a few times

two years, by some funding from the Jacksonville

beads are being used to embroider flowers and vines to
embellish a cream, silk fabric.

helped our chuppah evolve. Hopefully, the love and
devotion of this group will be felt by the couples who

chuppah.

Committees
Henry Kramer Library and Resource Center
The Henry Kramer Library and Resource Center contains a rich collection of
books of Jewish interest — Bible, prayer, Talmud, Holocaust, philosophy, literature,
Israel, poetry, humor and fiction. All are available for reading by members of our
congregation.
The Solomon Schechter Day School collection continues to grow, and much
needed bookcases were donated to hold all the volumes enjoyed by young readers.
The Melvin Trager Family donated a book cart in memory of Melvin Trager to
display new acquisitions.
Library volunteers are needed to assist the Library Committee in continuing
to guarantee the accessibility of the extensive collections of books. Committee
members devote hours of service processing and cataloging, filing and shelving
library materials.
Throughout the year, the Library Committee joins the Center Sisterhood for
special Shabbats and for book reviews. At those times, the library cart is available
for checking out selected books.
The Library is open Sundays from 8:30 to noon.

Membership
Kathy Osterer, VP/Membership
Rhoda Goldstein, Chair
The Center Membership Committee is committed
to extending friendship, recruiting, retaining and mostly
involving newcomers in the life of our congregation. This
year, alone, over 60 new families have joined our ranks.
To meet the needs of our multifaceted Center family,
the Membership Committee provides a wide variety of
services, such as Shabbat synagogue greeters, hospital
visitation, newcomer welcome-bag delivery, outreach to
singles, and arrangements for holiday home-hospitality.
Since a family that “plays together, stays together”,
the committee sponsors brunches, casual dinners, cocktail
parties and other social events throughout the year.
Bringing our members together is the Membership
Committee’s greatest pleasure, and we welcome your ideas
and participation. Every member of our synagogue is
automatically a member our committee. Each of you is an
ambassador of good will, not only to new members, but to
all members. Together we can make the Center truly “the
center of our life”.
Traditionally, public worship requires a minyan, a
quorum of ten. Only in the presence of a minyan may the
Torah be read or the Kaddish prayer be recited.
Our dedicated minyan captains ensure that there is
a minyan twice every single day of the year. It is often a
hard and thankless job. It requires hours of phoning to
coordinate attendance.
It is because of them that you can recite kaddish
when you mourn or observe yarhzeit. We owe them not
only our thanks, but also our help, to continue providing a
minyan. Please call any minyan captain and become a
“regular” for at least one minyan a week.

Message from Rabbi Gaffney
Israel’s 50th Anniversary
It may seem an odd question to
ask on the eve of Rosh Hashanah but -
“why are these High Holidays different
from all of the holidays of the past?”
Without having you go through
the entire Haggadah, I ask my readers
to consider the dedication of our
yearbook—the fiftieth anniversary, the
Jubilee celebration of the establishment
of the State of Israel.
For those old enough to
remember May 1948 . . . the
establishment of Israel ranks as one of
the significant events of Jewish history.
For those conversant with the
liturgy of our Siddur—“May our eyes
behold Your return to Zion” or
“L’shana ha’ba’ah”—“next year in
Jerusalem”, the events of fifty years ago
signal the realization of a century’s old
dream.
For those informed of Jewish
history, there is no question but that
Israel has taken the schmerz — the
pain out of Jewish living. Instead of the
grave marker that has characterized two
thousand years of our history, Israel has
given us a banner around which to
rally; a sense that being a Jew is a
proud privilege. For two thousand
years Jewish history was shaped by
others—by coercion, by subjugation,
oppression and exile. This helplessness
reached its most frightening crescendo
in the lifetime of many of us—the
death of six million of our brothers and
sisters, one and a half million of our
children in the Shoah of Europe, a little
more than fifty years ago.
The birth of Israel marked the
beginning of our redemption. We have
gotten off our knees, straightened our
collective shoulders stooped by the
burdens of the long night of exile and
have returned to our homeland and, as
we read in the birkhat haMazon, the
Grace after Meals, with our head erect.
For those who are religious, and
how can you not be filled with a sense
of God’s presence when you consider
the events of our lifetime, Israel has
reaffirmed our faith and rekindled our
belief in miracles.
Rabbi David and Inge Gaffney
When I read the special prayer,
as we celebrate Israel Independence
Day, I add the blessing —uSheh-asah
nissim Vavotenu bayammim ha’hem
ba-zman ha ’zeh”—“who has wrought
miracles for our ancestors and for us.”
Plain and simple...Israel is a miracle.
Israel is the miracle forged by a
Divine human partnership. It is the
consequence of a people who would
never forget its homeland in its holiday
prayers and in family celebrations.
Every wedding would conclude with
the broken glass reminding us of our
need to restore Zion. It was made
possible by the passion of Herzl and
the founders of Zionism as well as the
countless generations of people who
supported Israel with their modest
donations made in little blue tzedakah
boxes. It was the consequence of those
who were involved in the first aliyah
movements, who came from Russia
and Germany to wrest a nation out of
a desert; it was the displaced persons,
the D.Rs who boarded the Exodus and
similar vessels of refuge in order to
reach Israel. It was the fledgeling army
of the Haganah and, later, the Israel
Defense Force, the IDF; it was the
sacrifices of the youngest and brightest
of our people who gave their lives
along with the halutzim, the pioneers
and thekitbbutzniks. It was the answer
to the prayers of those who gathered
to offer their prayers at the kotel, the
WesternWall. Israel is the miracle of
those who still serve on borders on the
Golan and in the territories and in those
places of continuing vulnerability. It is
the result of all those who lived —uode
lo avda tikvatenu”—“we have not yet
lost our hope”.
That our people could achieve
the fulfillment of this most cherished
and longed for hope at the precise
moment when the hand of despair lay
most heavily upon it—this is an
achievement to convert the most
skeptical and most cynical of men into
passionate believers. The rebirth of
Israel fifty years ago is the realization
of the prophecies of Isaiah and an
affirmation of the truth that “not by
might nor by power alone, but by My
spirit, saith the Lord.”
We are the most blessed
generation in the last two thousand
year history of our people. All that our
grandparents and great grandparents
have prayed and yearned for has come
true in our time. Israel is reborn. So,
yes, these High Holy Days are different
from all others. This year we can sound
the shofar in keeping with the Biblical
mandate that on this 50th Jubilee year
“you shall proclaim liberty throughout
the land and to all of the inhabitants
thereof ”. This is the year that we can
say the words — ule-shana ha-ba-ah
b’yerushalayim” — “this coming year
in Jerusalem.”
One thing further—we need to
assure that our children share these
dreams. On a majestic night, four
thousand years ago, God made a
promise to Abraham that his children
would grow to be a great and mighty
nation. We are those children who have
been privileged to see that promise
fulfilled. We have experienced
homelessness and suffering—but we
have survived and persevered all of our
countless exiles. Our glory has been
reborn in the present and will continue
to glow in the future.
On this High Holiday, we have
every reason to recite the shehecheyanu
prayer, thanking God for sustaining us,
uplifting us and enabling us to reach
this joyous moment in our people’s
history.
Inge and I join in wishing all of
our friends a healthy and joyous New
Year.
Rabbi David Gaffney

Cantor Heller’s Message
Rosh Hashanah — the start of a
New Year — what an appropriate time
to make a new beginning! It is the time
when we reflect on our lives and thank
the Almighty for our blessings, ask
forgiveness for our sins, and pray that
He grant us a new year of health,
happiness and prosperity.
It is with those thoughts that I
approach my first Rosh Hashanah in
the United States of America, and more
importantly, at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. I have been blessed to realize
my aspirations of bringing me and my
wonderful family to this warm community. This is a
congregation that has a deep appreciation for Jewish
values and traditions, a congregation that places a
premium on teaching its children the importance of a
Jewish lifestyle.
The greatest challenge that faces Jewish parents
and educators can be summed up by these words of the
Shema: “... v’shinantam Vvanecha, v’dibartah bam...
Thou shalt teach them diligently unto thy children.”
Nothing is more gratifying to me
than to be part of the team teaching
the leaders of tomorrow — your
wonderful children.
Together, let us join in song
and prayer over the High
Holidays to set the foundation of
a spiritual new beginning for the
year ahead, as well as those to
come.
I look forward to meeting
many more members of this
unique congregation during the
months to follow. On behalf of me and my family, I
would like to take this opportunity to wish each and
everyone of you a Shanah Tovah Umetukah. May we
all be inscribed for a year blessed with good health,
happiness and prosperity.
Best wishes,
Cantor Aron Heller

Scouting
Cub Scout Pack 14
Cub Scouts is the phase of Boy Scouting that
includes boys from 2nd through 5th grades. The
purpose of this program is to develop good character,
and to encourage spiritual growth, good citizenship,
good sportsmanship, friendship with other boys, and a
sense of personal achievement.
Pack 14 has four very successful and rewarding
dens who met weekly under the guidance of volunteer
leaders. A special thanks is due Patricia Stacey Sail,
Stella Finck, Elissa Vining, Lori Schoettler, Stacey
Epstein, Marie Joseph and Deena Richman, this
year’s den leaders.
Once a month, the whole pack and their families
met for such activities as: Pinewood Derby Car Race
held at River Garden Home, Scout Shabbat, Blue and
Gold Banquet, and of course the Cubaree and end of
the year family campout. These were the times the boys
shared their den accomplishments, put on skits, hunted
for treasure, learned about knife safety, and were
awarded badges that they had earned.
The success of Cub Scouts depends entirely upon
adult support and involvement. We have been very
fortunate in this area. Scoutmaster Jim Richman gets
a special thank you from everyone involved. I would
personally like to say that working with such a nice
group of parent volunteers makes my job of Committee
Chairperson and Treasurer a very easy one.
GO — MIGHTY, MIGHTY PACK 14!
— Cindy Demri, Committee Chairperson

Want to support the Center without spending any more money ?

Through the Jacksonville Jewish Center’s merchandise scrip
program, the V. I.P. Club, Vested Interest Purchasing,
a portion of what you spend is given to the Center by the popular
merchants listed below. You simply order merchandise certificates
directly from the Center for each business you plan to patronize and
spend them as you would any other certificate. So every time you
shop, you contribute to the Center without spending any more money!
Great for all your regular shopping and gift giving, too. Imagine
buying cash certificates to so many different stores all in one place!
What a time saver.
The V.I.P. Club is one fundraising program in which everyone in
the congregation can participate. Synagogues all across the country
have instituted similar programs with great success. The program
taps into an existing vehicle for revenue — your purchasing power.
It allows the synagogue to benefit without any change in your
spending habits or in the places you shop. It simply requires you to
purchase certificates in advance, to spend when you like. Each
certificate is issued by the merchant, and is just like those you would
buy in their establishment. So you can give them as gifts or spend
them yourself, as usual. There are even mail-order and hotel
certificates available. Best of all, each time you order, the Center
gets a donation from the merchant, not from your pocket.

Monday by 2 p.m. at the Jacksonville Jewish Center (no phone
orders).Your order will be ready for you to pick up at the Center on

Executive Director’s Message
When the sage Hillel was
challenged to describe the teachings of the
Torah while staying balanced on one foot
he was able to define the essence of the
Torah in a concise answer : “Do not unto
thy fellow man what is hateful to thee -
the rest is commentary.”
If Hillel were asked a similar
question about defining the essence of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center he might
quote a verse from the book of Exodus :
“Let them make me a sanctuary, that I
may dwell among them.”
If you have any doubts that God
is dwelling among us you need only see some of the
miracles that happen everyday at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. Walk through the halls and hear the voices of
young children reciting Birkat Hamazon and you know
that God dwells among us. When you look down a hallway
and see our youth groups preparing a Mitzvah project for
the community you will know that God dwells among us.
Attend a Shabbat service at the Jacksonville Jewish Center
and you will know that God dwells among us.
These miracles happen on a daily basis at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center in large part because of our
dedicated staff. We are blessed to have the quality of
committed religious and education professionals, teachers,
administrative staff, catering department and maintenance
staff, all who work tirelessly on behalf of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center.
We are blessed with a magnificent
facility, worthy of God’s presence. We are
blessed with the finest group of lay
leaders, volunteers and generous
contributors who make everything we do
possible.
In the year ahead we will be adding
new programs, greeting new faces and
rising to new spiritual levels together as
a congregation. We will be challenging
our congregation to help meet the needs
of our physical building so that it too can
be maintained as a worthy dwelling place
for God and his people.
As we greet the New Year 5758 it is my hope
and prayer that each of you will join me in working
towards the goal of making this synagogue one where
God can continue to dwell among us. Come to me with
your ideas of new programs. Come to me with ideas about
how we can make our existing programs even better. Focus
on all the good that we do and think about how we could
do even better!
Let us continue to build a sanctuary where God
can dwell among us. The rest as Hillel says, is
commentary.
On behalf of Edith, Sam and Max, I wish you a
happy and healthy new year.
Bruce Horovitz

Purim — Haman Hoedown
Purim is not just for children! With Haman hanging from the rafters, and “Chag Sameach “replaced by “howdy”,
the adults of our congregation had a rollicking celebration, following the Megillah readings. Denims, bandanas, cowboy
hats and boots were derigueur. The chuck-wagon cuisine
included hot dogs and hamantaschen! Some of the most
unlikely “pardners” joined in the square dancing, line
dancing, and other western-style activities. A mini-carnival
kept younger children happy all evening. A foot-stompin’,
toe-tappin’ good time was had by all!
Special thanks to Ava Axelrod, Justin Axelrod, Gayle
Bailys, Shirley Bielski, Beverly & Daniel Eagerton, Marilyn
Garber, Rollaway Square Dance Club, Dana Zaifert of Party
City, and the Center catering and maintenance departments.
Yom Ha’Atzmaut — “Flight to Israel”
The Jacksonville Jewish community celebrated Israeli Independence Day with a special
“trip to Israel” courtesy of Rabbi Dov Kentof, Center volunteers and the JCA. The Center
was transformed for this fantastic fantasy which featured a mock El A1 flight to Ben
Gurion Airport. An exciting tour of the country included
simulated stops at a Bedouin tent, the Western Wall, a biblical
petting zoo, an archeological dig at Masada, a busy Tel Aviv
market place and even a real camel ride!. Authentic Israeli
food, a lively Klezmer band and dancing with a Hora group
from Miami were enjoyed by young and old. Over 800 parents
and children experienced this exceptional fun-filled,
community celebration.

Organizations
Center Men’s Club
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Men’s Club provides an excellent opportunity for the men of our congregation to
become involved in many enriching activities throughout the year. Ranging from synagogue service to community
action projects, religious and educational in nature to recreational and social, the Center Men’s Club has something
gratifying to offer all men and their families. The Men’s Club is just a portion of the total picture, but it is vitally
needed to help our synagogue realize its fullest potential in working toward Jewish values and practices.
The activities of the JJC Men’s Club have multiplied since its inception five
years ago. This past July, Randall Smith (Smitty), our past president, accepted the
coveted Gold Torch Award from the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs for the
New American B’nai Mitzvah Program. This project, the first of its kind in the
nation, gained international recognition. Credit goes to Ken Organes, project

About the Cover:
A special illustration was created and
rendered in colored art pencils by
Raymond W. Cohen, in honor of the 50th
Anniversary of the founding of the State
of Israel.
Notes from the artist:
“Nearly nineteen centuries passed
between the loss of Israel’s independence and
its declaration on May 14, 1948. The rebirth
of Israel is a golden moment in our people’s
history that will shine forever in our Jewish
memory. It was made possible by men and
women who dared to dream of, and
relentlessly fight for, the creation of the State
of Israel.
The cover illustration was inspired by
those people, from many lands and many
walks of life, whose skills, devotion,
experiences and talents made a dream into a
reality.”
