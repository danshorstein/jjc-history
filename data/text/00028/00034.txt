EXPLANATION OF THE COVER OF THIS YEAR BOOK
Every Jewish festival has a symbol or action which marks it as being
different from other holidays and certainly from the ordinary days. The
cover shows the different symbols for each month of the year, depicted
in wood cuts of I 8th century Holliand.
Rosh Hoshanah — September — the sounding of the Shofar as
a call to repentance and as a reminder that man always has
the opportunity to change himself, if he but wills to do so.
Sukkot — October — two men are carrying green branches with
which to cover the Sukkah, as commanded in the Book of Le¬
viticus. The agricultural festivals are a link with the Land of
Israel.
The Sabbath — November — the lovely ceremony pictured here
is that of the housewife blessing the light of the Sabbath lamp
with the traditional blessings.
Hanukah — December — the lighting of the Menorah. The use of
two Shamashim instead of one Shamash is unusual, but perhaps
the size of the Menorah made it necessary.
Havdalah — January — the traditional symbols: The goblet of wine,
the Havdalah candle and the spice box, escort the Queen
Sabbath.
Tu B'Shevat — February — portrayed by trees in full leaf with a
table laden with fresh fruits, a dream that in Eretz Yisrael mir¬
acles of nature can come true.
Purim — March — a joyous Holiday celebrated by three Purim
Spielers. In Israel today this is marked by a carnival.
Pesah — April — the search for leaven before Passover. The en¬
tire family is involved in getting rid of the Hometz.
Shavuot — May — Moses is seen going up to the top of the moun¬
tain to receive the Torah.
Weddings — June — a ceremony conducted under a Huppah
which is under the open sky.
Tisha B'Av — July — the destruction of the Temple is marked by
a tearful recitation of the Book of Lamentations.
Rosh Hodesh — August — blessing of the New Moon. Special
prayers are recited on the Sabbath preceding the new month.
