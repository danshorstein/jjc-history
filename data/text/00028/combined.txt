Whatever was our yesterday, today, as every day, we start anew.
Will we -- as individuals, as families, as nations -- meet problems today we have not yet
solved? Of course we will.
We can, therefore, without despair, without discouragement, set ourselves to any problem we
face today and tomorrow.
We need only realize that ours is not to complete the task, yet neither can we neglect it.
To know this gives each man more power than the atom, more fire than the stars.
And we can be sure of its daily truth, not only from life around us but from the Book of 0od,
wherein He made us a promise - - a covenant with the perpetual generations of Man - - that He
will never destroy the world or permit Man utterly to destroy himself.
We have this promise. Yet the decision to choose the good remains for each of us to make
anew each day. Indeed, each man is the earth's greatest promise.
You have the opportunity to fulfill this promise every day in each of the six different relation¬
ships given to Man:
1. In your relationship to yourself: Respect yourself for being made
in the image of Sod; and do what you can to fulfill the talents
you have been given.
2. In your relationship to your family: Realize that the greatest gift
you can give your family - far more than any other possession -
is yourself.
3. In your relationship to your community: Love your neighbor as
yourself.
4. In your relationship to the nation: Give your country your passion
for freedom and justice.
5. In your relationship to the world: Seek to unite the nations of the world
in brotherhood and peace.
6. In your relationship to God: "Do justly, and love mercy, and walk
humbly with thy God."

On behalf of the Administrative Staff we extend the entire membership and our many friends best wishes for a
Happy and Prosperous New Year.
$rief Scope of Nc tivities
Religious Services — Traditional prayer services are held daily, morn¬
ing and evening in the Chapel. Friday Family Services, Sabbath and
Holiday Services are observed in the Sanctuary throughout the entire
year.
Personal Religious Observances — Traditional Jewish customs are
observed in connection with Circumcision, Naming of Girls, Pidyon
Ha-ben, Bar Mitzvah, Bat Mitzvah, Confirmation, Marriages, Conver¬
sion to Judaism, Bereavement.
KENNETH BOWDEN
Controller
Education — Daily Kindergarten and Nursery with outstanding pro¬
gram of secular and religious content. Hebrew School, carefully graded
with intensive Hebrew content; Sunday School, with a curriculum in¬
cluding Jewish history and customs; Solomon Schechter Day School,
a graded secular and Hebrew School, 1st through 3rd grade; with Pre-
Jr., Junior, Teen and Confirmation Congregations and Minyan Clubs.
Extension Services — Library, outstanding collection of juvenile and
adult Jewish books; Adult Education, religious and cultural Jewish
courses; Music programs of religious and cultural Jewish music; Sis¬
terhood and Men’s Clubs; Social activities provided by Mr. and Mrs.
Club, Single Adults; Family Recreation Program (Olympic Pool),
summer weekdays and Sundays for all ages at our Campsite.
Youth Activities — ATID for college age youth; United Synagogue
Youth, various age levels are served by Center Youth League, Charles
Palot Club, Pre-U.S.Y.; Scouting (Boy Scouts, Cub Scouts); Girl
Scouts; Brownies; Athletics, programs for youth and adults; Center
Day Camp & nine weeks summer program at our 36 acre Campsite on
State Road No. 13.
Family Recreation Program — Center Campsite . . . Swimming,
athletics, snack bar, available for members during the summer months.

The Rabbi’s Page
Rabbi Jerome Lipnick has been the Rabbi of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center for the past three years.
The years have flown by swiftly, but we will remem¬
ber him for a long time to come.
The Rabbi and his wife, Joan, and their family
will be missed. May the years ahead bring them health,
happiness and fulfillment.
We are all looking forward to the future. With
this in mind, Rabbi Lipnick wrote the following pray¬
er which was read in our Synagogue when the Astro¬
nauts landed on the moon.
“Our God and God of our Fathers: Help us
to satisfy our curiosity with what we have rather
than with what we have not.
Teach us to be content with that which is
close to our hands, to work with, to shape and to
improve rather than that which is far, far away. . .
^ O Heavenly Father: We are grateful for Thy
wonders and Thy miracles, for the oceans and the
seas and the planets in the sky, and for this
earth. ..
God of all worlds and all ages and all men:
Teach us how not to fly away from the now, to
seek refuge in some other planet, and to direct
our skills, the finest of our minds, and the abund¬
ance of our materials to mysteries that repose
beyond.
Let us not be diverted from our proper course,
for the proper study of man is man, and there is
so much for us to understand and learn and per¬
form here on earth. . .
God of Revelation: What we do today re¬
veals how strong and mighty we are, how re-
RABBI JEROME LIPNICK
sourceful we are, how inventive we are, what
powers are ours, if we but apply ourselves and
concentrate our strength to explore the unknown
that is before our eyes, the unattained that is
here and now, in this place, in this city and on
this planet. . .
Let there be food, dignity, peace, learning,
health and joy for all, for those we know and
those we do not know, the stranger and the home-
born. . .
O Heavenly Father, let us not be enticed,
let us not be lured, let us not be diverted away
from the present challenge, the present respon¬
sibility, the present obligation to transform our
world into a kingdom of God.
Teach us how to come down to earth and
there to make our landing for food, for peace, for
dignity, for learning, for health, and for joy.”
AMEN

Message from the President
Behold, in deep humility
I stand and plead before Thee, God on high;
Great Lord who art enthroned above all praise,
O hearken and give heed unto my prayer.
Though unworthy of my sacred task,
Though imperfect, too, and filled with awe,
/ before Thy holy presence here,
To supplicate Thy pardon and Thy grace,
To as£ Thy mercy, Thy continued love
Though unworthy of my mission, Lord,
Though I stand not flawless in Thy sight,
Condemn Thou not my people for my faults,
Consider but their virtues, Righteous Judge.
Forgive us our iniquities,
A/id turn Thou our afflictions unto joy.
Thou great, exalted God who hearest prayer,
Hoar ozzrs, and bless us all with life and peace.
NATHAN FREIDLIN, President
PHILIP SELBER
Chairman of the Board
The above prayer is from our High Ploliday Prayer Book and is known
as the “Hineni”. I remember reading this prayer aloud, to those assembled
at my election as your President, nearly two years ago. Its lines stirred me
very deeply then. They are even more meaningful and relevant to me today.
Standing on the threshold of a new year, each and every one of us
should read this prayer as if each of us was its author. For each of us is in¬
deed praying for “Kol Yisrael.” Each of us is not only our “brother’s
keeper,” we are also our brother’s brother. Therefore, let us all work to¬
gether to build a better congregation, a better community, and a better
world.
May the Holy One, blessed be He, inscribe and seal for each of us in
the book of life a year of health of body, a year of enlightenment of the
mind, a year of hope and joy and above all a year of peace.

In Memory of
BENJAMIN SETZER
We Dedicate This Year Book to the late Benjamin Setzer, of
Blessed Memory who passed away in March of this year.
“Perhaps as a portent of things to come was
the scene last Yom Kippur when the Congregation,
as it gathered for the concluding Neilah service,
realized that Ben Setzer, who for years had always
opened the Ark, was not present. The incident,
although temporary, no doubt caused many of the
congregants to ponder what might be the situation
if Ben Setzer were not there to open the Ark. The
short reflections of the moment ended as he en¬
tered the synagogue slightly late to fulfill his long
standing Mitzvah.
But now the Congregation must reflect on
the certainty that Ben Setzer will not in person
ever again open the Ark for Neilah. At this mom¬
ent, however, it is more important to reflect on
the thought of where the Congregation and Jewish
life in this community would now be, had Ben
Setzer not been present for so many years To open
the Ark’ for Jewish living in our community.
The histories of institutions reveal that their
destinies are usually the result of the mingling of
circumstances and men. Where the circumstances
of the moment find the men capable of the proper
response, institutions develop and prosper. Fortu¬
nate indeed was the Jacksonville Jewish Center
that when the need for the post war expansion of
its facilities and programs bore down so heavily
on it, there was a man like Ben Setzer who was
ready to respond. It was also the good fortune of
the congregation that such a man was so whole¬
heartedly supported in his efforts by his beloved
wife and children.
His foresight in offering the initial substan¬
tial gifts, his maintenance of a continuous flow of
substantial financial contributions over a period
of more than twenty-five years and above all, his
counsel and determination made it possible for the
synagogue to have had available in the ’50s and
’60s the building facilities so desperately needed
then for the religious education and development
of the greatly increased numbers of congregants
and children.
His contributions were not restricted to the
Building Fund for he was also the prime dues con¬
tributor to the annual operational fund of the Cen¬
ter as well as the main financial supporter of the
Foundation School.
His zeal and enthusiasm in leading off the
first campaign for the post-war expansion of the
Center building facilities are part of the well
known history of the congregation, but what may
not be well known was his leadership in supplying
the drive and in instilling the confidence to pro¬
ceed even when all of the financial resources were
not on hand. What was ‘on hand’, however, was
Ben Setzer’s familiar urging that ‘we proceed and
the congregation will respond/
Ben Setzer never looked backwards — his
eyes were always to the future. It is rather in¬
teresting that he carried into his Synagogue
leadership the same qualities which made him so
successful in business. Just as he had built a sec¬
ond successful business after creating and dispos¬
ing of one, so too was he looking forward to the
task of constructing the new synagogue plant on
State Road 13. No one was more enthusiastic than
he as the efforts of the synagogue went forward
in concretizing the plans for the new structure.
The Neilah service on Yom Kippur will never
be the same at the Jacksonville Jewish Center be¬
cause Ben Setzer will not be present to open the
Ark, but his spirit will always be present. His con¬
tributions of leadership, his resolve to perpetuate
Judaism, his determination and drive shall always
be a part of the heritage of the synagogue he loved
so well.
This is why each year as the Ark is opened
for the Neilah service, the worshippers will remem¬
ber Ben Setzer as they reflect on his life and his
contributions.”
From The Center Bulletin

EXPLANATION OF THE COVER OF THIS YEAR BOOK
Every Jewish festival has a symbol or action which marks it as being
different from other holidays and certainly from the ordinary days. The
cover shows the different symbols for each month of the year, depicted
in wood cuts of I 8th century Holliand.
Rosh Hoshanah — September — the sounding of the Shofar as
a call to repentance and as a reminder that man always has
the opportunity to change himself, if he but wills to do so.
Sukkot — October — two men are carrying green branches with
which to cover the Sukkah, as commanded in the Book of Le¬
viticus. The agricultural festivals are a link with the Land of
Israel.
The Sabbath — November — the lovely ceremony pictured here
is that of the housewife blessing the light of the Sabbath lamp
with the traditional blessings.
Hanukah — December — the lighting of the Menorah. The use of
two Shamashim instead of one Shamash is unusual, but perhaps
the size of the Menorah made it necessary.
Havdalah — January — the traditional symbols: The goblet of wine,
the Havdalah candle and the spice box, escort the Queen
Sabbath.
Tu B'Shevat — February — portrayed by trees in full leaf with a
table laden with fresh fruits, a dream that in Eretz Yisrael mir¬
acles of nature can come true.
Purim — March — a joyous Holiday celebrated by three Purim
Spielers. In Israel today this is marked by a carnival.
Pesah — April — the search for leaven before Passover. The en¬
tire family is involved in getting rid of the Hometz.
Shavuot — May — Moses is seen going up to the top of the moun¬
tain to receive the Torah.
Weddings — June — a ceremony conducted under a Huppah
which is under the open sky.
Tisha B'Av — July — the destruction of the Temple is marked by
a tearful recitation of the Book of Lamentations.
Rosh Hodesh — August — blessing of the New Moon. Special
prayers are recited on the Sabbath preceding the new month.

Our Sisterhood is one of the 800 which make up
the National Women's League. Over 200,000 women
throughout the United States, Canada and Puerto
Rico, striving toward the same goals, have compiled
a . . .
PERSONAL PROGRAM FOR THE
MODERN JEWISH WOMAN
• To help you raise a family knowing its heritage
and taking pride in it.
• Bring beautiful and meaningful Jewish ceremonials
into your home.
• Give you opportunities for further study of Judaism
and its history, art, literature, music.
• Guide you to intelligent participation in the affairs
of your Synagogue and community.
° Inspire you to action on the vital local, national and
international issues of our times in accordance with
the ethics of Judaism.
• Strengthen your bond of kinship with the Jewish
people everywhere.
• Instill renewed faith and courage in moments of
doubt and despair.
It is this program that influences our Center Sis¬
terhood's activities.
Adult Education, celebration of Jewish Book
Month, Holiday Institutes and observance, Social Ac¬
tion and stimulating monthly programs are all part of
this goal.
Cur participation with Girl Scouts, Camp Ramah,
Center Youth Clubs, Religious School, Kindergarten
and Foundation School has been educational, (Holi¬
day explanation and observance, Purim and Hanukah
celebrations, Confirmation bibles, Sunday School
awards) and financial (salaries for Sunday School
teaciiers, purchase of up-to-date teaching aids). Per¬
sonal commitment by the parent has perhaps the
greatest effect in giving the child a positive Jewish
feeling.
Our Gift Shop has been steadily progressing to¬
wards bringing beautiful Jewish objects into the home.

items can be unique and meaningful gifts.
Each program on our busy Sisterhood calendar
keeps in mind the modern Jewish woman. To insure
the greatest participation, our Coordinating Vice-
President will organize teams of our membership to
plan and execute each monthly meeting. Each mem¬
ber will be able to use her talents and ideas.

The Men’s Club pledges a 1969-70 season as suc¬
cessful as the season we have just completed. Once
again we plan to have a full well rounded schedule of
programs.
This past year our members were treated with an
election program which featured a speaker for each of
the three major candidates debating the election from
the standpoint of Jewish ethics and morality; Rabbi
Yaacov Hilsenrath, speaking on the disturbing issue
of modern American youth and a delightful program
for sports fans featuring two of the A.F.L. players
here for the All Star Game in January. In addition we
played host to Etz Chaim’s Men’s Club and the Tem¬
ple Brotherhood, a program which was highlighted by
Mr. Kivie Kaplan, the National President of the Na¬
tional Association for the Advancement of Colored
People.
As always, the major concern of the Men’s Club
has been the youth of the Center. The Club once
again sponsored the Sunday School Picnic which not
only gave a wonderful time to the parents and chil¬
dren who attended but earned extra funds for the
Committee headed by Dr. Ronald Levin. This Com¬
mittee, an integral part of the Men’s Club, provided
funds for and supervised Air Conditioning and Heat¬
ing, enlarged sanitary facilities, etc., in the White
House at the Campsite. In addition the interior had
a complete paint job. These improvements will make
the House more useable for the Campers and the vari¬
ous youth organizations who may desire to use it.
Our plans for the coming season call for an even
better selection of programs and an enlarged group of
projects to help make the Jewish Center a more dy¬
namic force.

Our standards have remained high as shown by the many real
accomplishments the school has been able to achieve during the past
year. Three of our graduates are in colleges of Jewish studies. The
maximum Bar Mitzah goals of commitment to Hebrew High School,
Torah reading, and service leadership were attained by a rising per¬
centage of students. Our synagogue-affiliated clubs have repeatedly
demonstrated their national leadership. Fourteen pupils increased their
sensitivity to complete Jewish living in the atmosphere of Ramah
camps.
Giant steps have been taken for the advancement of our primary
educational system. Leading authorities were brought in to analyze
and systematize the elementary departments, at great expense.
With educational needs as the primary goal for fund raising ac¬
tivities of our Sisterhood, the school was provided with the latest de¬
vices in audio-visual equipment which will help us to pay greater
attention to individual problems. We can learn more from the theory
of general education than from its application, and the steps taken
during the past year will help us to attain even higher standards.
Our special thanks are due to the many laymen who gave so de¬
votedly of their free time in every aspect needed to maintain such a
wide variety of activities. We thank the chairmen of each of the com¬
mittees as well as the people who replaced the professional teachers
we could not always obtain.

strip on a subject that would interest the boys. Films
on undersea geology, first aid, nature, and Walt Dis¬
ney were shown. Movie night was a great favorite and
was enjoyed by all.

in coordination with Rabbi Jerome Lipnick, launched on eight week series
of discussions on “Moral Issues of Our Time”, and featured outstanding

SCENE AT CENTER CEMETERY
Selection Of Memorial Properity
Family ownership of memorial property simply makes good sense. The Center has plenty of family lots available and it
is not likely that the cost will decrease. As a matter of fact, with the increasing costs of maintenance, prices will likely increase.
Those of us who have seen a cemetery lot selected at a time of immediate need, perhaps by the widow, or by a son, daugh¬
ter, know that this is the worst possible time. There are too many other items demanding attention and it is difficult to give
the matter proper consideration. A cemetery lot should not be selected hastily. Since fate makes no appointments, the best time
to purchase family memorial space is when you DON'T need it.
Visit our Memorial Park on 40th Street east of Main Street. Look around. We take pride in the fact that the property is
well tended. Then, at your convenience, call the Center and arrangements can be made to meet and proceed with the space se¬
lection under ideal conditions.

Serving the entire Jewish community of Jackson¬
ville, the Jewish Community Council is the central
Welfare Fund and Community Relations body of the
Jewish community. An association of individuals,
synagogues, organizations and social service agencies,
the program encompasses key activities which give
unity, cohesion and coordination to local Jewish com¬
munal life.
A community-wide annual Welfare Fund Cam¬
paign provides support to significant local, national
and overseas organizations. The National United Jew¬
ish Appeal has expressed American Jewry's respon¬
sibility for the welfare and rehabilitation needs of
Jewish communities the world over for over 20 years.
River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged and the
Jewish Family and Children's Services are the local
beneficiaries.
The Council publishes a monthly newspaper known
as the Commentator. It is the official organ of the
Council and its member organizations.
The Council conducts a leisure time activities
program which includes Youth Council programs such
as college preparatory workshops, a youth news page
in the Commentator, and a youth division in the
annual Jewish Welfare Fund Campaign. The Council
also provides a community summer Day Camp for
children between the ages of 4 and 15.

Founded in 1917, the JFCS has been helping the
members of the Jewish community face and overcome
the personal problems that remain constantly with us.
Some years ago we became aware that, suddenly or
slowly, subtle or startling shifts occur in the kinds of
problems that we work with under our umbrella of
services.
The shifts this year were more slow and subtle.
Gradually, we become aware that although our com¬
parative record remains fairly good, that the few cases
involving ‘conflict with law’ had reached a number
and a regularity that it had to be counted separately
instead of being included under Others. We had been
aware for some time of the sharp shift to ‘acting out’
rather than the more passive forms in these conflicts
and in other areas of our work. We have tried to help

What is the framework of the Z.O.A. program?
What is its ideological foundation?
This organization functions in three distinct areas
— in the United States, in Israel, and on the Interna¬
tional Scene.
In this country Z.O.A. activities center around:
Public Affairs - our constant and unremitting
struggle to safeguard Israel’s security and independ¬
ence, and our involvement in general Jewish com¬
munity life;
Zionist Adult - Education through culture pro¬
grams, dissemination of Hebrew, periodicals, publica¬
tions, speakers, visual aids, etc.;
Our expanding Youth Programs, include the
campus and Z.O.A. summer programs in Israel;
The promotion and development of Aliya.
Other Z.O.A. endeavors such as membership
drives, publicity, fund raising, are not programmatic
goals in themselves but rather tools for creating the
means to carry our programs forward.
In Israel there are the three large and growing
Z.O.A. projects:
The Kfar Silver Agricultural High School, the
Mollie Goodman Academic High School, and last but
not least, the Z.O.A. House in Tel-Aviv.
On the international scene is our unique World
Zionist Affairs effort, our participation in internation¬
al bodies like the World Zionist Organization, the
World Jewish Congress, the World Union of General
Zionists, and the UN program.
Essentially, the Z.O.A. is an ideological movement
of a General Zionist orientation. Its philosophy is
rooted in the old but ever new concepts of Jewish
