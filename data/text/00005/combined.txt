“True conservatism is substantial progress; it holds
fast what is true and good in order to advance in both.
—To cast away the old is not of necessity to obtain
the new.—To reject anything that is valuable, lessens I 1 n
the power of gaining more. That a thing is new does
not of course commend; that it is old does not dis¬
credit/'—Tyron Edwards.
Nothing is more apt in describing Conservative Judaism
than the above excerpt. The Jacksonville Jewish Center for
the past two decades has constantly striven to maintain
in Jacksonville a type of Judaism that would embody all
the tradition, principle and richness of the old orthodoxy
and at the same time to render it appealing and interesting
to the modern generation.
Many changes have already been made in the tradi¬
tional orthodoxy wh ich h ave given it strength without
sapping its virility. English readings and sermons have been
added . . . prayer books have been made uniform . . :
separate pews for men and women have been abolished.
But in all of the se changes, nothing has been taken or
deleted which might render our religion less potent. In
fact most of these changes have been in the nature of ad¬
ditions and improvements rather than omissions.
The Center has conserved as much of the old orthdoxy
as possible. Prayers are still read in the traditional and
ancient Hebrew language . . . the daily Hebrew School
has been enlarged and constantly improved . . . youth
facilities have been increased . . . the traditional funeral
and burial practices have been retained. . .a daily minyon
has been maintained . . . dietary laws have been strictly
adhered to. It would be possible to go on at great lengths
in detailing those principles and tenants of Orthodox Ju¬
daism that h ave been conserved.
However, many members of our institution have as¬
sumed that other practices and customs were also such an
integral part ol orthodoxy that any change to them would
be a desecration o I their conception of Orthodoxy Judaism.

By ABE NEWMAN
President of the Jacksonville Jewish Center
It is but natural that at the advent of a New Year,
we take note of conditions around and about us. Tbe dark
clouds of hunger, despair and bitter persecution that hovered
over millions of our people have only been lifted for those
who are no longer among the living, and the only silver
lining beckoning to us is the fact that the problem con¬
fronting the Jew has become the problem that confronts
all mankind.
Outstanding among the events ol an outstanding
year in the world s history, was the conference held by
President Roosevelt and Winston Churchill at which were
announced certain principles that gave new hope to the
downtrodden masses of mankind. 1 hey were based upon
the fact that all people are entitled to life and to liberty
and it is my hope that the time is not far distant when
they will be a fact instead of a hope.
The world is composed of many individuals and the
actions and attitudes of many of them determine the kind
of world we are going to live in. It is our duty as individuals
to start with ourselves and by our own deeds and actions
help to build the kind of world we would like for it to be.
Locally we have had a fairly successful year. During the
pas't year, we inaugurated the plan of trying to maintain
our entire budget by dues. We had two objectives in mind,
the first of which was the elimination of a certain type of
Page Twenty-six
fund raising that was not only objectionable to many
people but also detracted from the dignity of our services.
Secondly and more important is the fact that it will give
us more time to devote to the constructive work for which
the institution stands, and it is my hope that we will be able
to contact the remainder of our members very shortly and
put this plan into effect.
Most oustanding ol the problems yet lacing us as
we enter the New Year is the problem of Jewish education
in this community. It is one of the most serious problems
facing Jacksonville Jewry. If the parents would realize
how much a Jewish education and Jewish background
would mean to their children they would cooperate with
us more fully in our effort to increase our educational work.
Let us enter the New Year fully resolved to carry out
the tasks which we have undertaken through the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center. Let us pray that God will bless the
work of our hands and that we shall enjoy a year of good
tidings, of peace and harmony in our midst, of prosperity
and happiness.
I want to thank the Rabbi and the other members of
the staff, the fellow officers and directors for the assistance
that has been given me during this past year, and wish
them and all our members a Happy and Prosperous New
Year.

AND ITS PROBLEMS
Four times a week in the afternoon and five times a
week when sessions can be held in the morning, from eighty
to a hundred children enter the doors of the Center Talmud
I orah and apply themselves to the study of the Hebrew
Language, the Prayer Book and the Bible in its original.
Those who complete the six-year course provided by
our curriculum emerge with more than the mere a bility to
read and translate Ivre . They acquire a sense of reverence
for the Jewish religion, a deep interest in the ideals and
aspirations of the Jewish people and the ability to partici¬
pate intelligently in the observance of the Festivals, customs
and ceremonies, both in the synagogue and at home.
This modern Hebrew School maintained by the Center
is the only one in the city. It is staffed by a group of com¬
petent teachers composed of Rabbi M. D. Margolis as
principal, Mr. Joseph Robin, Mrs. Zvi Abrams and Mr.
Morris Nimovitz.
Like every other educational enterprise the Center
School faces its problems. Two of them are sufficiently
important to deserve mention. A large number of people
start their children in Hebrew School when it is too late.
Instead of enrolling them at the age of seven or eight, they
wait until the time is close to Bar Mitzvah. This procedure
tends to defeat the entire aim of the schoo I. Our B oard
of Education has ruled that a child must have a minimum
of two years instruction before he may be prepared for
the B ar Mitzah ceremony. Even this is insufficient to make
Judaism meaningful to our children for they must receive a
thorough training which will provide them with a rich
background. Only such training can instill in the child
loyalty to his religion and a sense of dignity and self-respect
as an American Jew.
The second problem which must be met consists of
the fact that many homes lack the proper Jewish atmosphere
necessary to complement the instruction received by the
child in Hebrew School. I his produces confusion in the
child s mind. He begins to wonder whether the Jewish
festivals, customs and ceremonies are merely something to
be learned about for the sake of mental exercise, seeing
that his parents do not practice them. It is hoped that the
parents will seriously consider these problems and cooperate
fully with the teachers of our Hebrew School in overcoming
these obstacles.
During the past two years, the work of our J almud
Torah began to bear good fruit. For the first time in its
history, the J almud J orah was able to institute graduation
for those pupils who have completed the required courses
of its curriculum. Six pupils were graduated in 1940 and
five in 1941. These eleven graduates are a splendid example
of the results that can be obtained by the proper coopera¬
tion of parent and teacher.
Alumni Association of Hebrew School
Last June the graduates of the Center 1 almud 1 orah
formed the first Alumni Association of the Hebrew School
wi th Sonya Heyman as its first president. We hope that
the ranks of this organization will increase from year to
year and that it will eventually become a strong factor in
the life of our communitv.

Local Drive Reaches $55,000 Total
To Set Standard For the Country
DAVID H. LASAROW
President
The Jacksonville Jewish Commun¬
ity Council came in to its own during
the past year as one of the outstand¬
ing philanthropic and welfare agen¬
cies in the entire country.
The Council set the pace for the
entire country when it more 'than
doubled its 1940 campaign total by
reaching the total o f Fifty-five Thous¬
and Dollars ($55,000.00).
Although the tremendously increased
overseas and National needs were in
some part responsible for this amaz¬
ing increase, the large share of credit
must: go to the men who directed this
year s campaign. D avid Lasarow, who
assumed the presidency of the Coun¬
cil in December 1940, proved to the
community his dynamic ability as a
leader, and too much credit cannot
be given him for the community’s
outstanding achievement. Aiding Mr.
Lasarow, and of equal importance to
the success of the drive, were AI ex-
ander Brest:, Chairman of the Cam¬
paign and Harold Cohn, co-chair¬
man.
In addition to increased allocations
for overseas relief work and National
Jewish Defense, several new local ag¬
encies were added to the budget. An
allocation of Four Thousand Dollars
was set aside for the local Public Re¬
lations Program to be administered by
a sub-committee of the Council. An¬
other allocation of Two Hundred and
Fifty Dollars was made to the local
U. S. O. Campaign.
As a result of the highly succes sful
local campaign, the United Jewish
Appeal received $50.000.00 as com¬
pared to the $13,500.00 that it re¬
ceived last year. The Council also
set up for the first time a local wel¬
fare fund for the specific purpose of
child care, thus relieving the Atlanta
Hebrew Orphan s Home of responsi¬
bility locally.
At the conclusion ol the drive, honor
plaques were awarded to David Las¬
arow, Alex Brest, Herbert Meyerson,
and I. M. Lieberman, for their out¬
standing service and devotion to the
cause of the Community Council of
Jacksonville.
Much credit for the success of
the Initial Gifts Campaign can be
atributed to the cooperation and dili¬
gent work of the co-chairman, Sam¬
uel Kip nis and Colonel R. L. Seit-
ner. Mrs. Joseph Hackel, as Chair¬
man of the Women s Division, and
Mrs. D. L. Lasarow, as co-chairman,
rendered invaluable service both dur¬
ing the campaign and throughout the
year, especially in the collection ac¬
tivities of the Council. Keeping apace
with the work of the senior divisions
was the enthusiastic Youth Commit¬
tee, which was headed for the second
successive year by Dr. Carl Hackel.
In a recent letter to all contributors
to the Council Drive, David Lasarow,
President, made this significant state¬
ment:
‘In the midst of so much despair
and suffering it is truly encouraging
to know that things can be done, are
being done and can continue to be
done to bring help and life to large
numbers of our people in the war
zone in the European Countries and
Palestine, and even on our own
shores. ’
“When we receive the urgent ap¬
peal for additional help, somehow
nothing is gained by assuring the ref¬
ugees that your pledge is good, but
that they will have to wait a little
longer. As you know the local Com¬
munity Council depends only upon
voluntary workers for its collections.
Won t you please do your best to
make their task lighter by mailing your
check immediately?
“The people who need your help
cannot wait. It is purely and simply
a question of doing everything you
can to help as soon as you can.

During the year, the girls were also active in Arts and Crafts work and
sewing. Ash trays were modeled out ol pine and necklesses were made of
paper straws.

became second class Girl Scouts. All of the girls are prepared to continue their
fine work during the coming year.

1) Rosh Hashonah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and lasts
two days. It is also the beginning of the 10 days of Penitence, which end with
Yom Kippur. Sincere prayer and meditation during these days help us to ex
perience the reality of God and to dedicate ourselves anew to the establishment of
His Kingdom on earth—the reign of freedom, justice and peace.
2) Shabbat Shuba derives its name from the Haftorah of that day. Hosea
xiv, 2-10 which begins with the word Shubah^Return’, i.e., Repent.
3) Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when
we strive to rewaken the latent spiritual power in our souls so that it may become
an effective and directing force in our daily conduct.
4) Sukkos (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of thanksgiving
after all the products of the field have been gathered in. The sukkah symbolizes
the wanderings of our ancestors in the wilderness of Sinai.
5) Simchath Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of Shemini
Azereth. Then the reading of the Pentateuch is completed and begun anew for the
coming year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos—procession with the scrolls
of the Torah to the accompaniment' of songs and dancing.
6) Rosh Chodesh is the term for the beginning of a new month. As the
Jewish year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coin¬
cides with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered
the first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodesh of the month following

THE KADDISH
Its origin is mysterious; angels
are said to have brought it down
from heaven and taught it to men.
About this prayer the tenderest threads
of filial feeling and human recollection
are entwined; for it is the prayer of
the orphans! When the father or
mother dies, the surviving sons are to
recite it twice daily, morning and
evening, throughout the year of
mourning, and then also on each re¬
curring anniversary of the death—on
the Yahrzeit.
It possesses wonderful power.
Truly, if there is any bond strong
and indissoluble enough to chain
heaven to earth, it is this prayer. It
keeps the living together, and forms
the bridge to the mysterious realm
of the death. One might also say
that this prayer is the watchman and
the guardian of the people by whom
alone it is uttered; therein lies the war¬
rant of its continuance. Can a people
disappear and be annihilated so long
as a c hild remembers its parents? It
may sound strange: in the midst of
the wildest dissipation has this prayer
recalled to his better self many a dis¬
solute character, so that he has
bethought himself and for a time at
least purified himself by honoring the
memory of his parents.
Because this prayer is a resurrec¬
tion in the spirit of the perishable in
man, because it does not acknowledge
death, because it permits the blos¬
som which, withered, has fallen from
the tree of mankind to flower and
develop again in the human heart,
therefore it possesses sanctifying
power.
To know that when thou diest,
the earth falling on thy head will not
cover thee entirely; to know that there
remain behind, those who, wherever
they-be on this wide earth, whether
they may he poor or rich, will send
this prayer after thee; to know that
thou Ieavest them no house, no es¬
tate, no field by which they must re¬
member thee, and that yet they will
cherish thy memory as their dearest
inheritance — what more satisfying
knowledge canst thou ever hope for?
And such is the knowledge bequeathed
to us all by the Kaddish.
L. Kompert.

A valuable addition to the Center-affiliated organizations is the Jewish
War Veterans and its Ladies’ Auxiliary.
Organized three years ago, the Post has been active in promoting patriotism
and the ideals of democracy and has been instrumental in dispersing prejudice
against Jews through a campaign of information acquainting non-Jews with
the part played by the Jewish soldiers in the first and second World War.
Members of the post meet twice a month on the second floor of the Center
rear wing. Throughout the year, members of the post pay good will visits to
local neighboring hospitals and distribute gifts and magazines to their Jewish
and non-Jewish patients. On national holidays, the Post participates in parades
and other patriotic manifestations.
A highlight of the past year was the visit of the National Commander
Harris who met with members and friends of the organization in a local hoteh
and spoke on the work of the National organization.
The Ladies’ Auxiliary of the Jewish War Veterans meets on the same
nights as the post and assists its members in all its undertakings.

By MORRIS D. MARGOLIS
Rabbi of Jacksonville Jewish Center
As the year 5701 is drawing to a close, the democratic
world and the Jewish people in particular, have good
reason to look more hopefully into the future.
During the past two years a monstrous and brutal war
machine has ro lied across more than a dozen countries
destroying everything in its path. It registered victory after
victory and it seemed that its power was inexhaustable. An
era of darkness and slavery faced the world, which accord¬
ing to one boast, was to last a thousand years.
The situation in Palestine was particularly grave. Alter
the conquest of the Balkans and the invasion of Crete it
was considered a foregone conclusion that Syria and Pal¬
estine would fall within a short time. Dark fears settled
in the hearts of our people regarding the fate of the half
million Jews in the Holy Land and the hundreds of Jewish
settlements, colonies and cities built there with so much
blood, sweat and tears. The enemies of Zion and the pro¬
fessional gloom mongers in our midst found a morbid satis¬
faction in predicting the catastrophic results which were
to iollow.
Within a short time, however, a miraculous change
took place. New hope welled up in the hearts of men. Even
those already subjugated by terror and tyranny dared to
raise their heads. The dark clouds which threatened Pales¬
tine Jewry brightened considerably.
WLat has happened is familiar history to our people.
Over-confidence in its own strength, an insatiable desire
for more and more conquests, unwillingness to tolerate the
existence of any other power — these have brought ruin
to military nations of the past. These blind passions are
also sure to bring the downfall of the despotic conquerers
of our day. The road to disaster is clearly marked for those
who would dominate the world by the power of the sword.
The Jew, whose memory is long, and who has witnessed
the rise and fall of many tyrants, cannot but have complete
confidence in the defeat of the enemies o f God and man.
With the Psalmist, he reafirms his faith in the supremacy
of the spirit. Some trust in war chariots, some in horses
but we shall put our trust in the name o f the Lord. The
conviction is growing in the hearts of man that righteousness
and justice, kindness and decency shall prevail; that evil
shall be destroyed, that a just and peaceful society shall
ultimately be established, based on the ethical and moral
principles of the Law of God.
On Rosh Hashonah, we Jews, heirs to a great spiritual
heritage, gather in our synaogogues to give expression
through our prayers and songs to this hope which still lives
in the hearts ol millions of men already overwhelmed by
a brutal power. May our petitions be acceptable to God.
May He send redemption to our people and to all mankind
during the coming year. Amen.

During the second year of its existence the Jacksonville
Chapter of Masada has developed into one of the most
active men s organizations in the city. Its growth has been
so phenomenal that it has been rated as one of the best
chapters in the Southern region.
The group of boys which last year hesitatingly formed
the chapter became fully conscious of the ideals and aspir-
ations of this splendid youth Zionist organization, and from
its ranks have emerged a number of excellent leaders. On
January 27, 1941, local Masada voted unanimously to
become affiliated with the National Masada Organization.
At that inspiring meeting it became evident that the young
men comprising the local group had become fully aware
of the important tasks they had undertaken and of their
responsibi lity as part of the world-wide Zionist movement.
J he chapter had its first opportunity to prove to the
local community its interest in Jewish affairs when it of¬
fered its services to the Jewish Community Council during
last year s drive. Jointly with Junior Hadassa h it led in a
campaign for funds among the Jewish youth of our city
which brought highly satisfactory results. Many of its
members also worked as solicitors for the campaign at
large. One of the highlights of the past year was the
celebration of Masada s eighth birthday. Simultaneous with
the observance of this event by the national organization
in New York, the local chapter sponsored a banquet and
dance at which the Charter was presented to the chapter.
This, affair was the first of its kind to be given by young
people on such a large scale. The principal address was
delivered by Mr. Charles W. Bergman of Atlanta, and
the proceeds of this event went to the Haifa Nautical
School in Palestine. This undertaking was highly praised
by the national organization. On the Friday night pre-
ceeding the banquet, a special service dedicated to Masada
was conducted at the Jacksonville Jewish Center synagogue.
Members of the chapter led the service and Mr. Stanley
Joseph delivered the sermon.
At the National Convention held in Atlantic City,
July 3rd, the local chapter was represented by Norman
Moscovitz, Julius Friedlin, Harold Ezzes and Harold Leit¬
man. In recognition of the splendid work of the chapter,
Harold Leitman, its president, was elected a Vice-president
of the National organization.

successful first Regional Conclave of chapters from Geor¬
gia, Alabama and Florida, held on Labor Day Weekend.
This conclave coincided with the meeting of the Junior
Had assah Regional Board which took place in Jacksonville.
Th is fact lent color and interest to the conclave. The
fine group of girls that came from the neighboring states
as well as members of the local Junior Hadassah partici¬
pated in the social and cultural events held in conjuction
with the conclave. J he highlight of this event was the
luncheon held on Sunday at the Semino Ie Hotel. The
guest speaker, Rabbi M. D. Margolis, brought a vital and
inspiring message to the assembled delegates which set the
tone of the entire conclave and elicited an enthusiastic
response on the part of the young people.

lem and devoting all her attention to
it until the problem was solved and
the aim fulfilled. In the course of her

lend it a dramatic quality which never
failed to draw the interest of even the

onistic.

ficance. As though racing against

ugees.

ciety with an inscription commemo¬

HARRY FINKELSTEIN
Last November the Jewis li Com- fusing th e rich traditions of bis ortbo-
munity lost one of its outstanding
personalities through the death o f Har-
ry Finkelstein, the first president of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Uncle Harry, as he was affection-
atey known, was like a sturdy tree
which had struck deep roots in the
soi I. He bore precious fruit which, in
the I anguage of the Bible,
was beautiful to behold
and good for sustenance .
The most valuable thing
he had bequethed to his
fellow Jews was the repu¬
tation he had established
for the name Jew. He never
hid his Jewishness nor his
Jewish name. He did not
believe in counteracting pre¬
judice by hiding behind a
mask. Instead, he succeed¬
ed in making his name re¬
spected and honoured by
living up to the highest
standards of business ethics
prescribed by his religion.
The honorable name of
Harry Finkelstein & Com¬
pany was a strong factor
in bringing about a more
friendly relationship be¬
tween Jew and Gentile in
Jacksonville.
A lasting contribution
that Harry Finkelstein had
made to the local Jewish
community is the part he played in
the erection o f the religious and ed¬
ucational institution known as the
Jacksonville Jewish Center. Coming, as
he did, from a deeply religious and
scholarly family, he possessed an
abounding loyalty to his faith. He
often stated his desire to live and
die as an Orthodox Jew. But he also
knew that the life, habits and customs
o f the old world cannot wholly be
transplanted to America. In the Center
movement he saw the possib ili'ty of
ing 'the first two years of its existence.
He watched his beloved institution
grow from strength to strength and,
even when his health was failing, his
interest and zeal did not diminish.
Last Rosh Hashonah Uncle Harry
entered the Center for the last time.
Although already a very sick man, he
humbly and reverently sat through the
major part ol the religious service.
One could see that those were happy
moments for him; this synagogue, tra¬
ditional in its customs and yet speak¬
dox faith with the ideals and habits
o f the new life that was being fash¬
ioned in this country.
This version brought him to active
leadership in the Jewish community.
He gave of his time and substance to
make possible the building o f the
Center. He served as its president dur¬
ing the language of the American Jew,
was the realization of a long cherished
dream.
When a man dieth, he shall carry
nothing away, his glory shall not de¬
scend after him . Thus speaks the
psalmist, referring to man’s material
acquisitions on this earth. Harry Fin¬
kelstein, however, left something
worthwhile behind,—his good deeds,
his honourable name, his virtues as a
Jew and as a citizen. These shall live
on and become a blessing to his family
and to the community at large.
May his soul be bound up in the
bond of life eternal .
Two ships were once seen to be
sailing near land. One of them was
going forth from the harbor, and the
other coming to the harbor. Every
one was cheering the outgoing ship,
and every one was giving it a hearty
send-off. But the incoming ship was
scarcely noticed.
A wise man was looking at the
two ships, and he said: ‘Rejoice not
over the ship that is setting out to
sea, for you know not what destiny
awaits it, what storms it may en¬
counter, what dangers it may have
to undergo. Rejoice rather over the
ship that has reached port safely
and brought back all its passengers
in peace.’
It is the way of the world, hat
when a human being is born, all re¬
joice; but when he dies, all sorrow.
Rather ought the opposite to be the
case. No one can tell what troubles
await the child on its journey into
manhood. But when a man has lived
and dies in peace, all should rejoice,
seeing that he has completed his
journey, and it is departing this
world with the imperishable crown
of a good name.
Talmudic Parable

During the past ten years, the olfice ol Sexton
(Shammas) of the Center synagogue was graced by Mr.
Joseph Robin. If it is true, as it most certainly is, that the
character of an institution is determined not by the size
and cost of its building but by the men and women who
are a part of it, then the Center owes much of its character,
its friendly atmosphere, its warmth and its religious faith
and enthusiasm to the personality of Joseph Robin.
Mr. Robin and his faithful wife came to Jacksonville
at the end of the year 1931. Having been a businessman
all of his life, it never entered his mind to become a relig¬
ious functionary by profession, though he had a deep
interest in the synagogue, having been for years vice-
president of the conservative congregation in Sioux City,
Iowa. Mr. Robin consented, however, to take over tempor¬
arily the functions of Shammas, an office for which the
Center had a vacancy at the time.
It was for the Center a most fortunate event. His
genial personality, his truly religious character, tempered
by a modern outlook on life, immediately won for Mr.
Robin many friends among young and old. He decided
thereupon to devote the rest of his life to the service of
the religious needs of the Jewish community of Jacksonville.
During the past ten years, Mr. Robin has devoted
all of his talents and energies to serve our institution. He
took care of the daily minyon. He officiated on the High
Holidays as Baal Shacharis or Baal Musaf, as the occasion
required. He prepared nearly a hundred boys for Bar
Mitzvah. He assisted the Chevra Kadisha in performing the
last rites for the deceased. He was immeasurably helpful
in the collection of the membership dues. He became, with¬
out exageration, a most useful and indispensable part of
the Center.
Aside from these mutiple functions, Mr. Robin, being
a devout Zionist, has given much time to Zionist work in
Jacksonville, his pet project being 'the Jewish National
Fund. It was largely through his efforts that the J. N. F.
collections in our city equal and surpass that of commun¬
ities many times the size of Jacksonville.
In April 25th of this year, the Center dedicated
a Friday Night service to honor Mr. Joseph Robin on the
occasion of his 10th anniversary with the Center. Rabbi
Margolis in his sermon on the True Test of the Religious
Man” extolled Mr. Robin as the finest example of the
successful synthesis of Americanism and Judaism. At the
reception which followed the services Mr. Robin was pre¬
sented with valuable gifts by the Jacksonville Jewish Cen¬
ter, the Daughters of Israel, the Zionist District and the
Ladies Aid Society and Home for the Aged.
We pray to the Almighty that Mr. Joseph Robin and
his family may continue to enjoy good health and vigor and
that he may be able to carry on his useful and noble work
which has brought much satisfaction to himself and great
benefit to the Jewish Community of Jacksonville.

Having set as its ultimate goal the

tion.

wasted. This year, the project initia¬

becoming a rea lity. The B oard of

and the Jax Jewish Center) and the
initiation of a campaign for recruiting
members for the new organization.

mond Ball League tournament after

erated with the Jewish Welfare Board

after much thought. The editors had in mind to por¬

cision, which seemed so simple, was nevertheless most
fortunate for none of the characteristics of this institu¬

opportunity for a full, active and happy life and to elicit
from it the finest qualities of a good citizen and a loyal

Great Number of Transients, Local
Indigents and Refugees Are Helped
DAVE DAVIS, President
Due to lack of resources and insuf¬
ficient funds for social service work
in Jacksonville, the Jewish Welfare
Society is called upon to meet the
needs of the people that the Public
Welfare Departments are not able
to meet. The types of cases handled
during 1940 were: residents totally
unemployed, clients too old to work
but not old enough to receive Old Age
Assistance, residents who were not
citizens or who could not prove their
citizenship to meet the requirements
for O. A. A., those people who were
mentally ill but not commitable to an
institution, and those suffering from
chronic or acute illnesses. There were
also cases where the head of the house¬
hold was employed full time but not
earning enough to maintain his family
on a decent standard of living, or
where the person only had part time
work, or his earnings could only pro¬
vide for the b are necessities and when
illness came, he needed help and ad¬
vice. The agency supplemented Old
Age Assistance allowances and Moth¬
er’s Aid when their assistance was not
adequate.
During 1940, the agency handled
274 local cases and 409 transients,
totaling 683 cases. Of this number,
there were 305 new cases, of which
8 were local and 297 transient. The
worker interviewed 1,293 persons in
the office. She made 503 home visits
and 248 collaterall visits. The agency
supplied 684 meals and gave 327
lodgings to transients. There were 488
units (a unit being an individual,
couple, or family) aided with cash
relief. 102 persons were provided with
free medication, prescribed by doctors
or hospitals. 84 quarts of milk were
paid for by the agency, and 151 per¬
sons received either clothing or house¬
hold articles.
I he Jewish Welfare Society coop¬
erated with the National Refugee Ser¬
vice, Inc. of New York and carried
on the social work for the Jacksonville
Resettlement Committee. 1 he worker
assisted in securing affiants and
helped with affidavits. Many times
it was necessary to contact people for
jobs and find lodgings for refugees.
1 he Jacksonville Jewish Community
Council allocated $1,200 for 1940 to
be used locally for the maintenance
of the refugee units until they were
self-supporting. 1 his money was han¬
dled by the social worker. She met
with her Case Consultant Committee
for Refugees to determine the amount
of assistance to be given, to discuss
the problems presented by the new¬
comer, to help adjust him to his new
environment, and to interpret to him
the American standards of business
practices. Wherever possible, the ref¬
ugee was placed in the same trade or
profession in which he had made his
livelihood prior to coming to this
country. When this was not possible,
the man s adjustment to his new job
often took longer, and sometimes was
more difficult.
It should be kept clearly in mind
that the assistance given the refugee
was handled on an entirely separate
basis than the assistance given the
local and transient relief case, for the
latter received funds from the Com¬
munity Chest. The worker had to
secure permission from the Commun-
itv Chest before she could volunteer
her services to do the work for the
Refug ee Committee.
In 1940 the financial outlay for
transients was great due to the type
of transient cases handled. The ma¬
jority of men that came for assistance
to our office had skilled trades and
many of them had promises of jobs
either at Camp Blanding or at the
Air Base even before they came to
the city. After the social worker veri¬
fied in each case that the jobs were
avai Iable for the men, they often had
to wait two weeks before they were
paid. In such cases this agency assisted
them during this waiting period; some¬
times the society was reimbursed by
the transient.
In any social agency, it is most es¬
sential that the social worker be alert
to a II the various problems that the
family may present, even though her
agency is not equipped to solve them.
The Jewish Welfare Society has co¬
operated and worked jointly on cases
with the following agencies in Jack¬
sonville: County Health Unit, Dis¬
trict Welfare Board, Florida State
Employment Service, Girl Scouts, La¬
dies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society,
Legal Aid Association, State Reha¬
bilitation Department, State Welfare
Board, Tuberculosis Association, U. S.
Probation Office, Work Projects Ad¬
ministration.

The outstanding characteristic of the High Holidays is that they concern themselves primarily with
the Jew as an individual. All other festivals have either national or agricultural origins, and serve to strength¬
en the bonds between the Jew, his people and its past. Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur bring home to the in¬
dividual Jew the meaning of his daily struggle for existence, his relationship to his fellow human beings
and to God, the Creator and Ruler of the universe.
The great hold that these Days of Awe (Yomim N oroim) have upon us in this age of irreligión may be
partially explained by the fact that the ideals and emotions stimulated by them probe deeply into the private
life of every Jew and lift him to a high spiritual level.
If the ritual of introspection and self-purification of these solemn days were accepted by the world at
large, it would bring inestimable good to humanity and help to dissolve the world’s ills.
In a world corrupted by greed, hatred and bloodshed, Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur, with their call
to repentance, their prayers for universal peace and the establishment of the Kingdom of Good on earth, offer
a valuable message to humanity.
IRaalj Iftaahnttalj
Rosh Hashonah aims to improve human nature. The
prayers, the ritual, the music, the sounding of the Shofar
are calculated to elicit from the worshipper the finest ana
highest expressions of his personality. They impress upon
him the vanity and uselessness of envy, greed and hate,
and stimulate in him emotions of tolerance, love and the
proper understanding of the real values of life.
During these days, God, as it were, sits in judgment
over us, reviewing our good and bad deeds. This, more
concretely, takes the form of every individual searching his
inner self and resolving to eliminate that which is cruel and
u gly in his life. The New Year ritual prepares him to meet
the coming year cleansed in spirit and determined to follow
the path of good life so that he may find favor in the eyes
of God and man.
fom HCtppur
One of the prime functions of Yom Kippur is to break
down the natural resentment on the part of human beings
to admit that they are wrong. The solemn Kol Nid re service
is opened with a petition to God that he may permit us
to pray in the company of the transgressors, a term which
may be applicable to every member in the congregation.
The public recitation of the long catalogue of sins, among
whi ch we are sure to find some that apply to ourselves,
the request for forgiveness, which fill the prayer-book, the
custom of asking forgiveness from those whom we know
that we have wronged, all of these are calculated to bring
about a mood in which the worshipper acknowledges his
errors and sins. This is the first step necessary in making
any improvements, for without acknowledging error there
can be no correction of error.
Yom Kippur also serves to demonstrate the supremacy
of the spirit over the flesh. Twenty four hours of fasting
is not only a denial of the natural hungers of the body but
also a test of a man s willpower to withstand the temp¬
tations to which he may be exposed in the year to come.

DAVID MOSCOVITZ
The 58th issue of the Jacksonville Jewish Center Year
Book is dedicated to Mr. David Moscovitz, honorary president
of the Center, whose life has been closely identified with
the Jewish community for many years.
Mr. Moscovitz came to Jacksonville in 1906, a youth
of eighteen. Jacksonville Jewry (having outgrown its small
house of worship maintained in the old Masonic Temple (Cun¬
ning s,) was then engaged in building its first synagogue,
the B nai Israel, corner of Duval and Jefferson Streets.
Mr. Moscovitz became actively interested in communal
life soon after his marriage to Miss Fannie Frank in 1913.
In recognition for his loyalty to Jewish causes he was elected
to various positions of trust and leadership in the Jewish com¬
munity. For many years he served as vice-president of the
Y. M. H. A ., which at that time was the pivotal point of the
cultural and social life of local Jewry. From 1916 to 1925 he
served as treasurer of the Hebrew Free Loan Society. Mr.
Moscovitz was also one of the organizers of this institution
which helped many immigrants gain an economic foot hold
in the new land. From 1924 to 1926 he served as president of
Congregation B nai Israel.
It was at that time that the movement was started for
the building of the Jacksonville Jewish Center in order to
meet the needs of the rapidly growing Jewish Com¬
munity. Since 1927, the year when the new building was
completed, Mr. Moscovitz served in various capacities with
the Jacksonville Jewish Center. He was chairman o f the
important Religious Committee; an active member and
treasurer of the Center Cemetery Committee; vice-president
of the Center during the long period of Mr. Rubin s ad¬
ministration, and for 2 years, 1938-1959 he served as presi¬
dent of the Center. I his imposing record of communal
service speaks for itself. His life has become integrated
with the life of the community as a whole.
Mr. Moscovitz outstanding characteristic is his
fove o f the synagogue, its prayers and its songs. His de¬
votion to the Sabbath morning services should serve as
good example to other Jewish business men.
The dedication of this book to David Moscovitz is
a small token of recognition for the many years of service
he has rendered to the Jewish community.
On the occasion of the New Year, the Rabbi, the
Officers, the Board of Directors and the members of the
Center, extend to Mr. Moscovitz and his family their best
wishes for a year of good health, prosperity and happiness.

I he National Council of Jewish
Women is an organization whose pur¬
pose it is to unite all Jewish women in
unselfish service work and in concrete
manifestations of their unlimited pride
in Americanism. Although differing
widely in 'their backgrounds and
modes of religious worship, the se wo¬
men nevertheless possess a common
bond in their desire to aid the under¬
privileged and the needy.
Established in Jacksonville only two
years ago, the Council has already
found a place in our community. Its
primary aim was to demonstrate to
our fellow citizens who do not share
our racial and spiritual background
that we feel the same community re¬
sponsibility as they do and wish to
join hands with them in bettering the
conditions of all human beings.
This we have done very concretely.
The Toyery, the Immigration and Nat¬
uralization Bureau, our hospital visit¬
ing and our participation in the work
of the Red Cross, all these are ac¬
tivities designed to serve all classes
and creeds.
On the other hand, our social ad¬
justment program for New Americans
and our part in the program o f the
Jewish Welfare Board for the enter¬
tainment of Army-Navy men are de¬
voted to making newcomers of our
own faith feel more welcome in our
community.
Although the National Council has
never turned a deaf ear to any crying

the many civilian activities revolving
around National Defense. In this field
also, the Jacksonville section is doing

The Jacksonville Jewish Center, since its inception has
been striving to serve the Jewish community with a pro¬
gram of activities that is all embracing. While it is
mainly a religious institution, the Center has also an ex¬
tensive educational program. Its services are not limited
to members only. It caters to all Jewish groups and assists
every worthwhile Jewish undertaking. It is, therefore, the
home of the great majority of Jewish organizations in the
community.
The Center Synagogue
The Jacksonville Jewish Center has succeded in satis¬
fying the religious needs of the orthodox and conservative
type of Jew. The daily “Minyon”, conducted in the
Prayer Room morning and evening, is used by members
and non-members alike. Our Sabbath and festival ser¬
vices, as well as ‘the late Friday night services are open to
the public and are always attended by many visitors. On
Rosh Hashonah and Yom Kippur, special provisions are
made to serve all Jews of the community. The conservative
minded, to which group most of our members belong, are
given a modern, dignified traditional service in the main
synagogue, while the more orthodox group are accomodated
in the Center auditorium.
The Rabbi in the Community
A graduate of the Jewish Theological Seminary and
Columbia University, Rabbi M. D. Margo lis is beginning
his tenth year of service with the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
His interest is extended to every Jewish cause, including
Page Thirty
fraternal, charitable, Zionist and Youth organizations. Dur¬
ing the past year Rabbi Margolis conducted services for
the service men at Camp Blanding which, although having
a large number of Jewish soldiers, did not have a Jewish
chaplain. Prominent transients, visiting Rabbis and guest
speakers always find hospitality in the home of our Rabbi.
Those in distress and in need of help always receive his
attention and no one is turned away empty handed.
Directing the Hadassah Study Group, making radio
addresses in behalf of the Community Chest and other
organizations, addressing the Interdenominational Fellow¬
ship Group, addressing meetings and banquets, sponsored
by the various organizations in town, may be listed as some
of the other activities of the Rabbi outside of his syna¬
gogue duties and his ministering to individuals in time of
joy and sorrow.
Educational Activities of the Center
The Cent er maintains two schools, a daily Hebrew
School which holds sessions eleven months during the
year and a Sunday School, which meets each Sunday
morning from October through June. No Jewish child in
Jacksonville has ever been denied a Jewish education
because the parents were unable to pay for the tuition
of their children.

for Adult Jewish Studies, which was affiliated with the
National Academy for Jewish Studies sponsored by the
