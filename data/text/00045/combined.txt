Seniors who are fortunate enough to be residents of Mt. Carmel Gardens are convinced that there is no finer place to live. When
the time has came for you to enter a new phase of your life and really start living, Mt. Carmel has all the answers.
The physical aspect of our building is breath taking. A17 story highrise with 208 beautifully equipped apartments and the lower
floor devoted to Offices, Auditorium, Kitchens (including a Kosher Kitchen), Ceramics Room, Rec Room, Library and
Maintenance.
Practically everything you could ever need is here - the Community Kosher Nutrition Program serves a delicious nutritious lunch
5 days a week with frozen dinners available for weekends and holidays. Sam Friedlander, Jewish Family and Community Services
in his office three days a week to counsel and assist with any problems we might have. A 24 hour total Security System is in effect
here with an emergency alarm in every apartment connected to the Master Board at the desk. When foe alarm is sounded
immediate help is available with Oxygen and Rescue on the way. With this service at the desk, you know help is there if and when
you need it.
Then too we have our Administrator, Charles Caplan, whose door is always open for us, together with Margaret lovito they form
an unbeatable team. We have our Grievance Committee to handle any problems we might have. Comprised of Tenants - Mary
Foor, Chairman, Abe Sugarman, George Berkowitz, Dorothy Levine, Buddy Wood and Mollie Hollander they work with the
Mt. Carmel Board of Directors to see that the problem is solved. We have our own Bus Corporation leasing buses to take us
where we want to go, established in 1981.
Our Podiatrist, Steven Merritt, M.D., is with us on the 1st and 3rd Friday of each month. Mt. Carmel Chapter of AARP #3350
meets here on the 2nd Wednesday of every month, and foe Jewish War Veterans and their Auxiliary meet here on the 2nd
Thursday this making it easy for us to attend these very important meetings.
Jewish Family and Community Services has a Discussion Group twice monthly where everything under the sun is discussed.
They also take us out on field trips, movies, etc., frequently.
We have our own kiln for Ceramics and a class once a week, we also have a creative Needlework class weekly and Bingo open to
the Senior Community every Monday night at 7:30 P.M. Add our Choral Group from Florida JuniorCollege taught by Jim Jarrard
and you will see we have a very full schedule.
We also enjoy a very close alliance with Mayor Godbold. As his guests we attend everything the City has to offer in the field of
entertainment from football games to concerts to fishing both on the Pier and at Hanna Park.
Our Shuffle Board Court and Horse Shoes are very popular. Parties abound with a birthday party on the last Thursday of every
month, a dance with bands furnished by the Musicians Performance Trust Fund, Musicians Local 444 and lots of other
entertainment. So - in other words, if you gotta grow old, Jacksonville is the place to do it in and Mt. Carmel is THE place to five
while you are doing it. It is indeed the place to go when you’re really ready to start living.

Solomon Schechter Day School
Founded in 1961
The Solomon Schechter Day School is designed as the first step in preparing students for their religious role
in Jewish life as well as their role as successful and contributing members of the our society. The school offers
an integrated curriculum of Judaic experiences, Hebrew language and general studies. The goal is to create
informed, intelligent American Jews, cognizant of their history and heritage and proud of their tradition and
values.
Located in the school wing of the Jacksonville Jewish Center, the Day School has the unlimited use of a
library, computer room, gym, sportsfield, stage, swimming pool, tennis courts and chapel.

Center for so many years.

proceeds derived from the Cantor’s

Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14, founded in 1919, completed another memorable year. Blending the traditional and the modem, the Troop continues to
foster the needs and growth of today’s youth.
Central to scouting is the active, outdoor program which it offers to youth. Troop 14 opened its camping program this past year
with a weekend trek to beautiful Manatee Springs near the Gulf Coast for swimming and canoeing. Troop 14 then turned its sights on
the River Bend District Camporee where the Troop distinguished itself in competition as the outstanding Troop in the district.
A contingent of some 50 scouts and family converged on Fort Wilderness for a weekend of camping and fun at the annual Scout
Scamporee at Disney World. Winter saw the Troop camp at beautiful Blue Springs where the manatees take refuge from the colder
St. Johns River.
The Osceola National Park hosted Troop 14 for the weekend of the reenactment of the Battle of Olustee, the largest Civil War
battle in Florida. Troop 14 also camped at Fort Clinch State Park where the scouts completed the rugged 16-mile historic hike. Troop
12, sponsored by the Temple, joined our Troop for an “Old-Timers’ ” reunion and campout. The boys hosted former scouts and their
families to celebrate the proud history of the Troops and their great contributions to the community.
Service to others continues to be a fundamental principle of scouting. In 1985, Troop 14 began a Thanksgiving Day tradition of
serving the residents of Mount Carmel Gardens with a special Holiday meal. The scouts learned that helping others can truly be an
enjoyable and enriching experience for all.
Troop 14 and Cub Pack 14 combined for a another memberable Scout Sabbath with scouts participating in the service led by
Rabbi Kentoff.
The highlight of the past year for six scouts and three adults was a journey to Philmont Scout Ranch in New Mexico. The group
hiked the inclines of the rugged Rocky Mountains at altitudes over 14,000 feet. The hardy group treked over 100 miles carrying all
their food, clothing and equipment on their backs. The group still managed time to pan for gold, visit the Kit Carson museum and
partake of other activities which make Philmont perhaps the ultimate scouting experience. Congratulations go the scouts Michael
Weitzen, Adam DePaul, Jeff Ghelerter, Life Wilensky, Roger Golcher, Sean Usoff, and adults, Dan Wilensky, Bill Wilensky and Dr.
Jerome Weitzen.
Many of the remaining scouts, 15 in all, attended summer camp at nearby Camp Shands. Scouts Sean Usoff and Roger Golcher
served as counselors in training.
Troop 14 is for boys ages 11 or having completed the fifth grade through adulthood. Interested persons may contact the Center
office.

Jacksonville Hevrah Kaddisha
The Jewish way of death is one part of a philosophy of life in which all people are endowed with dignity and given respect. Even
after death, the body which once held a human soul retains its sanctity. Our sages have compared a corpse to an impaired Torah
scroll which, although no longer usuable, retains its holiness.
Tradition dictates that from the moment of death until burial, the body of the deceased must be attended by a shomer, a guard.
The importance of this mitzuah, is emphasized by the fact that the shomer is relieved of all other religious obligations during his
watch.
The body should be buried as soon as possible, usually within one day, except on the Sabbath; recent traditional practice allows
slight delays if a close relative or friend must travel long distances to attend the funeral.
Before burial, the body is to be cleansed by tahara, ritual washing.
The body is then dressed in tachrichim; clean, simple, white, handmade shrouds. Close to two thousand years ago, Rabban
Gamliel noted that the rich were buried in ostentatious clothing, which embarrassed the poor who could not afford them; he
insisted that he be buried in plan linen shrouds, and the custom became a central symbol of the egalitarianism of Jewish burial
practice.
The use of the plainest wooden coffin is required—to maintain the sense of equality in death and to follow the principle: “Dust
you are and unto dust you shall return.” The coffin must be made entirely of wood and must not retard the natural decomposition
of the body.
Since burial must be prompt, there should be no public viewing of the body, nor should there be embalming or cosmetic work
which are regarded as mutiliation and desecration. Today, embalming is only permitted by Jewish law in those instances when it is
required by civil law. Cremation, regarded as a total desecration is not permitted.
These traditions have been supervised in Jewish communities by a chevra kadisha—sacred society—which consists of
volunteers who aid the bereaved and ensure that law and custom are followed. Assisting in the preparation and burial of the body
is a highly valued mitzua and is considered a chesedshel emet, an act of kindness performed without ulterior motive, because the
dead cannot repay this act of kindness. Membership in a chevra kadisha has always been considered an honor, bestowed upon
the pious of the community. The Jacksonville community is fortunate to have a functioning Chevra Kaddisha active since 1974.
The members perform the necessary tasks on a rotating basis in committees of two men or two women. New members are
welcome and needed to continue these services important to our fellow Jews.
For further information call Joel Bressler at 733-4400.

Benjamin Setzer

RABBI HAROLD SCHULWEIS, of Congregation Valley Beth Shalom,-Encino, California, is one
of the noted and distinguished Rabbis serving the American Jewish community. An extraordinarily
popular speaker, Rabbi Schulweis has moved audiences with his remarks, and with his keen
observations and analyses of the Jewish condition. His provocative and insightful lectures
encompass every theme of importance.
Rabbi Schulweis spoke Sunday, December 15th at 7:30 p.m. on the “Jewish Conceptions of Love:

Want For Our Children”. A panel of professionals provided questions and further insights. The
audience was encouraged to participate. Both sessions were well received.
SHABBATON WEEKEND '85
Featuring Dr. Leonard Fein
DR. LEONARD FEIN is a scholar and writer who is widely recognized as one of the most
provocative interpreters of the Jewish experience of our time. His essays on every aspect of
the Jewish agenda appear regularly in the pages of Moment Magazine, which he serves as
editor. His essays on the Middle East, published in Moment, have been widely quoted in the
New York Times, Time, Newsweek, and dozens of other publications.
THE SHABBATON WEEKEND began Friday evening March 28, with Shabbat Services.
Following a lovely dinner, Dr. Fein addressed his remarks to the issue of “Israel-Diaspora
Relations”. Dr. Fein spoke from the pulpit Saturday morning March 29, on “American
Jewry-What should be our goals beyond those of just Jewish survival”. A cordial and
informal dialogue with Dr. Fein was the highlight of the Shabbat luncheon. Saturday evening,
a wine and cheese reception preceded Dr. Fein’s concluding talk on “Religious Pluralism”.

WOMEN’S AMERICAN ORT, Organization for Rehabilitation Through Training, is
an international organization which has provided over 100 years of vocational and
technical education for the Jewish people. By providing the tools of hand and mind, ORT
has enabled two million Jews to enter the mainstream in the societies in which they live.
Here in Jacksonville, 400 plus women give of their time, their funds, and their commitment to insure that our schools and our
children will continue to grow. Our activities are as varied as our members: Theater parties, gift wrapping at the malls, visits to

Message from the President
Once again the New Year is upon us, a time for reflection, a time for introspection, and a
time for giving thanks for having reached another milestone. As the President of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center I have much to be thankful for. I am thankful that the Center has
excellent professionals that guide our many and varied programs. I am thankful for the
concerned and dedicated lay leadership that has and will always be the backbone of our
congregation. And most importantly I am thankful for you, our members, for it is through you
and for you that the Center exists.
A Synagogue-Center doesn’t just happen. It evolves through the needs and the desires of
its members. It adds programs as its membership grows and makes demands. Our
Synagogue has continued to meet the needs our members have expressed. We are by no
means finished, for the needs of our congregants change, and as they do, more demands are
placed upon us to satisfy these needs.
And that’s why I am the most thankful. For as members of the Jacksonville Jewish Center you have not only demanded that the
Center meet your needs, you have come forward with the ideas, the physical efforts, and the financial where-with-all in order to
meet these demands. You have not allowed the Center to stagnate, and become complacent. And for this I am truly thankful.
BECAUSE of your demands we have continued to grow and to mature into one of the finest Synagogue Centers in the nation.
At this holiday time I can only hope that we can as a congregation go forward together and that the Jacksonville Jewish Center
will always be “The Center of your Life.”

Three Members Honored on

Sol H. Proctor
Hatan Torah
Two words describe Sol Proctor, “always
there.”
Whether it be Shabbat and holidays in Shul,
committee work or advice, Sol Proctor is always
willing to serve — unselfishly, modestly, and with a
deep-rooted concern for the financial stability and
welfare of the congregation.
A third generation member of the Center, Sol
grew up in a home where commitment and devo¬
tion to Jewish life were very important. His father,
Jack Proctor, of blessed memory, served as
minyan captain for many years. As a child Sol
would accompany his father, learning early on the
importance of religious commitment and duty.
Our Hatan Torah is proud of the religious train¬
ing he received at the Center and continually
strives to see that the present and future
generations are as fortunate as he has been. He is
a past president of the Center and now serves as
chairman of the board. Sol’s dedication extends to
the community, as well. He is a past president of
the Jacksonville Jewish Center, the Lion’s Club
and Israel Bonds and has also served on the
Federation board.
We honor Sol Proctor for his deep-devotion to
the Center and to Jewish life. Sharing this honor
are his daughters, Terri and Sandy; his brothers,
Samuel, Meyer, David, George and Irving, and
other members of the family.
Joel V. Bressler
Hatan Beresheet

Marton, beginning almost forty years ago.

Kaddisha (Jewish Burial Society) and has served

Camp Ramah
Adds a New Dimension to your Child’s Growth as a Jew
Camp Ramah provides the opportunity of a total Jewish living experience
for children and teenagers in the relaxed atmosphere of a summer camp.
Values built on a shared experience and emotion reach our sons and
daughters in a manner impossible in a formal school program. Over the years,
RAMAH has helped instill a deeper commitment to Judaism in many of its
participants.
The Center believes that benefits accrue to the congregation, not just to the
individual. We have therefore made monies available to reduce the financial
cost of qualified students regardless of need. These monies are not part of the
regular Center budget, but rather are raised by donations to the Myra Thier
Lazarus Ramah Fund, Center Sisterhood, scholarships and from other donations made throughout the year.
Two families have helped us greatly. The Gordon Family has established a scholarship fund in memory of the beloved Robert J.
Gordon. A long list of deserving boys and girls have been able to go to Ramah because of this scholarship. Abe Selber established in
his will a perpetual trust for specific Jewish education purposes. With th full support of his children, Iris and Franklin, who have never
lost interest in the Center, the proceeds of the Abe Selber Trust have been used to send campers to Ramah.

HADASSAH, the largest Women’s Zionist Organization of America, will be
celebrating its Diamond Jubilee during 1987.
As Jews we have always been linked to Israel in a bond of love, loyalty and deep
commitment. HADASSAH, since its inception, has filled a need for healing, teaching and
research. 75 years later HADASSAH is Israel’s and the world’s medical pace-setter. We
established medical and dental schools, public health and social welfare systems. Our
hospitals at Kiryat Hadassah and Mt. Scopus in Jerusalem represent the latest in
medical equipment, technology and treatment. Results of research completed at our
facilities are published to the medical community, worldwide.
In 1934, at the start of Hitler years, HADASSAH organized a youth rescue
movement bringing out groups of German youngsters. Since then Youth Aliyah has
cared for and cared about over 225,000 youngsters from 80 lands - changing to meet the
needs of each successive group. Our latest challenge was absorbing 2,000 Ethiopian
youngsters.
In 1942, through the HADASSAH Israel EDUCATION Services, a vocational
education program was established which has expanded to include a four year
comprehensive high school and Jerusalem’s first Community College. Our
commitment is to youth and to developing an educated skilled work force which is
essential to Israel’s continued economic growth.
In the United States, HADASSAH, through a monthly magazine, provides our
385,000 members with information on Israel, women’s affairs and other current issues -
such as separation of church and state and human rights.
In Jacksonville, HADASSAH instituted “EDUCATION DAY” which has been an
annual event the Jewish Community looks forward to. HADASSAH has grown to meet
the needs of our members and so did we in JACKSONVILLE. We have four active
groups; 3 EVENING and 1 DAY; Avodah, Hannah Senesch, Shir Li, and Tikva.
HADASSAH offers you a means of fulfilling your Jewish heritage. We look forward
to meeting you and involving you in our 75th Anniversary activities during the entire
year.

There is excitement at River Garden. While building upon the residential and care programs which are an extension of Jewish family life locally
and serve as a model nationally, River Garden is preparing for an extraordinary metamorphosis:
A campus for good living with gracious apartments for adults, lakes, streams, wooded walkways, and recreation areas with service availability to
respond to every need.
A “Town Center” with shops and lounges, restaurants, medical clinics, and craft centers and, of course, a campus Chapel;
A new residential geriatric care center, our own “River Garden Hebrew Home,” which will provide all of the programs of a nursing home and
rehab center without looking a bit like a medical facility, incorporating the latest environmental gerontological research to help each resident make
the most of abilities and the most out of life — a place for care and caring, but, perhaps most importantly, a place that will truly feel like home;
A center for outreach geriatric services, both social and medical, making available to every Jewish family the best in clinical and support services
right in their own homes;
Fields for youngsters to play with grandparents looking on where both can then go feed the ducks and have a slice of kosher pizza later.
All of this and more will be developed on 34 beautiful acres on St. Augustine Road not more than 7 minutes from all Jewish congregations and
right off 1-295.
River Garden’s 40-year tradition of service as part of the Jewish community, under inspired leadership, requires the Boards and Auxiliary of
River Garden to plan for the highest quality of life for each of us. Daring to dream and acting to make the dream real is a must at a time when living
well beyond 80 is commonplace, when our Jacksonville Jewish community is poised for major growth, and in face of the urgent need to emphasize
Jewish values, assert Jewish identity, and support inter-generational Jewish family living.
Our sense of mission rose out of the past 40 years. Our sense of purpose addresses the next 40 years and beyond. With the moral and financial
support of our own Jewish community, the dream can become real before the decade is out. At that point, in addition to “Shalom,” we’ll be able to
say, “Hi, Neighbor!”

In today’s very specialized world, aging is becoming more and more a much-inquired about topic. Modem medicine has expanded a healthier
and longer lifespan for our senior citizens. We of the Auxiliary are often asked what we are all about. With our motto, “Service with a heart,” we
dedicate our time and efforts exclusively to the residents of River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged. Under a very structured volunteer
program, we participate in and sponsor activities for our residents such as “Out to Lunch” on a one-to-one basis, Special Holiday Programs on
Mother’s Day, Father’s Day, Hanukah and Puiim. We sponsor a very successful annual blood bank drive for the exclusive use of our residents,
volunteers and staff. Our Sunshine Program provides hospitalized residents with visitations and a little get-well gift from the Auxiliary. We staff
residents’ dances and our “Little Store” with volunteers. We provide friendly visitors and assist in shopping, letterwriting, and many of us have
“adopted a friend.”
For our general membership, we this year have held our first and successful paid-up membership dinner. The Auxiliary is also proud to provide
for extra budgetary and equipment needs for the exceptional facility we call our home. Through annual dues, life memberships, Tombola, Tree of
Life, Donors, Residents’ Memorial Plaques, Fifth Commandment Plaques and linen shower we have been able to contribute to the purchase of
medical equipment and furnishings for the use of our residents and professional staff. Our three general membership meetings in October,
January and May — are planned carefully by the various chairpeople and have become a fun time to lunch, socialize and enjoy excellent
entertainment and programs. Anniversary Day in March is always on a bigger scale and is open to the entire community. It is usually planned in
conjunction with the Board of Trustees’ and professional staff, and is hosted by the Auxiliary ladies. This year we celebrated River Garden’s 40th
Anniversary and had festivities planned for the whole weekend of March 15,1986, with activities geared to accomodate the very youngest child to

Rabbi’s Message
The Forgotten Sin
On the forthcoming High Holidays, assembled in prayer, we will confess our
transgressions. The catalogue of sins is extensive as we recount the wrongs committed
during the course of the year. We seek forgiveness for the offenses committed against God -
violations of religious law, i.e. the Sabbath, Kashruth, non-observance of the holidays, etc.
and other dictates of our faith. We confess sins committed against our fellow man - against his
property, his person, his dignity, his sensitivities and feeling.
But there is a third category not included in the above, but of great ultimate consequence -
the sin each of us commits against ourselves, against our own spirit, our own selfhood - a sin
which often eludes our awareness. If I violate a Divine Command, a community and a
tradition witness my failure. If I wrong my neighbor, he will call me to task. But if I wrong
myself, if I deny my spirit, if I abuse the power of my selfhood - no audience admonishes me,
no witness warns me. The High Holidays call us, therefore, to focus our gaze inward and see
the stain we have caused upon our own souls. What are they? - they should be obvious. We
have capacities for greatness that atrophy, visions of goodness that fade, beautifies of mind
and heart that wither.
I missed seeing the movie “Amadeus” when it was playing as a first-run film. I caught up
with it during the course of my vacation in a movie house that features old but classic films.
Briefly, “Amadeus” is a story of how Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart was kept from becoming a
recognized genius in his day by the Court Composer, Antonio Salieri. Salieri was possessed
by jealousy for Mozart’s creativity and his genius. What weighed most heavily on Salieri’s
mind is the realization that Mozart’s genius was ignored whereas his own mediocrity was
celebrated.
Salieri’s life was a testimony to mediocrity - of not being able to stand comfortably in the presence of genius. For thousands of
years the Jewish people have been taught to revere genius, to shun mediocrity, to search for truth, to suspect popular taste and
convention, to be skeptical of contemporary fads and fancies. But Amadeus was a warning for me as well. It underscored the theme
of how frequently we settle for so little, how willing we are to place low ceilings over our lives and to see the horizons only of the
foreground, never attributing or granting ourselves the visions we might have.
When we sin against ourselves we also sin against God. When we look upon ourselves as creatures incapable of change
(...“Rabbi...don’t ask me to be more than I am - you can’t teach an old dog new tricks”.) we sin against God. As our horizons
become limited, we no longer remember our genealogy, for we are children of God. Rabbi Shlomo of Karlin used to say: “What is
the worst thing the evil urge can do? It is to make man forget that he is the son of a King.”
God is not affected by our wrong doing...most especially when we wrong ourselves, but we are. When we lose a sense of the
significance of our lives we are diminished. We can renew our lives. The Bible is replete with stories of false starts or poor
beginnings. Adam and Eve have two sons - Cain and Abel, but the human story begins again after the first crime, with their child
Seth. The first set of Tablets of the Ten Commandments is destroyed, but Moses fashions a new set. Over and over again the Bible
has stories of false starts and of botched beginnings and of man’s need to recoup and start over. This is what Rosh Hashanah and
Yom Kippur are all about - the celebration of the human ability to re-create, to begin again. We are not everything we would like to
be. Therefore, these days provide the opportunity to evaluate and refocus, to restart our lives. Not to realize this is to commit the
real sin - the one we commit against ourselves. In our indifference to the world around us and to the possibilities inherent within us
we fashion a prison for our own soul. Self-centered people, no matter how relentlessly they indulge every whim, aré ultimately
troubled with a deep inner unrest because they sense that their lives are empty. To be stingy with our love, parsimonious with our
sympathy, calculating with our warmth, we become walled in by our own selfishness.
No one of us comes to shul during the year 5747 the way he/she was in 5746. A year has past. And we have changed. Coming to
shul these days means that we know we can change and grow even more. We can become renewed from the experiences of our
lives and those of our tradition which remind us that we can begin again. Let us use these days well. Come to services with your
family and with your loved ones - get in touch with them and with yourself; make these days a time of new beginnings and when you
rise for the prayers of confession add one more transgression... “for the sin which I have committed against myself’ and follow that
with one more resolution - “to be kinder and more thoughtful to the self you might become.”
Inge and I take this opportunity to express our heartfelt prayer that this be a year of health and happiness, of inner peace and
tranquility, a year of peace for you and for all humanity. Amen
Rabbi David Gaffney

Performing Artist Series
“ 1986-1987
In September the Jacksonville Jewish Center began the fourth season of its highly successful PERFORMING ARTIST
SERIES. In 1985-'86 this cultural program was viewed by 1,615 combined series audience, and two of the three shows were to
sellout crowds...an even greater audience is expected for the current season.
AND — WOW — WHAT A SEASON!
On September 21 at 7:30 P.M., showman MIKE BURSTYN launched the series
with an exciting show accompanied by the Johnny Jelinek Orchestra.
Mike Burstyn’s Jacksonville show was patterned after his acclaimed nightclub
performance and featured songs from his recent show on the S.S. Rotterdam, as
well as familiar Broadway tunes.
Featured performer on NOVEMBER 16 will be a very funny comedian - SY
KLEIMAN. Wherever Sy makes an appearence, he’s always a smashing success.
His sharp wit, combined with his uncanny talent as a mimic and storyteller, provoke
the audience to non-stop rolls of laughter. Sy provides classic Jewish-American
humor at its best, yet, it is fresh and without the stereotypes that pervade the
routines of many Jewish comics. As a result of his previous appearance in
Jacksonville, Sy has already won over an enthusiastic local audience. We are
privileged to include Sy Kleinman in our series, through the generosity of Edith and
Milton Berman, who are sponsoring the November 16 show in loving memory of
their son David Randy Berman.
We often hear of musical groups that specialize in folk music from one culture or
another. But it is rare to find a band like the FABRANGAN FIDDLERS that not
only plays traditional folk music, but fuses it with other musical styles. Our
performers on March 15, 1987, the Fiddlers are that rarity. They mix Hebrew,
Yiddish, and Ladino lyrics with bluegrass, traditional jazz and folk melodies. In
recent years, these five versatile musicians from Washington DC have placed
greater emphasis on old-time “freilach” (happy) Chassidic swing style in which the
clarinet has the ability not only to wail, but to laugh and cry as well. ...Joy abounds at
a Fiddler’s Concert, so it is most appropriate that this performance has been
scheduled for Shushan Purim.
All three Performing Artist shows are scheduled for Sunday evenings at 7:30 at the Jacksonville Jewish Center. With the
addition of professional sound and lighting technicians, this year’s series promises to provide first rate entertainment.
We are grateful to the Performing Artist Committee:
Eleanore Dorman, Joan Levin, Peggy Organes, Martha Savage, Debra Setzer, Cathy Yergin; Chairwoman Carol
Freedman who have devoted their time and talents to making this a successful project.

For every Problem there is a
simple Solution ...
which is usually wrong • attributed to H.L. Mencken
Two men are crossing a desert. They are three days from the nearest water hole.
One of the men is carrying a canteen. The canteen holds three days’ supply of water—for one man.
Should they divide it? Then both will die.
Then what is the obligation of the owner of the canteen? One opinion says—A man must not stand by
and watch his fellow man die. He should share the water with his companion. Another says—Preservation
of one’s own life takes precedence. The owner of the water must drink it and live.
Not so simple, is it? If you don’t see a simple, obvious solution, you’re in good company, because the
discussion is nearly 1900 years old.
It is recorded in the Talmud, and here is the interesting thing:
Both opinions are presented in the Talmud, the prevailing and the dissent.
Why both? Because Judaism recognizes life’s dilemmas and the difficulty of knowing how to handle
them. The truth is, for most significant issues there is no simple solution.
Euthanasia? Abortion? Freedom of expressioiVPomography/Skokie?
In most cases, it just isn’t clear what God wants us to do. Nor is it clear what is right.
In time like these, we are sorely tempted to acepted an easy fix for our problems, and there are surely
enough political and religious groups ready to offer them. Unfortunately, simple pre-determined answers
tend to be simpleminded, and therefore dangerous.
But there is an alternative.
Although there is no one simple answer, we have a process for struggling with the issues.
For us, Judaism is that process.
It begins with the Torah, a set of guidelines for living. And it continues in the recorded history of people
wrestling with life’s dilemmas—people who, with one eye on the Torah and one eye on the present situation,
have used their God-given intelligence to find a way to act in a confusing world.
We are not helpless.
We have the Torah.
We have the recorded history of Talmudic and continuing arguments.
We have each other.
If we can wrestle with each other’s ideas and traditions with respect for one another and respect for
those traditions, we will be strengthened. And perhaps we will be fulfilling what God really wants of us.
The process is hard. Harder than the easy fix. But for us it is the only mature, responsible, and truly
religious way.
It makes us Jews.
Our very best wishes to all our friends. May you be recorded in the Book of Life for a year of peace and
happiness.

Jacksonville Jewish Federation
The Jacksonville Community Council was founded in 1935 and
became the Jacksonville Jewish Federation in 1979. This year we are
celebrating our 51st anniversary and are continuing to serve the
Jewish community in a variety of ways.
On June 5, 1983 we moved into our new building at 10829 Old St.
Augustine Road. The Jacksonville Jewish Federation is the central
fundraising, planning, budgeting and coordinating agency for the
Jewish community. Annually the Federation coordinates and funds
the annual Yom Hashoah Observance (Holocaust Remembrance
Program) and Horn Ha’atzmaut Celebration (Israel Independence
Day) programs in cooperation with our synagogues.
The major function of the Federation is to conduct the annual
Federation Campaign which raises funds for local, national and
overseas Jewish obligations. The major beneficiary of this fundraising
effort is the United Jewish Appeal, our local beneficiaries are River
Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged and Jewish Family &
Community Services.
The Federation also has a very active Women’s Division and a
Leadership Development Program.

contains a vital commitment to the dignity, potential and worth of our fellow man. Every person, young and

of programs designed to enrich the quality of life by helping deal with forces that threaten the integrity of the

the population we serve.

Early Childhood Department
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Early Childhood Department has
four position statements that speak directly to major issues being
discussed in early childhood education. As your Director (and with
the total support of the Jacksonville Jewish Center’s Board), I have
pursued this task with great hopes! I have not been disappointed!
The four critical points that we have identified as needs are:
1.Supporting Parents:
The time and resources needed to maintain the family economically
and give the parents the emotional substance needed to support the
children’s emotional development.
Education for parenthood is a part of the school-community
curriculum. Parents have access to needed services such as Health
Care, quality Day Care and personal growth opportunities.
2.Quality Day Care:
The child feels that the situation is a safe and comfortable place for him.
The child feels good about himself.
The child feels there is consistency in his life and a shared concern for him among the important people in
his life - his parents and his caregivers.
3.Quality Educational Curriculum:
A quality program that will provide appropriate educational experience for each age.
4.Environment:
Beautiful classrooms and campus that provide a safe, supportive, stimulating and motivating physical
environment for learning and play.
In a good Pre-School program, the child is learning every minute of the day. We offer you and your child
that “kind of program” at the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Marilyn Sandler
Director, Early Childhood Department

A Message from Rabbi Dov Kentof
Youth and Education Director
The Hebrew word for “repentance” is tesuvah. But it is also interesting to note that,
literally, teshuvah means “return”. This teaches us that atonement in Judaism implies
returning to the right direction in life.
An automobile often needs a wheel alignment to keep it going in the right direction.
The steering mechanism goes out of line through constant use, and the car begins to
wobble. Just as often a human being begins to wobble morally and needs a new
direction and sharper reading of the signs on the road of life.
How is it that we need our guidance system corrected? Some of us are too far¬
sighted, and we neglect to see those* near and dear unto us. Some of us are too near¬
sighted, concentrating on ourselves alone and ignoring those who are distant from us.
We must make sure that we are concerned with our whole community and the whole
human race; and that they, too, are included in the range of our vision. Some of us are
at the age when we need biofocals so that we can read the signs of the time. But if we
want to have the correct vision we can always turn to the right path.
A Hasidic rebee once asked, “If you are going East and suddently you want to travel
West, how far do you have to go?” His congregants gave many complex answers; but
the rebee himself answered his own question in this way. “If you are going East and
suddenly you want to go West, all you have to do is turn around. It is as simple as that.”
To return to Judaism requires only one turn. To return to reasonable living takes but
one turn. To return to a society that is sane and sensible takes but the right turn.
Are you ready to take the turn to commit yourself this coming year with your family
to a more involved educational and youth program? We have certainly provided the
finest staff, facilities and opportunities for the entire Jacksonville Jewish Center families.
We are eager to provide your children and yourselves with the best Judaism can offer.
We need you to turn to us so we in return can turn to you.
Let me, along with Alina, Gil and Tali, wish you a happy and fulfilling New Year.

SHALOM,
“For Everything There Is a Season and a
Time..Kohelet 3.1. This is our National theme
for the year and truly reflects our own feelings.
As our Season begins, our expectations are
extremely high that this will be our Time for a
most successful year for our Center
Sisterhood. We will be working very hard to
raise our membership to where it should be. I
urge each and every female member of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center to either join or re¬
enroll your membership in our Sisterhood. We need your
involvement in all of our activities that will benefit the Center,
Sisterhood, and yourself. In return, your board hopes to educate,
enlighten, and of course, entertain you during the year.
As our sisterhood enters its 63rd year, we can look back with
much “nachas” on all that we have done for our Synagogue. The
future is unlimited for us. We will always be there for our Adults and
Youth; as, we are a vital part of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Ours is a highly thought of Sisterhood throughout the Florida
Branch. We always come home from Conferences with awards in
many areas. We are blessed with having so many creative and
talented members. Pm very proud to be a member of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center Sisterhood. I ask you to share your pride with me at
this Time and throughout the Season; and again, become
INVOLVED.
Best wishes for a happy and healthy year.
