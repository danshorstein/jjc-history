Women’s American ORT, Inc.
Women’s American ORT, a fundraising group which builds
schools, trains teachers and provides high tech tracts of
education in various schools, is alive and active in the
Jacksonville area.
Women’s American ORT - Afternoon Chapter
This group meets the fourth Monday of the month as its
regular meeting. The second Monday, the Board Meetings
are held.
ORT has schools in Israel, the U.S. and throughout 53
other countries. With our commitment to technical educa-
tion, we will see the fruition of our students by giving them
the education and start they never would have had
otherwise.
Locally, the Riverfront Chapter of ORT has women from
26-85, housewives and professionals, singles and married.
We are active in community, and speak out on Jewish

ABOUT NCJW
Established in 1893, the National Council of Jewish
Women is the oldest Jewish women’s organization in
America. NCJW has more than 100,000 members in 200
Sections nationwide that are active in the organization’s
priority areas of Women’s Issues, Jewish Life, Aging,
Children and Youth, Israel, and Constitional Rights.
NCJW Jacksonville Section as been instrumental in
developing and implementing many significant programs in
our community, currently including:
P.A.C.E. - Practical and Cultural Education a struc-
tured academic and vocational program for teenage girls
who have become involved in the court system as status
offenders.
Mental Health Forum - a one-day public forum of mini-
workshops, such as: coping with personal relationships,
family life and stresses, co-sponsored with the Mental Health
Association.
Kosher Kart - delivery of balanced, hot meals to home-
bound individuals.
Guardian Ad Litem - a volunteer program for court
appointed child advocates to represent abused and ne-
glected children in judicial hearings.
Tallahassee Institute - a conclave of concerned women
advocating the issues of NCJW priorities to legislative
representatives.
Ship-A-Box - packaging and sending personal articles to
children in Israel.
Pre-School Vision Screening Since 1971, Council has
participated in an eye screening program for the benefit of
public and private kindergarten students. Hundreds of chil-
dren have been tested under this program each year.
Social Legislation - Through programs and study groups,
and in accordance with recommendations from national
and state legislative chairwomen, this committee brings
legislation to the members and recommends actions.
Green Circle Program - Volunteers present a three
session dialoue with second graders to promote a positive
sense of self-worth and a better understanding of human
differences and similarities in children.
Art Enrichment Program - Picture Ladies - In coopera
tion with the Jacksonville Art Museum and the Duval
County school system, volunteers are trained to go into
classrooms on a regular basis to supplement the art re-
source program in public and private schools.

Tim Riley
And generations shall mourn his passing . . . this man who gave 42 years of his life
to the families of the Jacksonville Jewish Center as a dedicated employee. Tim Riley
gave new meaning to the word loyalty ... It seemed to everyone that the Center was
paramount in Tim’s life because he was here most of the time. He always had a warm
welcome, a big hello and wide smile, even a “Good Yom Tov” greeting. But it was
the High Holidays he enjoyed the best because he could see generations he cared for
sitting together at services. He often reminisced about the “good ole days” at Third
and Silver when he drove the school bus. Oh the stories he could tell!
Winning the Center raffle was one highlight in Tims life, however, his greatest thrill
was his trip to Israel. For months afterwards Tim would proudly show his photo
album to anyone he saw. He was so inspired. Although Tim never converted to
Judaism, he embraced many aspects of the religion. He was very concerned about
Israel’s security. A Zionist, he believed in Israel and supported it as a member of
ZOA.
Tim there are so many things we wanted to say to you, but you left us so
suddenly. We wanted to tell you that you made a difference in so many lives. Your
warmth, caring and loyalty will never be forgotten. You made everyone feel so special
and that’s what made you so special.
Remembrances of Annie Mae
Editor’s Note : On October 10, 1988, Annie Mae Fort, devoted employee of the
Center, passed away. For more than forty years, Annie Mae was the “chief” cook of
our kitchen. During her years with us, she prepared thousands of holiday meals,
school lunches, life cycle receptions, convention meals, camp lunches, scout and
youth meals, board dinners and more. She took the utmost of care with each meal
she prepared, because this was her art. Three generations were priviledged to have
enjoyed this lady’s talent. Rabbi Gaffney, in his eulogy, said it so beautifully: “When I
was told that Annie Mae’s heart failed. / didn’t believe it, Annie Mae’s heart never
failed.”
To honor Annie Mae’s memory, the Jacksonville Jewish Center will dedicate and
purchase a piece of kitchen equipment with the contributions made to the Center.
The following is a beautiful message written by Judy Paul and Marsha Pollock:
It is impossible to speak of Annie Mae Fort other than in superlative terms—kind,
gentle, lovable, capable—a woman in the very best sense of that meaningful term.
We who knew her, loved her; the longer we knew her, the more we loved her.
She possessed an endearing charm that children were drawn to her irresistibly and
adults delighted in her goodness. She saw many of us through kindergarten, Bar and
Bat Mitzvahs, Confirmation, and marriage only to begin again with our children -
experiencing and sharing these same celebrations one generation to the next. Annie
Mae was a tradition, a foundation, an institution! Her spirit remains to comfort us;
she is deep in our hearts and will continue there warmly. We shall miss her.
There are favorite dishes that will be synonymous with our memories of her—
kugel, chopped liver, the famous salad dressing; latkes and so many more tasty treats
prepared with a significant ingredient to make them superb—a touch of love and care
from Annie Mae.
Our dear friend looked for the best in others and gave the best that she had. She
had something of value to contribute, an important expression of her personality that
set her apart and made her different from all others.
Just as her special meals live on in our hearts, in our minds she will always be in
“Annie Mae’s kitchen.”

Cub Scout Pack 14
Cub Scout Pack 14 had another outstanding year. Under the sponsorship of the Jewish Center, this group of 2nd through 5th grade boys
has grown to number 45 active and motivated scouts. Smaller numbered den meetings were held once a week either at the Center or in the
den leader’s home. Pack meetings with all the boys and their families were held once a month.
Cub Scouts aim high: at developing good character, spiritual growth, good citizenship, sportsmanship, comaraderie with other boys, and a
sense of personal achievement. Thanks to our extremely competent and caring den leaders (Peggy Organes, Colleen Gelman, Vivian Levine,
Brenda Brown, and Jay Fiske) the Cubs have earned many badges of achievement while having fun.
Led by Cubmaster Lanny Zimmerman, our pack meetings have been highly successful. The excitement of Pinewood Derby, a representative
from the Supervisor of Elections Office conducting a mock election, a master carpenter handcrafting a tool box right before our eyes, and
many more added to the delightful presentations of the dens. Scout Sabbath, our own Friday evening service, was led by a parent, Sandy
Kadish, and all the scouts attending. A great time was had by all attending our annual father-son campout preceeded by a family dinner and
swim on the Center grounds. The highlight of our year was the Blue and Gold Banquet with our special guest speaker, Sgt. Sonny Connell.
Along with the den leaders, our success depended on the Pack Committee. Special thanks to Lanny Zimmerman, Anne DePaul, Laurie
Farrington, and Debby Kaye. Cub Scouts is based on adult support and involvement. Please call the Center office if you would like to
volunteer.

Adult Education
Adult education has become an integral part of
the ongoing activities of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. During the year new classes and study
groups have been meeting regularly. Whether it
was attendance at a Hebrew class, the Sisterhood
Bar/Bat Mitzvah class, the Shabbaton weekend,
a book review or holiday workshops, our members
came together to grow Jewishly. They thus lent
testimony to the fact that men and women of all
ages in our congregation are interested in be-
coming better informed Jews.
As we look to the new year in terms of adult
education, there is reason to feel excited. Stimu-
lating speakers for the Shabbaton weekends are
going to be visiting and sharing their expertise
with us; mini-sessions are being planned as well
as the popular Hebrew classes. Most importantly,
however, one of the most basic tenets of Judaism,

The River Garden Auxiliary is a dynamic organization committed to serving
the River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged. Dedicated members direct their
energies to service and fundraising projects that provide the residents with
special extras that make their lives more comfortable and enjoyable.
The Auxiliary’s service activities include: an out-to-lunch program providing residents the opportunity
to go to a restaurant on a one to one basis; a “Give Sarah Two Hours” project that recruits volunteers 4
times a year to host residents at various annual Home celebrations such as the Chanukah, party, an
annual blood drive to help replenish River Garden’s blood bank, a sunshine program to personally
welcome each new resident with a small gift. In addition, many Auxiliary members serve as volunteers in
the Home performing a myriad of functions.
Fundraising activities include annual dues, life memberships, Tombola (a raffle), Housewarming party
and Donor. The Auxiliary also maintains three beautiful plaques where names of loved ones may be
placed to be honored or memorialized.
Each March, the Auxiliary joins with the Board of Trustees and Staff to celebrate Anniversary Day by
graciously hosting this annual event.
“Helping Hands” the Auxiliary’s excellent quarterly newsletter keeps the membership appraised of
upcoming events and is sent to each member.
In November, River Garden Hebrew Home will move to its beautiful new campus in Mandarin. The
Auxiliary invites all of its friends to join its organization and become part of the River Garden Family.

Message from the Rabbi
THE ART OF LISTENING
RABBI DAVID GAFFNEY
One of the first prayers an observing Jew recites when he or she begins his or her
day is to thank God for the perceptions with which we are gifted, i.e. the gift of sight,
speech, hearing. On the Days of Awe, Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, it is of parti-
cular importance to develop the ability to listen; to cultivate, if you will, the art of lis-
tening. The central prayer of every service, year round, is, of course, Sh’ma Yisrael . . .
Listen (hear) O Israel. Then there is a prayer which is specific to the holidays — Sh’ma
Kolenu — a plea that God listens to the urgency of our voice. During the Shofar service
the traditional blessing recited by the Baal Tokeah . . . The “Shofar blower” is . . .
“praised are you, O Lord . . . who has commanded us to listen to the Shofar. Note, he
doesn’t say “who has commanded us to sound the Shofar.” Rather “ . . . who has com-
manded us to listen to the Shofar.”
It would appear that the essence of these days is to focus attention on the crucial
importance of listening.
This isn’t the most popular skill in our time. John O’hara, a contemporary author
wrote a short story called “The Man Who Had to Talk to Somebody.” The hero of the story craves to talk about his
problems to anyone who would show him some interest and sympathy. And so he invites a new acquaintance or a fellow
worker to join him at lunch or dinner, hoping that ultimately he might find someone who would listen to him as he poured
out his heart.
He is not an unusual character. He is each one of us. We desperately need someone with whom to share our burdens,
our fears and loneliness, our yearnings and our frustrations. We need someone to release us from the isolation cell into
which we are so frequently manuevered because no one cares enought to liberate us with a sympathetic ear. We are all in
search of someone who has developed the fine art of listening. The Torah portion underscores this need. When Sarah
complains to Abraham about an injustice done her God says to Abraham: “all that Sarah says to you, “Sh’ma b’/co/a”
—listen to her voice. “Try to hear”, God says “not only what she is saying but also to feel the anguish that she is trying to
convey.” It’s great advice for every Abraham and for every Sarah.
During the holiday we will read a contemporary prayer which states, “the person who attends a concert, but whose mind
is on business hears, but does not really hear. The person who walks amid the songs of birds and thinks only of what he will
have for dinner hears, but does not really hear.” On Rosh Hashanah the Shofar exhorts us to cultivate a sense of
hearing ... to develop the art of listening so that we may live a better life. The High Holidays challenge us to what are our
ears attuned. Are we intent upon hearing one another? Do husbands and wives listen to each other? Do parents really take
the time to listen to their children? The art of listening is one of the most crucial skills that we need to develop if we are to
play our role effectively in the unfolding drama of a healthy family. There are also other voices and sounds to which we must
listen. Do we hear the call of our brothers and sisters in the Land of Israel? Do we hear their urgency? Will we respond to
the concerns of Soviet Jews? Will their needs leave its imprint on our lives? Do we respond? As we look about the Jewish
community and consider all of the agencies and institutions that serve the well-being of our Jewish community ... do we
really hear?
The synagogue calls on us for its support. Are we listening? Do we not understand that it is in the synagogue that our
children are educated, our youth guided, our holidays observed, our milestones celebrated, our prayers heard and our faith
and our identities and history nourished and sustained. Do we hear the urgency with which the synagogue calls to us for
support? Will we listen?
These Days of Awe prompt us to remember that each Jew must hear the call of the past. The Shofar sounds remind us
of the revelation at Sinai and the covenant with God which unites us with all the generations that have preceded us and
those, who will, hopefully, follow. The sounds of the Shofar of Sinai, in accordance to tradition, are still sounded. But only for
those who have cultivated the ability to listen.
At Rosh Hashanah the Jew hears the still small voice of conscience that speaks to him. In the Unetana Tokef prayer the
author describes two sounds, “. . . the great Shofar is sounded . . . and the still small voice is heard.” That is the still small
voice of conscience which summons us to do justice, love mercy, and walk only with God. It may speak in whispers in our
age. For this reason it requires, especially, an acute sense of hearing.
In these Holy Days we are asked not to limit our listening to these sacred sounds only to one season of the year. We are
urged to return to the synagogue regularly to listen to the words that are offered in the house of prayer, lest we lose the
strength of our spirit and forget the way our people has journeyed over our long history.
As we approach these holidays let us not only recite the words . . . but listen as well to the prayer, “praised are You, O
Lord our God, King of the Universe who has sanctified us by Your Commandments and commanded us to listen . . .”
From Inge and myself, to you and to yours, best wishes for a healthy, happy and fulfilling year ... a year that will cultivate
the art of listening.
Rabbi David
and Inge Gaffney

A HIGH HOLIDAY THOUGHT'
They tell of Adam:
How frightened he must have been when
for the first time
he saw the sun disappear,
ending the light of day.
It was Adams first darkness!
How could he understand the night,
when he had never seen a dawn?
After the splendor of the sun,
how astonishingly dark the darkness was;
how desperate the long terror of the first fall of night . . .
until Adam learned day would come again:
that there is order in the universe.
And he could begin to see
how much light remains in the sky at night:
the stars, and their enduring promise of the sun.
The returning star of day.
Adam learned
the night is never wholly dark, and no night is endless . . .
even as each of us must learn it
in our own times of trouble and of darkness.
The light is never far.
Adam's story is our story:
Certainly, there is much that is
dark and wrong in the world today
and in ourselves:
meanness , anguish , brutality ,
greed , frustration , despair.
Yet always there is also order
and goodness in the world and in ourselves:
love , courage , sacrifice, creativity,
growth , dedication, regeneration.
In the midst of chaos and change,
whatever may have been changed into evil
can be changed again into goodness,
whether it be a moment or an era.
There is always an order and a permanence
to which we can relate ourselves,
even as, in a booth of straw,
one may look up to the permanence of the sky.
We have been given the power and responsibility
to live our life as if the scales
of what is right and wrong in the world
were exactly balanced,
and our actions change this balance.
To learn how to live this way is not easy.
The occasional impulse toward goodness is not enough.
To meet the challenge of a fulfilled life,
we must train ourselves
to the habit of goodness,
to the continuing act of goodness
at every moment of choice we have.
This power to train ourselves
is one of the greatest of all the powers of Man.
And there are many people—
in the present and of the past—
who can help us develop it.
We are not alone.
The decision is ours to make,
but the achievement is with others.
We are not alone,
and the light is never far.

of the Lord in the land of the living’ *

Solomon Schechter Day School
Being A Cheerleader For Children
With the completion of our new educational facilities at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center, the Solomon Schechter Day school is provided the very best educational
environment in which to teach its students.
The school has at its base an excellent curriculum, qualified staff, supportive PTA, and
untiring support services of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. As one examines our
strength, the question might be asked, “What more is needed?”.
One answer that comes to my mind is that we need to become Cheerleaders for our
children.
We all want our children to be as successful as possible, and we usually believe that
success brings happiness. When we see our children attempting to do something in a
difficult way, we tend to stop them. We want to show them an easier way to accomplish
the feat so that they can’t fail. We are also guilty occasionally of telling them, after the fact, what they could have done to be
more successful in the first place.
There are women who have mothers who still enter their daughter’s kitchens and try to tell them about easier and better
ways to cook. There are men who have fathers who still offer suggestions about how to fix something around the house.
And, most adults resent this kind of help.
We need to know that children feel the same way. They feel they have a right to do the job “on their own”. They may be
open to suggestions — but they certainly don’t want to hear what they did wrong if they fail.
Yet, children need parents and teachers to be their cheerleaders. Children do need encouragement. They need
acknowledgement when they are trying. They do need a “cheerleading” kind of support most of the time.
Let’s analyze the nature of cheerleading as we know it. The purpose of cheerleading is to provide encouragement to
athletes all through a game — even if the team is losing. Good cheerleaders want their teams to receive positive
reinforcement no matter what is happening. They never give up, and they refuse to let others give up on the team no matter
what the score.
As parents and teachers, we can do the same for our children. We can point out the good things our children do. We can
be pleased when they do what we ask. We can notice when they are behaving, instead of only when they are not. We can
pay attention to the number of times we correct their mistakes — and the number of times we praise them for doing
something right.
Make no mistake offering guidance to children does not have to be a negative experience for them. We now know
enough about cheerleading to know that positive reinforcement works better than criticism. Our children will fail
sometimes. The way we handle such failure is as important as the way we handle success. We must assure our children
that we are behind them. Most important though, we need to know that our children need cheerleaders and we are the
best ones they can have. In truth, we are the ones they should have.

The River Garden Hebrew Home . . .
Keeper of the “Fifth Commandment”
“Honor thy father and mother that thy day may be long ...”
It was this commandment which inspired the founding of River Garden Hebrew Home in 1946. It was this commandment
which nourished the love and care that over the years has made River Garden a nationally recognized model of excellence
in its field.
It is the commandment which underlies the tradition of meeting 100% of local Jewish community needs and of serving
rich and poor alike with excellence and caring.
The new River Garden Hebrew Home is on a 33-acre campus in Mandarin, near the intersection of 1-295 and Old St.
Augustine Road. The design of the Traditional Care, Special Care and Short-Term Rehabilitation pavilions recognizes that
people with medical needs do not have to live in medical settings. The new River Garden Home will be part of a larger
community which will include apartments and villas; a town center; a general store; a fitness center, and a campus club.
Overall, River Garden is today and tomorrow a community where people live, love, and care; a community of many life
styles and of rich Jewish content, and a community which every day strives to live up to the mandate of the Fifth
Commandment.
JACKSONVILLE JEWISH FEDERATION
8505 San Jose Blvd. • Jacksonville, FL 32217 • (904) 448-5000
The Jacksonville Jewish Federation is the central fundraising,
planning, budgeting and coordinating agency for the Jewish com-
munity. It strives to address the needs of the Jewish community,
encouraging community participation. Its diverse committees offer
many opportunities for volunteer involvement.
The Federation funds and coordinates community relations and
education programs. It publishes a monthly newspaper of Jewish
news of local and national interest. The Federation’s Israel and
New York Teen Experiences and Young Leadership program
examine Jewish life and history and give participants an oppor-
tunity to experience their Jewishness in unique and meaningful
ways.
The Federation conducts an annual campaign that raises funds
for the United Jewish Appeal, as well as other national and
overseas agencies. Local beneficiaries are the Jewish Community
Alliance, Jewish Family and Community Services, the Kosher
Nutrition Program, River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged and
the chapters of various Jewish organizations.

but no less important, holidays.

Camp Ramah
Camp Ramah is a very special place where you not only learned about Judaism, but you
also live it. Anyone who has ever visited Ramah has been touched by the unique spirit that
grows there. Ramah offers a full range of camp athletic and social activities in addition to
its special blend of Jewish cultural and social activities.
Over the years, RAMAH has helped instill a deeper commitment to Judaism in many of
its participants. The Center believes that benefits accrue to the congregation, not just to
the individual. We have, therefore, made monies available to reduce the financial cost of
qualified students, regardless of need. These monies are not part of the regular Center
budget, but rather are raised by donations to the Myra Thier Lazarus
Ramah Fund, Center Sisterhood, scholarships and from other dona-
tions made throughout the year.
Abe Selber established in his will a perpetual trust for specific Jewish
education purposes. With the full support of his children, Iris and Franklin,
who have never lost interest in the Center, the proceeds of the Abe
Selber Trust have been used to send campers to Ramah.
We are grateful to Horty Gendzier who coordinates this worthwhile
program.

Rabbi Dov Kcntof Honored
On Saturday evening, September 9, 1989, the Jacksonville
Jewish Center hosted a very special dinner and program
honoring Rabbi Dov Kentof, Director of Education and
Youth, for his eleven years of service and dedication to the
Jacksonville Jewish Center and the Jewish youth in our
community .
Rabbi Kentof also helps plan a one-week regional encampment during August
for USY and Kadima members - Leadership Training Institute (LTI) - North
Carolina. LTI allows our young people to interact with other Jewish youth on
both a social and educational level.
The Junior congregation at the Jacksonville Jewish Center, run by Rabbi
Kentof, provides a perfect way for the students of our schools to put into
practice, every Shabbat, the skills they have attained in the classroom setting.
Rabbi Kentof has organized and presented such programs as “The Land of
the Torah”, “The World of our Grandparents”, “The Jerusalem Fair”, and each
year, a Holocaust Commemoration.
Rabbi Kentof, and his wife Alina, also an educator have devoted much time
and effort into the development of a strong supplemental school curriculum, as
well as a highly respected and admired Youth Program.
To Rabbi Dov Kentof education is a living experience that is
not limited to a classroom. During his eleven years at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center he has continually demonstrated this
through his many camping experiences and hands-on
programming.
Rabbi Kentof has used his talents at the Jacksonville Jewish
Center to perpetuate three youth groups, each one very well-known
for their service projects in our community - Chalutzim, Charles
Palot Kadima, and the United Synagogue Youth Group (USY).
His devotion to our young people and his ability to create
activities for specific age levels have helped the organizations
thrive. He is a role model with which young people can identify,
whether it be leading and coordinating night games, camp fires,
day trips or conventions, Rabbi Kentof constantly encourages
independence and leadership while nurturing leadership and com-
mands respect by being a devoted leader himself - always full of
energy, willing to listen, and share the ideas of others.
In addition to being Director of the Youth Program at the Center, Rabbi Kentof is the principal of the Supplemental
School Programs - Sunday School, Afternoon Hebrew School, and Hebrew High School. As is part of his philosophy, Rabbi
Kentof encourages parental participation and involvement in Jewish education. He helped create a parent/teacher associa-
tion for his supplemental programs, called AUTO-Adults United to Organize. The parents of this Supplemental School PTA
help Rabbi Kentof and the teachers plan learning experiences and special events both in and out of the classroom. Some of
the most significant and meaningful events occur at Rabbi Kentof’s Shabbat camp-outs, where he, and his wife Alina,
organize and participate in a truly beautiful Shabbat experience for everyone who
attends.

In Memoriam
Judge Louis Safer
The Jacksonville Jewish Center mourns the loss of Judge Louis Safer. A past president of the Center, Lou was truly an
exemplary individual—a beautiful man who embodied the highest ideals of Judaism.
An a very early age, Louis Safer was taught by his parents to be a “mentsch.” It is apparent that he took this advice to
heart and followed it in his personal and professional life. As a Jew he was a model in terms of leadership and ideals. He had
a genuine and untiring concern for his fellow man.
One of the youngest presidents of the Jacksonville Jewish Center, Lou’s dedication and wisdom always set a fine
example of leadership for our congregation. When the Jewish Center met on Third and Silver streets and two High Holiday
services were conducted at the same time, our honoree helped conduct one of those services. This is how he got the name,
“Upstairs Rabbi.” Lou even filled in as a lay rabbi when the Center was without professional rabbinic leadership. Throughout
his life he attended all the holiday and festival services.
Just as he was highly regarded in the Jewish community, Judge Safer was also extremely respected by the legal
community. He was highly rated in the yearly polls conducted by the Jacksonville Bar Association on area judges. In 1987,
the lawyers ranked him as the top judge in the circuit in terms of legal ability, objectivity, demeanor and diligence. Chief
Judge John Santora Jr., called him the judiciary’s “nicest guy,” and said Judge Safer “was always the No. 1 judge in the 4th
Judicial Circuit in popularity with the lawyers. Likewise with the judges.
“He never, ever said one bad word about a living soul his entire life that I heard,” Santora said.
Lou Safer’s life was dedicated to helping young people. His son, Eliot Safer, who is a vice-president of the Center, said he
felt his father was proudest of his involvement “with the beginning of the Guardian Ad Litem program,” in which residents
represent abused and neglected children before community agencies and courts.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center as well as the City of Jacksonville extend condolences to Judge Safer’s wife, Marilyn; his
son Eliot; and his daughters, Cynthia Baron, Deena Richman and Shana Safer and the other members of the family.
Louis Safer changed the course of many lives with his wisdom and dedication. There is an emptiness in this world that
may not be filled for a very long time.

Cantor Emeritus Abraham Marton
of Blessed Memory
Although he’s gone — his melodies linger on
The Jacksonville Jewish Center mourns the loss of Cantor Emeritus Abraham Marton. The Center has received hundreds of
cards , letters and contributions in his memory. The following article was written by Howard Rosenblatt, former student of
Cantor Marton and son of Gert Rosenblatt. Howard expresses very beautifully the sentiments of the congregation.
This past Shabbat I returned to Jacksonville and as I sat in Shul I sensed an emptiness in the air. As I dovened Minchah in the
chapel, I noticed as I completed the silent devotion that there was no one there prompting the Ba’al Tefilah to begin the
repetition of the Amidah. Cantor Marton always did that. From now on, in my mind’s ear, I’ll hear his prompting; but this
Shabbat, the silence was deafening.
But I don’t want to dwell on what we’ve lost so much as what we had. For parts of five decades (’40s - ’50s), Cantor Marton
worked with the children of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. At least four of his students have served congregations as cantors.
Other have become leaders in both the general and Jewish communities in Florida and elsewhere.
Although he officiated at some of our brittim and many of our weddings, to most of us Cantor Marton will always be identified
wth our Bar or Bat Mitzvah. It was he who prepared us for this momentous change in status. Even today as I teach Bar and Bat
Mitzvah students in Gainesville, Florida, I find myself reaching back for a phrase, a technique or perhaps a style of response with
the ruler to keep time to the music at his fingertips. I can hear his deep voice giving life to the notes.
For the last several years when I visited the Center, Joel Bressler was serving as Cantor, but whenever anyone asked “How’s
the Cantor?”, it was clear that it was Cantor Marton who was the subject of the question. For many of us he was “The Cantor”.
I have studied with several cantors these past 25 years, but Cantor Marton was always, “The Cantor”. I learned from watching
him, from listening to him, and from studying with him. He encouraged me and was very supportive. We had several honest and
frank discussions and even if we disagreed, I always regarded him as a mentor. Even though the man may be gone, his ideas, the
wisdom he imparted and the beautiful sounds he gave to our lives are a living legacy. From the Shabbat melodies we know so
well to the high holiday tones which lifted us up to higher levels of spiritual awareness to the concerts which broaden and deepen
our appreciation of Jewish music, Cantor Marton found ways to reach each of us. I trust the traditions he started will continue
and I join with the Jacksonville Jewish Center in mourning the loss of our cantor and friend, Abraham Marton. May he rest in
peace.
Reprinted from the Jacksonville Jewish Center Bulletin of July 28,1989

Dignity After Death
While the world around us continues to debate death with dignity and “when to pull the plug” on patients kept alive by artificial
means, few people appear to care what happens to the body after death has occurred.
For almost two thousand years, traditional Judaism has cared to treat the deceased with as much dignity as one would have in
life. Plain wnite shrouds to cover the dead replaced ostentatious garments. Public viewing of the remains was not allowed
—better to remember the deceased as they were in life.
Burial societies were formed in every town and city to make sure that standards of loving kindness were maintained for all.
Much respect was given to a member of the Holy Society, or Hevra Kaddisha, as these groups were called.
Jacksonville has been fortunate to have an active Hevra Kaddisha for almost thirteen years, although the number of active
members has been smaller than desired. Additional members can serve to enhance dignity after death even more than has been
done in the past.
A “Hevra” member serves without pay to handle some aspect of caring for the deceased or the remaining close relatives.
Various ways they can serve are:
(1) participating with other members of the committee to wash, shroud and place the body in a plain wooden coffin,
(2) telephoning other members of the committee to arrange for them to meet as the need arises,
(3) providing transportation for the Shomer, the person (or persons) who sits with the deceased from the time the
body has been placed in the casket until the funeral,
(4) preparing the Meal of Consolation, the first meal which the family members eat after they have returned from
the burial,
(5) making arrangements in the house of mourning for the 7 day Shiva period, including preparation of meals,
(6) baby-sitting the small children of the family when necessary, and so on.
“How can I expect someone to care for me after I die if I don’t help others who need me now?”
Please contact your rabbi , the local Hevra Kaddisha president, if you are interested in serving on our committee of
dedicated workers.

Boy Scout Troop 14
Troop 14 continued its proud heritage in 1988-89. The Troop, founded in 1919, forged into its eighth decade
of serving the community’s youth riding high on a wave of success.
The Troop featured outdoor activities, interesting programs, service to the congregation and community,
and dedication to advancement and the ideals of scouting.
The year was highlighted by scouts Jeff Ghelerter and Sean Usoffof receiving the coveted Eagle Scout
Award. The rank of Eagle signifies excellence in character and achievement. The Eagle presentation was made
Blanche Slott at the annual Blue & Gold Banquet before several hundred onlookers. Scouts Adam DePaul
and James West have completed the Eagle requirements and been approved for the rank of Eagle. They are
slated to receive their awards this December.
Camping destinations this year included: Ginnie Springs where the scouts swam in the crystal clear springs
and the Santa Fe River along the banks of which they camped and made “campfire” style pizza; Fort Clinch
State Park in Femandina Beach where the scouts competed the rugged 16-mile historic trail hike and toured
the fascinating pre-Civil War bastion; Cumberland Island where the scouts explored the Carnegie’s private
island and amazed at the wild horses and unusual terrain; and Blue Springs where the scouts sampled the
ultimate low in camping cuisine.
Other camping adventures included Paynes Prairie State Preserve, featuring visits to the Florida State
Museum in Gainesville and attendance at a University of Florida football game, following which the Troop took
the field for a game of their own; the annual trip to Walt Disney World where the scouts “roughed it” at Fort
Wilderness in the Magic Kingdom; Scout World and a campout with the Cub pack at the Center.
Program highlights this year included a CPR training course; instruction by representatives of the Sheriffs
office on the history and techniques of criminal identification; attendance at a JU basketball game; and a hike
through beautiful San Marco to the Museum of Science and History punctuated with a luncheon stop at
Worman’s Deli.
The scouts’ efforts were not entirely nihilistic. Troop 14 served lunch to the residents of Mount Carmel
Gardens on Thanksgiving Day as has become an annual tradition. The Scouts performed road clearing work
and ground improvements at the JJC, built shelters for ducks which inhabit the Center pond, collected
clothing for the needy and landscaped a church, all under the planning and direction of our Eagle Scouts.
Scouts attending summer camp this year included Bemie Echt, David Moskovitz, Brian Usoff, Adam Dean,
Andy Ussach, Hershel Bettman and Jason Dacks. James West provided outstanding leadership as Senior
Patrol Leader.
Troop 14 is for boys grades 6 (or age 11) through 12. Those interested may contact Randy Silverberg at
398-0038 (w) or 733-6649 (h) or Harry Peltz 737-0309.

AUTO — Adults United to Organize
AUTO, Adults United To Organize, is an organization that encourages communica-
tion among parents, students, teachers, and the administrator of the Sunday and
afternoon schools of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. It provides a means to share our
concerns about our children’s learning environment and to work for common goals.
This organization enables us to know more about our children’s experiences in the
supplemental schools and during junior congregation.
AUTO has had some outstanding programming. We have holiday programming for
Hanukkah, Purim, and Passover. These programs involve preparing traditional foods,
explaining traditional practices, having workshop units, and singing of songs associated
with the holidays.
A favorite activity is our annual December campout at Goldhead State Park. Friday
night and Saturday morning services are held in the serenity of the outdoors. During
the afternoon there is time to relax, take walks through the lovely grounds, and listen to
Rabbi Dov Kentof tell stories. The day draws to a close with a Havdalah service. We
leave with something more than what we came with.
Sue Ann Blumenthal

Message From Education
Youth Director
Rabbi Dov Kentof
The importance of education is paramount in Judaism. The building of the sane-
tuary was certainly of great consequence to the Children of Israel, but the Talmud
nevertheless rules, “Children who attend school are not to be removed from the
study of the sanctuary” (Shabbat 119). So, throughout Jewish history, parents have
made sacrifices to ensure that their children have received a good education.
The obligation to educate ones children is addressed in the Torah to the parent,
as stated in the first passage of the Shema — v'shinantam I'vanecha, “and you shall
teach them diligently to your children” (Deuteronomy 6). But this was not always
possible since sometimes the child was an orphan, and the first-century Rabbi
Yehoshua ben Gamla ordained that the education of children was to be a communal
responsibility.
The Talmud discusses the question, which is of greater importance, study or
action, and unanimously concludes that education and knowledge have priority,
since they teach a person the correct manner of action and observance (Kiddushin
40). Ignorance has always been frowned upon in Judaism: an am haaretz (a “person
of the soil”, an ignoramus) was considered the lowest category of individual in the
community.
Certainly, the age of bar mitzvah or bat mitzvah is no time to finish ones Jewish
education. It is, in fact, the signal for further study in a more serious vein, now that
one is obliged to fulfil ones religious obligations with a fuller understanding and appre-
ciation of their purpose.
The Hebrew word for education is chinuch (“consecration”), aimed at teaching a child not merely how to make a living, but how
to live. Parents who see education only as a means to ensure a reasonable livelihood or a successful career for their children are
providing but a fraction of the total education called for by Jewish tradition.
“Train a child in the way he should go, and even when he is old he will not depart from it,” declares Proverbs. The proper purpose
of education is to instill into a child’s mind the ethical and moral values of Judaism; the active observance of the mitzvot through
knowledge of the sources; a strong sense of identification with the Jewish people and Eretz Yisreal together with concern for all
mankind.
We pride ourselves in having a fine education and youth program at the Jacksonville Jewish Center and as being members of
United Synagogue of America, a Conservative body of all affiliated congregations.
Experience shows that education in theory without personal involvement and enforcement throughout practice has no impact
upon young people. Therefore, we have put into practice youth activities, Junior Congregation, family Shabbat services and Family
Council programs, graduation, Confirmation, Consecreation, etc. In this manner, our school and home are functioning as one.
We would like to hope that all families of our congregation will support our endeavors. After all, the welfare of your children are
our first concern.
Let me wish you and yours a happy and healthy New Year.
Carole Scott Edith Horovitz
Secretary Bar/Bat Mitzvah

President’s Message
The pages of last year's calendar diary have been filled, and we marvel that another year
has passed.
The advent of the High Holidays encourages us to pause before plunging into the New Year,
to reflect before filling the pages of next year's calendar. We have completed another yearly
cycle, its days cannot be reclaimed. Are we fully satisfied with the way we used our time?
In his book, Agada - The Language of Jewish Faith , Rabbi Samuel Karff suggests that the
lure of the High Holy Days demonstrates our awareness of unspoken obligations in our lives
—of a life long contract. He says that . . . “the liturgical theme of that season responds to the
deepest sensibilities of . . . Jews. We are a year older. Another chapter in our book of life has
been completed. We do not deny that something is expected of us, or that at least in some
ways we have failed to fulfill our responsibility . . . We need confirmation that we are worthy
despite our flaws, and that new opportunities will be granted us to more adequately meet the
needs of our contract.”
We should therefore ask ourselves about the year past: How well did we use our time? How
much did we attempt? How hard did we try? How much did we grow?
Personal growth comes as a result of willingness to accept challenges, to risk failures. When
asked to assume a position of leadership our impulse may be to respond with a safe “I can’t”
rather than the riskier “I’ll try”. When asked to participate in study or synagogue activities our inclination may be to shield our ignorance
by responding with the safe “I don’t have time” rather than the riskier “I’ll do my best”.
We, at the Center, have one of the finest synagogue facilities in the country with programing to complement it. Let’s use it! In the
coming year, plan your life cycle events here, study here, socialize here, Volunteer here!
The first steps are always here; but having made the commitment we gain a new sense of fulfillment.
Thanks to my Women’s League Colleague, Carol Witt, who introduced me to the concept of a life-long contract.
The blank pages of our calendar diary for the year 5750 are open before us. Once again we are challenged to meet the terms of our
contract.
From my family to yours best wishes for a happy healty New Year.

To keep Israel strong, we work hard to keep the Zionist Organization of America
strong. Since 1948 we have generated funds for ZOA projects in Israel and Zionist Historian
programs in the United States. ZOA has organized young adults leadership missions, JNF Chairman
instituted the first series of Christian Zionist dialogues, educated over 30,000 Jewish
students at Kfar Silver Technical/Agricultural High School in Ashkelon, services over 25,000 Americans and Israelis each month with
cultural programs in the ZOA House in Tel Aviv. Besides publishing The American Zionist and ZOA insider, there is the Zionist

and letters to the editor of topical US-Israeli, Zionist and Jewish community issues, testified before Congress on Free Trade legislation
and moving the US Embassy in Israel to Jerusalem, and petitioned and demonstrated on rescinding US resolution 3370, rejecting US
dialogue with the PLO and opposing the sale of AWAC plans to Arab nations.

Jacksonville Jewish Center Receives
Six Awards at Biennial Convention
The Southeast Region of the United Synagogue of America
held its Biennial Convention in Miami November 18-21. The
Convention brought together delegates from seven southeastern
states and Puerto Rico for four days of study worship, workshops
and social events. .Representing the Center were Marsha and
Ronnie Pollock, Wendy and Joe Honigman.
The highlight of the weekend came when Center President
Marsha Pollock accepted SIX MAJOR PROGRAM AWARDS
on behalf of the congregation:
• Holocaust: Programs designed to teach the Holocaust have
been continually presented to the Center Schools and general
membership. From the Holocaust Museum to the Hebrew High
Curriculum, Rabbi Kentof has made this a priority program. Last
year a Holocaust survivor visted the Center schools. She related her experience at Auschwitz and how she became the
possessor of the “Auschwitz Album.” She further related her story to members of the AUTO and Family Council.
• College Outreach: In an effort to “keep in touch” with our collegians, the Center sends care packages to them for
holiday celebrations and observances. Letters from Rabbis Gaffney and Kentof are always included with information about
the holiday as well as Jewish current events and issues. This Hanukkah, Shari Steinberg received her package at the
University of Alabama. Excited with her menorah and candles, she invited the other Jewish students in her dorm to join
her as she lighted he menorah. A little bit of light touched so many who could not be home for the joyous holiday, and the
Center is glad to have been the torch. Our thanks to Horty Gendzier and her committee for coordinating this worthwhile
effort.
• Youth Activities: Once again our youth activitities received the award for its quality programs. Through leadership
training, programs and social events, the synagogue is able to stimulate the educational, spiritual and social growth of its
young members. Chalutzim, Kadima and SY programs have been responsible for building the leaders of tomorrow just as
they have developed the leaders of today. Our thanks to the advisors: Evelyn Moskovitz and Sheree Spriggs, Marilynn and
Ira Goldfield and Robert Cantor: the youth commission and Rabbi Dov Kentof, youth director.
• Family Council: The Family Council was designed by Rabbi Gaffney and Rabbi Dov Kentof to broaden the opportunities
for education and celebration of Jewish life for the entire family. In addition, the programs help to promote the home as an
equal partner with the school in creating a vital Jewish environment for the children. Holiday workshops and celebrations,
campouts, as well as group discoussions create healthy environments for Jewish learning and interaction. The group meets
on a regular basis. Families are encouraged to join.
• Solomon Schcchter Day School: In its twenty-eighth year, the Solomon Schechter Day School continues to offer
quality education to all Jewish children of the community. Fully-accredited by the Solomon Schechter Day School
Association and the Florida Council of Independent Schools, the Day School educates students from kindergarten to the
seventh grade in General and Judaic Studies.
Our appreciation to the principal, Willard Kennedy, and the education committee for helping to make the Solomon
Schechter Day School a “School of Excellence.”
• Afternoon Hebrew School: This school of the Center received an award for its curriculum which is designed to develop
a working knowledge in the students of Hebrew, the Siddur, prayers, holidays and mitzvot. Jewish values are also learned
through the many awareness programs. When the parents drop off the children, they are encouraged to “drop in.”
Education on Wheels and study sessions áre planned for parents while the children are in their Hebrew School classes. Our
We are grateful to the professional staff and the
devoted corps of volunteers for making these pro-
grams so successful. The award-winning programs
and other Center program services are created to
inspire and educate our members. Take advantage
of these wonderful opportunities for Jewish growth
for yourself, your children and your grandchildren.

Torah Reading 1988-89
Recently, I had an opportunity to review a video recording in which I
stressed the words of Waldemar Haffkine, quoted in the Supplementary
Readings of our Siddur. He was a biological scientist who saved thousands
of lives of the people of India. He says, “The Torah is a fountain of life. It
is a protection greater than in fortresses.” This eternal truth is still valid
for us today. The observance of the Torah and its continued weekly
readings maintains the vitality of Jewish life. It is interesting to note that
while the previous generation of Torah Readers have remained loyal and
continue to meet the advancing need for synagogue leadership, a new
generation is coming into the ranks. Haffkine’s encouraging words will
always remain as an inspiration for this new group.
We are thankful to the many “young” people who have added reading of the Torah as a
meaningful source of value to their major activities. We hope that their reward will include
satisfaction in all their endeavors.
Ted Gross
