In recognition of his untiring and loyal efforts
in hehalf of this institution, the Jacksonville Jewish
Center dedicates this 3óth edition of the Year Book
to Mr. Harry Finkelstein, its Honorary President.
J oday when we take the Center for granted,
and accept its services and facilities as naturally as
the air we breathe, we are prone to forget the hard
struggle which, not so long ago, some of our valiant
and far sighted leaders waged to make the Center
possible.
Harry Finkelstein, first president of the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center, from 1927 to 1929, bore the
brunt of the fight to assure the erection of the build¬
ing and the establishment of the institution upon
a firm foundation. His impeccable reputation as a
business man, his position of esteem and honor among
Jacksonville citizenry, and his tenacity and persistence
in pursuing the cause of the Jewish Center, Were
weighty factors leading toward success.
We who reap the fruit of his labor, are happy
that U ncle Harry , as he is affectionately known,
is wi th us, and we rejoice with him in the constant
growth of the institution to which he gave much
ol his time, energy and substance.
I his book dedicated to him is but an inadequate
expression of our gratitude, and our esteem for him.
At the beginning of the New Year we pray that God
may grant him and his family, good health, prosperity
and many happy years.

An Operetta in 6 Scenes Based on Jewish hoik Songs
Orchestra Conducted by
FIERCE P. DROHAN
Arranged for Orchestra by
W. H. PERRINS
Produced and Directed by RABBI MORRIS D. MARGOLIS
On Marc h 12th the J acksonville Jewis h Com¬
munity witnessed a dramatic and musical event which
may rightly be considered the most elaborate and
most beautiful ever given in Jacksonville by the Jew¬
ish Community.
J he operetta J he Wedding attempts to mirror
in song and pantomine a fragment of Jewish folklife
typical of Eastern Europe. I he music consists of
twenty well-known Jewish folk songs translated into
English and woven into a story ol the love of a
Y esh iva student and a Jewish girl.
Rabbi Morris D. Margol is, who wrote several
original melodies for the operetta, also directed the
entire production and trained the large choir. Mem¬
bers of the W. P. A. Florida Symphony Orchestra
provided a string ensemble consisting of two violins,
two second violins, two violas, cello, bass and piano.
The orchestration o f the songs was made by W. H.
Pe rrins o f the W. P. A. composers forum, under the
direction of Mr. Charles LePaige. I he Federal Theatre
costume shop designed and made the costumes. J he
dance numbers were under the direction of Miss
Ednis Dunbar of the W. P. A. Recreation Project
and Mr. Ran Meyer ol the Federal J heatre was in
charge ol make up.
I o assure the success of the operetta, Rabbi
Margolis secured the cooperation of the Junior Ha-
dassah and the A. Z. A., whose members took an
active part in the chorus and cast, as well as the
assistance of all other Jewish organizations meeting
in the Center. The result was most gratifying. Over
600 witnessed the first performance and about 250
attended the repeat performance on March 22nd, both
of which were held in the Scottish Rite Temple.
From the opening of the curtain for the first
scene to the closing of the curtain for the last scene
the audience beheld a spectacle of simple beauty, of
pulsating rhythm and enchanting melody. The entire
cast performed admirably and showed little evidence
of its amateur standing. The orchestra under the di¬
rection of Pierce F. Drohan called forth the admira¬
tion and the repeated applause of the large audience.
J he chorus, under the direction of Rabbi Margolis,
sang beautifully and with precision.
The cast consisted of the following: Sylvia Price,
Mrs. Abrah am TrattI er, Meyer Leibovitz, Morris Lei¬
bovitz, Morris Nimovitz, Melvin Margol, Raymond
Cohen, Isadore Setzer, Leo Moskovitz, Irvin Slott, Mrs.
N. V. G ross, Sylvia Leidman, Israel Safer, and Mrs.
D. Torn narrator.
The following committee was in charge of ar¬
rangements: I. M. Lieberman, Murray Glickstein, Hy¬
man Selber, Mrs. B. Chepenik, Miss Ruth Davis, Ben
Chpenik , Philip Selber and Joseph Hackel.

DAVID MOSCOVITZ
Among the important things we have to do dur¬
ing the coming year is the renovation of the Center
building, especially the synagogue. It is my leverent
hope that we shall approach this task with reverence
and that we will secure the full cooperation oí our
members in this undertaking.
We believe that the great masses ol the Ameri¬
can people are against the vicious, barbaric and
undemocratic ideas which agents of foreign powers
are attempting to disseminate among them. We have
good reason to look with con fid ence towards the New
Ye ar 5700 and to prepare ourselves lor the constructive
task of furthering the religious, social and educational
program of our institution.
American Jews face the New Year with mixed
feelings of fear and con fid ence. During the pasi
year the condition ol Jews all over the world has
become aggravated, and we naturally looked with
apprehension at the increased anti-semitic agitation in
this country. At the same time we were greatly im¬
pressed and encouraged by the numerous Christian
organizations that have undertaken the struggle against
religious intolerance.
1 think that we may be proud o f the fine progress
our institution has made during the past year. o ur
Synagogue services were rendered with dignity and
were well attended. Both our Hebrew School and
Sunday School enjoyed a successful school term.
1 hose who were present at the closing exercises will
long remember the many beaming faces of the pupils
as they received the awards for good attendance, ex¬
cellence in study and general progress. It was grati¬
fying that so many parents attended and showed keen
interest ol the Jewish education ol their children. I
believe that we cannot fail to remember the very
impressive and beautiful confirmation service on the
second night ol Shovuos, which deeply moved the
large congregation. We are proud of the many cap¬
able boys who became Bar Mitzvah during the year.
I he fine attitude ol these boys and girls toward their
religion, their continued interest in Jewish life through
Center club activities, is an encouraging sign for the
future.
Perhaps the most impressive affair of the year was
the presentation of the operetta The Wedding ”, whose
tuneful Jewish melodies, beautiful costumes, excellent
singing and acting and able direction of the chorus and
orchestra, will be remembered as a cultural event which
has never been excelled in the past.
h is also satisfying to record that we have been
able to meet all of our financial obligations, an ac¬
complishment which is largely due to the loyal work of
our finance committee, headed by Mr. B. Baker.
Personally I feel grateful that Cod has spared me
to be with you. I pray that He will fully restore my
health so that 1 may be able to serve the Jewish com¬
munity to the best of my ability.
1 should like to take this opportunity to thank Mr.
H yman Selber, vice-president, who during my illness
assumed and ably performed the duties of my office.
I urge the members of the Jacksonville Jewish Cen¬
ter to patronize our advertisrs who made possible this
year book. 1 o all our members, friends and advertisers,
1 extend the greetings of the season. May the Almighty
C »od inscribe you in the book of lile, prosperity and
happiness. Amen.

RABBI MORRiS D. MARGOLIS
With a world hovering on the brink ol war,
and mankind facing the problem of resistance to
aggression or submission to the power of ruthless
brutality, the Jew finds his position indicated in
the prayers of the High Holy Days.
During the entire penitential season the Jew
recites Psalm 27 twice daily. The Lord is my light
and my salvation; whom shall I fear? The Lord is
tlie stronghold of my life; of whom shall I be afrai d?
When evil-doers drew ni gh against me to eat up
my flesh, even my adversaries and my foes, they
stumbled and fell. Though an host should encamp
against me, my heart would not fear; though war
should rise against me, even then would 1 be con¬
fident. One thing have I asked of the Lord, that will
1 seek after: That I may dwell in the house ol the
Lord, and to inquire in his temple.
The Jew who clings to his religion and finds
com fort in the house of God, does not despair under
even the most trying circumstances. Cmd is his refuge
and strongho Id. O nly those Jews whose attachment
to their faith is weak try to find escape in conversion,
as did many Jews in Italy, or in suicide, as did many
Jews in Austria and Germany. Such Jews fall like
withered leaves blown by the wind.
J he Jew h as survived the oppressions, inquisi¬
tions and wars o f the centuries because the bulk of
our people remained firmly attached to the Etz
. Because of it our people
has remained full of the sap of life and creative
energy to this very day. The achievements of Jewish
pioneers in Palestine in spite of terrorism and White
Papers is symbolic of our essential health and vigor.
We must realize that our fate does not depend
upon a government or human dictator, but upon our
own moral and spiritual strength. As the psalmist
put it, Do not trust in princes, in the son of man,
in whom there is no help, when his breath departeth,
lie returneth to his earth; in that every day his de¬
signs perish.
Our future depends upon our faith in God and
in the ethical ideals of our religion.
When tragedy has befallen so many of our
brethren, we must and confirm our belief that ulti¬
mately the world will be perfected under the kingdom
of the Almighty”, and that the dominion of wicked¬
ness and cruelty will disappear.
This should be our faith at this critical moment

Jewish undertaking. It is, therefore, the home of the

THE CENTER SYNAGOGUE

J he Jacksonville Jewish Center maintains a mod¬
ern Hebrew School, which meets four times a week,
in the afternoons and five times a week when sessions
are held in the morning. Last year the Hebrew School
had an enrollment of 90 children. There were 7
classes; a beginner s class for young children between
the ages of 6 and 8 years; a beginners class for
children between 8 and 10 years of age; one ele¬
mentary Hebrew class; one advanced Hebrew class;
one Chumosh class; one Bar Mitzvah class and
one post-Bar Mitzvah class. There was also a class
for advanced pupils in Sunday School, who had
failed to attend Hebrew School, which met twice a
week. A bus was operated to provide transportation
for pupils residing in the Riverside section.
The curriculum of the Hebrew School aims to
develop in the child a sense of reverence for his re¬
ligion, a deep interest in the ideals and aspirations
of the Jewish people and to develop in him the ability
to participate actively in the observance of Jewish
festivals, customs and ceremonies, both in the Syna¬
gogue and at home. The text used during the past
year included, Sifri Part 1, Hamehim Lamikra Part
1 and 2, Olami Part 1 and 2, Sipuri Hatorah
(Chumosh) and the J. P. S. Junior Bible in English.
The Center maintains a Sunday School, which
last year had an enrollment of over 200 children,
divided into 12 grades. J here was one junior kinder¬
garten, with children from 4 to 5 years of age, one
senior kindergarten, with children from 5 to 6 years
of age, nine regular classes, the last of which is the
Confirmation Class, and one Post-Confirmation Class.
TL
Le curriculum of the Sunday School aims to
give tne Jewish child a thorough knowledge of Jewish
customs and ceremonies. Textbooks used during the
past year included, a kindergarten manual and mater¬
ial; J he Bible Jingles Coloring book, supplemented
by teachers’ stories (grades land 2); Children’s His¬
tory of Israel, Part 1 and 2 (grades 3 and 4); When
the Jewish People Was Young (grade 5); How the
Jewish People’ Grew Up (Grade 6); History of the
Jew In Modern limes, Zelig’s (grade 7 and 8); The
Story of the Jew or Post Biblical Jewish History
(Confirmation and Post-Confirmation Class).
An excellent teacher s staff was in charge of these
classes, the attendance was very good and all classes
made progress. An honor point contest, in which all
children participated, was conducted throughout the
year. Fifteen prizes were presented to those who
had the highest number of honor points. Points were
given for good attendance, for excellence in study
and for participation in the various extra-curricular
activities of the school. Louis Safer, Lillian Green-
baum, Annette Hammerman, Sonya Heyman and
Gertrude Meyerhoff were the first five on the list
of winners. The El ias H. Pilton memorial cup, do¬
nated by the children of the late Elias H. Pilton was
awarded to grade 7 for having scored the highest
average ol points in the contest.
Jluring the year the school sponsored many
interesting events on the occasions of major and minor
festivals. 1 he Chanukah program and the Purim
Carnival were happy events in the lives of the
children. Other occasions celebrated were Simchas
J orah. Cham is ho B She vat, Lag B omer and the
Children s Seder. The school choir also participated
in the operetta.

Each Sabbath morning, while services are being conducted in the main
Synagogue, children of the Center s religious schools gather in the small syna¬
gogue and hold separate services under the supervision of two members of
the Hebrew school staff.
I he ritual consists of selections in English and Hebrew from the prayer-
book, congregational singing and responsive reading. In this way, the Junior
Congregation has been instrumental in developing our children s knowledge
of Hebrew.
During the past year, weekly “sermonettes” dealing with highlights of the
Bible were delivered by the attending instructors, Mr. M. Nimovitz, and Mr. J.
S. Gallinger, the Rabbi and selected members of the congregation. These
talks, especially adapted for youngsters greatly increased their understanding
of Jewish history and the basic principles of Jewish Law.
By electing officers, cantor, sexton, and page announcer from their own
midst, children are being prepared to conduct their own services. Th is year a
special class lor the training of Junior cantors was formed and conducted on
Friday afternoons by Mr. Morris Nimovitz.
Every major and minor Jewish holiday is appropriately celebrated by mem¬
bers of Junior Congregation. When there is a Bar Mitzvah ceremony, the
juniors adjourn to the main synagogue for the rites and the Musaf service.
On the occasion of certain holidays, the Juniors are tendered receptions
by the mothers. Last summer its members had a much-enjoyed outing to the

] he serious crisis experienced by the Zionist movement during the past
year, following the issuance by Great Britain of the White Paper, had an
unexpected effect upon all workers unto Zion. Instead of resulting in the
adoption of a defeatist attit ude, Zionist leaders were heartened to note a
renewed vigor and stubborn determination on the part of all Jews to continue
working for the achievement of the Zionist ideal.
I he Zionist youth ol Jacksonville, grouped in the four local Young
Judean clubs, reacted to the crisis in no different manner. I hroughout the
year, under the inspiring leadership of their sponsors, Young Judeans worked
enthusiastically; they debated on pertinent problems in Zionism, collected
funds for the purchase of land in Palestine, gave parties, organized outings,
and participated in the competitive Merit System, devised to familiarize them
with Zionist personalities, Palestinian songs and dances, and enrich their
knowledge of the Hebrew language and the geography of Pretz Israel.
Along the religious lines, Young Judea sponsored a Friday ni ght service
at the Center, at which time Rabbi Margolis spoke on “H as Zionism Failed?
Members of Rothschild Young Judea gathered at a banquet held on the
evening of May 19 in Begal s Restaurant where Philip Selber, member of the
Young Judean Council, addressed the participants on the subject of Palestine.
During the summer, several Y. J. members ol the Henrietta Szold Club,
attended the Cejwin Camps.
Jack Snider, president of Rothschild Young Judean was elected to rep¬
resent the local clubs at the regional convention held on August 15th in
Memphis, I ennessee.
Mrs. M. D. Margolis, president of the Y. J. Council was appointed to
serve on the National Board of the organization.

J lie past year has witnessed the complete re¬
juvenation of the local Adolph B. Weil Lodge of
B nai B rith. From a weak disorganized group of
approximately 175 members, it has shown excep¬
tional progress until today it is a greatly strengthened
organization, highly active and efficient, boasting a
membership of well over 350.
The climax o f the year s activity came with the
recent convention of the State Federation of B nai
B rith Lodges, to which the local lodge was host.
The two-day program accomplished much in the way
of concrete progress, as well as presenting a number
of outstanding speakers, among whom were Dean Wal¬
ter J. Matherly, of the University of Florida; Alvin
Kartus, President of the Fifth District of B nai B rith;
and Judge J. Fromberg, past president of the Fifth
District. Nat Shorstein, general chairman, deserves
much credit for the fine manner in which the meeting
was conducted.
Another highlight o f the year s activity came on
March 27th at the initiation exercises held before
an estimated audience o f 500 persons, marking the
induction of a class o f 175 new members into the
Lodge. Alvin S. Kartus, President of the Fifth Dis¬
trict, in whose honor the class was named, delivered
an inspiring address on the history and development
o f B’ nai B rith. Much credit for the success o f the
membership drive is due to the efficient work of the
co-chairmen, Nat Shorstein and Harold S. Cohn.
Not content with rebuilding its own lodge, the
local group went beyond its own bounds and became
very active in the organization of two new lodges in
nearby cities, having successfully installed groups at
both St. Augustine and Gainesville. Its sphere of
influence has been similarly felt in other state-wide
projects, having been instrumental in bringing to the
state educational institutions at Gainesville and Tal¬
lahassee for the first time a Rabbi to direct the spiritual
and cultural activities of the students.
The I ocal Lodge also took a most active interest
in the activities of the District Grand Lodge as Philip
Selber, secretary, attended the District Convention
held in Charleston.
Outstanding in the events of the past year was
the second annual regional A. Z. A. Winter Tourna¬
ments and Convention, which was held in this city
on December 27th, 28th, and 29th. The local lodge
is proud of the part that it played in the inspiring and
successful gathering staged by its junior order. It
also takes just pride in the splendid accomplishments
of its junior auxiliary during the past year, in attaining
the complete fulfillment of its Five- Fold and Full
program.
J he organization has continued to safeguard the
rights and liberties of local Jewry by the vigilant de¬
fensive activities of its Anti-Defamation Committee,
headed by Joseph Glickstein. The lodge also con¬
tinued to sponsor its scholarship fund, and during the
past year aided two young men in their pursuit of a
hi gii er education. The local group also pressed for¬
ward its efforts to ascertain those among the Jewish
faith who have not as yet been naturalized and to
aid them in such naturalization work.
Although the Adolph B. Weil Lodge has just wit¬
nessed the most glorious year in its long existence,
its prospects for future achievement and accomplish¬
ment are even brighter. Supplied with an abundant
force of conscientious and capable leaders and boast¬
ing the largest membership in Jacksonville, the local
ledge is well equipped to safeguard the future welfare
and security of local Jewry.

Outstanding among local Jewish groups is the Junior Order of B’nai
B rith. Led by a group of energetic young men and guided by an efficient
advisory board, the A. Z. A. has gained during the past year a prominent
place in the Jewish community of Jacksonville.
J he second annual Southern Regional Convention tournament of District
5, held in Jacksonville during the month of December was a memorable event
in the life of the local chapter. I he convention was attended by 150 boys
from the four states of Georgia, North and South Carolina and Florida.
Lor three days, debates, oratorical contests, luncheons, dances and athletic
tournaments gave new impetus to the young participants. The convention
ended with a banquet attended by 350 persons, at which Julius Bisno,
executive director of the order, delivered an inspiring address.
Concomitent with similar observances by A. Z. A. chapters throughout
the world, the local chapter sponsored an A. Z. A. Day, a Youth and Democ¬
racy celebration, a Mother’s Day and A. Z. A. Sabbath at which members
of the group, assisted by their own choir, conducted the religious services
in its entirety.
Jn order to bring about a closer relationship among the Jewish youth
ol Jacksonville and the neighboring communities throughout the Southeast,
local Alephs have motored on several occasions to out of town communities,
where they helped organize new chapters and resurected inactive groups. J he
loca I A. Z. A. is largely responsi ble for the huge success of the operetta
The Wedding to which it Iended talent, choir elements, a stage manager,
and assisted with the sale of tickets.
I hrough the membership drive, A. Z. A. nearly doubled the number of
Alephs during the past year so that today, the chapter prides itself with a
membership o f 42.
J he chapter particularly excelled in its fine athletic accomplishments of
the past year. Its d iamondbafl team won the coveted regional championiship
trophy in the tournament held in Savannah and the basketball team went to
the finals in the regional tournament held here last December. J he diamond-
ball team has also compiled an outstanding local record, having won the
local Jewish Community League championship and being tied at present
for first place in the second half of the Recreational League race. In addition,
the Alephs engaged in bowling and touch football. Sidney Cohen is diamond-
ball coach, while Moe Bono handled the destinies of the basketball team.
The season was brought to a close with the presentation of A. Z. A. s
3rd Annual Minstrel and dance.
A. Z. A. is a Jewish Youth Organization with an approximate member¬
ship of 8,500 boys in 347 chapters' throughout the world. Every Jewish
boy of good character between the ages of fifteen and twenty-one is eligible
for membership. When a boy reaches the age of twenty-one he is considered
a life member and then becomes a B’nai B rith.
’Five Fold and Full” is the terminology which designates the A. Z. A.
program and which found its origin in the fertile mind of the late Dr. Boris
D. Bogen, International Secretary of B’nai B’rith. Its activities consist of the
following lines of endeavor: Rel igious, Social Service, Cultural, Social and
Athletic. The creed of the A. Z. A. is composed of seven cardinal virtues
which are as follows: Patriotism, Judaism, Fil ial Love, Charity, Conduct,
Purity and Fraternity.

Junior Hadassab is an American organization
of Jewish young women who believe in Zionism, a
great modern social movement that more than any
other has deepened the meaning of "a full Jewish
life.
7 he Idea:
Schooled in the ideals of democracy, the mem¬
bers of Junior Had assah believe, as do all Zionists,
that the Jewish people, like all people, should have
their own Homeland for collective creative endeavor.
They believe that th is Homeland shou Id be in Pal-
estine, the ancestral land of the Jews. J hey believe
that now more than ever Palestine must be made
the haven for large numbers of homeless refugees.
Spok en some years ago, these words of former
U. S. Supreme Court Justice Brandéis are the more
striking today: J he suffering of the Jews due to
injustices continuing throughout the nearly twenty
centuries is the greatest tragedy in history. Never
were the injustices more glaring. Yet the present is
pre-eminently a time for hopefulness.”
I hat hopefulness embraces Zionism and Pales¬
tine.
The Program:
1. In America Junior Hadassah fosters an ap¬
preciation o( the whole body of Jewish culture —
through past and contemporary history, literature,
music, the Hebrew language. An understanding of
the cumulative experience of the Jewish people is
an invaluable equipment for tackling the critical pre¬
sent-day problems of the Jew.
2. In Palestine Junior Hadassah s share in the
practical upbuilding of the country is the support ol
specific enterprise, mainly for the youth of Palestine.
Th ese are:
Meier Shfeyah: a self-governing children’s village
where nearly 200 boys and girls live, study and work.
Among them are scores of refugee children from
Europe.
Pardess Anna: a citrus farm for specialized
training of Meier Shfeyah graduates.
Henrietta Szold School of Nursing; Training
school for nurses which is regarded as one of the best
in the Near East.
Participation in Youth Aliyah, which settles Eu¬
ropean relugee children in Palestine.
.Participation in the Jewish National E and for
land purchase and reforestation.
The Action:
An idea and a program live only by action. By
unremitting efforts in carrying out the two-point pro¬
gram of Junior Hadassah, the 20,000 members of
the organization contribute effectively to a just so¬
lution of the Jewish problem. American Jewish wo¬
men who are convinced that only a positive approach
to the Jewish question is worth consideration will
find in Junior Hadassah an idea, a program and a
course of action that answer the inner urge of every
young Jew.
Plans for the coming year include having one
business meeting a month and one cultural meeting
a month, at which time seasonal holidays will be
celebrated and brought to the attention ol members.

After nearly eight years of earnest
and intensive work on behalf of charity,
cultural enlightment, assistance of needy
students and the Zionist cause, the Jew¬
ish Progrssive Society, lately operating
under the name of Zionist Progressive
Society, gave up its Zionist charter in
order to facilitate the establishment of
a local Zionist district, devoted exclusive¬
ly to Zionist work.
Although the past year marked the
end of the organization as such, the
Zionist Progressive Society showed no
let down in its activities. Under the
efficient leadership of its founder and
president, Mr. I. M. Lieberman, the
Society presented an outstanding and
varied program.
o n D ecember 14, in collaboration with the local Hadassah, the Society
sponsored a talk by Adrian Schwartz, ardent Zionist worker, who was visiting
the United States on a tour of lectures.
In this same month of December, the Progressives had their installation
of officers banquet. A success ful “I atke and card party was held during the
Chanukah festival, on the evening of December 25, at which time a radio
was raffled off.
Recognition of the Society s efforts for Palestine came on February 8,
1939, when it received its charter from the National office of the Zionist
Organization of America.
In June, two delegates from the 1 ocal group attended the Zionist Organi¬
zation of America convention which was held in New York. 1 he delegates were
Mr. Harry Sachs and Mr. Philip Selber, who upon their return presented a
most colorful and enlightening report of the convention.
During the Shekel campaign, members of the society helped substantially
to assure its success. 511 Shekels were sold and a check for $263.00 was
mailed to the Central Shekel Committee. J he local chairman of the campaign
was Mr. Joseph Robin.
Mr. I. M. Lieberman and officers of the Progressive Zionist Society
wish to thank all of its members, local organizations and the Board of the
Jacksonville Jewish Center for the splendid cooperation they have given to
make the life of the society one of substantial and worthy accomplishment.
They most heartily urge all of their former members to join the new Zionist
group and to continue the work for the establishment of a political and cultural
center for the Jews in Palestine.
The organization will continue to function as a society still maintaining
their varied cultural, charitable and welfare program. Election of officers will
take place with the opening of the Fall season.

to plan for their respective under¬
takings without conflicting with the
other organizations.

1) Rosh Hashonah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and lasts
two days. It is also the beginning of the 10 days of Penitence, which end with
Yom Kippur. Sincere prayer and meditation during these days help us to experience
the reality of God to dedicate ourselves anew to the establishment of His Kingdom
on earth—the reign of freedom, justice and peace.
2) Shabbat S'huba derives its name from the Haftorah of that day. Hosea
xiv, 2-10 which begins with the word Shubah-‘Return’, i.e., Repent.
3) Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is a day of fasting and prayer, when
we strive to reawaken the latent spiritual in our souls so that it may become
an effective and directing force in our daily conduct.
4) Sukkos (Feast of Tabernacles) is the Jewish festival of thanksgiving after
all the products of the field have been gathered in. The sukkah symbolizes the
wanderings of our ancestors in the wilderness of Sinai.
5) Simchath Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) is the second day of S'hemini
Azereth. Then the reading of the Pentateuch is' completed and begun anew for the
coming year. This event is celebrated with Hakofos—procession with the scrolls
of the Torah to the accompaniment of songs and dancing.
6 Rosh Chodesh is the term for the beginning of a new month. As the
Jewish year is based on the lunar calendar, the beginning of the new month coin¬
cides with the new moon. When a month has thirty days, the last is considered
the first of the two days which constitute Rosh Chodesh of the month following.

1) FAST OF ESTHER commemorates the day when the Jews of Persia fasted
and prayed that they be spared from the persecution planned by Haman.
2) Shabbath Zakor derives its name from the special portion from the Torah,
Deut. xxv, 17-19, read that day, which begins with the word Zakor—“Remember
Amalek.” It was assumed that Haman was an Amalekite.
3) Purim commemorates the day when, through the intervention of Mordecai
and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly escaped being victims of Haman’s
plot to exterminate them.
4) Shabbath Parah: Preceding the Passover, proper steps to become ritually
clean had to be taken as preparation for the eating of the Paschal lamb. Hence
the law concerning the ashes of the red heifer, Parah Adumah, (Numbers xix),
was read.
5) S'habbat Hahodesh derives its name from the Haftorah of that day, Exodus
xii, 1-20. This is the “portion about the month/’ referring to the special con¬
secration of the month of Nisan which is the most eventful in the history of Israel.
