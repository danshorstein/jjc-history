One of the most gratifying achievements of the ever
expanding program of the Jacksonville Jewish Center has
been the evolution of the Center Year Book into the un¬
usual publication that it is today.
Not only does the Book chronicle the progress of the
institution year by year, but it has developed into an un¬
paralleled fund-raising activity of the Center Sisterhood.
This development is underscored by the character of this
year’s publication, representing as it does the largest and
most complete book ever published, and including the
events which took place as part of the dedication rites of
the Center’s New Activities building. It is a certainty that
this year’s book will become a permanent record in the
home of every Center member.
A highly commendable feature of the Center’s year
book is the fact that no paid solicitors are engaged, and
outside of actual printing costs, all proceeds go to the
educational, religious and charitable work of the Center.
It is for this reason that the Center membership sincerely
appreciates the cooperation and good will of those ad¬
vertisers who have this year inserted their advertising to
the greatest extent ever.
Much of the success of this year’s edition is due to the
sacrificial devotion and zeal of the chairman of the Com¬
mittee, Hyman Selber and to the two chairmen of the
Sisterhood’s special solicitations committee, Mrs. Hyman
Selber and Mrs. Joseph Krestul. Thanks also go to Miss
Ethel Joel for her efficient handling of the section devoted
to New Year’s greetings.' Remittances for these greetings
should be made to Miss Joel at 2772 Post Street or at the
Center office.

RABBI’S MESSAGE
“BROTHERHOOD AND PEACEFUL FRIENDSHIP”
The motto inscribed on the cornerstone of this mag¬
nificent building, “Dedicated To Our Youth” is by now a
by-word in our community. It became the dominant term
at the splendid celebration held at the Sabbath Service on
Friday evening.
Over the terrace entrance is an equally significant in¬
scription, in a way supplementing the first. Four Hebrew
words are rendered approximately, “Shalom Friendly
Welcome.” We did not translate the Hebrew literally
because one must know the story behind them to ap¬
preciate their significance over the portals to our Ac¬
tivities Building.
This story I would like to relate in dedicating this
structure to the highest purposes which it is to serve.
The Palestinian Talmud tells us that the Priests and
Levites in the Jerusalem Temple would welcome their
colleagues arriving for their week’s service with a prayerful
greeting: “May He whose presence dwells in this House
plant among you Aharah v’Ahvah Shalom uRe’ut love,
brotherliness, peace and friendship.” You may recall these
phrases from the marriage ceremony. The words are in¬
deed among the most sentimental found in any language.
But only from the original context do we get the cue to the
aims and purposes that glorify our edifice.
We are a Conservative Synagogue. We are a Syna¬
gogue-Center. Both of these designations emanate- from
the American Jewish scene. We believe that sociability
and recreation round out our program of education and
divine worship. Let those who will question to their
heart’s content, why a House of Prayer needs an Activities
Building. We stand committed to the idea contained in
the greeting of welcome that originated in our ancient
sanctuary.
I will have you note, if you please, that it was taken
for granted by the officiants of old that their fellow
officiants would conduct the sacred ritual in the prescribed
order. In their devotion consisting of the four Hebrew
words we took for our motto, they emphasized blessings
and qualities that the act of worship are to bring in its
wake.
We must be mindful, on the other hand, that these
terms become shallow and insipid when divorced from
their religious connotation. In professional language a
building of this type would be termed a place for “group
activities.” Its purpose would be to promote “group
work.” To many these expressions are hollow, lacking in
their truly human connotation. They ring of the herd
instinct and regimentation. From our stand point, from
the stand point of the Synagogue-Center, the exercise of
sociability, the creative use of leisure time, elevates the
tone of daily living and becomes part of its spiritual
pattern.
To insure that this Activities Building will live up to
its lofty purpose we shall reiterate one Hebrew word used
in the priestly greeting, “may He plant”. I see in that
choice an important implication for us.
A plant is different from any other subject. No matter
how important, no matter how sacred. Suppose we take
a book as an example. It may be put away on a shelf.
Whenever the spirit moves us, or the need arises, we may
RABBI SANDERS A. TOFIELD
return to it for reference and guidance. Not so a plant.
A plant is an organic living thing. It calls for nurturing
and cultivation, constant and loving care. Only then will
it yield flowers, fruit or just attractive foliage.
Significantly a public building is also called a “plant.”
The wonderful potentialities of this building become simi¬
lar to the benefits of a plant. This building is not a place
of amusement where one may buy an hour’s fun for the
price of admission, even though it is a place of enjoyment.
The blessings to be found here are not nicely packaged
and filed away ready for delivery to anyone who will pay
an occasional visit. As is the case with a plant, potential
blessings of our activities building have to be nurtured
and cultivated, tended and loved. Your constant interest
and application of initiative will obtain for you wonderful
qualities to which you are cordially invited. Concerted
action, team work, cooperation and planning will make
of the structural plant a thriving garden of “affectionate
neighborliness and harmonious brotherhood.” Study groups
meeting in our spacious library, social gatherings in the
exquisite lounge. Games played on this floor, down to
the physical exercise in the athletic department, when
integrated with the Synagogue ideal as a whole, will yield
the soul satisfying qualities for which every human heart
yearns and thirsts, “loving brotherliness and peaceful
friendship.”
Our thanks to the Building Fund Committee, our
gratitude to the generous donors calls for dedication on
our part. Their sacrificial service and giving interlaced
with the concrete and steel. The building we dedicated is
the “plant” of their efforts. We therefore commit our¬
selves to the understanding and to the implementation of
those four glorious Hebrew words Aharah v’Ahvah
Shalom u’Reut. With the help and inspiration of the
Almighty we shall cause them to blossom from the simple
“Friendly Welcome” to flourish as “Loving Brotherhood
and Peaceful Friendship.”

PRESIDENT'S MESSAGE
With the dedication of the new Activities building
during the past year, a complete change has taken place
in the character and scope of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. To its previously well developed religious and
educational programs have now been added social, athletic,
and recreational activities.
The primary challenge to the Center today is not how
to interest the community in the program offered, since
this interest has already made itself quite evident, but
rather how to secure the necessary financial support for it.
It is unfortunate that my message to the congregation as
President must concern itself primarily with this phase of
the Center’s administration; but in the final analysis, the
spiritual success of the synagogue depends to a great degree
on the type of financial support its members are willing
to give.
Our primary objective this year shall be to obtain a
balanced budget. Because of the considerable increase
last year in the Center’s deficit, the Board has decided
that this deficit can not be further increased but instead
it must be decreased. It has become apparent to those who
have studied the budget in detail, that it can not be
balanced by cutting expenditures.
Since the present schedules of dues was set some five
years ago, the effects of inflation on the synagogue budget
must be given the same consideration as is given to the
problems in counting every item affecting the cost of
living. Savings can not be affected by lowering the present
high standards of Center personnel since this is foolish
economy secured at the expense of our children whose
sacrifices of time would go unequalled by our own efforts
to make these sacrifices worthwhile. Nor can savings be
realized by returning to the outmoded facilities of the old
Center building.
The inevitable conclusion is that increased dues income
offers the only solution. To secure the required dues in¬
come for the coming year, the finance committee developed
an overall dues schedule suggesting a minimum require¬
ment for each member, using as a basis in determining
the individual amount the sum of $125.00, which is the
average cost per family of the Center budget. Since the
Center is a cooperative enterprise depending upon no out¬
side assistance, each member must conscientiously deter¬
mine what his share of the responsibility is and then make
every effort to meet it. Individual failures can only mean
collective failure of the synagogue in the final analysis.
How well the individual’s dues responsibility is met
depends on the priority which he gives to it. If the needs
of the Center are placed “high up” in the scheme of
things, the requirement can be easily met, but if the re¬
sponsibility is placed far down the list, after luxuries and
after all other charitable contributions, 'then naturally the
requirements can be met only with great difficulty.
Up to the present time, the reaction of the membership
to the proposed dues increases has been extremely grati¬
fying. If this attitude continues, much of the pressure
ordinarily exerted upon the leadership can be relieved and
these efforts turned to more productive endeavors.
PHILIP SELBER
Once this challenge of proper financial support has
been met, other problems can take their proper perspective.
A program for the rededication of the membership to the
religious ideals of traditional Judaism is being planned,
and will begin to emerge during the year. The teaching
curriculum of the schools will be varied and increased
and school enrollments will grow. The social and athletic
programs will come into their own.
Attention can once more be turned to building ac¬
tivities. Already planned for this year is the completion
of the second floor youth recreational center. Efforts will
then be directed to the remodeling, redecoration and air-
conditioning of the synagogue and the balance of the old
building. With proper cooperation most of this program
can be completed within eighteen months.
On the occasion of the New Year, I would like to
express my own appreciation for the sincere devotion of
the officers and members of the Board of Directors in
bringing the Jacksonville Jewish Center so much closer to
its goal. My gratitude likewise goes in a large measure to
the loyal membership of the Center, whose willingness to
meet every challenge has been heartening.
Personally I am very grateful for the inspirational lead¬
ership given to the Center by Rabbi Tofield, whose fore¬
sight and active direction have brought realization to many
of the Center’s aspirations. May I also express my thanks
for the cooperation and devotion of the splendid group of
persons comprising the Center staff.
To all may I extend my sincerest and warmest greetings
for a Happy New Year.

Dedicated to faith and üierniee
address delivered by Harry Gendzier,
chairman of Building Fund Committee, at
Dedication Banquet, April 15, 1951
This is a thrilling, vitalizing, and momentous
occasion. For all of us it is a realization of a dream,
brought to reality by the sweat and toil of many of
us, and by the contributions of all of us. Each of
us, present tonight, has in some measure helped
place brick on brick toward the erection of this
awe-inspiring, useful and magnificent building.
Each of us can take pride in full measure at this
occasion, the Dedication Ceremony of the Youth
Activities Building.
The task has not been an easy one. There have
been years of toil, struggle and backbreaking effort
to reach today’s events. To those of you who have
served faithfully and diligently on the Building
and Expansion Committee, I wish to extend my
earnest and sincere thanks. To my co-chairmen,
Ben Setzer and Ben Yoffee, about whom you will
hear more later in the program, I say well done
my friends. I realize that in naming names, I shall
overlook some, but I hope that in this hour
of joy they will forgive me, if I do so, yet it would
be ungrateful on my part if I did not mention for
special Kudos, Philip Selber, Ralph Mizrahi, Joe
Becker, Bob Gordon and Joe Hackel, and yes there
are many more that deserve a page on this scroll.
To all those who aided in our money raising cam¬
paigns I wish to extend my thanks, and only hope
that they will find it possible to continue stead¬
fastly until the program is completed.
In reviewing the years that have flown past,
how can I help but say to the entire membership
of the Center, thanks to you. Your loyal support
and approval has been a tower of strength. When
things looked blackest, when it seemed that ob¬
stacles came across our path, the membership came
through, nobly, admirably and conscientiously.
Yea, in the days when it seemed that each task
required further deeds and steadfastness, our
Honored Rabbi, stood with us, staunch, advising,
cajoling, pushing, and working like a trojan. In
all these thanks to those I mentioned, and those
who would be mentioned if time would permit,
the Rabbi’s name must stay right up on the top.

Man’s service to God and Judaism is an act of
faith. That I had faith, was because the cause was
great. Great causes deserve great faith. Great
faith inspires generous support from good people.
Good people inspire progress. Progress nurtures
enthusiasm, and enthusiasm engenders further pro¬
gress.
Jewish religious life of a traditional nature in
our city had its modest beginnings in a small loft.
This was the first synagogue attended by the
fathers and mothers of many present here, before
the fire. I know it was the first synagogue attend¬
ed by my wife’s father and mother, of blessed
memory. Then came B’nai Israel. Most of the
congregants then lived around the synagogue.
The migration all over the city commenced soon
after the end of world war one.
We, who are striving to build for the youth
were young ourselves, and so it is proper at this
time to review what youth did in the 20’s. In
1925 the Hebrew Junior League was formed.

CANTOR AND CHOIR
Jewish music during the past year continued to play
an increasingly important role in the development of the
design of the Jacksonville Jewish Center for positive Jewish
living.
Since Jewish music of necessity must assume a major
role in the molding of the character of American Judaism,
increased emphasis has been given by the Center to this
phase of its program. Jewish music must perpetuate and
reinterpret the traditional music of the synagogue; it must
transmit the cultural values of Jewish life of the past as
well as the new life of Israel; and yet it must adapt both
to the tempo and environment of the Jewish Community
in America today.
Much of the success of the Center’s musical program
is due to the untiring work of Cantor Abraham Marton in
directing and inspiring the various phases of the Center’s
music program. The nucleus of the Center program is the
Center Choral Society composed of some 40 volunteer
voices. The activities of this group during the past year
was highlighted by the third annual concert sponsored by
the Center Men’s Club and held on May 27th in the new
Center auditorium. The concert, which had as its guest
soloist Cantor Jacob Goldring of the Miami Beach Jewish
Center, was well received by a large audience. The group
also appeared on Radio Station WPDQ on February 11th
as part of the local observance of Jewish Music Month.
They also participated in the regional convention of
Hadassah on February 6th and in the dedication exercises
of the new Center Activities Building on April 15th.
The group presents a varied repertoire of liturgical and
Jewish folk music. The work of this group has contributed
CANTOR ABRAHAM MARTON
in large measure to the meaningful and inspirational de¬
velopment of conservative Judaism in Jacksonville. From
the larger group of the Choral Society are selected the
volunteer choir for Friday evening services and the
augmented choir for High Holiday Services.
In addition to his work as director of the Choral So¬
ciety, Cantor Marton has directed the program of training
students of the Hebrew School department in the tra¬
ditional Hazanut chants. Many of these students have
not only mastered an ability to lead all Sabbath services,
including the Kabbalat Shabbat, Shaharis and Musaf
services, but they have also learned many of the special
chants pertaining to the holidays.

THE SEMINARY’S PROGRAM
It Trains Leaders
In its schools it prepares 2,000 students
in the East and on the West Coast for the
rabbinate, teaching and other careers of
service to the community.
It Advances Jewish Scholarship
Through its faculty, its research scholars,
and its unrivaled library, it clarifies for our
age the enduring spiritual values of thirty-
five centuries of Jewish life.
It Interprets Judaism To The Millions
Through the Eternal Light broadcasts
and through the Jewish Museum it reveals
to people of all creeds the meaning of Jewish
ideals and their relevance in the present.
It Furthers Intergroup Understanding
Through the Institute for Religious and Social Studies,
it brings together hundreds of Christian and Jewish min¬
isters for graduate study of the different traditions and
their responsibility for a spiritual and democratic America.
It Breaks Down Community Isolation
Hundreds of local communities and congregations are
guided in their programs of religious activities for men,
women and children by the United Synagogue of America
and the Rabbinical Assembly of America.
JACKSONVILLE’S CONTRIBUTION

America, located at 122nd Street and Broad¬
way in New York City, is headquarters for
Conservative Judaism in America, under the
presidency of Dr. Louis Finkelstein. It co¬
operates with the United Synagogue of
America which in turn sponsors the Women’s
League, the National Federation of Men’s

Rabbinical Assembly of America is another
arm of the movement.

ica and all of its corresponding branches.

inspiration.

Realization of dreams eome true with possibilities of
unlimited aetivities should be the keynote of the Center
Men’s Club in 1951-52.
The past year’s aetivities were not merely marked by
dreams and anticipation. Services to the Center and the
Center Family can not always be measured in dollars and
cents. As for dollars and cents, we were there too. Our
finest single contribution was the presentation of a check
in the amount of $1,000.00 to aid the Building Program.
In accepting the check, Mr. Harry Gendzier acknowledged
the constant good work of the Men’s Club by assisting in
various ways and means in completing the building and
expansion program.
This organization further adopted a project of present¬
ing to the Center a portable bar, sixteen feet long, to be
used by any other organization or family celebrating a
wedding, anniversary, Bar Mitzvah or any other simeha.
This was no small project and should bring joy to all on
these particular occasions.
This past year’s Center Choral Concert was sponsored
by the Center Men’s Club. The results as shown in our
handsome program and the attendance of those interested
in Jewish and Hebrew classical music will attest to the
work performed by our committee. Cantor Jacob Goldring
of Miami Beach was presented as the guest soloist of the
Concert. And more important than presenting a cultural
program for an evening was the benefit of $250.00 to the
Cantorial School of the Jewish Theological Seminary. This
project was received with enthusiasm by the members of
the organization and has been adopted as an annual event.
Thus we will offer to all of the Jewish people in Jackson¬
ville a cultural program and at the same time support the
Cantorial Assembly.
In athletics the Men’s Club has always been active.
This year our softball team has been outstanding. Benja¬
min Cohen has served in a very capable capacity as chair¬
man of the athletic committee. It is that field in particular
that the Men’s Club will lean towards in this new year.
We have every facility available to develop intramural
competition as well as inter-city play in such sports as
basketball, volleyball, ping-pong, and numerous other in¬
door sports. Athletics will certainly be a major project
with the Men’s Club.
Socially we are ready. Our most recent September
Frolics Dance is also the beginning of an annual affair.
Where the facilities are available and the manpower will¬
ing the Center Men’s Club will always be there to do more
than their share of work for the Center Family.
Probably our biggest “unsung” project is the work
being done with the Minyan Club. This project has been
the work of the Men’s Club religious committee since the
conception of the Minyan Club. The work of preparing
meals each Sunday is only the surface of the work. The
religious aspect involved is in bringing the Bar Mitzvah
youth to the Sunday morning minyan and keeping him
within the religious scope for which he has been trained.
The men who have given their personal time and
efforts to work for a better organization are too numerous
to mention. Yet we know that their rewards must come
to them in various ways. Every one who benefited by the
good work of the Men’s Club must appreciate this work.
But these men work for the Men’s Club and the Men’s
Club works for the Center. This circle tends for a happier
Jewish community and thus makes everything worthwhile.

Jewish Center is its service to the Jewish community in
providing appropriate and dignified burial facilities for

The practice of lighting candles in the Jewish home
upon ushering in the Sabbath every Friday evening and
the Festivals whenever they occur in the cycle of the
Hebrew Religious Calendar, is one of the most beautiful
rituals in Judaism. Tradition has conferred this charming
and edifying spiritual touch to the sanctity of the home
upon the lady of the house.
Jewish women, the wives and mothers of many genera¬
tions, made it a practice to add a personal prayer to the
required benediction. The impromptu prayer was usually
given in the language most familiar to the lady. It in¬
cluded requests for every day blessings such as physical and
spiritual welfare for the home and the dear ones it repre¬
sents. Accordingly the prayer varied with the mood, the
occasion and the need of the hour. For this reason one of
the frequently observed Jewish rituals has very few prayers
handed down from tradition. The primary significance of
kindling the Sabbath and Festival candles is the merging
of the light from the soul with the Light of the Universe
at the time light is spread in the family abode.
With hesitancy we suggest a prayer found in the book,
“Sabbath, the Day of Delight” by Abraham E. Millgram.
Let it serve as a model of a type prayer that each woman
may find it in her heart to utter and not become a perfunc¬
tory stereotype.
Judaism has associated its fondest ideals with the sym¬
bol of a light.
“For good deeds are as a candle, and the Torah is
light.”
“For with Thee is the foundation of life, in Thy light
do we see light.”
I hope that including the text of the benedictions for
the lighting of the candles on various occasions and the
suggested prayer in our Year Book will help the women
of our congregation practice the observance with faith¬
fulness.
RABBI TOFIELD

L. C. “Light Sabbath or Festival candles.”
Rosh Hashanah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year and
lasts two days.
Shabbat Shuva the Sabbath occuring between Rosh Hashanah and
Yom Kippur, takes its name from the first words of the Haftorah,
“Shuvah, Yisrael—Return, O Israel.”
Yom Kippur the Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and prayer,
a day for confession of sin and supplication for forgiveness. “For trans¬
gressions between man and God, repentence on Yom Kippur brings
atonement. For transgressions between man and man, Yom Kippur
brings no atonement until amends are made.”
Sukkot the Feast of Tabernacles commemorates the period when our
forefathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings through
the wilderness of Sinai after their Egyptian bondage.
Simhat Torah (Rejoicing in the Torah) celebrates the completion of
the reading of the five books of Moses. On this day, the reading is
begun anew for the coming year.

We are pleased to devote this section of the Year Book to the Jewish Organizations of Jacksonville, with
their own descriptions of activities and purposes.

BEN STARK
The Jewish Community of Jacksonville totals some
1,100 families of 3,500 individual men, women and chil¬
dren. These families and individuals are members of some
25 different Jewish organizations. Each of these organiza¬
tions and their membership are interested in their own
programs, as well as in those activities which are of value
to the entire Jewish community.
What are these activities which cut across the lines of
any single organization and have to do with the group
interests of the entire Jewish community?
There is, first, the United Campaign, an annual Drive
which directly or indirectly supports either local organiza¬
tions or assists in the support of the national parent organi¬
zation of a local group. Among the local groups which
are either direct or indirect beneficiaries of the funds
raised through the Jewish community-sponsored United
Campaign are B’nai B’rith, Zionist, Pladassah, Council of
Jewish Women, River Garden Plebrew Home for the
Aged, and the Jewish Welfare Society. Fund raising is
big business for in the last four years, a total of well over
$1,000,000 has been raised through your Community
Council as a result of its annual Campaigns.
The Council also functions through its Public Relations
Committee. This body looks into attacks against Jews as
individuals or as a group. It fosters better understanding
between the Jewish and the non-Jewish community. Where
anti-Semitic or undemocratic attacks occur, it studies ways
to eradicate or lessen the effectiveness of persons or groups
so engaged. Representatives to the Public Relations Com¬
mittee are appointed by the president. This Committee
has an important job to do and that job is taken very
seriously. The chairman of the Committee is Eli Fink.
Another function of the Council was the decision to
accept 24 Refugee Family Units. To date, over a period
of 2 years, about 60 individual men, women and children
have been welcomed direct from D. P. Camps in Europe
and an expenditure of some $20,000 for this group was
well spent. Today, all are employed and self-supporting.
This job was done by the Refugee Committee of the
Council with the very able assistance of the Jewish Wel¬
fare Society. The chairman of the Refugee Committee is
A. H. Rothstein.
The situation in Korea necessitated re-establishment of
the Council’s Armed Services Committee. The job of this
group, sponsored and financed by the Council and made
up of representatives of Jewish organizations is to provide
services to Jewish men in the armed forces stationed in
and about Jacksonville. The provision of home hospitality,
community seders, personal service; in short, “a home
away from home”, is the basic emphasis of this Committee,
chaired by Ira Stein.
To keep the community informed about activities, a
monthly periodical, “The Commentator” is mailed free
to all Jews in Jacksonville. One significant aspect of the
Commentator’s columns is a Community Calendar to list
all activities of our community, and thus to avoid conflicts
in dates, which without previous clearance, are inevitable.
More recently a Presidents’ Council has been initiated,
made up of the presidents of the various organizations in
our Jewish community. Its purpose is to serve as a sound¬
ing board and a forum and to exchange ideas and prob¬
lems common to all groups.

River Garden is serving the aged and infirm Jewish
people of Florida by providing a home with all modern
facilities for their comfort and well being. Since its in¬
ception in 1946; when the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid
Society opened the Home, eighty-five residents have been
served. Eleven Florida communities have benefited from
the services of River Garden.
*
The Home provides large airy bedrooms., modern
bathrooms, cheerful sitting rooms, dining rooms, and game
rooms. Orthodox services are held on the Sabbath and
Holidays in our own Chapel. All dietary laws are ob¬
served. A separate infirmary wing provides specialized
care for the chronically ill and senile. Another well
equipped infirmary and clinic assures good medical atten¬
tion for the ambulatory aged. Our Medical Board repre¬
sents all the specialties of medicine and conducts a positive
and preventive medical program, as well as supplying the
most modern methods of treating the illnesses of the aged.
A physiotherapist is on duty daily to administer prescribed
treatments. A complete nursing staff is in attendance
twenty-four hours a day.
The residents enjoy an activity program designed to
meet their needs. Our Hobby Shop is open three days
weekly, with active participation by the residents. An
annual Hobby Shop Fair is held where their articles are
exhibited and sold. Movies, parties, and games continue
throughout the year, sponsored by the Home and com¬
munity organizations. The Golden Age Club holds its
meetings at the Home. Television now brings many inter¬
esting hours to our residents. This year the residents have
initiated their own newspaper, which is written, published,
and circulated by them.
Our Home has been filled to its capacity of forty-three
residents, and there is a constant demand for our services.
With increasing longevity, the problem of caring for the
aged is becoming a matter of concern to many communi¬
ties. Jacksonville and the Florida communities are fortu¬
nate that the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society had
the foresight to anticipate the growing need for a haven
for the aged and infirm.
