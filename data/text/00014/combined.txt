The Center Men’s Club, the service and social organi¬
zation for the male adults of the Jacksonville Jewish Center,
is rapidly approaching maturity in its thinking and in its
ideals. Óur Club has become affiliated with the National
Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs, and is taking the lead
in the formation of a Southeastern Region of Men’s Clubs,
by sponsoring the first convention of this body in Jackson¬
ville over the Labor Day week-end.
The Men’s Club seeks to attain the following objectives:
A. Bring renewed Jewish inspiration to the men of the
synagogue by identifying them with the highest so¬
cial, cultural and religious goals of Judaism.
B. Inculcate Jewish men with a more thorough knowl¬
edge and appreciation of their Jewish heritage; deep¬
en their loyalty to the Synagogue and its ideals;
strengthen their Jewish interests and activities.
C. Train Jewish laymen for active responsible leader¬
ship and participation in the Synagogue and com¬
munity.
ALVIN P. RUBIN
D.Incorporate the teachings of Judaism as a vital force
in the lives of their members.
In striving to arrive at the ultimate completion of these
goals, the Men’s Club will have during the year a series of
well planned programs, designed to bring to its membership,
through fellowship and companionship, a working knowl¬
edge of the satisfaction to be derived from conscientious
service to the Synagogue and community.
The focal point of the Men’s Club’s interest lies in the
youth work of the Synagogue. To accomplish this aim, our
Club sponsors the Minyan Club, the annual Sunday School
Awards, the Sunday School picnic, Hebrew School awards,
The Bernard G. Glossman award to an outstanding Scout
of the Synagogue troop, and in addition takes an active in¬
terest in the affairs of the C.Y.L. and Y.P.L.
The Men’s Club has established and maintains a blood
bank for the use of its members and families.
The officers of the Men’s Club pledge their full co¬
operation to the various Synagogue affiliates and to the
Synagogue itself in every endeavor.

The Progressive Credit Union, sponsored by the Jewish
Progressive Society, has continued to grow in its 15 years
of operation. It is one of more than 10,000 Credit Unions
in the United States, having almost 5 million members and
more than one billion dollars in assets.
From a very modest beginning of 20 members and
$500.00 in assets, we have grown to over 350 members and
more than $200,000.00 in assets. During our existence
we have made over 1500 loans to members, amounting to
approximated $2,000,000.00 without a single loss. We
are organized to encourage thrift and provide a safe, con¬
venient medium whereby members can invest and accumu¬
late savings. We are chartered by the State of Florida and
regularly examined by the Comptroller of the said State,
and the savings of members are safeguarded in several ways.
The Credit Union being a cooperative, assists its mem¬
bers when circumstances require and permit such assistance.
The purchasing and business power of the members is in¬
creased by their ability to borrow and repay loans under
convenient arrangements such as are afforded them by our
Credit Union. Life Insurance equal to a members savings
up to $1,000.00 and Loan Protection Insurance is paid by
the Progressive Credit Union without any cost to the in¬
dividual member.
The climax of each year’s activities comes with the an¬
nual meeting and banquet held in the Jacksonville Jewish
Center auditorium. Last year die board declared a 2%
dividend on savings and 15% rebate of interest to borrow¬
ing members.

Athletics
COMMITTEE
Chairman Louis Ansbacher
Edward Bossen Morty Rosenkranz
Nathan Krestul Stanley Rosenkranz
Albert Levy Jack Shorstein
During the past year, the Center-sponsored
athletic teams represented themselves well in play
throughout the city. Each Monday night the Center
Gym was crowded with basketball and volleyball
enthusiasts. Tuesdays were set aside for the calorie¬
conscious feminine set as they stoically went through
their exercises in repentance for the past week’s
lapses in will power. During the winter basketball
season, the Center Varsity Five battled through a
winning season, taking measure of 12 out of 16
intra-city opponents.
The Center junior clubs also had a very active
season. This included, not only other clubs within
the Center organization, but games with clubs in the
surrounding towns. The Spring opened a highly
competitive softball program which sparked the jun¬
iors into a challenge game with the Seniors as a
finale to the athletic season. The Juniors’ 23-8 win
closed the season on a high note.
The Center Athletic Board has planned an exten¬
sive program of sport and recreational activities for
the coming year. Intra-mural Leagues are being
formed which will provide competition for all in
many sports, including basketball, volleyball and
softball. Watch the Center Bulletin for the starting
dates. Come and play.

The Jewish Community Council is the one organization which
speaks for and represents all the Jews of Jacksonville. This is true
whether or not they belong to many different organizations or to
none. It is one of hundreds of Jewish Community Councils
throughout the country, each of which is a member of the parent
organization—the National Council of Jewish Federations and
Welfare Funds.
The Community Council is a democratic organization made up
of representatives of all Jewish groups which in turn elect the offi¬
cers, set the policies and the framework within which the Council
operates. Any activity which concerns the entire Jewish com¬
munity is the direct concern of the Council. As such, it functions
for a larger number and on a broader plane than does any single
organization.
The Council is an incorporated body under the state laws of
Florida and it has its charter, dating back to 1935, from the state
of Florida. Since that date, it has served the Jewry of Jacksonville
in many ways.
The By-Laws of the Community Council best describe its func¬
tions:
1. To help maintain the dignity and integrity of Jewish life;
2. To develop an articulate, intelligent and effective public
opinion on community problems and interests
3. To foster cooperation among all elements in the Com¬
munity of Jacksonville
4. To raise, receive, collect, hold and distribute funds for Jew¬
ish causes, either directly or through presently existing or
subsequently established agencies
5. To enable the Jewish Community of Jacksonville through
coordination of fund-raising activities to meet its obligations
to local, national and overseas causes in an intelligent and
effective manner
6. To cooperate with other agencies for better mutual under¬
standing; and
7. In general, to plan for the welfare and development of the
Community as a whole, engaging in activities as will con¬
tribute to that end.
To carry out these purposes, the Council has created various
structures. The most important has to do with fund-raising. Each
year an annual Campaign is conducted to provide funds for agencies
—some 40 in number—so that they can carry out their objectives.
These include aid for overseas needs through U. J. A., Hadassah
and other groups; combatting anti-Semitism; providing hospital
facilities for the Tubercular and others who are ill; developing
cultural facilities; maintaining religious institutions and, of course,
meeting needs of our own local institutions such as the River
Garden Home. Almost 2 million dollars have been raised for
these several purposes since the inception of your Community
Council.
To carry on its many other activities, the Council has estab¬
lished various committees. The Refugee Committee in past years
has done a most important job in the acceptance, settlement and
integration of over 24 refugee families. It has again accepted ad¬
ditional refugee family units as its responsibility and when these
families arrive, the costs of housing, medical care, education, in fact
the fitting of these families into the American scene will be done
again through the Refugee Committee.
Men and women in the Armed Forces are cared for through
the Armed Services Committee. Home hospitality during the re¬
ligious holiday is provided and other needs of service personnel are
met as the demands arise.
The Public Relations Committee quietly and effectively not only
stands ready to combat anti-Semitism, but has in the past, and is in
the present, active in promoting a positive program within the
general community.
This past year, as a prime example of the high calibre of Coun¬
cil work, a census was taken. The results of that self study were
so well received that requests for the Study were received by many
national organizations, public libraries, including the New York
Public Library and the Study itself was given a national award as
the "best of its kind’’ at a National Convention.
The Commentator, a monthly publication of the Community
Council, has similarly been given national awards as a Community
Newspaper. Serving the Jewish people of Jacksonville, its purpose
is to keep you informed of activities in the Jewish community.
The Community Calendar is another service of the Council and
it has already demonstrated its value by reducing conflicts in dates
between organizations. All organizations now clear dates with the
Council.
In addition to these functions, the Council maintains an up
to the minute list of the entire Jewish Community; brings the
presidents of different organizations together regularly to discuss
mutual problems and, in general, functions as the service organiza¬
tion for the Jewish Community. Perhaps this last can sum up the
Council’s program ... It is essentially a service organization cre¬
ated BY the Jews of Jacksonville, FOR any community-wide pro¬
grams of benefit TO the Jews of Jacksonville.

The Jewish Welfare Society is the family and children’s case
work agency of the Jewish community. Affiliated with the Commu¬
nity Chest-United Fund, the local program is almost entirely sup¬
ported by your contributions to the Chest. One of its programs, the
re-settlement of New Americans, is financed by funds that you con¬
tribute through the Jewish Community Council.
Since its inception in 1917, it has been helping individuals and
families with their personal problems. Through the years, with in¬
creased knowledge about the human behavior becoming available
to the professional and layman, the professionalization of the agency
and the growth of the city, the services of the agency shifted in stress
and the clientele broadened to include all segments of the commu¬
nity. The changing times we live in, is reflected by the annual
increase of the number of people served, in spite of the relative
prosperity we have been having during the past years.
About ninety per cent of the local requests are for counseling,
planning and other services that do not involve direct outlay of
funds. In our reports and statistics, so that we can see the trends
and needs, it is necessary to classify each request in a specific cate¬
gory; some of them are marital difficulty, difficulty in parent-child
relationship, unmarried parenthood, individual personality adjust¬
ment, planning substitute care for children, care for physically or
mentally ill (diagnosed-suspected), intellectual retardation, plan¬
ning with the aging, economic assistance, employment, New Ameri¬
can resettlement, migration, adoption requests, housing, transient
care, etc. We know though, that personal problems are like finger¬
prints with no two exactly alike. Usually they do not lend them¬
selves easily to categorization for they often appear in combinations.
For its counseling services, the agency adopted a sliding fee
schedule for those clients who are able and want to make such
payment. This same service, however, is available to all Jewish
residents and no one will be turned away because of payment or
non-payment.
With the developments of case work, there has not yet been
discovered a means to fill an empty stomach or assure a roof over
one’s head as well as money. The agency has been able to help
families through emergencies till they became self sufficient. It has
been able to supplement the small grants of the State Welfare
Department to widows, children and aged so that a minimum
standard of decency could be maintained. It has taken full responsi¬
bility for those who were ineligible for this help because of citizen¬
ship requirement, while helping the persons meet these requirements,
and for the totally incapacitated under 65, who until now, were ex¬
cluded from State Aid. It helps the transients with shelter and
board and when indicated, with counseling, employment or con¬
tacts with family. It conducts a limited social service program with
the Jewish prisoners at Raiford.
While helping the aging person remain in the community as
long as they can, the Society works in close relationship with the
River Garden Flebrew Home for the Aged in processing applications
and providing case work services to the residents and their families.
The Welfare Society for years has been the only licensed Jewish
child placing agency in Florida. We are pleased to announce that
the Jewish Social Service Agency in Miami has inaugurated a child
welfare program this year. Also, the demonstration Family Consul¬
tation Service that the Jewish Welfare Society sponsored, has borne
fruit in the establishment of a non-sectarian Family Consultation
Agency.

A Message From The Rabbi
Summit has become a household word. The hopes for world
peace were focused this summer on the statesmen of the summit
level meeting at Geneva. With the hopes went the prayer of the
millions who inhabit the globe.
How fitting is this mood at the approach to the Jewish New
Year. The key-note of our age-old prayers is "Inscribe Us in the
Book of Life and Peace." May our worship together on Rosh
Hashanah and Yom Kippur lead us to the summit of life’s values—
personal happiness supported by good health, the world’s salvation
founded on peace!
In the Jacksonville Jewish Center fellow-ship we are striving
towards the summit of the aims and purposes of the Synagogue.
Our Sanctuary is about to be adorned by a new Ark and Pulpit.
Our place of worship for year round purposes is to be completely
beautified and appointed. God grant us the insight to seek and de¬
rive spiritual fulfillment in the sacred environs.
Our wish of L’Shanah Tovah embrace the supreme good that the
Almighty can confer from the Summit of the world’s source of
goodness.

Troop 14, Boy Scouts of America enjoyed a very successful year
with emphasis placed on outdoor activities. The Troop participated
in an over-night camping trip, or its equivalent, every six weeks
from October to June. In June the Troop went to Camp Echockotee
for a week of Scouting activity.
in mind, Gene Shafton was promoted to the rank of Junior Asst.
Scoutmaster and Stephen Hackel promoted to the rank of Senior
Patrol Leader. The boys run the troop, and do an excellent job.
Mr. Grauer sincerely believes that responsibility should be given to
those who show the desire and ability to lead.
The Ner Tamid Award, the biggest religious award in scouting,
was presented to Herbert Fishier, Victor Schneider, and Ronnie
Levin. These honors were received at a very impressive Scout Sab¬
bath Service in which members of the Troop participated.
Scouts George Glossman and Ronnie Levin were selected by the
Troop Committee to go to Camp Philmont in New Mexico. The
boys selected for Camp Philmont must be fourteen years of age and
meet the qualifications set forth by the Troop Committee. Three
weeks of intensive Scout activity at Philmont is one of the ambitions
of every Scout.
At Camp Echockotee, Gene Shafton was inducted into the Order
of the Arrow, which is a National Scout Honor Society.
The Troop participated in all district activities, the highlight be¬
ing the Scoutorama at the Gator Bowl, where our Troop set up a
model camp in record time.
It has been the desire of Scoutmaster Grauer to see the Troop
run by the boys themselves under his guidance. With that thought
It is significant to note, that under the able leadership of Scout¬
master Grauer, and the chairmanship of Manny Goldstein and the
Committeemen listed above, Gene Shafton and Stephen Hackel be¬
came Eagle Scouts, and Ronnie Levin, George Glossman, Herbert
Fishier and Stephen Entman became Life Scouts.
This is the first time since 1951 that Troop 14 has had a pro¬
motion to Eagle Scout, and the first time since 1945 that two
Eagle Awards were made in the same year. As we go to press,
history is in the making as a third application for Eagle Scout is
in the process of being submitted by Herbert Fishier. This year
will be the first year since Troop 14 was organized in 1920, that
three Eagle Scout Awards will be made in one year.
In tribute to the memory of our late Scoutmaster, we close this
description with a paraphrase of his own prophetic words and
say, "The seeds that were planted by our late Scoutmaster Bernard
Glossman have flourished."

Building Fund
Progress and Prospect
by Harry Gendzier, Chairman
Last year on this page we published the picture that
appears below with the caption “Make This Picture A
Reality.’’ As we go to press this year that plea has almost
been realized. We reached this stage of progress by means
of the Holy Ark “Hai” Fund. This is how it happened.
At a meeting of the Building Committee and of the Sis¬
terhood’s Beautification Committee an idea was born. The
Holy Ark should be built by as many people as possible; by
parents honoring children, children honoring parents, rela¬
tives and friends showing reverent courtesy to one another.
It was agreed that “Hai” — Life — spelling in Hebrew 18,
translated into that many dollars would be in reach of every¬
one in multiples.
Amidst the solemnity of our High Holiday service, prior
to the Yom Kippur Yizkor, when we bring back memories of
departed dear ones and pray the blessings for our cherished
and beloved, the membership was advised in calm tones.
They were told that the contract for the Ark must be signed
soon and that this was an opportunity for registering faith
in the congregation, in Israel, and in Conservative Judaism.
The response came out of overwhelming appreciation
of life, family Love, Loyalty to tradition. Never before in
the history of the Jacksonville Jewish Center was a response
so spontaneous. Within a short time the offerings brought
us near our goal. Additional offerings were made later.
Hundreds of names are ready to be inscribed in the Golden
Book which will perpetuate the respect for Torah that
dwells in the heart of every member of the Center.
To rebuild the Sanctuary a great deal of money was and
still is required. Therefore, despite the wonderful support
of the “Hai” Fund we have to continue to appeal for larger
amounts. We still recall the joy with which we dedicated
the Activities Building and the Youth Center. May we re¬
joice equally at the dedication of our Synagogue and other
facilities yet to be built.
There is still time to reserve inscriptions in the Golden
Book with “Hai” contributions. Pages will be kept open
until after the Holidays. Link your name with the Torah
and its message. Continue to support the Building Fund
with larger contributions. Maintain the interest in our
Building Program that you have demonstrated up until now.
Maintain pride in being part of an outstanding Congrega¬
tion in Israel.

THE JEWISH RELIGIOUS CALENDAR
EXPLANATORY NOTES
The Jewish Religious Calendar is the result of many stages of
development. In ancient times the unit of time was the month,
determined by the phases of the moon. The Hebrew Calendar was
thus lunar. However, since religious Festivals are observed annual¬
ly, the calendar soon became related also to the solar system. The
lunar month consists of 291/2 days. In seven of a 19 year cycle an
extra month is added so that the religious occasions that are ob¬
served on the same date of the lunar month will occur in proper
season, which is determined by the solar calendar. Listed below are
the months in the Jewish Religious Calendar with their corre¬
sponding dates in the general calendar.
Tishri September-October
Heshvan October-November
Kislev November-December
Tebet December-January
Shebat January-February
Adar February-March

GENERAL NOTES
In this year’s edition of the Year Book the months run accord¬
ing to the Civil Calendar with the Hebrew date appearing in the
lower left corner.
On Fridays and on the eve of Festivals, as well as on the first
day of Festivals, there appears the symbol, L.C. "Light Candles."
Rosh Hodesh, Beginning of the Month, is observed with special
prayers in the service for that day and with the Blessing of the
New Month on the Sabbath previous. When the new moon appears
in the middle of the day the outgoing month consists of 30 days
and the last day is considered the first day Rosh Hodesh for the
coming month.
The Sidrah, the portion of the Torah read on Saturday, morn¬
ing, is indicated. Special Sabbaths will be explained below under
the month in which they occur.
TISHRI
Rosh Hashanah, Jewish Religious New Year.
Fast of Gedalia, commemorates the assassination of the gov¬
ernor of Judea who attempted to restore Jewish independence after
the destruction of the First Temple by the Babylonians.
Shahhat Shuva, Sabbath of Repentance, derives its name from
the Prophetic reading, "Shuva Yisrael,” "Return, O Israel.”
Yom Kippur, Day of Atonement, is a day of fasting and prayer,
a day for confession of sin and supplication for forgiveness. "For
transgressions between man and God, repentance on Yom Kippur
brings atonement. For transgressions between man and man, Yom
Kippur brings no atonement until amends are made.”
Sukkot, Tabernacles, commemorates the period when our fore¬
fathers dwelt in booths (Sukkot) during their wanderings through
the wilderness of Sinai.
Hoi Hamoed, Intermediate Days of the Holiday, are observed
as semi-festivals.
Hashannah Rahha, the day on which prayers with the refrain
Hosanah ("O Save Us”) is recited.
Shemini Atzeret, Eighth Day of Assembly, marks the conclu¬
sion of the fall festivals.
Simhat Torah, Torah Festival, is the second day of Shemini
Atzeret and celebrates the completion of the annual reading cycle
from the Five Books of Moses and the beginning of readings for
the New Year.
KISLEV
Hanukah, Feast of Dedication, commemorates the rededication
of the Temple by the Maccabees in 165 B.C.E. The Maccabean
triumph over Syrian forces was the first victory for a people’s right
to the freedom of worship. Lights are kindled in the synagogue
and in the home on every one of the eight evenings.
TEBET
Asar ah BeTehet, Fast of the 10th of Tebet, commemorates the
beginning of the siege of Jerusalem by the Babylonians in 586
B.C.E. which led to the destruction of the first Temple.
SHEBAT
Hamisha Asar BeShehat, also known as Tu BeShebat, 15th of
Shebat, is Holy Land Arbor Day or New Year of the Trees.
Shahhat Shekalim, the Sabbath when every adult Jew in the
days of the Temple was reminded to pay his poll tax of a half a
shekel on the first day of Adar.
ADAR
Shahhat Zakhor. On this Sabbath before the Festival of Purirn
a special portion of the Torah is read beginning with the word
"Zakhor”—"Remember Amaelk.” Haman is presumed to have
been an Amalekite.
Purim, commemorates the day when, through the intervention
of Mordecai and Queen Esther, the Jews of Persia narrowly escaped
being victims of Haman’s plot to exterminate them.
Shahhat Parah. A special portion of the Bible concerning the
ashes of the red heifer is read, reminding us of the ritual purifica¬
tion by our ancestors in preparing for the Passover Festival.
Shahhat Hahodesh. A special portion of the Bible is read, em¬
phasizing the significance of the month of Nisan in which Israel’s
great deliverance occurred.
NISAN
Shahhat Hagadol, the Sabbath which precedes the great Festi¬
val of Passover, derives its name from the Haftorah which con¬
tains a reference to the "Great Day of the Lord.”
Pesah, Passover, celebrates Israel’s deliverance from Egyptian
bondage. The festival lasts eight days during which matzot are
eaten in the place of leavened bread. The celebration of the Pass-
over with the beautiful ceremony of the Seder on the first and
second evenings has fostered by Jewish people the ideals of liberty
and freedom.
IYAR
Yom Ha’atzma’ut. The new State of Israel declared its in¬
dependence on May 14, 1948, which was the 5th of Iyar accord¬
ing to the Hebrew calendar.
Lag B' Omer, the date of a victory by Bar Kokhba in the final
Jewish struggle against the Romans. He was supported by the
sage Rabbi Akiba and his disciples. It is known as "The Scholars
Festival” and marked by field day outings.
SIVAN
Shavuot, Feast of Weeks, completes 7 weeks from the second
day of Passover on which the Omer (a measure) of the new barley
was brought to the Temple. It was also the day when Bikkurim
(first fruits) were presented. Tradition assigned to Shavuot com¬
memoration of the Covenant established at Sinai between God
and Israel and the giving of the Ten Commandments.
TAMMUZ
Shivah Asar Be’Tamuz, Fast of the 17th of Tammuz, that com¬
memorates the breach in the walls of Jerusalem by the Babylonians
in the Year 586 B.C.E.
Ab
Shahhat Hazon, precedes Tisha B’Ab derives its name from
the first words of the Haftorah "Hazon”—the vision of Isaiah,
which foretells the destruction of Jerusalem.
Tisha B’Ah, the Ninth Day of Ab, is a fast day which marks
the destruction of the First Temple in Jerusalem by the Baby¬
lonians (585 B.C.E.) and also of the Second Temple by the Ro¬
mans (70 C.E.)
Shahhat Nahamu, Sabbath of Consolation; the first word of the
Haftorah is "Nahamu, nahamu, Comfort ye, My People.”
ELUL
Selihot, Penitential Prayers, recited at midnight in preparation
for the holiday season, "The Great Days of Awe.”

Young Judaea
During the past season, 1954-55, Young Judaea sponsored 8 Clubs, four Junior
and four Senior. The program of Judaea consists of inclucating members with its four
point program—Jewish Living, American Affairs, Group Activities, and Israel and
Zionism. Towards these aims, all activities center, as follows:
1. Celebration of Jewish Holidays and an appreciation of Jewish living.
2. Living an American life on a civic level and learning of National affairs.
3. Group activities, which teach getting along with our fellow man, whether it
be putting out a newspaper, group dancing, or playing basketball.
4. Extending our hand in friendship to Israel and aiding however possible in its
growth.
Senior Clubs meet weekly in the Center or in private homes, and once a month
Chug sessions (discussion groups) are conducted under the direction of Mrs. H. Klausner.
The Senior Clubs also held two Oneg Shabbats during the year, at which time evaluations
were held, Israeli Dancing and Singing enjoyed and the Sabbath ushered out with the
Havdallah Service.
The Junior Clubs meet every other Sunday afternoon at 3 P.M. in the Center at
which time Holiday programs are presented, Israeli Dancing and Singing are taught, a
movie or speaker presented, and occasionally Arts and Crafts are enjoyed.
Outstanding affairs during the year were The Sabbath Service in the Center Synago¬
gue; Parents Night, held at the Center, at which time a program was presented on the
Tercentenary theme. Senior members attended the Conclave of the Southern Region
of Judaea in Daytona Beach at which the Trumpeldor Club were winners of the dance
contest for the Region. The annual Israel Fair was held at the River Garden Home in
February and all proceeds went to the Tsofim, the Scout movement in Israel. The Mac-
cabbee Club’s Basketball Team traveled to Savannah for its first out of town game. The
Ben Gurion Club published one edition of the Judaean Star. The year’s activities were
concluded with a dinner and Bikkurim Festival at the Jewish Center. A lovely cele¬
bration of the Bikkurim was presented in Dance, Song and Narration by members.
Prizes were awarded to outstanding members on the point system and Mrs. Klausner
was speaker for the occasion. Ten members of Senior Judaea were honored with
scholarship to Tel Yehudah, the National Camp of Judaea in Barryville, N. Y.
Young Judaea is sponsoed jointly by Hadassah and the Zionist Organization of
America, with a local Youth Commission of which Mrs. Dorothy Marx was Chairman
for a time. Mrs. Irving Brukner is Director of Young Judaea activities in the city.

Friends of The Seminary
Every great institution reflects the par¬
ticular genius of the wise, inspired and
dedicated men who help to create and sus¬
tain it. The Jewish Theological Seminary
of America is no exception. Since its found¬
ing in 1887, the Seminary has been blessed
with an extraordinary number of outstand¬
ing scholars and skilled interpreters who, in
every decade, have lent their piety and
greatness to building the Seminary and the
Movement which it represents.
On November 19, 1955, four decades
will have passed since the death of Solomon
Schechter, perhaps the greatest single spir¬
itual influence in American Judaism, in all
the three hundred years of its existence.
World famous as the foremost Rabbinic
scholar of his time, discoverer of more than
100,000 ancient Hebrew manuscripts which
had been stored for centuries in a neglected
room in a Cairo synagogue, master of Eng¬
lish literary style, Solomon Schechter won
his permanent place in Jewish history for
his reorganization of the Jewish Theological
Seminary of America in 1902, his establish¬
ment of the United Synagogue of America,
and of The United Synagogue of America,
and of The Rabbinical Assembly of Ameri¬
ca. Insofar as American Conservative Juda¬
ism is the work of any individual, it owes
its existence to him.
Schechter was born in Roumania, studied
SOLOMON SCHECHTER
in Vienna and Berlin, and at the age of
forty was brought to England by Claude
Montefiore. He was soon appointed Reader
in Rabbinics at Cambridge University.
Knowing his great talents and deeply in¬
spired by his personality, a group of Amer¬
ican Jewish leaders, including Cyrus Adler
(who was to succeed him as President of
the Seminary), Jacob Henry Schiff, Felix M.
Warburg, Louis Marshall, Sol M. Srtoock,
Leonard Lewisohn, and Irving Lehman, per¬
suaded him in 1902 to come to America to
be head of the Seminary. He became Pres¬
ident of its Faculty, and served until his
death.
His works on Jewish Theology and his
three volumes of Studies in Judaism are still
widely read texts. One of his most impor¬
tant Jewish historical discoveries was a doc¬
ument describing the life story of an ancient
Jewish sect, whose very existence had been
so completely forgotten that no Jewish his¬
tory mentioned it. It is this sect, described
in one of Schechter’s books, which was re¬
sponsible for the preservation of many of
the Jericho Scrolls now being brought to
light from caves near the Dead Sea.
Each congregation, and every one of its
members is a beneficiary of Schechter’s dedi¬
cation to Judaism, for, without his guidance,
the Conservative Movement in its present
form, and with its present energy, could
never have been developed.
The wisdom of Solomon Schechter, is for
all time, a living, vital force in American
Judaism, lending strength to those who love
and study Torah, and live by its eternal
precepts.

with its national institutions through the annual Friends of the

nual membership campaign. Last year the effort was in charge of a

nent personalities in the Conservative Movement have visited Jack¬
sonville for conferences with Center leadership and for pubkc
appearances. This year we had Prof. Shalom Spiegel, Dr. Abraham
Halkin, Dr. Bernard Mandelbaum, Dr. Hugo Weisgall and Rabbi
Harry Halpern, President of the Rabbinical Assembly of America.

To report on the services and activities of the
River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged during the
last year, is to tell the story of the lives of our resi¬
dents and their families. The role the Home plays in
our community of giving protection and care to the
helpless, the lonely, the senile aged has been well
demonstrated over the years. It is not a dramatic role
but that of a day to day living pattern which strives to
make people more comfortable. That the Home has
succeeded in carrying out the vision of the Ladies
Hebew Sheltering Aid Society has been proven by
the growth and progress of River Garden.
The Home, today, is on the threshold of new
developments and expansion. The increasing needs
of older people requiring special care has made it
imperative to build again. The Home plans to start
shortly on the construction of a new building adjoin¬
ing the present buildings. This new unit will include
a thirty bed infirmary to provide the most modern
facilities to care for the many medical problems pre¬
sented by an aging resident population. A new and
modern kitchen will enable the Home to provide for
special dietary needs as well as facilitate the prepara¬
tion and serving of Kosher foods. The new Chapel
will be large enough to allow all the residents attend¬
ing services to sit within the sanctuary of the Chapel
rather than among desks and filing cabinets. It will
also provide a wing where the well aged residents
who require minimum nursing services, can lead a
more active life.
When the new addition is built, the Home will
be able to care for 75 residents and give them the
necessary services to meet the needs o feach indi¬
vidual. It will provide the setting in which the aged
oerson can find security and comfort at the time of
ife when they need these supports most.
River Garden, in accepting the responsibility of
caring for the older Jewisfi people in our State, has
discharged its responsibilities to its residents, their
families, and the Jewish communities in a most
creditable mnner. The Home has anticipated many
of the problems of the aged and is prepared to meet
the challenge of the future.

Council of Jewish Women
MRS. J. L. MACK, JR.
The National Council of Jewish Women was organized in
1863 and is one of America’s oldest Women’s organizations. There
are 245 Sections with over 108,000 members.
Council is dedicated to the full achievement of the demo¬
cratic way of life. Its purpose is "to bring about a closer fellow¬
ship among Jewish women, to further united efforts in behalf of
Judaism, to foster a program devoted to social welfare, community
affairs, education and international understanding for peace.
Council’s overseas projects are threefold: the support of the
School of Education of Hebrew University in Jerusalem, mainte¬
nance of graduate scholarships to students from Israel and European
countries for study in American Universities, shipments of clothing,
plan and study material to Jewish orphanages in the Middle East.
In Jacksonville, Council has made great strides through our
community services of the Medical Loan Shelf, Golden Age Club,
Sewing, and the Naval Air Station parties. This year our new
project in the city will be the weekly entertainment of a group
of girls at the Children’s Home Society. Our Book project in which
we joined with 2 other local groups, the United Church Women
and the Women’s Club of Jacksonville in furnishing books for a
new Junior High School, received the Necchi Award as the out¬
standing Civic project in Florida for last year. We plan to continue
the collection for other schools in our community.

President and Chairman of the Board
PHILIP SELBER, PRESIDENT
In the preparation of this message to the Congregation on the eve of
the advent of the year 5716, I reviewed my previous messages to the
Congregation. It is interesting and satisfying to note the changes and
trend in the character of these messages as the congregation progresses
toward spiritual maturity.
In the earlier years of my tenure of office, the appeal to the con¬
gregation was for more adequate financial support, both for the ordinary
budgetary requirements and for the building program. My later messages
dealt more with the need for an expanded educational program. It is there¬
fore quite logical that this year’s message should deal with the necessity
for a spiritual rededication of the congregation to our religious practices
and beliefs.
Such a religious rededication becomes a climax to the years of our
building a program and a physical plant to maintain it, because this year
will be the year for the dedication of our synagogue and its new Ark.
But even more important in causing this to become a climax to all of
our efforts is the fact that it emphasizes that the final and all-important
goal of all our programming is the spiritual development of the members
of our congregation.
Some may say that it is impossible to effect a return of our congregants
to the traditional religious practices of our people because of the environ¬
ment in which we live. Others may blame the deviations of our congregants
on the changes which have taken place in the character of the Jewish
community in America from what it was in Europe. Many other ex¬
planations can be offered, but reasons and alibis, no matter how logical,
will not supply us with the means to correct these failures.
The prominent challenge for us today is whether or not it is possible
for synagogues in America to effect a return of their members to
Judaism. It is inconceivable that the history of our people’s religious
development is destined to have its closing chapters in America. It is
inconceivable because the Jewish religion has always, through the years,
shown a tremendous capacity to adapt itself to the climate and to the
ROBERT J. GORDON, CHAIRMAN OF THE BOARD
circumstances in which it found itself—whether those circumstances were
of freedom and opportunity or of persecution and oppression. Certainly
the Jewish religion itself has lost none of this vitality in being able to meet
new situations.
Secondly, it is inconceivable that the religious rededication is not poss¬
ible in America, because America is a land of religious freedom and oppor¬
tunity. Even more so, America is a land based on religious principles and
a land where church and synagogue loyalty is a national ideal.
Thirdly, it is inconceivable because of the strength and resourceful¬
ness of the American Jewish community. Never in Jewish history has
there been a community equal to the contemporaneous Jewish community
in ability and opportunity. No community in Jewish history has had
more concentration of wisdom, intellect, wealth and affluence than has had
our own American community.
The only possibility of failure in our quest for religious rededication
then lies within ourselves. Failure will only come from our own lack
of conviction that we can succeed in America in fashioning a great
religious culture. If we are convinced that Judaism can regain its role
as a dominant influence in the shaping of our lives, then it will.
This challenge for religious rededication thus becomes a real one for
our congregation as it stands on the eve of the rededication of its syna¬
gogue and Ark. May we summon to our cause the zeal and the determina¬
tion that even here in Jacksonville we shall meet well this appointment with
history. May we be privileged to achieve in 5716 these goals for a more
profound and more inspirational Jewish life.
In the name of the Officers and members of the Board of Directors
and the Board of Trustees, I extend greeting to all of our members with
the traditional blessing for a Happy New Year, “Le Shanah Toavah
Tiketevu,”.
“May the Almighty to whom we pray for personal welfare and for
the welfare of humanity, render the coming year auspiciously inscribed in
history and in His will.”

B’nai B’rith is the world’s oldest and largest Jewish service
organization. Founded in 1843 in New York City, B’nai B’rith
has today more than 350,000 members, united in almost 2,000
lodges and chapters in 300 countries of the free world. Members
of B’nai B’rith, over a period of 112 years, unselfishly gave of
their time to help others, fought for the principles of democracy and
represented the interests of Jewish people whenever the need arose.
B’nai B’rith has never altered its aims which were stated in
the first constitution of 1843, as follows:
"B’nai B’rith has taken upon itself the mission of uniting
Israelites in the work of promoting their highest interests and those
of humanity; of developing the mental and moral character of the
people of our faith; of inculcating the purest principles of philan¬
thropy, honor and patriotism; of supporting science and art; alle¬
viating the wants of the poor and needy; visiting and attending
the sick; coming to the rescue of victims of persecution; providing
for, protecting and assisting the widow and orphan on the broadest
principles of humanity.
The local lodge has over 200 members.

Organized in 1953 by a small group of men and women, the
Mr. and Mrs. Club has attained a membership of one hundred
couples, fulfilling a long needed social functioning group in the
Jacksonville Jewish community. Created for the purpose of social
get-togethers, this club has been the means of bringing its members
together in a harmonious spirit of friendship and good fellowship.
In electing officers and board of directors, it has been the cus¬
tom for husband and wife to serve together, thereby creating a
governing body that works cooperatively and wholeheartedly to¬
ward providing the maximum benefits the organization has to offer
for its membership.
Among the gala events this year were the installation cere¬
monies ,the first anniversary celebration, a New Year’s party, and
many costume and informal affairs, planned for the entertainment
of members, their families and guests.
The sincere efforts of the officers, board of directors and com¬
mittees who served during its first year of organization, have estab¬
lished a firm foundation for the Mr. and Mrs. Club, and we feel
that it will continue to progress and prove to be one of the most
outstanding social groups in the city.
Success for its affairs is due also in no small part to the co¬
operation of the Jacksonville Jewish Center and for the facilities
made available for our use.

The Jacksonville Chapter of Senior Hadassah is com¬
posed of approximately 750 Jewish women who meet month¬
ly to serve together in the ranks of the American Zionist
movement. Meeting in the afternoon on the second Tues¬
day of each month, or in the evening with the Hannah
Senesch Group, on the second Monday, Hadassah’s mem¬
bers join in efforts to affect the National Hadassah pro¬
gram of health and vocational education services to Israel;
and the dedication of our local Jewish women to active par¬
ticipation in the life and expression of community and
American affairs.
The story of Hadassah is the tale of a group of women
who were instrumental in sending two American trained
nurses to Jerusalem in 1913 to ease the terrific mortality
rate, and who remained to develop a great health program,
not only to save the mother and the newborn child from
painful death, but also to build education and health facili¬
ties to rear the infant into healthy childhood and produc¬
tive adulthood.
Jacksonville’s Hadassah membership joins with 300,000
Jewish women throughout the country to maintain seven
hospitals, a school of nursing, the Hebrew University-Ha-
dassah Medical School, the New Hadassah-Hebrew Uni¬
versity Medical Center, Youth Aliyah training farms, and
general youth medical services emanating from the Jeru¬
salem child health centers, together with a network of vo¬
cational education facilities which range from printing to
hotel management and fashion design.
Today Hadassah’s experiment is preventive and com¬
munity medicine at Bet Mazmil encompasses the entire
family unit, and promises to serve as a pattern for health
programs for the world’s progressive governments. Belgium
and Burma have asked for blueprints of the Met Mazmil
plan, and the Hadassah woman in Jacksonville is aware
that through her active participation in her chapter activi¬
ties she is sharing in this historic movement.
The Hadassah motto, “The Healing of My People”
serves to remind that the healer is made well even as the
bedridden and diseased are restored. By working through
established civic organizations, and through the Hadassah
education program, the Hadassah woman joins in molding
a healthy community here.
Together with Z.O.A. Hadassah supports the Jewish
National Fund for the rehabilitation of the land of Israel,
and Zionist Youth Work through Young Judaean Clubs.
The projects are carried out through individual con¬
tributions, and through fund earning events. Highlights of
the coming year will be the opening Brunch to be held
in the Jewish Center on October 11th, the Membership
Tea, an event of November 8th, and the "Salute Florida-
Margaret Newman Fashion Show” which will be presented
in the early winter. December will feature Henrietta
Szold-Youth Aliyah Parties. On the evening of February
1st Hadassah will join with the other Jacksonville Zionist
groups to present the Israeli Fashion Show at the Center.
The year 195 5-56 will also include a "Dollars for Linen”
shower for Hadassah-Hospital Supplies; and the Fund-
earners Luncheon in the early spring which will honor
Hadassah Donors.
Organization membership is open to all Jewish women.
