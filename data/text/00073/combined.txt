>-A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
THE TRUE ESSENCE OF JEWISH TIME: TEN YEARS LATER
A little more than a decade ago, I wrote the following in my very first Yearbook message
to the congregation:
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
Formally, a rabbi becomes the spiritual leader of a particular synagogue the day his
contract indicates. Yet the most significant defining moments of the relationship are
elusive of any specific date on the calendar A rabbi becomes the spiritual leader of
a community through moments of shared joy and sorrow . . . one family at a time. As
these interactions unfold over time, the worshippers in the pews are transformed into
the rabbi s flock - congregants, neighbors and friends. As for the fellow standing on
the bimah, he is no longer simply the rabbi, but has become instead your rabbi.
What I meant to say in that message was that rites of passage can only describe the liminal
moments of life, the outer shell of existence, but they shed little light on the content of life
itself. A simhat bat or bris confirms the beginning of one’s Jewish journey, but says nothing about the kind of Jew one will
become; a wedding ceremony makes a marriage official, but cannot determine the strength of the union between spouses; a rabbi’s
installation acknowledges a professional association, but won’t tell you anything about the relationship that a rabbi will have with
his congregation. It is only in the grand total of days lived, lives touched, and lessons learned that one can grasp the meaning of
any experience.
Of course, there is nothing inherently significant in marking the end of my tenth year at the Jacksonville Jewish Center - Was
I less your rabbi a year ago? Will I be more your rabbi twelve months from now? Still, ten is a nice round number, a convenient
place to pause and take stock of the road traveled and the distance yet to cover.
Looking at the photos from the 2004 Yearbook, I am struck by how much less gray I had in my beard. Susan’s hair was
longer, and our children were oh so young! I see class pictures of infants who are now in junior high, school-age children who
have become adults, and elder statesmen who have gone to their final rest. There have been so many changes, it would be
impossible to count them all.
In the last ten years, we have become an egalitarian synagogue; engaged in keruv to reach out to intermarried families, as well
as gay and lesbian Jews; created a Second Rabbi position; added instrumental music to Friday evening services in the summer and
to Sunday morning services; created an endowment; merged with Beth Shalom and integrated its members; revamped our Hebrew
High program; engaged talented new staff to serve in key positions on multiple occasions; organized the Galinsky Academy and
named each of its constituent educational pillars; added members; increased our commitment to tikkun olam and social justice;
connected more effectively with members in their twenties and thirties; re-imagined our religious school pedagogy and introduced
21st century technology to all our schools; in addition to myriad other changes. In some of these changes I played a critical role,
in others a supporting actor, in still others I was simply an appreciative bystander.
Innovation is not synonymous with accomplishment. While some of our initiatives have been very successful, others have
only attained more modest levels of success. There are those who welcome change simply because it is new, and those who fear it
for the same reason. As a synagogue we cannot afford to embrace either extreme - targeted change, consonant with our values and
principles as a Conservative synagogue and undertaken for the sake of maximizing Jewish learning and living, is good. Lowering
the bar, simply to take the easier path of least resistance, is not.
No matter what, our ultimate goal remains the same: to inspire Jews to live more connected and committed lives - to the
mitzvot of the Torah, to the State of Israel, to fellow Jews around the world, and to the larger
Jacksonville community of which we are a part. As for me, as much as I have changed
outwardly and inwardly these past ten years, I will continue to strive to be and to become
your rabbi - one family, one person, one milestone, one Shabbat, and one day at a time.
Susan, Avichai, Elior and Itamar join me in wishing you a year of life, health, and
blessing. May 5775 usher in a year of continued growth for our synagogue, and a year of
peace for Israel, America and humankind.
“I said: Let days speak, and many years teach wisdom.”
-Job 32:7

Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner

b- A Message from Rabbi Tilman —I
NEW BEGINNINGS
Shabbat has always been an important part of my life. From dinners at home with
family, to afternoons by the pool and on the softball field at camp, to walks around the park
in Los Angeles, Shabbat has always been something I look forward to. As a teenager, just
about every Shabbat was the same for me. I would spend the morning at services, and then
in the afternoon would gather together with friends for multiple epic games of Monopoly.
Many people say you can learn a lot of lessons from Monopoly - skills of negotiation, long
term investments, managing a budget - but the most important lesson I learned from those
games was the thrill of passing “Go.” Every time around the board, you received $200 and
were able to celebrate a new beginning.
New beginnings have been on my mind a great deal recently. Beginning my career
as a Rabbi, beginning my life in Jacksonville, and beginning my time with the JJC
community, I always strive to be someone who approaches new beginnings with a sense of optimism and excitement.
Sure there are a great many questions and a great many reasons to be nervous, but I consistently find that the causes for
excitement outnumber the causes for questioning.
These beginnings though, are not always easy. We learn in Pirkei Avot that the original new beginning, the creation of
the world, took ten utterances from God in order to be completed. (Pirkei Avot 5:1) Why did God use ten utterances when
He could have used only one? To reward those righteous people who protect a world that was created with ten utterances.
In beginning anything, just like creating the world, there can be an easy way to do things. But there can also be a
longer, fuller way to complete the task. It is this longer way of beginning that truly excites me. To dive deeply into the
community, to find out what makes it unique, to find out what makes it special, and then to find those places that could use
a new beginning.
Each of us can do this in our own lives. To use ten utterances in starting our years anew. To take the long way in
learning what makes each of us special and then finding the places that could use a new beginning. The High Holidays are
just this opportunity - the chance to approach our new year as a new beginning. A chance to be excited about those new
opportunities that will present themselves. A chance to let go of that which has been holding us back over the past year.
A chance to start anew.
“Hayom harat haolam ” we read following the Shofar blasts on Rosh Hashanah. “Today is the birthday of the world.”
If we approach this High Holiday season as our collective birthday, our collective new beginning, I know that each of us
can use our own ten utterances towards creating something new.
I thank you all for giving me the opportunity of sharing my own new beginning with each of you and for welcoming
me so warmly into the JJC family. I know that 5775 will be an incredible year for all of us and I am thrilled to be spending
it with you.
Shanah Tovah u ’Metukah, may we all have a happy, a healthy and a sweet new year filled with many new beginnings.
Rabbi Howard Tilman
Rabbi Howard Tilman

►— A Message from the Hazzan —I
THE SHMITA PROJECT
Since creation, tradition has guided us towards a perfect cocktail to guide
our lives - six parts work, one part rest. This sense of Shabbat, a seventh period
of rest, is paralleled later in our scripture when the Israelites are instructed to let
the land rest for an entire year following six years of hard labor. Thus began the
“sabbatical” year. Yet the seventh year is far more than a period to relax and let
loose. The seventh year is given the title Shmita, meaning ‘release.’ The Jewish
non-profit Hazon, a group that fosters the goal of a healthier and more sustainable
planet, has created a The Shmita Project to raise awareness for the Shmita cycle.
The next Shmita year actually begins on this Rosh Hashanah 2014. The core
values center on reimagination, renewal, release, forgiveness, and rethinking.
During these High Holidays, we reflect not only on the past year, but on the past
six years. It is a chance to explore and reimagine ourselves through the lens of
time. It is a chance to release ourselves from what is comfortable and norm of
practice so that we may rethink the ways in which we can renew ourselves and
our community.
This Rosh Hashanah also marks my own Shmita year as cantor of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. No, it
is not time for a sabbatical. It is, however, a chance to review and reflect on all the relationships and events
that have shaped my family’s experience here in Jacksonville over the past six years. The title Hazzan,
taken from the same Hebrew root as the aforementioned Hazon, meaning ‘vision.’ One would think that
the title would mimic its English equivalent, cantor, meaning ‘to sing.” Being a Hazzan is not only about
coming into a pulpit, but into a community with a vision in mind. It is about revisiting that vision with the
acknowledgement that the status quo must be pushed musically, programmatically and spiritually.
As we begin the New Year, the vision is clear: more music. We have expanded our Shabbat musical
repertoire. We now offer Friday Night Shabbat in the Round and Koltrain family services. We’ve introduced
new melodies into our services. We’ve created special Shabbat Shira programming, Shabbat at Sea
adventures, dynamic Purim Shpiels, as well as community wide concerts and sing-a-longs. Our Harmonia
and High Holiday chorales have enriched our services. This is the beginning. Let us take this Shmita year to
take all of this and more to the next level. I encourage you to contact me if you are interested in becoming
more involved in the music of our community.
Our family has been blessed to be a part of this sacred community. Leora and I arrived here a week after
our wedding and we have embraced and been embraced by this
congregation. We have grown together, laughed together, shared
in simchas and times of sorrow. Our daughter was named in our
sanctuary. May we continue to share for years to come.
Leora, Rena and I wish you all a happy, healthy, and sweet
new year. May we all be proud of what we have accomplished.
May we all find joy and excitement in what lies ahead.
Hazzan Jesse Holzer

DAILY LITURGY
|= —= - ■ —
Scott Zimmerman
Ritual Director
“Praised be You, God...who commands us to busy ourselves with words
of Torah.” Talmud Torah (the study of sacred Jewish texts) is so important in
Jewish tradition that the daily liturgy begins with this formulaic blessing.
It is followed by short passages from the Bible, as well as the Mishnah and
the Talmud, the central works of rabbinic Judaism. All of Jewish religious
literature can be considered “Torah.”
Elsewhere in the daily liturgy, Jews thank God for expressing love for the
Jewish people by granting them Torah: Both the Five Books of Moses and, more
generally, Jewish teachings. Words of Torah “are our life and the length of our
days,” the prayer book says; “day and night we will meditate upon them.”
More than simply a means to learning the content of divinely revealed law, Torah study is an end in itself,
and according to some, another way of worshipping God. The Torah is portrayed in rabbinic tradition as
predating all history; the very blueprint according to which God constructed the universe. To study Torah in
depth, then, is to do more than leam prescribed behaviors; it is to approach an understanding of the foundations
of all existence and the pathways of the divine. According to some rabbinic sources, therefore, the Israelites’
acceptance of the Torah at Mt. Sinai enabled the world to continue to exist and not be plunged back into
primordial chaos.
I would like to thank the many members of our congregation who have added their voices to the process
that is Torah. Through their dedication and devotion, we have maintained a high level of participation in
reading Torah. The commitment and encouragement of our synagogue organizations and their leaders will
ensure that this quality standard will be continued for many generations to come. I would like to thank all of
our teachers for their continued support and cooperation.
On behalf of Suzanne, Casie, Jared, Aron, Berk and myself we wish the entire congregation a heartfelt
Shanah Tovah,
Scott Zimmerman

A Message from the President -4
REWARDS IN VOLUNTEERING
What a great year it has been to have the opportunity to share Shabbat, the
High Holy Days, meetings and life cycle events with many of you. I have also
been fortunate to work with a terrific group of leaders who have come together
to form the Board and Executive Committee of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
If you ask anyone who is involved as a volunteer at the Center or in our
community they will tell you how meaningful and fulfilling it is to give back.
These volunteers have carved out time from their busy schedule for meetings,
returning emails and making phone calls. It is my hope that I can persuade
you to get involved at the Center in our many committees or get involved in
our Sisterhood or Men’s Club. Your giving back will make this a bright and
rewarding New Year. Give our office team a call and they will tell you how.
Speaking of volunteers, this year we faced the challenge of replacing
three of our professional staff positions. We formed three search committees. Volunteers stepped up
to the challenge spending hundreds of hours doing Skype interviews, working on travel logistics and
arranging meet and greets. The hard work turned out to be very successful with all three openings filled in
late spring.
Join me in welcoming three top professionals to our family: Rabbi Rogozen, our head of the Galinsky
Academy, Rabbi Tilman, who will work closely with our youth, schools and Shabbat programming for our
kids, then last, but certainly not least, is Steven Resnick, who will lead our youth groups. These gentlemen
are a great addition to the Jacksonville Jewish Center and will truly enhance the programming for our
children. They will help us complete the fourth pillar of education at the Galinsky Academy.
We go into the New Year with a solid game plan in place for our children. My challenge this year is to
work on programming to get you more involved and engaged. Let me know what I can do to get you more
connected to the Center. We want to be the center of your life; tell us how.
On behalf of my wife, Wendy and our children, Jereme, Zachary and Noah, we wish you a happy and
healthy New Year.
Shanah Tovah,
Fred Pozin

MEMBERSHIP COMMITTEE
The Membership Committee at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center wishes all of our
Center family, “L’Shanah Tovah!” We on the
committee serve as the hosts and hostesses of
the synagogue. Our goal is to try to make all
of our members, both new and old alike, feel
welcome in our combined spiritual home. All
Center members should feel like a member of the
Membership Committee, inviting the unaffiliated
to join our family and to reach out to unfamiliar
faces when in the building to connect with those
you do not know.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is one large
family. We come together as a community in the
celebration of life cycle events: Baby Namings
and Brit Milah, Graduations from our schools
of the Galinsky Academy, B’nai Mitzvahs,
weddings, and, sadly, our loved ones’ passings.
Many have chosen to celebrate the anniversaries
of these life cycle events with their Center family.
We are also excited about our renewed Havurah Program. Joette Anderson worked diligently to get the
program going and Amy Levinson and Paula Garfinkel have now taken over the reins as co-chairs. A havurah
is a small group of individuals, couples or families, who share common interests and the desire to meet
with others on a regular basis for the purpose of developing a more intimate community within the Center
family. Since the last High Holy Days we have been able to form six new groups. If you are interested in
joining a havurah, contact the Center office and fill in the havurah interest form so that we can do our best
in matching you with other members with common interests.
The weekly “Shabbat Greeter” program is coordinated by Aliza Morris, a member of the Membership
Committee. I want to give a big thanks to all our volunteers this year as it gives our synagogue a great first
welcoming impression each Shabbat morning.
I would like to thank all the current Membership Committee members this year who have supported
our programs. We look forward to continued participation in the support and growth of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center.

ADULT EDUCATION COMMITTEE
The Adult Education Committee continues
to be committed to offering a myriad of
educational opportunities for our synagogue and
community. The dedication and commitment of
our committee members in conjunction with the
support of Lois Tompkins, Staff Liaison, Rabbi
Lubliner, Rabbi Olitzky, and Hazzan Holzer,
helped our ideas become reality and ensure the
success of our programs.
The Marilyn and Louis Safer Shabbaton
featured Scholar in Residence Rabbi Steve
Greenburg, the first openly gay Orthodox Rabbi.
Prior to his visit, we provided programming to
learn about the LGBT issues within the context of Conservative Judaism. We co-sponsored Hazzan Holzer’s
Shabbat Shira with actor Jon Adam Ross as he performed his one-man show, Walking in Memphis: The Life
of a Southern Jew. Holiday programming included Selihot speakers Victoria Ruvolo, author of No Room
for Vengeance, High Holiday cooking class at Publix, and adult classes for Rosh Hashanah and Shavuot.
We organized an evening program featuring Nancy Soderberg, American foreign policy strategist and
Distinguished Visiting Scholar at the University of North Florida, and were involved with WJCT in presenting
the “Swastika to Jim Crow” program. We had a great turnout for Professor Stephen Cantor’s presentation:
“Genesis and the Big Bang Theory.”
Our weekly ongoing classes include Foundations of Judaism, Talmud, Monday Morning Mapquest, Torah
reading classes, and Mishnah. We offer three beginning Hebrew classes and are piloting a new Online Hebrew
class through a partnership with the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs and ShalomLearning, Inc. Monthly
classes include Coffee and Torah, Shabbat Meditation, movies, Nosh and Drash, B’nei Mitzvah classes for
parents of upcoming Bar/Bat Mitzvahs, and supporting parent education programs in the Galinsky Academy.
Through the hard work of Elaine Furman and Wendy Honigman, the Adult Library is finally a reality. The
Adult Library houses a wealth of Jewish texts and books that are available for checkout. We encourage you to
come and visit this invaluable resource.

RELIGIOUS LIFE COMMUNITY
During the year 5774, the Religious Life Community (RLC), under the leadership of Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
and Vice-President Steven Mizrahi, worked to shape and guide the religious direction of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center. This included the introduction of the triennial cycle of reading the Torah in its entirety over a three-
year period to encourage greater participation and improve the flow of Shabbat services. In addition, the RLC
conducted a “listening tour” this past spring to solicit feedback and perspective from a wide range of members
about Shabbat services at the Center. In the year ahead, we will continue to explore new ways to bring innovation
to our worship as we reach out beyond the current circle of regulars, while maintaining the vital traditions of the
Center’s religious heritage.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is proud to offer a daily morning and evening minyan, one of only a few
Conservative congregations in our region to do so. The RLC is committed to ensuring that minyan remains a
strong part of our synagogue’s core values. It affords individuals on a daily basis the opportunity to connect
with God and other members of the community, and provides mourners with a place to recite Kaddish for loved
ones. With the efforts put forth by the RLC, attendance at our daily minyanim has increased over the past year.
Many thanks to Hazzan Holzer, as well as our Ritual Director, Scott Zimmerman, for their hours of devotion
and service to our congregation. We welcome Rabbi Tilman to the JJC’s clergy team, and look forward to
working with him. We are also grateful for the efforts of the devoted members of the RLC for their hard work
in helping to shape the future of our spiritual home.

SOSHUL NETWORK
“The SoShul Network” was established in early 2013 as a group for 20 and 30-somethings of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center to network and enjoy the company of new and old friends. The committee plans Jewish-themed
social events to engage its members as well as promotes activities and programs. Whether a recent college
graduate, young professional, married couple, new parent, or single, there is something for everyone!
During its inaugural year, many socials were planned that brought the next generation of Center leaders
together. The first event was a pool party and cookout that took place at the Center in August 2013. In October,
The SoShul Network committee held a Shabbat dinner at the Center that included songs, blessings, and Jewish
trivia. In November, the women of The SoShul Network attended “ladies night out” at Painting with a Twist
where they painted a Shabbat tabletop scene on canvas. The committee threw a Hanukkah celebration at Intuition
Ale Works in December with food and drink. Most recently in May, the group hosted a potluck brunch at a
home in Mandarin to kick-off the spring season.
The SoShul Network was successful in attracting over 30 people to each event, including many young
Jewish members who have recently moved to Jacksonville. As the committee begins planning events for the
upcoming year, it hopes to reach out to additional 20 and 30-somethings in the community who have not yet
become involved. It also intends to maintain the interest and participation of those who are already active. The
SoShul Network committee has created a working list of group members and is continuously adding names
and contact information.
If you would like to attend any upcoming socials, please email SoShulNetwork2030@gmail.com.

SOCIAL ACTION
The Social Action Committee at the Center has continued to be involved with several ongoing projects.
Every other month on the second Sunday, volunteers spend several hours at Sulzbacher Center for the
Homeless preparing and serving meals to hundreds.
We have monthly drives to upcycle many items to the Teacher’s Supply Depot that are used in our public
schools for both teaching tools and art projects. Their list of needs is extensive and the creativity used in
reusing these items is limitless.
Current magazines have been donated to area hospitals, River Garden and to local senior centers.
We continue to support the ConKerr Cancer Miles of Smiles pillowcase projects by making and donating
pillowcases for each child who enters an area hospital. We need people to sew the pillowcases, people to
donate fabric or to donate money for the purchase of fabric.
One of our most successful social action projects for the past two years is the combined effort with The
Temple to provide Meals on Wheels on Christmas Day. This project involves hundreds of hands to prepare
and deliver food. The cost for the food is sponsored by the local Aging True agency. This past year an
additional component was added to the project. It was our goal to have cookies donated to take to each area
fire station. The support was AMAZING and we had enough cookies to not only bring dozens to each fire
station, but to each local police station as well. Over 3,000 cookies were donated and delivered and 410
dinners were cooked, packaged and delivered.
We continue to help where and when we can and always need more involvement.

HEVRA KADDISHA
During the course of a lifetime, no one can avoid an encounter with death;
yet, it is an experience for which no one is prepared. Judaism embraces all of
life, of which death is a natural part, and so ascribes rituals which dignify our
departure.
The care of the deceased immediately following death is the solemn
responsibility of the Hevra Kaddisha, the holy society, consisting of dedicated
laypersons from the community. Members are knowledgeable in the traditional
duties, and perform them with the utmost respect for the deceased. The process
begins with the Taharah, purification, a ritual cleansing and preparation of the
body for burial. In addition, a Shomer (guardian) will stay at the side of the
deceased reciting required prayers until the burial.
It is gratifying to have a completely independent Jewish organization, under
the sole leadership of lay people, maintain such a high level of commitment and
dedication to one of the most important traditions in Jewish life.
Members of the Jacksonville Jewish community can take comfort in the
knowledge that the services of the Hevra Kaddisha are offered to all Jews
regardless of financial means or synagogue affiliation. Please feel free to call any
of our officers or rabbis with concerns or questions. “Dedication” is our motto.
The Hevra Kaddisha would like to extend its gratitude and appreciation to
Nathan “Sonny” Sandler who served as president for over two decades. Sonny
presided with care, warmth and sensitivity. The Hevra Kaddisha is integral part
of our community thanks to his tireless and kind dedication.

SHABBAT AT SEA
This past February, eighty-eight members of the Jacksonville Jewish Center community all went aboard Royal
Caribbean’s Enchantment of the Seas for our second installment of Shabbat at Sea, or as Hazzan Holzer would
dub it, Shabbat at Sea Part Deux. Many of our cruisers were traveling via cruise ship for the very first time, but
we had a core group of repeat cruisers from our experience aboard the Carnival Sensation two years prior. Our
group ranged in age from infant to those in their 90s, with everyone keeping themselves busy while on board this
four day adventure. Participants enjoyed the sights and sounds of Nassau, Bahamas and relaxed on the beaches
of CocoCay, Royal Caribbean’s private island. There were variety shows and entertainers on board, but it was the
group experience that people enjoyed the most. Our Friday night acoustic service literally rocked the boat. Our
Saturday morning Torah study kept our minds working, while our impromptu Gan Shabbat program was a hit as
well. Karaoke was the highlight of the cruise - from circle dancing to “Hava Nagila” to having our young ones jam
out to “Single Ladies.” Shabbat at Sea is definitely ready for part three!

Shalom!
I am very happy to join the Jacksonville Jewish
Center community. Your longstanding support for
quality Jewish education is legendary within the
Conservative Movement. Your strong community,
based on warmth and respect for all, has been obvious
from the first time I visited Jacksonville. I have been

leaders who help guide JJC’s programs.
GALINSKY
ACADEMY
Rabbi Jim Rogozen
Head of Galinsky Academy
Chair - Alyse Nathans
The Galinsky Academy provides Jewish children of all ages, from infancy
to high school graduation, the highest quality education possible. Comprised of
DuBow Preschool, Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School, Martin J. Gottlieb
Day School, and Setzer Youth Education, the Galinsky Academy embodies the
commitment that all of our children - regardless of the path their parents choose - benefit from the finest teachers,
an engaged clergy, the highest-quality curriculum, and the most cutting-edge technology.
Our schools teach respect for the diversity of Jewish practice. The Galinsky Academy is a place for Jews,
regardless of background or practice, to come together around a shared heritage.
One of my goals is to integrate our marketing, development and recruitment functions so that we create an
educational institution that will have an even larger “presence” outside of the building. I want us to attract families,
as well as donors, thought partners and “investors.” Our schools must be seen by as many people as possible as
making a contribution to the larger community, improving the world through our graduates.
One of the most exciting projects the Galinsky Academy will undertake is to further align all schools, programs
and goals under one common vision, creating synergies that strengthen the impact on all of our children, preschool
through high school. Not many congregations have the level of staffing, programs and resources necessary to
accomplish this. The Center does! Staff, parents, volunteers will all be a part of this important project. Stay tuned
for more!
I am grateful for the wonderful, creative institution that Dr. Jon Mitzmacher has handed off to me. 1 am
pleased he will continue to be close, as a school parent, colleague and neighbor.
My wife Marci (who will be teaching in the MJGDS middle school) and I look forward to getting to know
you, and wish you all a Shana Tova U’metukah.

SETZER
YOUTH EDUCATION
When the Jacksonville Jewish Center created the Galinsky Academy in
2012, one of the key components of their vision was offering world-class
programming to Jewish children of all ages, from diapers to diplomas. That
vision will soon become a reality thanks to a just announced partnership with
the Setzer family.
The Setzer family have generously underwritten what will now be called
Setzer Youth Education. The newly-formed organization will encompass
USY (United Synagogue Youth), Kadima, and Chalutzim youth groups. Also
included will be Hebrew High School, Siyyum (a twelfth-grade, advanced
study program leading to confirmation at high-school graduation), Shabbat
youth programming, youth camping, as well as Boy Scouts and Girl Scouts.

Galinsky Academy, joining the DuBow Preschool, Martin J. Gottlieb Day School, and Bernard and Alice
Selevan Religious School. Don Kriss, Jacksonville Jewish Center’s Executive Director, is more than a little
excited: “The Setzer family’s very generous gift will provide an endowed fund to ensure the success of our
youth programming for many years to come. What could be better than that?”
“We look forward to continuing and creating great opportunities for children of every age,” says Fred
Pozin, President of the Jacksonville Jewish Center. “With great thanks to the Setzer family for their generosity
and vision, and with great pride in the Jacksonville Jewish Center and Galinsky Academy, may we go ‘mei
chayil el chayil,’ from strength to strength in the days and years ahead!”

Camp Ki Tov, Summer 2014, was full of fun!! We had a lot of
returning faces on the staff eager to see their campers. On the first
day of camp, the children arrived excited and ready for a memorable
summer. This summer, campers enjoyed some old favorites such as
fishing, archery, sports, and more. Some weeks we welcomed visitors
like a petting zoo, a magical clown, and bounce houses. Other weeks we
ventured out to go to Mandarin Mills, Bowl America, to the Jacksonville Beach, and to Fire Station
51. Our CIT’s had lots of fun leading arts and crafts along with the cooking program.
Each day, with varying themes such as Color Day, Crazy Day, and more, each cabin competed
with spirit and pride to win the beloved ‘spirit stick.’ We were also very fortunate to have both
Rabbi Olitzky and Hazzan Holzer lead our Week 1 and 2 Shabbat lunches on Fridays with lots of
ruach (spirit) and passion! We enjoyed being menches and doing mitzvot on Fridays to get in the
spirit of Shabbat. In July, we were excited to welcome Rabbi Tilman to our Camp Ki Tov family!
Our JJC clergy also joined each cabin for Judaic-related activities. We had the privilege of hosting
counselors from Israel at Camp Ki Tov and were excited about the love of Israel that they brought
to our camp! As usual, we also had a great time planning our annual Camp Ki Tov undernight/
overnight. There was special nighttime swimming, flashlight games, and kosher s’mores!
As always, it is our goal to build long lasting memories for the children at Camp Ki Tov and
we were more than successful! Thanks to the combined efforts of the administration, our clergy,
and to the loving parents. A big shout out to our AMAZING staff of young men and women who
dedicated their summer to making memories for our campers at Camp Ki Tov.
Have a wonderful, healthy, and happy year. We look forward to creating more friendships
during summer 2015 at Camp Ki Tov!!!

WHAT CAUSES MOST DISEASES?
Almost daily we read about this or that item causing physical or mental diseases. A short time later, a more
recent study is published, indicating that something entirely different is the cause. No one attempts to tie the cause
of one disease to that of another.
It is high time that we wise up to the real cause of most diseases-- taking harmful substances into the body.
The most common of these substances are coffee, tea, soft drinks, chocolate, and food preservatives, known as
sodium or potassium benzoate; all contain chemical compounds known as aromatic or benzene rings. Other toxic
substances include alcohol, such as beer, wine and liquor; tobacco; fluorides, found in most toothpastes and
mouthwashes; pesticides; drugs, in both prescription and non-prescription medicine, marijuana and other “street”
varieties; food dyes, sugar and artificial sweeteners, X-rays, aluminum antiperspirants, mercury and other metals.

system, causing mental illnesses, (ADHD, depression, suicide and other violent crimes, bipolar disease, alcoholism
and schizophrenia); and physical illnesses, such as cardiovascular ailments (heart attack, arrhythmias, aneurysms,
stroke, sickle cell crisis, high blood pressure, elevated cholesterol and triglycerides,); diabetes, hyperglycemia and
hypoglycemia; most types of cancer (brain, breast, prostate, uterine, ovarian, rectal, lung, leukemia and lymphoma,
and they all start with a malnourished pancreas!); nervous system problems (ALS, Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s, multiple
sclerosis and muscular dystrophy); eye and ear diseases; arthritis, (lupus, gout, psoriasis); osteoporosis, obesity,
birth defects and many acute diseases.
How can we avoid or postpone getting these illnesses? By abstaining from all the items in the previous two
paragraphs. What should we eat and drink? Fruits, vegetables, whole grains, freshly prepared soups, nuts, seeds,
beans, and limited amounts of proteins (fish, poultry, eggs) and fats (olive oil or butter). One should drink limited
amounts of mostly reverse osmosis or other purified water. Vitamins, minerals and other supplements are also
important to keep the body supplied with necessary substances to avoid diseases and overcome them. Both stretch
and aerobic exercises (swimming or walking) should be done almost daily to avoid illness.
