IRVING KLEPPER
The image of a "Home for the Aged" has changed radically in the past decade. The old concept of the "rocking chair" existence has
disappeared and has been replaced by dynamic and vital group living.
The Home has become a miniature society and all the vitality of communal living is manifested by a broad program of enrichment
services. In addition to the basic necessities of day-to-day living, the Home has developed a gamut of activities to encourage participa¬
tion of all residents on an individual choice basis.
The past year has seen the inauguration of new and interesting projects which have been most successful. A Sheltered Work Shop
was organized and sub-contracts were secured for simple assembly jobs from local manufacturers. The participating resident is paid for
work performed. This is limited to his strength and capacity as well as a concern for the conservation of his energies. The dignity of
work as well as earning pocket money is a great morale builder.
Through the cooperation of the Home’s auxiliary, the Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society, a weekly Senior Citizens Day is held at
the Home to which the older people in the community are invited. Thus they share with our residents an interesting afternoon of recre¬
ational, educational and social activities, as well as a coffee hour. This program has become meaningful to the older person in the com¬
munity because he is able to find stimulating experiences geared to his age and capacity. For the Home residents, it gives the opportunity
of sharing experiences with his contemporaries and thus broadens his community contacts.
These programs, plus the many other activities, such as provided by the Golden Age Club, Residents’ Community Council, a peri¬
odical publication and outings, have created a dynamic atmosphere in the Home where an older person can continue his life to the maxi¬
mum of his capacity and dignity.
