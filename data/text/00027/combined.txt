Message from the Rabbi
• • • •
The theme of this book is the twentieth anniversary of
the recreation of the State of Israel. What are twenty years
in the milennial span of human history? A drop in the bucket.
Yet twenty years is a generation, a cross section of a period,
an impressive chunk of an epoch. Our people is roughly 4,000
years old, and in the course of that sweeping stretch of time,
we have experienced Exile and Return, time and time again.
Now we have returned — for good, we hope. We have gone
forth from the land of Israel, but we have always come back.
Israel draws us, and we pull her with cords of love.
It has almost become commonplace. The commonplace
must be broadened: Each Jew who returns to Israel comes
not as tourist or visitor. The place is not strange to him.
The names of its cities and villages have been vivid house¬
hold words for centuries. Each of us has been there before.
It’s homecoming.
Whoever believed that the Jewish State would spring to
life after one of the most bloody and savage periods of his¬
tory? Out of the death of the holocaust emerged new zest and
life. In this phenomenon we discern our immortality, our
passion to endure, to build and create anew.
During this past year the two Jerusalems have
been reunited and the last vestige of the Temple, the
Western Wall, has become ours again. It belongs to
us, and to mankind. The Wall is symbol of the endur¬
ance and indestructability of our people, and of man,
and of our resolve to build around the Wall new cities,
modern and imaginative, where men might study, and
work, and live together, joining old dreams to new
accomplishments.
Witness the scene after the union of Jerusalem
and the taking of the Wall — a group of army officers
approaching the Wall. They do not fall to their knees
or kiss its stones. No prayer crosses their lips. They
stand at attention and salute the Wall, then turn and
leave.
Witness countless others who approach the Wall
wishing to be overwhelmed and carried away, to arti¬
culate prayer that wells up from the soul, to plunge
themselves into the totality of Jewish history that em¬
braces every person, every Jew who ever comes to this
place, or ever thought about coming.
Witness a man and woman who move near to shed
a crystal tear in which is reflected the whole world of
the sorrow and the search of the Jew.
Witness the Reform Jews who recently solicited
permission to hold a service at the Wall involving both
men and women. Permission denied. Their desire was
more than a gesture. It represented a desire for the
union with all of Israel, before the mystery of the
Wall. Students and disciples of all faiths now come
freely to the Temple site to be challenged by Isaiah,
that war may one day end and nations unite.
This book, issued in the twentieth year of Israel's
restoration, enters into the record the raw material out
of which Jewish history is constructed. For future his-
RABBI JEROME LIPNICK
torians we must leave the task to assemble out of the
names, and the pictures, and the partisan tales of suc¬
cess and achievement — and between the lines the
failures — the trend and direction of this community.
What are the forces at work here? What is our leader¬
ship? Where is it taking us? Where do we aspire to go?
The question: Are we moving closer to our soil,
and to our roots, or are we drawing farther away like
so many other Jewish groups of the past have done,
believing ourselves not only adequate but even super¬
ior, believing that we have nothing to learn or profit
from the reality of Israel or the Jewish people?
This, I believe, is the rule of our existence: Israel
and its achievements in the realm of the spirit in days
gone by, and its tantalizing potential for the future is
the bond that enables our congregation and this com¬
munity to retain its vitality, its dynamism, and its
purpose.
The new State of Israel compels us to reformu¬
late our conceptions of value and holiness wherever we
are. The language of holiness and faith again requires
restatement and reconstruction. We are never sure
where the secular ends, and the spiritual begins. Like
body, mind, and spirit, they cannot be separated in
America ... in Israel . . . Everywhere.
Thus we present another book for another year of
Jewish experience. No year was ever like this. The
Wall is ours, and we are its breathing stones, as we
plan anew the cities of God and man.
Rabbi Jerome Lipnick

Message from the President
When I became your President last January, I must
admit that I accepted this position with a great deal of
awe and trepidation. To follow in the footsteps of the long
line of distinguished Presidents, who have guided the des¬
tinies of our congregation for the past 67 years, filled me
with both pride and humility.
Perhaps, more than any other factor, it was a pas¬
sage from the Talmud that gave me the courage to accept
my nomination. That passage reads: “Lo alecho hama-
locha lig’mor, v’lo atoh ben horin Vhebotael mimenoh.”
Translated, it reads, “It is not your duty to accomplish
the complete task — but — you are not free to desist from
doing all you can.”
Regarding the first part of this Mishnah, I can say
that I was promised; I expected; and I have received a
great deal of help and encouragement from our officers,
our Board members, our professional staff and from many
rank and file members of our congregation. For all your
cooperation and assistance, I am truly deeply grateful.
Without your help, I could not succeed; with your con¬
tinuing help, we cannot fail.
As for the second part of the Mishnah, I pledge to
do my best to vindicate the confidence and trust you have
placed in me. Moreover, I urge each and every member
of this congregation to take this teaching to heart, to make
it part of your way of life. Judaism requires only that you
do the best you can. Your capability would not permit you
to do more. Your conscience should not permit you to do
less.
NATHAN FREIDLIN, President
For a general overall view of our accomplishments
during the past year, I invite you to browse through the
pages of this Year Book, which in itself is a most im¬
portant and necessary accomplishment for which we must
thank and commend Hymie Selber and his staff, espec¬
ially Esther Fishier and Florence Robin.
PHILIP SELBER
Chairman of the Board
Among our successful accomplishments this past year I would list the
refurbishing of our Daily Prayer Chapel; the enjoyable and profitable Purim
Fair; the fund raising activities of our Foundation School which allowed
them to successfully balance their budget, a Yasher Co-ach to Herb Plotkin
and his committee for a job well done. Our congratulations and thanks to
Blanche Slott and her staff and to the members of our Day Camp Com¬
mittee for the wonderful Day Camp program which they conducted this past
summer.
As this Year Book goes to press, negotiations are well under way with
one of the world's best known Synagogue Architects to produce plans for
our new Synagogue-Center which we plan to erect at our Campsite.
Like our ancestors in the wilderness, when they erected the first sanc¬
tuary, we too, each of us, must get behind this effort with all our hearts,
and with all our might. This will be our major contribution to our children
and our children's children for many decades to come.
On behalf of the Officers and members of your Board of Directors and
Board of Trustees, I extend to every member of our congregation our wishes
and prayers for a good year, a year of health, contentment and prosperity,
and above all a year of Shalom.
USHANAH TOVAH TIKOTEVU

THIS IS MY SYNAGOGUE
This is MY SYNAGOGUE. It is composed of people like me. We make
it what it is.
It will be friendly, if I am. Its pews will be filled, if I help to fill them.
It will do a great work, if I work. It will bring other people into its worship
and fellowship, if I bring them.
It will be a Synagogue of loyalty and love, of fearlessness and faith, and
a Synagogue with a noble spirit — if I, who make it what it is, am filled
with these.
Therefore, with the help of Sod, I shall dedicate myself t othe task of
being all these things which I want my Synagogue to be.
Compliments of
LOUIS PORTNOY - WALTER PORTNOY

Dr. Harry Yergin
Abraham E. Selber
The death of Dr. Harry Yergin during the past year
has removed from the leadership ranks of the Jackson¬
ville Jewish Center one of its most active and loyal work¬
ers on behalf of the congregation over the past fifteen
years.
Dr. Yergin’s contribution in the promotion and de¬
velopment of the congregation’s youth program represents
perhaps one of the outstanding individual contributions in
the history of the synagogue. His earliest interest was in
the Cub Scout movement in which he served both as Cub-
master and as Chairman of the Parents’ Committee. His
zeal and hard work, both of which were expended in far
above normal proportions, enabled the pack to achieve
its greatest degree of success during his years of leader¬
ship, being unquestionably one of the two or three best
units in the area.
Following the advancement of his own sons into the
ranks of Boy Scout activities, Dr. Yergin became Scout¬
master, and once again his leadership produced what will
probably be looked back upon as the golden period of
scouting at the Center, when the history of the scout¬
ing movement is written. His personal interest in the in¬
dividual achievements of each of the Scouts is something
which those who were privileged to come under his guid¬
ance will never forget or cease to appreciate. His willing¬
ness to give of his own time at great personal sacrifice
enabled the troop to maintain one of the finest outdoor
camping programs of any troop in the city. He was able
to personally influence the individual scouts to the
achievement of high rank, and more scouts attained the
rank of Eagle Scout during his tenure of leadership than
in any other period in the history of the troop.
When his scouting activities came to a close, the
interest of Dr. Yergin in the youth of the synagogue
did not end. He became chairman of the youth committee
and as such became involved in the work of the United
Synagogue Youth and ATID Programs of the synagogue.
At the same time Dr. Yergin became extremely active
in the work of the Day Camp committee and was serving
as its chairman at the time of his death.
Leaders such as Dr. Yergin are not easily replaced—
if ever. The example of his leadership and devotion to
the youth program of his synagogue will long be an in¬
spiration to the members of the congregation whose chil¬
dren he served so admirably.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center takes the occasion
of the publication of this Yearbook to mark indelibly in
its records its appreciation and indebtedness to Dr. Yergin
for the permanent influence he has left behind.
During the past year the congregation lost the serv¬
ices of one of its most dedicated workers and supporters
upon the death of Abraham E. Selber, a member of the
Board of Trustees.
Mr. Selber’s long period of service, both as a member
of the Board of Trustees and the Board of Directors,
extends over a period of more than twenty-five years.
He was active in the financial and religious phases of the
synagogue program but was particularly interested in its
program in support of the Jewish Theological Seminary.
As an indication of the deep concern which he felt
for the future education and training of professional reli¬
gious personnel, Mr. Selber provided in his Will for the
establishment of a special trust fund to assist in the
strengthening of those programs which are foremost in
this area. The purpose of the trust fund is to provide
scholarships to assist students of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center Religious Schools in securing additional training,
education and preparation for roles as rabbis, cantors,
teachers, educational directors, and youth workers in the
Jewish religious field.
The scholarships are to be used for the purpose of
assisting students in summer religious camps such as
Camp Ramah, all day religious schools of high school
level, seminaries, institutions, colleges or universities in¬
cluding both those which are designed primarily for direct
training and education for those occupations or profes¬
sions and those whose facilities can be useful in the pre¬
paration of the student for later specific religious educa¬
tion and training.
The creation of the Abe Selber Trust marks the
second such scholarship endeavor connected with the
synagogue. Several years ago, the family of the late
Robert Gordon established the Robert Gordon Scholar¬
ship for Camp Ramah, as a result of which quite a num¬
ber of local students have been enabled to secure the rich
summer experience which has influenced them in con¬
tinuing their Jewish interests at the college level.
The creation of the Abe Selber Trust Fund will serve
as a fitting and lasting memorial to this synagogue leader
who has provided for the continuation beyond his own life
time of those programs which were so dear to him.
The congregation takes this occasion to record not
only the many fine achievements of Abe Selber during
his life but also this noble act in the creation of this
mechanism for the continuous development of professional
religious leadership for the coming generations.
The Center Board of Directors has decided to accept
additional contributions to this fund. Those who are in¬
terested may make their contributions to the Center as
trustee.

In Memory of
MAX RUBIN
We Dedicate this Year Book to the Late Max Rubin
Max Rubin, the mortal, has passed on, but the
name of Max Rubin will forever be a part of the
immortal history of the Jacksonville Jewish Center,
and of many other fine causes. Max Rubin wrote
an indelible page that must indeed be an example
for dedicated people to follow.
As a person, he was a good husband, a fine
father and grandfather, and ever loyal to all his
family. Time and time again he came to the assist¬
ance of the members of his family.
His many continuing services were rendered
graciously. He made a covenant for himself. He
was a leader, a worker, and an inspiration. He was
capable, skillful and efficient. The record speaks
out loudly and soundly for a half century of im¬
portant contribution to the Center and the com¬
munity.
Let us review this record. He served as Presi¬
dent, Board member of the Jewish Center and as
Chairman of the Cemetery Committee. His progres¬
sive outlook helped sustain B’nai Israel, and when
the Hebrew Junior League proposed the Jewish
Center, no one man was more sympathetic to its
proposals, and no man labored more diligently to¬
wards its fruition. Mortgages never scared him be¬
cause he had faith in its membership.
Even though the frowns of misfortune fell upon
him, he was not willing to abandon, quit, or sur¬
render, like so many after the burst of the land
boom. He was a man of consummate skill, astute
and discerning, and with faith and assurance he
guided others so that the new Jewish Center was
built and completed. When the remodeling of the
old building and the Youth Activities Building were
proposed, no man put forth more efforts towards
these projects.
Lest we forget, Max Rubin was a charter
member of the Jewish Community Council and sev-
of Blessed Memory.
eral times was honored for his magnificent works.
His love for Israel, the Z. O. A., and the Jewish
National Fund transcended ordinary service and
he was recognized nationally. As a leader, a work¬
er, an organizer, he allowed others to receive the
honors while he himself did most of the work. He
was awarded a 35-year and then a 50-year pin for
membership in B’nai B’rith.
He was an extraordinary person. He didn’t
hate, dislike or despise anyone. He was a friend^
and sociable person, without pretentious airs. He
was a kindly man, agreeable and likeable. Every¬
thing he did was with faith and assurance, with
the ability to transact and administer. He was a
man of strong opinions. Often his views became
those of the majority. When he was not in agree¬
ment, he would declare himself, and graciously ac¬
cept the majority view.
His greatest achievement was his chairmanship
of the Cemetery Committee, and indeed it seemed
that each blade of grass meant something to him.
When the Center lost our original Cemetery ground
due to financial failure, it was Max Rubin, and
Max Rubin alone, who effected its return to the
Center by the Evergreen Cemetery Association.
When the new Cemetery was purchased, Max
Rubin again made a covenant with himself to see
to it that it be made to flourish. Without funds,
he arranged loans, signed contracts, spent days and
days towards its improvement. This alone can be
called a monument to his valiant efforts.
There are two ambitions. The bad seeks power
and reward. The good ambition is the kind that
drives a man to do something valuable or dis¬
tinguished, one that wishes to contribute some¬
thing valuable in his lifetime. The latter was the
credo of Max Rubin.

producing the plaque.

taking a course in First Aid under the instruction of
Mr. Ray Smith, a member of the First Aid Squad of
the Jacksonville Fire Dept. Several of the boys quali¬
fied for the First Aid Merit Badge as a result of this
class.

When I accepted the chairmanship of the study committee for the long range
planning of the new Jacksonville Jewish Center, I really had no idea of the scope and
complexity of this undertaking.
I thought it would be a comparatively simple task to find an architect, lay it all
in his lap, and let him accept the responsibility. But, it was not to be anything like that.
I first communicated with the Department of Synagogue Administration of the
United Synagogue of America, requesting a list of architects who are engaged in syna¬
gogue architecture. This list consisted of 22 names. I communicated with all of these
people and received replies from all. Some were very interested in our project, while
others were not.
I also communicated with the Jewish Museum in New York, where an exhibition
had been arranged of contemporary synagogue architecture, for the purpose of bring¬
ing to public attention some of the problems raised by the need for reconciling the
traditional values accumulated during the history of synagogue building with modern
social, economic and architectural needs.
Now my list of names expanded to 36, and I was becoming increasingly aware of
the myriad problems, the many facets and ramifications that confronts an undertaking
of this kind.
On my trips to New York I met several architects and
began to take side trips to see, in person, their works. And, in
the nearly two years since this project began, I have visited
several hundred synagogues, all over the United States — and
all over the world.
Many are now hazy, or forgotten in my mind, but others
are vivid and exciting. Some architects were eliminated from
consideration, not because they were not great, but because
they were really beyond our financial means—Grapuis, Phillip
Johnson, Kahn, and Yamasaki.
Thus, the list narrowed down to three architects for con¬
sideration. I made it a point to meet these men personally,
and had much communication with them. I then arranged to
have them each come to Jacksonville and meet with our Board
and Committee and to make a presentation, after seeing our
present facilities and our new site. I felt it imperative that we
get to know these men — to find one we could work with and
“live” with during the time it would take to complete this
“dream.”
A fourth name was added to this list of three, Norman
Freedman, a brilliant young architect and a member of our
Congregation. He, too, made an excellent presentation.
All four architects were questioned on all matters concern¬
ing every phase of this undertaking: Their feeling, thoughts
and impressions about: The site, the Sanctuary, the school,
social facilities, offices, toilets, lighting, heating, furnishings,
storage, landscaping, memorials, and the many different “ways”
of building.

decision, and the entire Board was invited. After summing up
the vast accumulation of materials and impressions, it was the
opinion of this Committee that we select Mr. Percival Good¬
man, and Mr. Norman Freedman as co-architects, and to see
if a joint venture could be arranged. It was felt that the com-

They could build a Sanctuary that would allow us to use
the lovely natural surroundings of our Site — the natural light
so that our Congregation could sense that night is falling —
or the sun climbing toward noon! It would be sensitive to its
environment — so it could allow our members to wander into
its course to sit and read—or meditate—or pray.
They could build a Chapel that is airy, and inspiring, to
draw the morning Minyan.
I approached Mr. Goodman, who had already met Nor¬
man Freedman and after several phone calls and a meeting in
New York, he agreed to the co-venture on May 7th.
Mr. Freedman was also enthusiastic about combining his
talents with those of Mr. Goodman.
Most of our members are familiar with the growing list
of credits that Norman Freedman is acquiring, and as a Cen¬
ter member and a Board member with a great dedication to
the Center, his on-the-spot-supervision will be a fabulous ad¬
vantage.
Mr. Goodman is unquestionably the outstanding synagogue
architect in the United States. He is the Professor of Design
in the Graduate School of Architecture at Columbia University
since 1946. He is a member of Mayor Lindsey’s Panel of Arch¬
itects in New York, and Who’s Who in America. He is a noted
Book Illustrator, Painter and Sculptor. He has designed over
60 Synagogues, and at present has three in progress in Syra¬
cuse, New York, Grand Rapids, Michigan, and Kings Point,
The Jews have historically built synagogues in the style
of the time in which they lived. There is no living tradition of
construction and style in the architecture of synagogues, yet,
too often, many people like the things they are already ac¬
customed to, no matter how poorly it is built, and how lack¬
ing in any aesthetic virtue.
I feel our selection of these two outstanding architects will
give us a Jewish Center that will be esthetically satisfying, but
more important, will be aware of tradition of our ‘congrega¬
tion,’ the tradition of our ‘Service’—the tradition of the func¬
tions for which we use our Center.
To quote Percival Goodman, “Tradition cannot be im¬
ported where it does not exist.”

In its 51st year, the Jewish Family and Children’s
Services, Inc., the casework arm of the Jewish Com¬
munity, served 327 different families and individuals
in one or more of its programs — some in short con¬
tact, others for services going on over years.
The main area, personal counseling done on an
individual and more recently, also on a family group
basis, involved persons of all ages, educational and
economic backgrounds and all degrees of religiosity
and covered topics in parent-child problems, marital
difficulties, mental and physical illness and finances.
About 40% of the clientele were senior citizens, some
of whom were also helped by financial and referral
services, a Friendly Visiting program or admission to
River Garden.
A foster care program for short term care and an
adoption program including services for unmarried
mothers, were also available. Day Camp scholarships
were awarded with the wonderful cooperation of the
Center Day Camp and Camp Leitman, and interest
free college loans through the cooperation of the
Jewish Children’s Services of Atlanta.
Short term financial assistance was available for
local families and one or two day temporary care was
available for transients. Besides this, a limited counsel¬
ing and religious services program was continued at
Raiford and begun at the State Hospital at Mac-
clenny.

to the United Fund and the Jewish Welfare Fund.

In the Twentieth Century, the Zionist Movement
created the tools for continuing Jewish survival, and
achieved the return of the Jewish People to the
Promised Land as a sovereign nation. Zionism stirred
the great Jewish community of America into an aware¬
ness of its past and potentials, and rallied it to action
for the preservation of its people.
Since the establishment of the State of Israel,
The Zionist Organization of America has been the
most steadfast supporter of the young democracy and
has simultaneously committed itself and its total re¬
sources to the creative survival of the Jewish people
in the United States of America and throughout the
world.
The goals of the Zionist Oragnization of America
are:
To intensify American Jewry’s commitment to the Zionist
ideal of creative Jewish continuity;
To be cognizant of the past, concerned with the future
and convinced of the oneness of the Jewish people and its
common destiny;
To actively participate in American life on every level;
To forge living fraternal bonds with the people of Israel
and Jewish communities throughout the world;
To assist each individual Jew discover for himself why
óeing Jewish is profoundly a part of his being;
To probe the deeper meanings and significance of Jewish
existence;
To provide the tools for studying the past, living as affirm¬
ative Jews in the present, and building firm bridges to the
future;
To be a strong, broad bridge linking the two great Jewish
communities of Israel and the United States of America;
To forge firm ties of mutual concern, respect and under¬
standing;
To work side by side as one people in re-creating a vig¬
orous, vital Jewish civilization which may fulfill prophecy and
become “a light unto the nations ”
The Zionist Organization of America welcomes all
young men to join their ranks to help achieve these
goals.

talents.
