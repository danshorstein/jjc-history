“Hayom Harat Oí am -
Today Is The Birthday Of The World”
“Hayom Harat Olam ” is a prayer we say following the blowing of the Shofar on Rosh Hashana. It
confirms the Jewish tradition that the New Year is celebrated as the birthday of the world and as the beginning
of human existence. More important, because it is the Jewish celebration of Rosh Hashana and not the rather
shallow observance of the secular New Year, it should give us pause for reflection.
With the passage of time, the New Year, like all birthdays, becomes a subtle blend of happiness and
sobriety. We enjoy the New Year cards of well wishers but we realize that we are a year older and hence closer
to the end of life. We become acutely aware of the passage of time.
It is of course inevitable that we should look back upon the past - not only last year, but the whole of
life, and recognize that while we have achieved much, there is a great deal which remains unfulfilled in our
life. We hope and plan and dream but much of it remains unrealized. The overriding themes of the Rosh
Hashana and Yom Kippur season are intended to be helpful. A sense of “Al het” - “for the sins we have
sinned”, the sense of remorse, for the mistakes that we have made, the seeking of atonement, the promise of
“Teshuvah” -the hope for change is fundamental to our psychological and emotional well being. No year
passes without some defeat, without some sense of frustration and without some sense of “what might have
been”. The New Year reminds us of the opportunities of renewal and beginning again.
There is no more imperious tyrant than a calendar. It is the outward reminder that we are all creatures of
time. A clock is the closest ally of the calendar. Unlike sports and athletic events we cannot “stop the clock”
of life. Worse, there is never any “replay”. The thought of our powerlessness might well paralyze us into
inactivity were it not for the holiday themes of the Mahzor, our holiday prayerbook that forces us to look
elsewhere. The notion of “Teshuvah” - that we can always return to God’s presence, to sensing His nearness
by realizing the best within ourselves is comforting. There are other ways of celebrating New Year birthdays.
Perhaps it is in our awareness that life is measured not only in days, but in deeds...that life is measured in
growth. What is alive - grows. What grows is alive. Time will take care of our getting older. We must look
after our getting wiser. We are summoned on Rosh Hashana to resolve that the New Year will make us more
understanding, more sympathetic, more deeply touched by the affliction of others. We need also be more
appreciative of the beauty around us and the gift of life itself. People who use the Siddur sensitively - people
who truly worship are aware of this.
Unless we are prepared to accept the changes which time brings, to be reminded of the calendar, we
cannot life sensible lives. We tear off the page of the calendar after a week or month has passed. Yesterday is
gone, today is fleeting, tomorrow is always on the way. Every moment offers us an opportunity for learning,
for expanding our knowledge. The challenge of the Torah way is described in the words “and you shall
meditate on it day and night.” Every moment should be an opportunity for making the life of some individual
happier and more fulfilling. Time is suffused with the sense of eternity. The Shabbat is such a time. So is Kol
Nidre. Only those who can experience these hours when we forget time can be said to be truly alive.
Living...true living, is the ability to see the enduring in the swiftly, vanishing moment of time.
When we rise to say “Hayom harat olam” on Rosh Hashana let us remember the true challenge of the
year to come.. .the true challenge of life.

Message from Rabbi Gaffney
Renewing the Irreplaceable
I am prompted to the consid-
erations that follow after reading an
article in the business section in one
of the important national daily
newspapers. I usually do nothing
more than skim the headlines of this
section of the paper.. .but one item of
business interest, and I think of
human interest, evoked my curiosity.
I read further. The article explained that
one publicly owned company was
resisting the fluctuations sometimes
found in bio-engineering stocks and
was doing quite well. The company in
question was into medical technology
and the research and development of
replacement parts for the human body.
I had not realized the extent to which
the bionic man and woman had become
a fact of life. Companies that replace
parts for a price, are a growth industry.
Indeed replacing human parts with
artificial ones has spurred the
development of a community of
manufacturers that endeavor to replace
what was formerly irreplaceable. There
are reports of millions of people who
have had artificial parts implanted
inside them. There are artificial hips,
knees, elbows, arteries, breasts, lenses,
and pace makers. Even the artificial
human heart has become a “fact of life”
challenge to these new creators. As
medical researchers contemplate the
future, they are confident that
everything in the body will ultimately
be duplicated by parts flowing out of a
factory.
I read the article with a mixture
of fascination and disquietude. Please
understand - I’m all for research in this
area. I believe it to be a part of a God
given command to mankind to realize
and develop itself to its fullest creative
potential. It is a mitzvah to prolong life.
It is a mitzvah to give people second
opportunities...to bring hope and
healing to those in need of help.
The psalmist explains about
God, “For You have sown this flesh;
You knitted these bones in my
mother’s womb; I offer my thanks for
I am astonishingly and wonderfully
made.” I do not know which blessings
Rabbi David and Inge Gaffney
are recited when one is provided with
an artificial limb or organ ... or
frankly, to whom thanks is due. Is it
the scientist? the technician? the
medical researcher? or the insurance
company that makes it all possible?
But it is not God’s honor that I am
concerned with. God wants and needs
partners to further His work in every
human and social sphere.. .no less this
area of modern medical development.
What fills me with disquietude
is that if we can replace the formerly
irreplaceable...the limbs and organs
that sustain life... what shall we do for
the human spirit that ails as well.
People have, not only a body, but a
spirit. There is in each human being
a Divine flame which thinks, creates,
dreams, hopes, cries . . . and prays.
There is in every person a need for
love ... for companionship, for a
sense of dignity and worth. There is
in each of us a need to make a
difference. There is in everyone a
talent to sing, to soar and to celebrate
the wonder of life.
Is there a company which can
provide replacements of the spirit when
it is deficient? Can a factory provide
an implant to those who need to be able
to love? to those who hunger for beauty
and long for truth? Can we ask the
researchers to look into the widespread
conditions of the shriveled souls and
withered spirits of modem man and to
provide their replacement parts? Is the
price too high? The technology too
complex? I suspect that any agency
providing replacement of the human
spirit would flourish . . . would add
significantly to its own stock market
average, if given the chance.
It dawned on me, as I read this
article on the replacement of the
irreplaceable, that the Jewish
community and tradition has had such
an agency ... one which had flourished
over these last many centuries. Rosh
Hashana and Yom Kippur may serve
as representatives of each day of human
life. The setting for this enterprise is
the synagogue. The human spirit cannot
be replaced - it need only be renewed.
We Jews call this Teshuva - the ability
to return to our fullest human
dimensions in the face of all that causes
the spirit to shrivel. There is an element
- a winged thing within us - an
invisible, but real entity which links our
life to the Eternal Spirit... the source
of our Being. I call it God.
Our tradition states... ’’Elohai -
neshama asher natata bee - tehora
hee ” - “Our God, the spirit and soul
you have placed within me is pure and
good.” (the Siddur) Is this the real
meaning of the Genesis description
of God providing us with the breath
of life? I think so. I venture to suggest
that though we may be able to replace
those physical organs with which we
were created, we will never be able
to do without that spirit which comes
from God and which is our most
fundamental of human endowments.
It is that human endowment which
cannot be replaced. It can only be
renewed.
I invite you to this renewal . . .
not only during the course of the High
Holy days which are upon us...but
rather over the course of the entire
year. This is a life long process . . .
but one we need to seek if we are to
realize that we are heirs to a noble
tradition and that we are members of
the human family.
Inge and I, and the members of
our family, wish you and yours a year
of health, happiness, purpose,
blessing, peace and renewal. Amen
Rabbi David Gaffney

Rabbi Kentof’s Message
What is Jewish Education?
Jewish Education in its true
sense is different from learning a
skill. Education is not a product
or an acquisition. Education is not
merely teaching or learning. No
one can sell you, or give you, or
deliver to you an education, as one
can a book or a picture. You can
acquire the ability to type, to fix a
leak, or to ride a bicycle, but you
cannot acquire an education.
Someone can teach you, and you
can learn any number of abilities
or attitudes or appreciations, and
you may still not have an
education.
What, then, is education? It
is a continuous, steady progression
in which the acquiring of one
ability, attitude, or appreciation
leads directly to another, and that
in turn to still another, and so on.
The test of an education lies not in
the particular content you acquire,
but in the need or desire which you
feel to add to that content.
Long ago our Rabbis said,
“Udlo mosifyoseif' - “he who does
not add to his learning decreases
it.” Years later, John Dewey,
America’s greatest philosopher,
expressed the same thought when
he said, “The most important
attitude that can be formed (in
education) is the desire to go on
learning.”
Rabbi Dov Kentof
Director of Education & Youth
Like any education, Jewish
education involves certain skills
that may be mastered, certain
information that may be acquired,
certain attitudes that may be
developed. But, also like any
education, Jewish education in the
sense of the term, must be
conceived as an ongoing process
of inspiring students, young and
old, with both the desire and the
ability to keep on learning. Viewed
from this angle, the quality of
learning is more important than the
quantity. In other words, more
important than “how much has
been learned?” is the question
“How much learning has taken
place?”
Quality in education is
decided by the learner’s desire to
learn more. And an appetite for
learning cannot be acquired on a
diet of teaspoonful doses. A certain
amount of quantity is necessary...
Therefore, Jewish education
cannot be ended at any stage in
life. There is no number of years
of Jewish study that we can call
enough. There is no list of
accomplishments that separates the
educated from the uneducated Jew.
There is no package of facts, no
collection of textbooks, no battery
of tests, the mastery or the
possession of which justifies the
boast of possession of a Jewish
education.
The good Jewish teacher
will so teach that his pupils want
to go on and on in their Jewish
studies. The good Jewish school
encourages and eases this desire,
and the good Jewish parent backs
up the teacher and the school to his
utmost in word and deed.
We have carefully planned
our curriculum and programs for
Supplemental School and our
Youth Clubs for the upcoming year
1996-97. We have engaged expert,
motivated staff and advisors to
present the material to your
children. Our Schools and Clubs
are designed to allow our students
to continue to grow in a positive
Jewish atmosphere. One that
provides them with a feeling of
pride in their Judaism.
Alina and I wish you and yours
a Happy New Year - one that brings
you the fulfillment of all your
dreams,good health, happiness, and
enjoyment of life through watching
your children grow from strength to
strength.

Executive Director’s Message
Xhe Jacksonville Jewish Center
is a remarkable place.
Where else could a group of
New Americans, who only a few years
ago were victims of unthinkable
religious persecution, celebrate their
B’nai Mitzvah, by reading Torah and
leading Shabbat Services?
What other congregation with
our demographics could fill an airport
with as many kids going to Camp
Ramah, USY on Wheels and various
other summer programs to Israel and
Poland?
Where else can you find a synagogue with such
a beautiful natural setting that includes our own
private campgrounds and pool facility?
What other synagogue can equal our long
standing commitment to Jewish education?
What other congregation can boast of the
extraordinary lay and professional leadership present
at the Jacksonville Jewish Center?
It is for these reasons that it is such a privilege
for me to have the opportunity to help direct the future
of a congregation that has meant so much to me and
my family over the years.
We have a great congregation, but so much
remains to be done.
Our tradition teaches us that
God deliberately left the world
unfinished because He expects each
generation - every man, woman and
child - to be His partner in the
continuous act of creation. So it is
with the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Our future depends on each of
us to use our individual talents and
resources to make the Center an even
better place.
What can we do? Support our
various educational programs. Attend
a daily minyan. Serve on a Center committee.
Volunteer for a needed project. Contribute to our
Building Fund. Support Performing Artists and Adult
Education. Invite a new member to your home.
Become a Patron. Talk to others with enthusiasm
about the Center. Become an active member of the
Men’s Club or Sisterhood. Share your talents where
they are needed.
The Jacksonville Jewish Center is a remarkable
place. Accept your role as a partner in its continued
development and we can secure our congregation’s
survival for generations to come.
Edith, Sam and Max join me in wishing each
of you a happy, healthy year and may the New Year
5757 be a year in which each of us makes a difference
for the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Bruce Horovitz
Executive Director

President’s Message
Excerpts from Installation Speech
There can be no doubt that while
all other organizations we belong to are
important for insuring the survival of the
Jewish people - no institution is more
important than the synagogue - for
insuring the survival of us as Jews.
This congregation has served each
and every one of us well. We are blessed
with dedicated professionals - our clergy
and our administration. More importantly,
we are blessed with the greatest lay
congregation, I challenge, in the country. The foundation
of this synagogue is a loyal, tireless corps of dedicated
volunteers who serve as board members, committee
members, advisors, officers and members of clubs and
organizations, fundraisers and as foot soldiers in all
activities of the synagogue, day in and day out, year
after year.
I looked up the word “President” in Webster’s
Dictionary. It means an official chosen to preside over a
meeting. The Hebrew word for President is Nassee,
which derives from the word “to carry/to lift up,” and
that definition I think is really a more appropriate
description of my role.
My duties as President may include presiding over
meetings, but my goal and responsibility to you and this
congregation is going to be to raise money, and most
importantly, to raise spirits.
I have been told, “Evan - you have
to make people feel better about the
Center”; but ultimately it is the members
who can make people feel better and more
appreciative of the Center.
You need to talk the Center up -
not down. If you have honest criticism,
voice it to the leadership. If you have
praise, voice it to the Community.
Over the next year every one of
us can do some of the following: 1) Give
to the Building Fund; 2) Buy an extra raffle ticket; 3)
Sell an extra raffle ticket; 4) Support the yearbook; 5)
Set up an endowment; 6) Make plans in your will; 7) Be
a patron for one event; 8) Get a friend to join; 9) Get
another friend to join; 10) Sponsor a kiddish; get a group
of friends to sponsor a kiddish celebrating combined
simchas; 11) Sponsor a bulletin; 12) And don’t turn us
down if we adjust your dues to make them more in line
with your fellow congregants.
My message for all of us can be summed up as
follows: I am only one, but I am one; I cannot do
everything, but I can do something; What I can do, I
ought to do; And what I ought to do, by the grace of
God, I will do.
On behalf of Arlene, Esther and Tania, I want to wish
you all a Happy and Healthy New Year! La Shana Tova.
Evan J. Yegelwel
President

Norman Moss

For over 50 years, Norman Moss
devotedly served the Jacksonville
Jewish Center by initiating programs
and bringing high standards to existing
procedures. One of his earliest efforts
was co-chairing the first Center family
picnic, over thirty years ago. This
wonderful family-oriented tradition
still occurs annually at Lag B’omer
A gentleman of many interests
and talents, Norman had also served
as chairman of the Cub Pack and Boy
Scout Troop 14 committees. He had
been an active member of the Center
Board, Men’s Club, Finance and
Religious Committees. For ten years,
he provided a much needed mitzvah
to the Center, as the Thursday Minyan
captain.
For thirty-one years, Norman
brought high standards to the solemnity
of the synagogue by volunteering as
Head Usher for the High Holidays at
Third and Silver Street and the Center’s
present location.
Norman served on the Board
and was treasurer of Jewish Family
& Community Services, for over
thirty years. He also lent a helping
hand to transients by making sure they
had a place to stay and money for
groceries until they were settled.
Throughout his many years of
service, Norman Moss always strove
for excellence and worked toward the
goals of the Center, often initiating
new programs , while upholding the
sanctity of traditional values. We
honor the memory of Norman Moss,
and his consistent devotion to Judaism.

Center Men’s Club
1 he Jacksonville Jewish Center Men’s Club provides numerous opportunities for the men of the congregation.
Activities range from religious to social, from synagogue service to community service, from education to sports.
There is something for everyone.
After four years, the Center Men’s Club has distinguished itself with a wide range of activities. One of the most
notable recent activities was the New American B’nai Mitzvah. For almost a year, twelve recent immigrants from the
former Soviet Union dedicated themselves to the study of Judaism, which culminated in one of the most moving
ceremonies in Center history. While learning English and the American culture, these people spent countless hours
learning Hebrew and studying the laws and rituals that they were denied in their previous homeland. The Center
Men’s Club is proud to have initiated and sponsored this event and, along with members of the congregation, to have
assisted in training the students. We plan to begin another class this fall.
The Center Men’s Club has several traditional
activities. There is an annual Men’s Club Shabbat
when the entire service is conducted by members of
the Men’s Club. We present a book as a gift to each
Bar and Bat Mitzvah, recognizing their
accomplishments and encouraging their further
participation in the congregation. Kadima and USY
members regularly see the Men’s Club serving
breakfast at their conventions; and the congregation
sees the Men’s Club serving at synagogue picnics.
On our own and jointly with the Sisterhood, the Men’s
Club sponsors Sunday morning brunches with
speakers on topics of interest to the community and
the congregation. We enjoy being a consistently
active part of the life of the congregation.
Each year we plan unique and different activities for our members, with an emphasis on family participation.
The annual family fishing trip is a great time for families and friends to spend a day with nature, the sea state
permitting. Other activities include “Boy’s Night Out” card games, sporting events and social events where members
can get to know each other better in a casual environment. We look forward to again celebrating “Havdalah Under
the Stars” with our families and youth groups, sharing our heritage and culture in a simple, enjoyable ceremony.
The Center Men’s Club is not just activities, it is people — people having fun, learning, helping and teaching.
The members of the Men’s Club are dedicated members of the congregation, who enjoy giving back a little of what
we cherish. The club is open to all men in the congregation. We strongly encourage members to join and bring their
unique talents and experience to an already thriving organization. If everyone participates and helps a little, we
cannot help but accomplish a lot.

Sisterhood Adult B’nai Mitzvah
February 16-17,1996/27 Shevat 5756
Xhe Jacksonville Jewish Center’s Sisterhood
B’nai Mitzvah Class, consisting of 40 women and two
men, celebrated a simcha that culminated almost three
years of study, personal growth, and commitment. The
curriculum consisted of the study of Torah, services and
holidays and culminated in
the b’nai mitzvah ceremony.
The study of Judaism
from its works, customs and
traditions gave everyone
much about which to reflect.
We were taught the prayers
that comprised the Friday
night service and the three
sections of the Saturday
morning service. We learned
why the Amidah prayer is
repeated three times daily.
We discussed the origin and
observances of the holidays.
The individual experiences
and memories of the B’nai
Mitzvah added relevance to lessons and worthiness
to the purpose of having taken the class.
The B’nai Mitzvah weekend was glorious. It
began with a traditional Friday night Shabbat dinner.
After a welcome, women stood to recite the blessing
over the candles whose light shone in the eyes of
many, and cast a glow on the families gathered to
celebrate enthusiastically the accomplishments of
mothers, grandmothers, sisters, aunts, daughters,
friends and husbands. Parents made the traditional
blessing upon their sons and daughters. Kiddish was
chanted with the sweetness that the Sabbath heralds.
The meal for over 350 people was delicious
nourishment for the body, while the occasion itself
was nourishment for our spirits. The birkat hamazon
and ru ’ach we shared after the meal were seasoned
with the closeness and pride we felt for ourselves
and for one another.
Friday night services provided an opportunity
to share some of the knowledge we had acquired.
We led prayers and sang hymns. Naomie Trager told
us that the emphasis within our religion to give
tzedakah and to dignify others with acts of kindness
inspired her to realize what is so good about Juda¬
ism. Then Tricia Bettman told us about her memo¬
ries as a Jewish child living in a predominantly Jew¬
ish neighborhood with her relatives, and of attend¬
ing a school with many other Jewish students. Her
memories of her grandmother blessing the candles,
her grandfather speaking yiddish, her cousins look¬
ing for the afikomen together and observing other
customs fulfilled a Jewish life that is harder to live
today when fewer and fewer people speak yiddish or
live in areas heavily popu¬
lated with Jews. The memo¬
ries with bubbes and zaydes
are changing, as homemade '
challahs and other Jewish
delicacies have become
brand name products. The
majority of the congregation
was able to relate to these
experiences, common to so
many.
Saturday services were
also led by members of the
class. It was heartwarming to
see those who had accom¬
plished the skill of reading
Torah use the yad that they
had given to the congregation. We were proud of those
who had aliyot and chanted the Haftorah. Together
they stood: Arlene Rademan with her husband Jerry,
Marion Tischler with her father Louis, Lois Peltz with
her husband Mitchell; Barry Setzer and Iris Kraemer
eloquently chanted the Torah reading. Janis Fleet ex¬
plained how by collecting a half sheckle from each
Jew it meant that no Jew was more important that
another and that only by coming together can a Jew
be whole. Later, Etta Drashin gave a d’var Torah
about how she had come to realize that the study of
Torah, observance of Shabbat, keeping Kashrut and
supporting Israel are important observances of Juda¬
ism. The B’nai Mitzvah enjoyed a deeper understand¬
ing of the prayers as they proudly led the service.
And so they came to the bima together to sing “Adon
Olam ” and to stand as one, a people of one.
And to celebrate even further, we acknowledged
Beck Setzer’s birthday, Risa Schneider’s birthday and
the naming of Julia Allerding, born during her
mother’s bat mitzvah preparation. There is a Hebrew
expression, m’dor Tdor, from generation to
generation. We underscored those words with the
celebration of our Bar/Bat Mitzvah along with our
family and friends, and in the tradition of Jews all
over the world.
— Sue Ann Blumenthal

Daily Minyan Captains
Traditionally, public worship requires a minyan, a
quorum of ten. Only in the presence of a minyan may the
Torah be read or the Kaddish prayer be recited.
Individual prayer is of course commanded in Judaism,
but communal prayer reflects the covenant of peoplehood.
God is fully hallowed only in the assembly of the people.
Our dedicated minyan captains insure that there is a
minyan twice every single day of the year. It is often a hard
and thankless job. It requires hours of phoning to remind
regular minyan-goers, to find additional men to make up the
quorum and often to arrange transportation.
It is because of them that you can recite kaddish when
you mourn or observe yarhzeit. We owe them not only our
thanks, but also our help, to continue providing a minyan.
Please call any minyan captain and become a “regular” for at
least one minyan a week.

Burial and Mourning —
Center Cemetery
Th e Center owns and operates two cemeteries, both adja¬
cent to the Evergreen Cemetery in North Jacksonville. Both are
beautiful pieces of property with large shade trees and mature
landscaping. The original cemetery is referred to as the “Old
Cemetery.” The new cemetery is known as the Center Memorial
Park. The Center provides complete maintenance of these
grounds. Both cemeteries are open daylight hours every day ex¬
cept Shabbat and Jewish
holidays. A Memorial Ser¬
vice is conducted each year
on the Sunday between
Rosh Hashanah and Yom
Kippur at each of the cemeteries. To reach the Old Cemetery, travel
north on North Main Street to Winona. Make a right on Winona and go
until you reach Gate 5. The Old Cemetery is on the left. To reach the
Center Memorial Park, travel north on North Main Street to 43rd Street,
which is beyond Winona. Make a right on 43rd Street and the entrance is

Renee R. Mibab Education Endowment Fund
Th e Center wishes to acknowledge
the loss felt by the passing of a caring
teacher, friend and colleague, Renee
Mibab, on June 14, 1996. Renee passed
away while attending an educational
training seminar.
We express our deepest
condolences to her husband, Perry, and
to her children, Charles, Ben, Alan and Miriam.
“The loss of Mrs. Mibab has left a very real
void which can never be entirely filled. We take
comfort in the fact that a part of Renee will continue
to live, reflected in the lives of the students she has
instructed and guided,” said Day School Principal,
Willard M. Kennedy.

to Renee, The Zucker Family (Jerry, Anita, Jonathan,
Andrea and Jeffrey, of Charleston, South Carolina)
has created the Renee R. Mibab Education

Schechter Day School of The Jacksonville Jewish

through the purchase of teaching supplies. Students
requiring financial aid in order to attend the Day
School will benefit, as well.

by her extensive and impressive love of Israel,
Judaica and most of all the character with which she
taught our children. Most important
was her commitment to the progress
of her students, some of whom she kept
in contact with for years after they were
“hers”. Empowering the children with
an understanding and enjoyment of
Hebrew culture, language and history
gave Renee much satisfaction and
fueled her desire to teach. Renee’s
positive attitude and sincerity was an important
ingredient in her ability to build Jewish esteem in
our children.

the way her momentum affected people around her.

the Tal Am method of teaching Judaica, Renee
expressed great enthusiasm for learning new teaching
methods to implement in the Fall. With vision and
perception Renee knew she would be growing and
learning alongside her students. In her lifetime,
Renee’s enthusiasm and thirst for learning has been
a positive motivator to the many lives she taught
and touched. In simple terms she was a good teacher
by virtue of her skill at teaching “for” the children,
not “at” the children.
It is appropriate to continue Renee’s legacy in the
Day School with the Renee R. Mibab Education
Endowment Fund. On behalf of the Center and the entire
congregation, we thank the Zuckers for their generous
memorial to Renee Mibab’s lasting contributions to the
Day School.

The Simon Selber Memorial Collection
In loving memory of Simon W.
Selber, Z”L, whose passing in 1993 was
profoundly felt by our entire community,
the Selber Family is creating an
endowment fund for the purchase of
books of Jewish content and traditional
character for the Henry Kramer Library
and Resource Center.
Simon Selber’s love for Jewish learning is
reflected in his own magnificent personal library,
containing volumes which he eagerly collected over
the years as he sought ever greater insight into our
tradition. It is in keeping with Simon’s love of
learning that the family has now endeavored to make
available the finest of Jewish books of a traditional
nature, especially to the youth, which Simon worked
so hard to imbue with our Jewish heritage.
Simon Selber’s thirst for living was a gift to those
people whose lives he touched. Simon channeled his
boundless spiritual energy and enthusiasm into helping
others. A man admired for his intelligence and foresight,
Simon is remembered as a charismatic “can-do” person
in all areas of his life. Because he genuinely cared for
other people, he enjoyed many friendships and was
called upon for advice and counsel by many who knew
him. Many in the community now call
upon his memory for comfort or guidance.
Generous with his hands and heart,
Simon helped build the emotional and
spiritual bridge between the Russian
resettlers to Jacksonville and the
community. His unselfish donation of
time, ideas and a private van for
transportation, lent dignity to many New Americans,
and for that he is a part of Jewish history, as well.
Above all, Simon will be remembered not only
for what he accomplished with others, but for the
cohesion these deeds will bring to the generations
that follow. Because of Simon, many people embrace
their faith and believe that anything is possible - and
probable, in this world. Hope, Jewish continuity and
family ties are the strengths Simon taught others to
draw on, by virtue of the way he lived.
Donations may be made to “The Simon Selber
Memorial Collection” to perpetuate the calibre of
Jewish works that will be in the collection. As the
memories of Simon continue to inspire his family to
live their lives with Yiddishkeit and enthusiasm, so
will the memorial collection serve to teach, pursue
and encourage Jewish knowledge, history and culture.

Marilyn & Louis Safer Shabbaton Fund
Th e Jewish Center is proud to
announce the establishment of the
Marilyn and Louis Safer Shabbaton
Fund. The purpose of the endowment
is to assure a continuing opportunity
for intense adult Jewish education at
the Center’s annual Shabbaton
weekend. The Safer family — Eliot
Safer, Cynthia Baron, Deena Richman
and Shana Temel — in announcing
this gift, in memory of their parents, explained that
Jewish study and knowledge was always paramount
in their parents lives. Rabbi Gaffney, in expressing
his gratitude to the family for this significant gift
said - “Our people’s faith has always been nurtured
by the notion that it is an honor to have one’s name
associated with deeds of charity (tzedakah),
especially when it is directed to significant programs
such as Jewish education. Together with the support
of the Shabbaton benefactors, supporters and patrons,
interest from this endowment fund will ensure the
continued high standard of speakers, lectures and
teachers that has always characterized the Center’s
Shabbaton weekend.”
The effect of this endowment will surely be
significant in our lives and in the lives of our children.
It is most appropriate to attempt to describe what
Marilyn and Lou’s friendship and involvement meant
to us. It would be unlikely to know one and not the
other, their relationship was close and loving. A
lifelong friend commented, “Lou
Safer was well-liked and trusted by
the entire community. Very fair and
square, very sweet and nice.
Everybody was crazy about him as a
lawyer even before he became a judge.
A character above reproach. How
many people do you find like that?”
Marilyn not only provided
encouragement and support of Lou as
a husband and in his community endeavors, but she
independently embraced the synagogue as a keystone
in her life. Her son, Eliot said his mother was
“involved in Hadassah and very synagogue oriented.
She grew up in a family rich in traditional values,
with both of her parents taking active roles in the
Jewish community.” Marilyn’s daughter, Deena
commented that “what impressed her most about her
mother was her unselfish devotion to helping friends
that were sick or needy, even taking them to doctor’s
appointments for cancer treatments or other
physically challenging, yet necessary activities.”
Marilyn + Lou. Together, equal partners and
stalwarts of the Jewish community. What better or
more fitting tribute for a wonderful, irreplaceable
couple whose philosophy was grounded in keeping
the synagogue as the center of Jewish life and
learning. Each Shabbaton, Lou and Marilyn will be
with us in spirit. As knowledge continues to grow,
so will our memory of what they meant to us all.

Message from the Director
Early Childhood Department
Located in the Selevan Pre-School
Marcia Robson
Director
Hands-on activities make learning an adventure at the Jacksonville
Jewish Center Pre-School. Teachers utilize the developmental approach
to education. They select topics relevant to their students’ age group
and then build an array of lessons around a central theme. Fairy tales
and stories are taught through art projects, cooking activities and play
acting, using developmental skills to achieve each manipulative skill.
Such an approach permits hands-on experiences with real objects
for children to examine and manipulate. It also allows children to develop
their five senses as well as acquire knowledge and skills based on what
they already know and can do.
The schools academic program includes: language arts; math readiness; perceptual motor development
activities; music; library; outdoor/indoor play; storytelling and comprehension; computer basics for Pre-
Kindergarten students; field trips; cooking; science projects; Kabbalat Shabbat; special holiday programs;
skills for holiday observances; and more.
Our goal is to provide positive, stimulating experiences so that a child has
an opportunity to develop self-confidence, curiosity for learning and an
appreciation of our Jewish heritage.
The Early Childhood Department includes our impressive Pre-School and
Extended Day Care. Our school is accredited by The Florida Kindergarten
Council and the National Association for the Education of Young Children. The
school is also a member of The North Florida Association of Young Children
and The Southern Association of Children Under Six.
We wish our Pre-School families a happy and healthy new year.

Rose Lynn Plotkin Retires After 34 Years
The Solomon Schechter Day School says
farewell to Rose Lynn Plotkin, devoted teacher and
Jewish education visionary, after 34 years of loyal
service to our children.
A firm hand and a loving smile
have guided two generations of Day
School students through the most
impressionable and important years
of their elementary education.
Through the years, Rose Lynn’s
presence has enhanced and in many
cases defined the basic tenants of the
goals and philosophy of the Day
School. Her devotion and expectations
for each child have been equal to their
parents in strength and importance. Her
positive reinforcement and gentle
cultivation of each child’s strength have
charmed and encouraged hundreds or
more children to reach their potential.
Rose Lynn Plot kin
Rose Lynn’s instrumental support and vision
during the early Foundation School years to her
definitive versatility and the experience and passion
she offers as an educator, has enabled her to
accomplish her many roles simultaneously and
effectively. From grade school teacher, resource
teacher, homework tutor, talent show coordinator,
student council advisor to a plethora of other roles,
Rose Lynn has succeeded in training, nurturing and
encouraging significant numbers of children to
become capable, self-confident young adults, parents
and business people.
Mr. Willard M. Kennedy, Day School Principal
holds Rose Lynn in the highest regard. In his opinion,
“She is leaving behind a rich legacy
of competence and affection, the
memory of which shall stand us in
good stead in the years to come. She
is one of the cornerstones of our
Solomon Schechter Educational
Program. Because of the vision of
people like her the school is built on
a solid foundation.”
Providing counsel to many
parents, Rose Lynn has been able to
guide not only children, but entire
families to greater understanding
through learning, thinking and
teaching each other for pure
enjoyment. “Teaching each student to think” is the
measure of Rose Lynn’s success with each student,
“for that is the basis of being independent and the
foundation for tackling any concept.”
Rose Lynn Plotkin’s involvement in each of
our lives has taught us a most valuable life lesson. A
good teacher is a child’s role model for life, long
after the learning is done. Plans are underway for a
retirement party in Rose Lynn’s honor in the Fall.
We wish her much happiness and she will be missed!

Boy Scouts
Troop 14
Troop 14, founded in 1919, carried on the scouting tradition at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center in fine fashion, enjoying another year of outdoor
fun, adventure and activities in 1995-96.
A camping highlight was the North Florida Council’s 75th
Anniversary Camporee at Silver Springs, attended by four thousand scouts
and scouters. Other campouts were at: Blue Springs State Park with scouts
from the Orlando JCA; Osceola National Forest, where the scouts hiked
the historic trail and witnessed the annual reenactment of the Civil War
Battle of Olustee; Goldhead State Park; the JJC; and Scout Blast at Jax
NAS.
Day excursions were made to Ginni Springs for swimming and
canoeing in the area springs and Sante Fe River and to Hanna Park for
trail biking.
The feature of the year’s outdoor program was summer camp at Camp Bob Hardin in North Carolina. The troop
enjoyed whitewater rafting, horseback riding, tubing, and archery, among other skills and activities. The summer
camp contingent was led by Assistant Scoutmaster Steve Solomon and sons Jerry and Kenny. Our campers were
joined by the Orlando JCA’s troop. Scouts Eric and Chris Moemke attended scout camp in Utah.
Meeting programs included: a tour of the Florida Times-Union where, fortunately, the scouts failed to make
headlines; a visit to the neighborhood fire station; a mock court trial; and visits from local Jewish heroes Rick Reiter,
a Navy rescue helicopter crewman, and Randi Glossman, a police patrolwoman. The year-ending program was a golf
excursion to Mandarin Mill.
The troop again exemplified excellence in oratorical skills in the annual Silver Cup of Oratory competition.
The event has become a renowned and fabled event.
Troop 14 joined the other scout groups of the Jacksonville Jewish Center to lead services at the annual Scout
Sabbath. At the annual Blue & Gold Banquet with Cub Pack 14, the scouts were recognized for their achievement and
entertained the audience with a creative skit.
Service to others was not forgotten. The scout’s good deeds included the annual visit to Mt. Carmel Gardens on
Thanksgiving Day to serve lunch and share their youthful vibrancy with the appreciative elder residents.
Scouts Eric Moemke and Aaron Finck attained Life Scout, one rank below the coveted Eagle award. All scouts
make admirable progress in their advancement, including new scouts David Harrington, Michael Demri, Martin
Birnbaum, Travis Jernigan, and Avi Richman.
Fine youth leadership was provided by: Eli Fleet and Justin Jackrel, the senior
and assistant senior patrol leaders: Asher Trager, troop guide; Avi Richman and
Eric Moemke, patrol leaders; and Jared Fleet and C.J. Dulberger, assistant patrol
leaders.
The Troop’s adult leadership was headed by scoutmaster Randy Silverberg,
in his 18th year. He was ably supported by assistant scoutmasters Steve Solomon
and Bill Wilensky. The troop committee chaired by Dan Wilensky included Anne
DePaul, Bob Fleet, Stella Finck, Debbie Jackrel, Kathy Moemke, Harry Peltz,
Charles Trager and Leon Yergin. Troop 14 is for boys ages 11 to 17. For
information call Randy Silverberg at 398-0038 (day) or 733-6649 (evening).

JFCS is Here for You
Iris T. Young Executive Director
Since 1917, Jewish Family & Community Services has worked to
ensure the stability of Jewish families. Mental health and marital counseling,
social services and support groups are just some of the ways JFCS meets the
emotional and material needs of those struggling with life’s problems.
JFCS has one simple goal: to “Strengthen the Community.. .One Family
at a Time.” Our Jacksonville Jewish community is not immune to the world’s
problems. We have poverty and hunger; social discord and divorce; abusive
spouses, unwed mothers, and a host of other frustrations.
We also have the ability to create families through adoption; heal
families through counseling; protect our seniors with outreach services; guide
our young toward meaningful lives and wrap these efforts in the warmth of
Jewish understanding.
JFCS professionals can help clients deal with a variety of concerns,
among them:
• Personal Issues, ranging from past abuse to relationship difficulties,
co-dependency to depression;
• Marital Problems, for individuals or with a partner;
• Divorce, helping clients sort through the emotional, financial, legal
and moral issues.
• Divorce Mediation and Divorce Custody Home Studies are also
offered.
• Family/Step-Family/Blended Family Issues, which take into
account the feelings of all family members. JFCS is a licensed child
placing (adoption) agency, licensed in Florida, New York and
Connecticut;
• Parent-Child Conflict, resolving difficulties between generations;
• Teen Behavior, addressing issues such as poor self-esteem, peer
pressure, respect for authority and self-awareness;
• Children’s Behavior, working with the child who bites, the child
who says “no,” the child who throws tantrums; and
• Abuse/Neglect; Bereavement/Coping with Loss or Change.
Services are offered on a sliding fee scale, and some evening
appointments are available. JFCS also operates the only three community
kosher nutrition sites in Jacksonville, at Mt. Carmel Gardens (Monday-
Friday); Etz Chaim Synagogue (Wednesdays) and Pickwick Apartments
(Mondays). Transportation to and from meal sites is available, as are senior-
oriented social outings, trips to doctors offices, religious services and
shopping.
JFCS also provides emergency financial assistance and non-perishable
food to those in need. Client confidentiality is strictly maintained and protected
at all times. For more information about any JFCS service, call 448-1933.
