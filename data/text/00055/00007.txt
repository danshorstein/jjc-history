Rabbi Kentof’s Message
What is Jewish Education?
Jewish Education in its true
sense is different from learning a
skill. Education is not a product
or an acquisition. Education is not
merely teaching or learning. No
one can sell you, or give you, or
deliver to you an education, as one
can a book or a picture. You can
acquire the ability to type, to fix a
leak, or to ride a bicycle, but you
cannot acquire an education.
Someone can teach you, and you
can learn any number of abilities
or attitudes or appreciations, and
you may still not have an
education.
What, then, is education? It
is a continuous, steady progression
in which the acquiring of one
ability, attitude, or appreciation
leads directly to another, and that
in turn to still another, and so on.
The test of an education lies not in
the particular content you acquire,
but in the need or desire which you
feel to add to that content.
Long ago our Rabbis said,
“Udlo mosifyoseif' - “he who does
not add to his learning decreases
it.” Years later, John Dewey,
America’s greatest philosopher,
expressed the same thought when
he said, “The most important
attitude that can be formed (in
education) is the desire to go on
learning.”
Rabbi Dov Kentof
Director of Education & Youth
Like any education, Jewish
education involves certain skills
that may be mastered, certain
information that may be acquired,
certain attitudes that may be
developed. But, also like any
education, Jewish education in the
sense of the term, must be
conceived as an ongoing process
of inspiring students, young and
old, with both the desire and the
ability to keep on learning. Viewed
from this angle, the quality of
learning is more important than the
quantity. In other words, more
important than “how much has
been learned?” is the question
“How much learning has taken
place?”
Quality in education is
decided by the learner’s desire to
learn more. And an appetite for
learning cannot be acquired on a
diet of teaspoonful doses. A certain
amount of quantity is necessary...
Therefore, Jewish education
cannot be ended at any stage in
life. There is no number of years
of Jewish study that we can call
enough. There is no list of
accomplishments that separates the
educated from the uneducated Jew.
There is no package of facts, no
collection of textbooks, no battery
of tests, the mastery or the
possession of which justifies the
boast of possession of a Jewish
education.
The good Jewish teacher
will so teach that his pupils want
to go on and on in their Jewish
studies. The good Jewish school
encourages and eases this desire,
and the good Jewish parent backs
up the teacher and the school to his
utmost in word and deed.
