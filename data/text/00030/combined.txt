The Progressive Credit Union sponsored by the
Jewish Progressive Society is organized to encourage
thrift and provide a safe, convenient medium where¬
by members can invest and accumulate savings. The
Credit Union carries life savings insurance of $2,000
and loan protection insurance without any direct cost
to the members. Last year the Board declared a 3%
dividend on savings and 10% rebate of interest to
borrowing members.
Ten Commandments of our Credit Union: The
Member is the most important person in our Credit
Union. The Member is not an outsider, he is an owner
of our Credit Union. The Member is not an interrup¬
tion of our work, he is the purpose of it. The Member
does us a favor when he calls — We are not doing
him a favor when we serve him. The Member is not
a cold statistic — He is a human being with feelings
and emotions like our own. The Member is not some¬
one to argue or match wits with. The Member is a
person who brings us his wants — it is our job to
fill these wants. The Member is deserving of the most
courteous and attentive treatment we can give him.
The Member is not dependent o nus, we are dependent
on him. The Member is the life blood of this Credit
Union.

Members of the Charles Palot Club have had a very
exciting year fulfilling the three-fold purpose of the
United Synagogue Youth program: learning more
about our Judaic heritage, helping others and social
activities.
Varied programs throughout the year included
guest speakers on the subjects of mental retardation,
cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy and dating prob¬
lems. Social programs included “The Dating Game,”
“Sensetivity,” a picnic at the Zoo, bowling and a swim
party at the home of Donald Zimmerman. Two excel¬
lent study weekends were held at the Center South-
side Site. The topics discussed were “Interdating” and
“Observance of Traditional Sabbath.”
Members also actively participated in National and
Regional Conventions and were co-hosts with our
Senior Chapter, C.Y.L., to the Tri-City Regional Con¬
vention held in Jacksonville June 15-16 with over 150
USY’ers attending from Tampa, St. Petersburg, Or¬
lando, Lakeland, Daytona and Jacksonville. During the
summer months USY’ers attended Camps Ramah and
Leadership Training Institute.
Many thanks are due the Advisors for their time
and direction given to our Club. We look forward to
their continued leadership for the coming year and wish
them and all friends of U.S.Y. a Shana Tova.

Founded in 1917, the JFCS has had a long tradi¬
tion of helping the members of the Jewish community
face and overcome their personal and interpersonal
problems. With the changing conditions and new or
increased knowledge, new programs and techniques
were added. In its 54th year, with the addition of
Mrs. Jane Staly to the staff we were able to add to
our traditional case work technique of individual per¬
sonal interviews and family therapy, joint therapy for
certain marital cases and co-joint therapy with a Teen
Age Rap and Encounter Group that has been in ex¬
istence since December 1970. We believe these are
most effective and valuable tools.

to doctors, hospitals and meetings and bringing medi¬

donor has enabled us to supply medcines for those un¬

and for those whose limited income makes it impos¬
sible to receive them through regular channels. The
regular and continuing actions of these two men have
truly been in the highest tradition and an inspiration
to all concerned.
Our monthly visits with the Jewish patients at the
State Hospital in Macclenny have proven to be effec¬
tive and popular with the non-Jewish as well as the
Jewish patients and have given the Jewish patients a
sense of belonging and hope. With the wonderful pro¬
gram of River Garden, we believe we have helped move
the last of the older Jewish patients back to our own
community and have watched with great satisfaction
their truly remarkable change in behavior and outlook
towards life.
We have had trouble in getting volunteers to reg¬
ularly lead weekly services for the Jewish prisoners at
Raiford. The riots and the ensuing discontent of the
Jewish prisoners at being the only major religious
group that could not hold weekly services caused us
to renew our efforts. With the help of Walter Levy
and Norman Moss and others we have been assured
of at least two meetings a month. The interest of
Samuel Rosenthal and Barney Gorenstein and others
who try to come from time to time gives hope that
we will be able to achieve this goal this year.

The comprehensive program offered by the Jacksonville Jewish Center
depends on the proper maintenance of our facilities. The efforts of Tim
Riley and Annie Mae Fort, loyal and devoted members of our maintenance
staff for many years, enable the various activities to take place in pre¬
sentable surroundings. Annie Mae Fort also maintains the Kitchen and
prepares many meals. Additional members of the maintenance staff are
Ralph Coleman, Mary Austin and Oscar Stewart.
The local caterers, Mr. and Mrs. Albert Meyerhoff and Mrs. Mary Safer
prepare many Simchas for our members and their families.
The Transportation Committee of the Center is very active in developing
a system of routes as well as providing the necessary vehicles.
We have three 66 passenger buses as well as a 12 passenger mini-bus.
Our staff of drivers is headed by Morris Montford, a twenty year plus
veteran with an enviable record. He teams with Alton E. Guinn and Charles
Jordan to transport our children from their homes and schools to classes,
activities and services at the Center.
Our transportation system, under the direction of Kenneth Bowden, is
efficient and constantly adapts to our ever expanding needs.

summer months. For a nominal charge, members of the community may use

River Garden Hebrew Home for the Aged this year
marked an historic milestone by celebrating its 25th
anniversary in our community. Even more important,
River Garden Auxiliary, the founding organization
behind the Home, marked its 50th year of service lo¬
cally.
Originally, Ladies Hebrew Sheltering Aid Society,
River Garden Auxiliary was established to give shel¬
ter, loving care, companionship and medical care to
our elderly and infirm. They have continued to be
the guiding light in maintaining a personal “Home”
atmosphere instead of permitting an institutional at¬
mosphere to prevail.
The Auxiliary comes to the fore in providing extra-
budgetary needs such as furnishings, decorations,
linens, hobby shop items, etc.
The Auxiliary contributes not only financially to
the Home, but also sponsors activity and volunteer
programs. Golden Sunshine meetings are held weekly.
The Red Cross Volunteer Program helps in yet another
way to personalize life for the residents. Special holi¬
days, such as Mother’s Day, are celebrated for all —
whether they have family or not.
The Auxiliary has established an outstanding pre¬
cedent during the past 50 years and looks forward to

year now ending, but much more remains to be done.
I will need the cooperation and devoted self-sacrifice of
every Center member in order to achieve the great
goals I have for our Synagogue-Center.
I pray that the year 5732 will bring peace to Israel,
freedom for the Jews imprisoned in the Soviet Union,
a total end to American involvement in Indo-China,
and peace and harmony in the cities and streets of
our great nation. We as Jews have much to contribute
to these causes. Our awareness, our concern and our
action in the coming 12 months will be a part of the
solution of the world’s many problems.

CAMP IS A CHILD’S WORLD
The magic formula for Camping begins with the
observation that Camp is a Child’s World. It is a great
relief to escape from the huge, complex, tense and
troubled world in which we all live. Adult-centered,
often scary to adults themselves, that world is beyond
the ability of many children really to understand and
adjust to.
Camp is so different. It is, first of all, quite small.
Any child can see its limits and achieve a feeling of
belonging within them. At Camp, everything revolves
around the child and his group. It is child-centered. A
child can understand it. Just this atmosphere en¬
hances his sense of worth as a human being and en¬
hances the development of his personality.
The Center Day Camp has been in continuous
operation since 1946. Located on the Center’s South-
side Site at 10149 State Road #13, the Camp is situ¬
ated in lovely natural surroundings which are most
conducive to camping fun.
During nine weeks of the summer, children of the
Jacksonville Jewish community have an opportunity
to spend their time in a creative Jewish atmosphere.
Services are conducted each morning, the Birchat
ha’motzi and Birchat ha’mazon are chanted by the
campers at meals, Shabbat is observed by lighting the
Shabbat candles, chanting the Kiddush and having a
special program with singing and dancing, and Tisha
B’Av is solemnly observed at our own Kotel ha’Maravi
(Western Wall). In addition, Hebrew instruction is
given to all who request or require it. Campers experi¬
ence the joy of Judaism in an informal atmosphere, in
Machonim Variety Skit
the hope that they will grow to appreciate their heri¬
tage and delve deeper into it.
The Camp has the finest facilities for growth in
other areas as well. An Olympic size swimming pool,
water-skiing, sports and arts and crafts are a few of
the many activities available to campers. Traditional
camp activities such as camp-outs and an end-of-camp
Color War help make Camp an enriching, fun-filled
experience for each camper. Each summer, field trips
are taken to places of interest in the Jacksonville area.
During the past summer, trips were taken to St.
Augustine and Fort Castillo de San Marcos, Fort-
George Island and the Kingsley Plantation, and the
Jacksonville Naval Air Station.
We plan to add many new activities in the future
and hope you will let your children join us for many
hours of fun and enjoyment at the Center Day Camp.
Melvin Glazer, Program Director and Camper

WHY WE MUST BUILD
There comes a time in the life of a synagogue, as in the
life of an individual, that moves must be made and
directions changed if the needs of the future are to be
met. Such a time is now at hand for our synagogue.
It is not enough in planning for the future physical
plant needs of the synagogue to think in terms of the
next ten years or even twenty-five years. Its planning
must encompass at least two new generations, or a
period of at least fifty years. Such planning demands
foresight and imagination, but above all it demands
a willingness of its present members to assume the
responsibility for coming generations as well as for
its own generation. Even more important, however,
is the demand to ask ourselves in the words of Hillel,
“if not now, when?”
With the urban redevelopment now beginning adjacent
to the Center location and with the Junior College ex¬
pansion program scheduled across the park from us,
there will be no better time than now for the synagogue
to dispose of its present plant.
A far more important reason, however, for the im¬
mediate launching of this effort to secure new facilities
is the compelling needs for updating the present facili¬
ties. It might be well to list the elements which consti¬
tute this updating.
1. The Jewish population has not only moved to
the San Jose area, but there is every indica¬
tion that this trend will continue in a southerly
direction. This should place our site on State
Road 13 within the center of what should be¬
come the area of greatest Jewish concentration.
Such a location will no doubt greatly enhance
the effectiveness of every facet of our program.
2. Any successful synagogue program demands
considerable space and land areas. Extensive
outdoor recreational and athletic facilities are
an absolute must for the school and Center
programs of our synagogue. Fortunately, these
facilities are already installed and ready to be
integrated with these school and Center pro¬
grams. Extensive and easily accessible parking
is a must for any program. The construction of
all facilities on a one-floor level is extremely
desirable. Sufficient areas for providing natural
landscaping is helpful in adding new dimen¬
sions in the use of all facilities. Adequate space
is necessary to create buffer zones to insulate
the synagogue’s usages from the influence of
outside and adjacent properties.
3. Now that the Center’s educational program has
been expanded to include a complete ele¬
mentary and pre-elementary day school pro¬
gram, the physical plant must keep pace. The
new school facilities now being built represent
tremendous changes both in concept and space
requirements. These newer type facilities are a
must if our school is to develop in the manner
envisioned by all of us.
4. The present Auditorium facilities do not meet
the expanding needs of the congregation. Our
present multi-purpose facilities have served the
congregation well but today much more and
better facilities are needed. There is a need for
multi-purpose facility for use by the school for
its recreational, cafeteria, indoor play, and
athletic programs. This type facility should be
separate from the Auditorium and available for
use in the general Center program. At the same
time, however, there is a need for Auditorium
facilities with the necessary decor and beauty
so as to make it possible for the functions, both
of the congregation and its individual members,
to be held within the synagogue in an atmos¬
phere equal to those available in hotels and
country clubs.
Such an Auditorium should also be located so
that it can be combined with the sanctuary in
making it possible for the entire congregation
to be seated together for one combined service.
5. There are many other needs, such as the need
for improved and expanded chapel facilities,
the need for an expanding library service for
Adults and students and the need for additional
office and administrative space. Many more
needs could be listed but only the most im¬
portant and obvious have been mentioned.
Important conclusions become obvious — the need
is here and the financial ability of the congregation to
provide for its needs is unquestioned. The only ques¬
tion to be determined is whether the membership has
the vision, the imagination, and above all, the desire
to begin now to meet the needs of the present and the
future.

Dear Friends:
Article II, of the Center Youth League Constitu¬
tion, states the following: The aims of the United
Synagogue Youth is to offer opportunities to the Jew¬
ish youth to continue to strengthen his identification
with Judaism and with the Synagogue as the central
agency of Jewish life in America, and to give Jewish
youth an awareness of the essential harmony between
the ideals and traditions of Judaism and American
Democracy.
I strongly feel that the past year the Jacksonville
US.Y. began to undertake the task of living up to the
objectives set forth in this constitution. The goal of
our organization was to apply the Jewish ethics to the
pressing problem of the day and to explicitly spell out
this harmony between Jewish ideals and American
Democracy.

I have often wondered if there are more lessons to be learned from
Jewish history than from world history. Certainly it is doubtful if any other
nation has ever reflected as well the events going on in the political situation
of powerful countries.
Educationally speaking as well, our entire school system was greatly
affected by the major issues facing our country today. The beginnings of
a new trend in American Jewish education were seen in the vast expansion
of our Day School. By the summer of 1971, there has been an almost com¬
plete reversal from the elementary afternoon Hebrew School to the Solomon
Schechter Day School. The trend continues in an ever greater fashion and
has strengthened our Kindergarten Department as well.
The Sunday Religious School also indicated the need for a new ap¬
proach to reach the teenagers. The planning of the past year has therefore
emphasized the opportunity of integrating the instruction of the secular
and religious departments. We were fortunate to obtain a staff of teachers
who were cooperative in the efforts to unify the curriculum.
We have had the facilities available for all these departments and the
transportation to accomodate such a tight schedule. Our school hums with
learning activities from early morning till late evening. We are happy that
the administrative provisions and modes of the past have enabled us to cope
with these increases.
We are thankful to the staff of teachers, the departmental heads and
the very active members of the Education Committee in seeing us through.

The Jewish Community Council is the social
planning, community relations and fund raising body
serving the entire Jewish community of Jacksonville
and its environs. Its representative Board of Direc¬
tors is composed of delegates of the major Jewish re¬
ligious, philanthropic and service organizations in the
area, plus members-at-large.
The annual community-wide United Welfare Fund
Campaign provides funds for the support of local, na¬
tional and overseas needs, including the United Jewish
Appeal.
Local beneficiaries receiving funds are River Gar¬
den Home for the Aged, the Jewish Family & Chil¬
dren’s Services. The Council is the sponsoring agency
for the Mount Carmel Gardens.
The Community Calendar and its clearance service
are maintained by the Jewish Community Council to
enable all Jewish organizations in the community to
clear dates for all their events.
The Council publishes the COMMENTATOR, a
newspaper published monthly. It is the official paper
of the Council and its member organizations.
The Council conducts community programs in
celebration of special events and, through its Youth
Division, promotes various programs for the Jewish
youth of all ages.
The Council is a member of the Council of Jewish
Federations and Welfare Funds, The National Jewish
Community Relations Advisory Council and the Com¬
munity Planning Council of the Jacksonville area.

at the Center’s past accomplishments (and failures)

and dedication for the future.

for adequate facilities becomes even greater.

forward to help?

one in which God enables you to meet the challenge
of a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity.
