FOREWORD
The Jacksonville Jewish Center originated almost a
half century ago as Congregation B nai Israel. For over
20 years it has carried the present name at the present
location, West Third and Silver Streets and has been
the Conservative Synagogue in Jacksonville.
The Center is affiliated with the Uited Synagogue
of America. All branches within the Jacksonville Jew¬
ish Center are affiliated with corresponding national
bodies of the Conservative movement. The Sisterhood,
Daughters of Israel, is a member of the Women’s League,
the Center M.e n s Club of the National Federation and
the youngest member of the Center Family, the Y. P. L.,
is also part of the national organization of the same name.
Progress recorded in the pages of this Year Book is
in keeping with the advancement on a national level of the
Conservative movement as a whole, which is the fastest
growing Jewish movement in America.

JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER
Dear Members:
In the months since I became your President I ex¬
pressed my ideas on the management of the Center on dif¬
ferent occasions. It was also my pleasure to extend
greetings when our members celebrated events in the
Synagogue. This message I would like to make brief,
but covering both of those points.
All the important projects and services we render
and the work of our affiliated organizations are covered
in separate sections of this Book. Our biggest and most
essential undertaking is the Building and Expansion pro¬
gram and calls for our best thinking and energy.
My Officers and I shall continue giving the Center
the best in us. All economies and close management
we introduced will not make possible the job we do in
the community without your financial backing. Please
give us your cooperation and may God bless you tenfold.
We wish you and your dear families a Happy and
Prosperous New Year.

Jerusalem, July 17, 1949.
20 J amuz, 5709.
To my Friends in Jacksonville:
I want to tell you how it feels to be in Israel, es¬
pecially in the city of Jerusalem.
Before I left many of you asked me How does it
feel to be going to Israel? ’ You realized that I was
possessed of a great sense of expectation. My restrained,
even matter-of-fact, replies were deliberate; I wanted to be
free of anticipations bred of fancy.
Years of dedication to Zionism, pent up aspirations
of Jewish statehood now fulfilled, Iife-I ong urging of
support to sustain a struggling Yishuv, public enlighten¬
ment and debate with Jew and non-Jew on the val idity
of the Jewish nationalist position, constant prayer for
Zion s restoration—all these, I feared, mig ht make me
see what wasn t there.
I landed on Lydda airport with Sheheheyony on
my lips, but a determined realist. I wanted to be free
of illusions and safe against disillusionment. Profound
thanksgiving has been reported by travelers arriving in
the Holy Land, but coming down on the sacred soil by
plane evokes instantaneous elation. Step off the run¬
way and you are in touch with hallowed ground. No
wooden piere, no concrete landing platform intervene. I he
shoreline for which you have been scanning eastward
while still over a solid carpet of sea is soon in view
with a fringe of sand dune holding back the waves.
Characteristic vegetation at once begins to fill the land¬
scape, hugging several settled areas. Before you realize
fully that you have arrived to the land of your fathers
the plane s door swings open to the greeting of Brukhim
Ha-b aim. Simultaneously the aroma of sunbaked
earth mixed with sea breeze and cedar penetrate the nos¬
tril s—a sensation that will follow you around from now on.
My mission for the Rabbinical Assembly auspicious¬
ly introduced me to the Holy Land by way of the Holy
City. Space will not permit a step by step account
of edifying and informative experiences along the Road
of Strength and the ascent into modern Jerusalem by way
of the S even Sisters hairpin curves. Now that I have
been here five days including a Shabat I recommend this
itinery unreservedly to every visitor of Israel. Despite
battle scars of a recent war, despite its being a pawn on
the international diplomacy table—perhaps because of
them, as added reasons—Jerusalem is still the King’s
sanctuary, the regal city.’’
Victory fell short of holding the break-through into
the walled Old City and the Shik Jerah junction sancti¬
fied by the lives of Dr. Yasky and his associates. How¬
ever, I saw with what admirable ingenuity Hadassah re¬
located its mercy and welfare service and the Hebrew
University its educational program in borrowed and im-

provised quarters. There is no slack in determination;
the city continues to inspire faith and gracious living.
I just spent Shabat in Jerusalem. Although the
day is observed throughout Israel in traditional spirit,
here it is something special. The «Friday afternoon in¬
creased bustle melts away with the declining rays of sun¬
shine. I attended several synagogues for different por¬
tions of the Sabbath prayers. Languid chants of Sep¬
hardic ritual, lusty cantations of Yemenite patriarchs and
urchins, blended in me with the fervent Hassidic incan¬
tations, even when I worshipped in the modern Jeshurun
synagogue.
Out on the streets joyous reverence prevailed. This
spirit remained unbroken till sunset on Saturday. It
was felt in all sections of the city. Oriental finery in
Sha are Hesed, kaftan and fur cap Sabbath garb of
Eastern Europe, in Meah She arim, westernized attire on
the principle business streets blended in expressing Sab¬
bath tranquility. I inquired from my promenading
companion, a native Yerushalmi, about lustier diversions
that younger Israeli are supposed to indulge in according
to reports. His reply came with indulgence for the
uninitiate and not without considerable pride, Don t
forget; this is Jerusalem.’’
Neither shall 1 forget to repeat the words o f the
Psalm when I leave tomorrow for other parts of the land;

SECOND UNIT OF JACKSONVILLE JEWISH CENTER CONSTRUCTION CEREMONIES SCHEDULED
The dates of November 13th or 20th have been set
tentatively for breaking of ground with construction to
proceed immediately. The dates are tentative depend¬
ing on the convenience of the nationally famous speaker
who is now being contacted to appear at the ceremony.
Working drawings and specifications, culminating
long months of planning, study and campaigning are
about completed by the architectural firm of Marsh and
Saxefby. Bids should open on November 1st. Every¬
thing proceeding on schedule, definite activities should
be noticeable by worshippers coming for High Holiday
services. The buildings now occupying the ground ad¬
jacent to the Center will be in process of demolition.

National Affiliation
As the Conservative synagogue in Jacksonville the
Center works in close cooperation with the national move¬
ment. Fountainhead of conservative Judaism in America
is the Jewish Theological Seminary in New York City which
coordinates all phases of laymens organizations within
the fold through the United Synagogue and the Rabbinical
Assembly. The former in turn sponsors the Cantors As¬
sembly, and womens, mens, and young peoples branches.
Financially these organizations are supported by community
funds and Friends of the Seminary groups within individual
synagogues. Jacksonville meets its obligations through
both of these means. At the Purim Seudah Rabbi Max
Routtenberg, Executive Vice President of The Rabb inical

gregations all over the United States on the Sabbath of

participated.

Troop 14 sponsored by the Jacksonville Jewish Center
began its 1948-49 year with 19 scouts. After a banner
year of scouting activities and events, the Troop boasts
a full complement of 32 boys.
During the year the Troop inaugurated the new Ad¬
vancement plan, with the Troop’s own committee reviewing
and passing scouts for advancement and merit ba dg es. This
plan was handled by our Troop Committee consisting of:
Robert J. Gordon .Chairman, Harry Katz, Dr. Harold
Levin, Irvin Rothstein, Hyman Selber, Rabbi S. A. Tofield.
The annual Boy Scouts of America Anniversary Sab¬
bath was held by the Troop on February 11. The entire
service was conducted by scouts. An outstanding event
took place on this occasion. The first Ner Tamid Award
issued in Jacksonville was presented to Jack Eff, Junior As¬
sistant Scoutmaster. This medal is issued to scouts of
Jewish faith who demonstrate their faithfulness to the
Scout Law, "a scout is reverent ”, and who meet require¬
ments set down by the national office. Similar awards are
available to scouts of the Catholic and Protestant com¬
munions.
Troop 14 also participated in the Armistice Day parade
and ushered at prominent football games and other events
at the Gator Bowl.
Silver Springs was the site of the most eventful camp¬
ing trip held during the year. Other trips were made to
Goldenhead State Park, Camp Echockotee, Fernandina and
Broward Woods. The latter was the location of the Sioux
District Camporee where all of the patrols made qualifying
scores for the North Florida Council Camporee. Very
few troops can boast of qualifying all of its patrols as was
the case of our troop. In the final event all three patrols
made excellent scores.
Parent’s Night on March 31 proved to be an outstanding
event. Several new scouts were inducted at an impressive
candle light ceremony. The Bat Patrol won a set of boxing
gloves for the best patrol skit.
JDE MIZRAHI
Bringing the year’s events to a fitting climax was the
second annual I roop 14 Scout Circus, hailed by all as a
thrilling success. Highlighted by the Troop s own band,
the “Big T op featured the excellent performers, Bob Book¬
er, magician and Sherwood Hicks, hand balancer. Chair¬
man for this event was Junior Assistant Scoutmaster Jack
Eff, who sparkplugged this grand circus. Most appre¬
ciated is the co-operation of all who lent a hand, the Troop
Committee, and especially the following.
Robert J. Gordon, Hyman Selber, T ed Friedman, Irvin
Rothstein, Manny Goldstein, Louis R. Mo ss, Ted Freedman
and George Proctor.
Special mention goes to the mothers committee headed
by Mrs. Morris Nachman. Numerous other individuals
were thanked publicly through the Center Bulletin.
Finale of the season was the annual banquet held in
the Center Auditorium on June 9. The S imon Rothstein
Memorial Trophy was presented by Abe Rothstein to Jack
Eff for 1948 and to Stanley Ruby for 1949 in recognition
of their attaining respectively the title, outstanding scout
of the year. Scout Franklin Resnick received the A. Z. A.
medal for achieving the most progress during the year while
exemplifying the ideals of scouting. Executive Horace S.

parents. Robert J. Gordon s request for voluntary contri¬

I he practice of lighting candles in the Jewish home
upon ushering in the Sabbath every Friday evening and the
Festivals whenever they occur in the cycle of the Hebrew
Religious Calendar, is one o f the most beautiful rituals in
Judaism. Tradition has conferred this charming and edify¬
ing spiritual touch to the sanctity of the home upon the lady
of the house.
Jewish women, the wives and mothers of many genera¬
tions, made it a practice to add a personal prayer to the
required benediction. The impromptu prayer was usually
given in the language most familiar to the lady. It included
requests for every day blessings such as physical and spirit¬
ual welfare for the home and the dear ones it represents.
Accordingly the prayer varied with the mood, the occasion
and the need of the hour. For this reason one of the fre¬
quently observed Jewish rituals has very few prayers hand¬
ed down from tradition. The primary significance of
kindling the Sabbath and Festival candles is the merging
of the light from the soul with the Light of the Universe
at the time light is spread in the family abode.
With hesitancy we suggest a prayer found in the book,
Sabbath, the Day of Delight” by Abraham E. Millgram.
Let it serve as a model of a type prayer that each woman
may find it in her heart to utter and not become a perfuntory
stereotype. Judaism has associated its fondest ideals with
the symbol of a light.
KI NER MITZVO, VTORAH OR.
“For Good deeds are a candle, and the Torah is
light”.
KI IMCHO MEKOR HAYIM, B’ORCHO NIRE OR.
“For with Thee is the foundation of life, in Thy
light do we see light”
Note: A prayer like the one given below should be re¬
cited with variations in keepng wth the occasion. When a
festival does not fall on the Sabbath the particular obser¬
vance may be named instead as indicated by the word festi¬
val in parenthesis.
I hope that including the text of the benedictions for
the lighting of the candles on various occasions and the
suggested prayer in our Year Book will help the women
of our congregation practice the observance with faith¬
fulness. On the Hebrew Calendar we have shown
the time of lighting candles each Friday of the year and
Erev Yom Kippur, next to the sym bo! L. C ., which stands
for “lighting of candles.” No hour is indicated for
Ho I id ays outside of Yom Kippur because the time of sun¬

Rosh Hashanah marks the beginning of the Jewish New Year

The Jewish Community Council is the representative
organization of all the Jews of Jacksonville. It actually
is the over-all agency for the Jewish community since its
broad concerns, it's scope of responsibility has to do with
any activity, other than religious, which is community
wide in nature.
Its functions can best be described by its constitution
which had been approved by every major Jewish group.
Each of these organizations ascribe to the Community
Council the following purposes as outlined in the Council
constitution:
1. to help maintain the dignity and integrity ot Jew¬
ish life;
2. to develop an articulate, intelligent and effective
public opinion on community problems and interests;
3. to foster cooperation among all elements in the
Community of Jacksonville;
4. to raise, receive, collect, hold and distribute funds
for Jewish causes, either directly or through present¬
ly existing or subsequently established agencies;
5. to enable the Jewish Community of Jacksonville
through coordination of fund-raising activities to
meet its obligations to local, national and overseas
causes in an intelligent and effective manner;
6. to cooperate with other agencies for better mutual
understanding and
7. in general, to plan for the we I fare and development
of the Community as a whole, engaging in activities
as will contribute to that end.
I he chief activities of the Council can be grouped
under three general headings:
1. I he United Campaign; 2* I he PubI ic Relations
Committee; 3. Extension Program (recreational, social and
athletic activities).
1 he United Campaign has a long history dating back
more than 13 years. In that period, the campaigns increased
from thirty-three hundred in 1936 to three hundred fifty
thousand in 1948 and the number of contributors increased
from a handful in 1936 to almost fifteen hundred last year.
Every segment of the community; indeed, practically every
Jewish family participates. Typical of the representative
leadership in the Council Ben Stein was 1948 general chair¬
man, Harry Gendzier chairman of the Men s Division, Sam
Wolfson, Sam Kipnis and Phil Coleman co-chaired the
Advance Gifts Division. Mrs. Ben Stein, Mrs. Joseph
Hackel, Mrs. Maurice Cherry, Mrs. I. H. Edward s, and
Mrs. I. D. Glickstein led the Women’s Division. Jerry
Kipnis, Louis Safer, Mrs. Don Mayerson and Dr. Roy Sloat
co-chaired the Junior group.
The PubI ic Relations Committee of the Council ex¬
emplifies an effort by a representative cross-section of our
community to deal with anti-semitism and other problems
which affect the well-being of Jewry of Jacksonville.
Harry Reinstine served as chairman of this very active group.
The third major function is the Extension Program.
It has recently been reorganized and is now under the chair¬
manship of Robert M. Rieders. Plans for this overall recrea¬
tion, social and athletic activity include programs for every
age group from 6 to 60. Already in operation are leagues,
such as bowling, which have blossomed out from a few
men to an organized activity involving eight teams of six
men, representing as many organizations. In 1948 we also
saw the initial entry of women in bowling league compe¬
tition. J he Council J heater Group, under the leadership
of Major and Mrs. Reiser, is another typical example of the
kinds of activity sponsored through this part of Council
program. Dances, basketball, golf, tennis and other team
and individual activities are included in the prospected
Extension Activities Program.
1 he Commentator, bi-monthly Council publication, not
only keeps the Community informed about on-going activ¬
ities but serves as the general medium of disseminating in¬
formation to every Jewish family in Jacksonville.
