A Message from Rabbi Gaffney
71" 2
September 1998
Elul 5758
Dear Friends,
Our tradition suggests that the
most authentic form of prayer is an
expression of gratitude. Jewish
children are taught to begin each day
with the words - “Modeh ani Vfa-
nech ...” -“I am grateful to you, O
God...” At the end of each meal we
recite the words “Nodeh Vcha...” -
“We thank you, O God”. At the
conclusion of the day the evening
services urges us to say -“Modim
anachnu ...” -“We thank you O
God.” On Shabbat and holidays the
92nd Psalm is recited -“Tov I’hodot
Vcha ...” - “It is good to give thanks
to You, O God.”
As I consider these past 25
years (...it is hard to believe ...a
quarter of a century??) with our
Jewish Center family, I find this
tradition of thankfulness to be the
most accurate reflection of my
sentiments. The words on this page
come in the form of a -“hakarat ha-
tov... ” -a recognition of the goodness
and blessing which is mine in the
association that I have enjoyed with
so many friends for so many years.
I am grateful that I am blessed
with a congregational family that is
second to none in its warmth and
devotion. From time to time, when
rabbis “let their hair down” at a
convention or meeting, there are the
inevitable complaints by colleagues
who describe their struggle with their
congregational board ...or their
membership. I find myself thinking
that I have no frame of reference to
understand their dilemmas. From the
very first day that Inge and I came to
Jacksonville, we were touched by the
warmth and pleasantness, the
sweetness and sensitivity of the
members of this congregation. We
Rabbi David and Inge Gaffney
have stood in awe in recognition of
the sacrifices of time, effort and
substance that so many have made
and continue to make on behalf of
our beloved Center. We are proud
to have been part of this grand
spiritual adventure which the Jewish
Center has experienced and which it
will be celebrating at its centennial,
in just a few years. I am blessed in
that so many of you have permitted
me to enter the inner sanctuary of
your hearts and souls. It is no small
achievement that over twenty-five
years a tapestry has been woven
with threads that link our lives in joy
and sorrow, in hopes and dreams.
The term rabbi is a term which
signifies relationship. As the word
“father” implies a child ...as the word
“husband” implies a wife ...the term
“rabbi” means teacher. In a rabbinic
text, The Ethics of the Fathers, one
rabbi confessed ... “I have learned
much from my teachers; but from my
students I have learned most of all.”
A rabbi learns most from those he
attempts to teach. I have learned so
much from you. I have learned about
the innocence of faith from the
children of our schools and have
thrilled in their excitement as they
grow in knowledge of Jewish life. I
have learned about the wonder and
mystery of love from couples before
whom I stand under the huppah\ and
have been moved by the idealism of
our young people as their youth
groups engage in a variety of activities
of Tikkun Olam. I continue to be
inspired by the depths of commitment
of the lay leaders of our congregation
and am heartened by expressions of
Jewish responsibility from those who
labor unceasingly for the community,
for Israel and for the Jewish people.
I have learned courage from those
who have lived with the burden of
sorrow and illness and the meaning
of joy from those who have been
touched by life’s blessings. I have
felt the warmth from so many of you
who attend Shabbat services ...and
devotion from those who attend our
daily minyan. Most of all I have
learned about friendship from each
of you who have offered your heart
in warmth and love to Inge and me.
Friends, as we mark this
personal milestone I am reminded of
the teaching of Rabbi Tarphon who
stated -“hayom ketzer v’hamlacha
m’rubah”... “the day is short and
there is so much work to be done.”
Whatever the personal achievements
and aspirations for our beloved
congregation, I recognize that it would
not have been possible without the
support, cooperation and effort of an
extraordinary professional staff. In
some cases these relationships
extend over a long period of time
...Rabbi Dov Kentof, Mr. Willard
Kennedy, Marilyn Sandler and Ted
Gross have shared a substantial
portion of their life, professionally,
with our congregation. In other cases
relationships are more recent but no
less valuable ...Cantor Aron Heller,
Bruce Horovitz, Shirley Bielski and
Dale Schemer. It has also been most
rewarding to be able to share each
day with numbers of devoted
individuals in our administrative
offices, beginning with my secretary
Dereen Murray and including Kim
