Blanche Slott is a truly remarkable
woman. A modern ayshet chayil , she is at
once a strong, capable and decisive leader,
wise and understanding advisor, highly
talented and prolific artist, loving friend,
mother and grandmother, wonderful
homemaker, great cook and a pretty good
bowler. With a quick smile, Blanche
ushers youngsters and contemporaries
into her confidence. She can be relied upon
for her candor, as well as for the quality of her counsel,
youthful vigor is envied by those much her junior. She is prized by
our congregation for her tireless and continued efforts in so many
areas, over so many years.
Her
Blanche is a hometown girl, the
middle daughter of Max and Ida Spitz.
She and sisters Miriam (Edwards) and
Harriette (Levin) attended LaYilla and
Central Grammar schools in
Jacksonville until a “boom” in 1932
lured the family to Sarasota. Blanche
remembers Clara Jaffe as one of her
early classmates in Jacksonville.
After graduation from high school, Blanche stayed in Sarasota
and enrolled in the Ringling School of Art. There she developed a talent
for drawing and painting that would become a hobby and avocation
throughout her life.
In the late 1930s, Blanche and her family returned to Jacksonville.
A young woman by this time, Blanche took a job at Worman’s bakery
and then with Lovett’s. Her brother-in-law, Abe Levin, introduced her
to a nice young man with whom he worked, Louis Slott. They began
dating and married in 1940.
