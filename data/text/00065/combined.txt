Committees and Organizations
Center Men’s Club
“Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life”
The Jacksonville Jewish Center Men’s Club is an organization dedicated to the
enrichment of Center life and the Jacksonville Jewish Community.
The Men’s Club provides an opportunity for the men of our synagogue to work
together supporting, sponsoring, and participating in programs that bring
meaning to our slogan, “Involving Jewish Men in Jewish Life”.
Our Men’s Club is an active member of the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs (FJMC),
an international organization dedicated to the ideals, philosophy and traditions of
Conservative Judaism. FJMC programs we participate in include the distribution of
hundreds of Yellow Holocaust Memorial candles so every congregant can remember
the Holocaust on Yom HaShoah. We offer educational programs for our membership
based on the FJMC “Hearing Men’s Voices” series on issues involving family, religion,
career and health for the Jewish man in today’s world.
We work closely with youth and education leaders to support the Center youth
organizations by helping out with conventions. We sponsor the Yad Shel Chai (Hand of
Life) and Double Yad Shel Chai awards that recognize and reward our youth for
reading Torah. The Men’s Club’s Martin J. Gottlieb Brit Hinukh award is given each
Spring to one teacher in the Center Schools who best represents the life commitment
necessary to bring a quality learning experience to Jewish children. We are pleased to
present hand-painted Israeli Kiddush cup sets to Confirmation students and a metal
finished Kiddush cup to our Bar and Bat Mitzvahs.
Most importantly, the Men’s Club has established the Horty T. Gendzier Summer
Scholarship Fund, which provides funding for children to attend Camp Ramah or
other Jewish enrichment programs and honors the memory of Horty T. Gendzier, who
was so instrumental in the pioneering of these programs. For the last seven years, the
Horty Fund has made it possible for many children to attend these summer programs
that they would otherwise not be able to afford to. Along with a portion of Men’s Club
dues, the Horty Fund is replenished each year by honoring a Man of the Year with a
“roast and toast” banquet. This annual event has become a fixture
on the calendar at the Jacksonville Jewish Center, with hundreds of congregants
attending these laugh-fests that are full of surprises.
We are visible friends of our synagogue, with programs like selling and building
sukkot for our Center families and helping to decorate and maintain the Center’s
Goldman Sukkah. You can find Men’s Club members at the Lag B’Omer picnic every
year, grilling and serving refreshments, and you will find us ushering at morning
services every Shabbat and holiday. Men’s Club members sponsor, lead and
participate in Men’s Club Shabbat.
These events have earned your Men’s Club the First Place and Honorable Mention
Torch Awards from the Federation of Jewish Men’s Clubs in years past and we are
actively pursuing new Torch Awards for community service and other activities.
The Men’s Club is also a great way for friends to have fun. We have gone on deep sea
fishing trips and had family outings to sporting events, such as the Jacksonville Suns
baseball and Barracudas ice hockey games. In the fall, we “kick off” our new year
with our annual membership party, featuring lunch and raffles, while we watch the
Jacksonville Jaguars play an away game on the BIG screen TV. Also, our recent pick-
up softball games have been a huge success, and we will keep on playing.
All this and more is awaiting you if you are at least 18 years old, male,
and a member of the Jacksonville Jewish community.
Join now, we need your help and appreciate your support!
— Dave Schmerer

5766 Simhat Torah Honorees
On Simhat Torah, we bring to a close the annual cycle of the reading of the Torah and begin anew.
Because of our perennial love of the Torah, it has been referred to as Israel’s “bride”.
A custom has evolved of recognizing worthy men and women through a beautiful ceremony,
as each is called to the Torah on this special day.
Our Hatan Torah
Born in North Dakota and raised in Indiana, Randall (Smitty) Smith has that Midwestern
sensibility, “Say little — do much.” A Past President of Center Men’s Club, he pioneered the Yad Shel
Chai program that encourages teens of our synagogue to continue reading Torah after their Bar or Bat
Mitzvah. His delight at helping youngsters continue this important mitzvah is evident
when he presents their awards.
An experienced leader of men, Smitty proudly served in the United States Air Force for twenty years.
When he retired with the rank of Major, Smitty settled in Jacksonville and soon became an officer in the
Center Men’s Club. He now serves as Vice President of Florida Region of the Federation of Jewish Men’s
Clubs and was Operations Coordinator for the FJMC International Convention.
Among his credentials, Smitty holds an engineering degree from Purdue, an MBA from Golden Gate
University, a BSBA in Management Information Systems from the University of West Florida and is
currently a Network Engineer at Honeywell.
A familiar face at synagogue events, Shabbat services and Shalosh Seudot, as well as
part of the Shabbat & Holiday usher team, we are pleased to call Randall Smith our Hatan Torah.
Our Hatan Bereshit
Sandy Zimmerman is a rising star in the Center’s galaxy of lay leaders.
Bright, enthusiastic and charming, Sandy is the newly elected President of our synagogue. Born and
raised in Jacksonville, Sandy attended our Day School, became Bar Mitzvah on our pulpit and developed
lifelong friendships among the youth of our synagogue. He is also a natural-born leader, something he
gets from his parents, who are honored community leaders themselves. A driving force in his family’s
Wachovia Securities firm, Sandy brings the same personal service, perspective and insight to the
Jacksonville Jewish Center as he does to his business. Moreover, Sandy demonstrates the importance of
investing one’s time to ensure the spiritual growth of our synagogue.
A graduate of the University of Florida with a degree in Finance, Sandy was recognized in 2003 by the
Jacksonville Business Journal as one of “Jacksonville’s 40 young Up & Comers.” Ranked among the top
10% of advisors nationally, he is First Vice President- Investment Officer of Wachovia Securities.
Sandy generously applies his financial know-how to the betterment of the Jewish community at large.
A member of the Center Board since 2000, Sandy has served as past patron chair for the
Center Raffle for Education, twice chair of the JJC Golf Classic, chair of the
Center’s Investment Committee, Vice President of Finance and now President.
With all this, Sandy is even more devoted to his wife Lynn and daughters, Brooke and Leah.
Our Woman of Valor
Edith Horovitz practices what she preaches, and teaches her students to do the same.
Her Mitzvah Program has given hundreds of students early, hands-on experience with tikkun olam
in the Jewish and general communities, both locally and statewide.
Edith inherited the wealth that only a dynasty of “doer’s” can bestow — commitment, moral judgment,
strength of purpose and strong devotion to Judaism and the synagogue. She and her husband, Bruce,
received the distinguished Shin Award from the Jewish Theological Seminary for their commitment to
Judaism, community, synagogue and the Seminary. For 24 years, Edith has been a dedicated Judaica
teacher at the Center’s Preschool and the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School that her parents were
instrumental in creating. As Middle School Vice Principal, Edith’s energy, enthusiasm and creativity
are revealed in the Mitzvah Program and other programs like the annual Day School “ALL Hebrew”
musical extravaganza. In addition, Edith has worked to prepare students for Bar and Bat Mitzvah,
and conducts Jr. Congregation and Holiday Youth Services.
Edith graduated with a degree in Psychology from the University of Pennsylvania, where she joined
Hillel. She has been a counselor to USY-on-Wheels, on the staff at Camp Ramah, and active on Center
committees serving our youth. Married for 26 years to her childhood sweetheart, Edith and Bruce have
raised two wonderful sons, Sam and Max, young leaders in their own right.

President’s Message
I am very excited about the future of the Jacksonville Jewish Center.
Having grown up as a “Center Kid,” I have wonderful memories of spending
time on the playground, swimming in the Yegelwel Family Pool, and at times, causing
mischief in the halls of the Center. Today it is my children, Brooke and Leah, who
are enjoying the great benefits of our playgrounds, pool, and facility, hopefully
without the mischief.
Over the past quarter century we have grown, not only in size but in Jewish
enrichment as well. Today our Martin J. Gottlieb Day School offers every Jewish
child in Jacksonville an opportunity to have the best Jewish education available.
The Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School offers children, three times each
week, an opportunity to have the same type of Jewish enrichment and education
available in our Day School. Our Preschool offers a warm and loving environment in
which young children can grow both Judaically and individually.
While we have many accomplishments to be proud of, it would be a mistake to
stop and rest on our laurels. We must continue forward. Our Board of Directors,
rabbinic staff and professional staff have outlined several areas that need and
deserve our attention. Over the next two years, I look forward to working with the
entire synagogue membership to make the Center the best it can be.
We have already begun work in three critical areas. First, we are committed to
enhancing and expanding the size and role of our membership committee. While we
have done well, we can do better. We need to give our membership committee the
tools and resources to expand and reach further into the Jewish community in
Jacksonville. While at the same time, we must ensure that we are offering relevant
programming and enrichment that our current membership wants and needs. We
need to take a holistic approach to marketing, using all the assets of the Center when
creating brochures or hosting programs.
Sandy Zimmerman
President
Lastly, we are committed to taking a fresh look at our board
governance policies. Our Officers of the Board, Board of Directors,
and Board of Trustees are all committed to helping the Center attain
greatness. It is our responsibility to ensure that these dedicated
people have the tools and ability to work in an efficient and effective
manner. Revisiting our board by-laws will allow us to make sure
that our current board structure is designed to meet our complex
needs as a synagogue.
With my wife, Lynn, and my daughters, Brooke and Leah, I
look forward to working together with our entire congregation during
the next two years as your Synagogue President.
The second area of focus is technology. This past year we purchased a
technology package from Apple Computers that will take our schools into a new era.
However, we have not invested enough in software to help manage the entire
synagogue. With investment in new membership-tracking software, we can keep our
entire congregation more informed and offer more programming specifically targeted
at different demographic groups. Once installed, we should have a data base that
allows us to contact groups such as parents with children between 13 and 18 years of
age, or parents with kids in college. With this critical upgrade, I
feel confident we will better serve our diverse congregation.
B’shalom,
Sandy Zimmerman

Ritual Department
A Message from the Ritual Director
Torah is the foundation of the Jewish people. It records our earliest history, outlines
fundamental laws, models significant values and standards of behavior, reflects
basic truths about human nature, and opens us to relationship with, and wonder of,
the divine. Its language is the portal to Jewish study, worship, and identity.
Torah study has been vital to Jews for centuries precisely because it lends itself to
search and discovery by each new generation. The text is accessible on some
level to all individuals. Our liturgy calls Torah a “tree of life.” Trees sway to and
fro in the wind, but they are firmly rooted in the ground. Trees can shelter us from
rain, and satisfy our senses with sweet fruit, delightful fragrance, rusting leaves,
and ever-changing beauty. Similarly, a knowledge of Torah can root us firmly in
Jewish tradition and keep us from being blown about by the changing currents of
modern life. Torah study can satisfy our need for beauty and poetry, a sense of
identity, relevance, and meaning.
I would like to thank the many members of our congregation who have added their
voices to the process that is Torah. Through their dedication and devotion, we
have maintained a high level of participation in reading Torah. The commitment
and encouragement of our synagogue organizations and their leaders will ensure
that this quality standard will be continued for many generations to come.
Shanah Tovah,
Scott Zimmerman

Religious Committee
Bruce Horovitz and Marco Rand, Chairmen
The Religious Committee is proud of its role in helping to maintain
the Center’s strong tradition as a standard bearer for the ritual
practices of Conservative Judaism. This past year saw the expansion of
the role of women on the bimah, with the P’sukei D’Zimra portion of
the morning service being led by women for the first time. In this
coming year we will witness exciting new developments in the
religious life of the Center.
Our congregation has applied for membership in the “Synaplex”
program, which will introduce alternative Shabbat programming that
will be consistent with our religious character as a Conservative
synagogue, while attracting new participants. Concurrently, our proud
traditions in the main sanctuary will be preserved and be accessible to
this increasing group of worshippers. The Religious Committee also
looks forward to some very important decisions that are upcoming .
With the leadership and guidance of our Senior Rabbi, Jonathan
Lubliner, we look forward to completing the egalitarian process that
will ultimately enable women to lead services and count in the
minyan. This will provide a unique opportunity for our congregation to
become educated about the Conservative movement’s interpretation of
relevant halakhic standards. We all look forward to learning with
Rabbi Lubliner and Rabbi Weiner about the many ritual issues that
must be resolved.

A Message from Rabbi Lubliner
Entering Open Gates
Anyone who has ever thwarted the closing doors of an elevator or made a mad dash to catch a
train will understand the feeling of urgency that propels one forward with single-minded
determination. To miss the elevator in a crowded lobby may cause a person to be late to an
important meeting; to not board a particular train can alter the course of one’s life.
Coming at the end of Yom Kippur day, the unique liturgy and melodies of the Neilah service
lend a similar imperative to the task of soul reflection, which is the raison d'etre of the entire
High Holiday period. The Hebrew word Neilah means “closing” or “locking.” While it is a
matter of Talmudic debate whether the term refers to the shutting of the Temple gates at the end
of the Yom Kippur ritual, or the metaphorical closing of heaven’s gates at the conclusion of the
Day of Atonement, the net result is similar.
Our tradition understands that procrastination is hard-wired into the human psyche; often enough
we put off until tomorrow what we can avoid doing today. With its emphasis on closing gates and
the sealing of fates, the imagery oí Neilah intentionally seeks to quicken our spiritual adrenalin,
reminding us that the span of our years is finite. We may be ready to embark tomorrow on the
path of Teshuva , the road of change for the better . . . but will tomorrow be there for us?
Rabbi Jonathan Lubliner
In his story Vor dem Gesetz , “Before the Law”, Franz Kafka describes a man who stands outside a
guarded doorway leading to “The Law.” He desires to gain access, but the doorkeeper,
while never physically barring his way, tells him that the time is not yet ripe for entry. The poor
fellow spends a lifetime waiting for an invitation to enter that never comes. It is only in the last
moment of his life that it occurs to the man that he has never seen anyone else seek admission at
this door. The keeper informs him, “No other person could ever be admitted here, since this gate
was made only for you. I am now going to shut it.”
Neilah's message is precisely the opposite of Kafka’s bleak tale. In Judaism sincere expressions
of the heart and contrition are master keys that raise the tumblers of the lock on our souls; they
open a door to the Divine. As Rabbi Elazar once said, “Even when the gates of prayer appear
closed, the gates of tears are never shut, as Scripture teaches (Psalm 39:13), ‘Hear my prayer, 0
Lord; give ear to my cry, do not disregard my tears’” (Babylonian Talmud, Berakhot 32b).
Our mission is not to alter the core values, beliefs and practices that
define the proud identity of a synagogue that has historically eschewed a
path of Jewish institutional minimalism. Yet it is incumbent upon us to
open as many entry points as possible into synagogue life to those who
stand on the margins. Let us furnish all in need of a spiritual home with
the key to enter whatever door of Center life that is most meaningful to
them. May God grant us the skill of master locksmiths and bless the work

Clearly, the arrival of a Second Rabbi will also enhance the Jacksonville
Jewish Center’s ability to offer a more eclectic spiritual menu consonant
with the values of Conservative Judaism. To that end I welcome Rabbi
David Weiner and his family, and look forward to working with him

Mark & Alexine Miller Endowment
Mitzvah Gorret Mitzvah
One Mitzvah Leads to Another
The names of Mark and Alexine Miller will live on for generations
to come, not only for their friends, but also for our congregation.
Because of the compassion of our members and professionals,
the Jacksonville Jewish Center has become the recipient of
a large bequest of $883,000 from the estate of Alexine Miller,
who passed away on August 20, 2005, at the age of 89.
Her husband, Mark, predeceased her on December 13, 1984.
It meant a great deal to Mrs. Miller that Edith Horovitz and
students from our Martin J. Gottlieb Day School paid
mitzvah calls to her. The kindness and thoughtfulness of Rabbi
Jonathan Lubliner during his visits to Mrs. Miller, while she
was ill, has been rewarded through this new gift that will now
be part of our general endowment.
Rabbi Emeritus David Gaffney also touched the heart of
Alexine Miller during his tenure with our congregation. The
Jacksonville Jewish Center is proud of all of our professionals
and children.
A special headstone in memory of Mark and Alexine Miller
was unveiled Sunday, August 6, 2006
at the New Center Cemetery.
In addition, plans are being made to construct an Entrance Pavilion at
the south entrance of the building and parking area that will be
dedicated to the blessed memories of Mark and Alexine Miller.
In his eulogy to Alexine Miller, Rabbi Lubliner said,
“There are those people in life who are loud and windy, flashy and
flaunting. Yet, it is not necessarily those who attract attention to
themselves and their efforts who most merit praise for doing God’s work
in this world. Frequently, it is the person of humble demeanor and
modest character who is truly deserving of God’s blessing.
Alexine Miller lived the life of a kol cTmama dakka ; hers was a ‘still
small voice’ possessed of gentle quality, or as the book of Proverbs
put it, ‘teachings of kindness were on her tongue.’
Mrs. Miller lived an unassuming and quiet life with her husband, Mark.
She believed in the importance of tzedakah and contributed to a variety
of Jewish causes. Her Jewish identity was an intrinsic part of her being.
In his poem, Zot Tefilati , “This is My Prayer,”,
the poet Hillel Bavli offers a fitting epitaph for Alexine Miller:
‘Grant strength to yield fine fruits, quintessence of my life, s
teeped in my very being, without expectation of reward.
And when my time comes — Let me slip into the night
demanding nothing, God, of humanity, or of You.’”

Center Youth Commission
The Youth Commission oversees all youth organizations and programs sponsored by
the Jacksonville Jewish Center including Chalutzim, Kadima, USY, Scouts and the
Center Day Camp. The committee is made up of youth group advisors, presidents of
our clubs and caring Center members who believe that the future of Conservative
Judaism can be secured through participation in innovative programs designed to
develop upcoming Jewish leaders.
In addition to membership in Center Youth Groups, the Youth Commission
encourages our youngsters to attend United Synagogue summer programs, such as
Camp Ramah, USY - on - Wheels, Ramah Seminar in Israel and USY Poland/Israel
Pilgrimage. These provide excellent opportunities for our children to share
wholesome fun and uniquely Jewish experiences with their peers from all over the
United States. The Youth Commission assists applicants with a variety of
scholarships.

the synagogue and community. Four times a year, at Rosh Hashanah, Hanukkah,

holiday observance. Help us to stay in touch with your children by keeping us

Kindergarten Shabbat
Parents and guests kvelled as Kindergarten students of
the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School presented “Shabbat
from another Planet,” on February 3. The thoroughly
enjoyable evening began with a family Kabbalat
Shabbat service and concluded with dinner.
In preparation for this event, students learned all about
Shabbat, made Shabbat candle holders, Kiddish cups,
hallah covers and head coverings,
and even baked the hallot for the dinner.
Middle School Judaic-Infusion Trip
Each year, Edith Horovitz, Vice Principal of the Middle School,
plans a trip to a prominent Jewish community in the United States.
Last April, students and chaperones spent a week in Chicago. The
tour included Jewish points of interest, a ball game, museums,
Kosher restaurants, fun parks and much more.

— The Samuel & Esther Galinsky Educational Programs —
Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School
A Message from the Religious School Director
The Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious School of the Jacksonville Jewish
Center has evolved into an exciting and vibrant program. The award-
winning curriculum, combined with educational and entertaining family
programming, concentrates on instilling a love of our rich cultural
heritage. Our dedicated and caring staff, along with Rabbi Lubliner,
creates an atmosphere where children can sort out overwhelming current
issues and events in a gentle and safe Jewish environment. A connection
to our synagogue and a sense of community develop as students see their
classroom friends and teachers at services and other functions.
The Sunday School program for Kindergarten through 2nd graders is
designed to introduce our eager youngest students to Hebrew letters and
vocabulary, Jewish holidays, and heroes and mitzvot in the Torah.
Students in grades 3-6 meet three days per week, Sunday, Monday and
Wednesday, concentrating on Hebrew fluency (prayer as well as
conversational), history, customs and rituals. We have ensured that our
curriculum runs parallel to the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School curriculum,
so that any student can easily transition into the Religious School and
remain challenged in all levels. The 7th grade “Etgar” class learns to pull
together all of this knowledge and put it to practical use, both personally
and communally, as young, Jewish adults. The addition of Cantor Yitzhak
Ben-Moshe to our staff has enabled the expansion of our tyülot
curriculum.
Extensive use of music, art projects, dramatics, and our outstanding
congregational library and computer lab allows each student to flourish in
an exciting, kid-friendly atmosphere. A special-needs professional and
individual tutors are available for children who need a bit of extra help
during school hours with their Hebrew and Judaic studies.
The introduction, this year, of a formal objective assessment system will
help us to track the progress of individual students in areas such as
Hebrew reading, prayer, and Judaica knowledge. This will allow us to
recognize problems as they develop, and thereby keep a child moving
forward in their studies.
Lois Tompkins

Martin J. Gottlieb Day School
A Solomon Schechter School
located in the Gordon-Hecht-Bossen Education Center
A Message from the Day School Principal
The educators of the Martin J. Gottlieb Day School consciously reflect on the past
and look toward the future. Our unique day school experience, in a “living” Jewish
community, integrates the Judaic and general studies curriculum throughout the day
and produces young people conversant in the Hebrew language. That which ensues
is a communal joy of celebration, combined with the valued tradition of study and
inquiry, ultimately fostering a love of learning.
Our faculty is motivated, caring and dedicated to providing optimal learning
experiences through the use of various teaching techniques. We use a spiraling
curriculum that builds on basic skills and expands with increasing levels of
sophistication, encouraging questioning, exploration, and analysis. Art, music,
technology, library, and physical education are woven into this curriculum, further
enriching its quality. The accolades received by the current students, and the
stunning achievements of our graduates, are testimonials to the school’s high
academic standards. The current acquisition of state-of-the-art laptop and desktop
computers and software will enable the students to integrate technology into all
aspects of learning and prepare them for the challenging future that awaits them.
Learning the ethical values of Judaism and applying this knowledge to the
performance of mitzvot is fundamental to every child’s education. As we strive to
create “menschen,” the values of social action and social responsibility become an
integral part of character building, so vital in our society of changing values.
Completing the educational panarama are several other essential components, most
importantly, parents. Through parent workshops held throughout the year, open
communication with parents is stressed and encouraged. The PTA and the
synagogue’s Education Committee work diligently on behalf of the students and the
school. The support of the Jacksonville Jewish Federation and their partnership in
our Family University program help to solidify the school’s presence in the
community. The guidance and support of the Jacksonville Jewish Center is central
to the school’s well being.
If you have not done so already, visit our school. Stroll through its magnificent,
physical plant and grounds. Marvel at the creative writing and other projects
displayed in the hallways, and “kvell” at the sound of children’s voices in prayer.
As you reflect on your own educational experience, I feel you will agree that you are
witnessing a promising future.
— Carole Goldberg

Boy Scout Troop 14
Boy Scout Troop 14 continued the scouting tradition at the
Jacksonville Jewish Center with another year of outdoor
adventure.
Living up to the scout motto “Do a Good Turn Daily,”
(at right, 1 to r) Andrew Vining, Eddie Norton, Kyle Vining,
and Adam Schwartz, along with Scout Master, Louis Schwartz,
volunteered to help clean up after Hurricane Katrina.
They spent four days at Salmon Scout Reservation in Gulf Port,
Mississippi, removing downed trees and brush.
As in the past, the scouts participated in campouts at the Riverbend
Camporee, Camp Thunder, in Georgia. Also, Cory Brenenson, Harrison
Safer, Adam Schwartz, Ben Gatewood and Max Rogozinski spent a
week at Camp Baden Powell Scout Reservation. They took part in shooting
sports: shotgun, rifle and black powder rifle and archery. They also worked
on advancement in astronomy, cooking, painting, home repair, weather,
citizenship and sports.
All the scouts made great strides in
advancement this year. Andrew Vining, son of
Elissa & Scott Vining, and Adam Schwartz,
son of Jeri & Louis Schwartz, earned Boy
Scouting’s highest honor, the rank of Eagle.
Among the rigorous requirements, these scouts
earned 21 merit badges, held leadership
positions and completed community projects.
Benjamin Hecht and Jacob Heclit, sons of Rose & Larry Hecht, were
awarded the Jewish Boy Scout religious emblem, the Ner Tamid.
Troop 14 looks forward to continuing its proud tradition of providing Jewish
youth with a Boy Scouting experience. If you enjoy camping, sports and a
variety of outdoor activities, and are between the ages of 11 and 18, we
invite you to attend our meetings at the Center on Thursday nights.
For more information, call me at 268-8717.

Hevra Kaddisha
The Hevra Kaddisha is one of the oldest and most respected Jewish institutions.
Its duties were so important that in early times it was one of the first
organizations formed by a new Jewish community.
This is true of the Jacksonville Hevra Kaddisha, an independent society
made up of 40 dedicated members who serve the entire Jewish community.
Their unselfish efforts are truly noteworthy.
The Hevra Kaddisha is available 24 hours a day to help families
in a time of need following the immediate loss of a loved one.
We are willing and able to offer comfort and answer your concerns
Our members are trained in die ritual practice of taharah , the traditional
preparation of the deceased for burial. We recite the required prayers to guard
them, forgive their sins and grant them eternal peace.

Hadassah
Judaism teaches that it is not enough to avoid iniquity or
remain silent in the face of injustice. We must actively pursue
righteousness and justice at home, in the community, and in
the world. In the spirit of Tikkun Olam, Hadassah began as a
study group in 1912 by Henrietta Szold, who gathered together
like-minded women to bring public health services to pre-state
Israel. Today, more than 300,000 American women (49,000 in
Florida) help repair the world by supporting Hadassah’s
projects and programs.
In Israel we save lives at our hospitals at Ein Kerem and
Mount Scopus, build self-esteem and employment skills at
Hadassah Career Counseling Institute, rescue children at risk
through Youth Aliyah, and educate at Hadassah College
Jerusalem. And whether you are a seasoned traveler or have
not yet been to Eretz Yisrael, you must see Israel through
Hadassah’s eyes! Let us help you find the Hadassah Mission
that exceeds all your expectations.
Across America, our members promote health education,
perform community service, and teach Jewish values.
Hadassah advocates on issues ranging from Israel-US relations
to First Amendment protections to genetic discrimination to
women’s rights and health concerns.
We are mothers and daughters, sisters and friends. We are
attorneys, physicians, social workers, nurses, bankers, real
estate brokers, and teachers. Jacksonville’s 1,000 Hadassah
members proudly invite you to join our wonderful organization
and experience the joy of doing mitzvot.
Please contact me to discuss which of our four groups, Aronson
Nurses & Health Care Professionals’ Council, Shir Li, Tikva,
or Yamit, will be able to welcome you as a new or returning
member: Jodi Seitlin, Jacksonville Chapter President,
(904) 254-2322 or lawladyl990@clearwire.net

Scouting
Senior Girl Scout Troop 969
For some girls of the Jacksonville Jewish Center’s Girl Scout Troop # 969,
this was not only their graduating year of high school, but also their graduating
year of Senior Girl Scouting. Stephanie Shacter., Kelby Siddons,
Sarah Pierce and Morgan Owens have shared nine years of learning, service
and friendship with Michelle Jernigan and Talia Fleet.
The troop concluded the year in late May with a beach condo stay in
St. Augustine, with day trips to the surrounding area. The highlight of the trip was
parasailing in sight of St. Augustine and its black-and-white spiraled lighthouse.
Three girls at a time sailed from a 1,000 foot line above the glistening waves.
While in St. Augustine, the troop also took advantage of the attractions of St.
George Street, such as a Ghost Tour, the Spanish Bakery, Kilwin’s Chocolates and
the Memorial Day fireworks that spangled the city’s sky, as well as nearby
National Parks, Fort Matanzas and Washington Oaks Gardens.
The girls all agreed their relaxing, beautiful and adventurous trip was an
excellent use of their cookie sale earnings and a wonderful conclusion
to the Troop 969 experience. So what’s next?
Michelle and Talia plan to finish their senior year as scouts through the “Zip”
program, individual girls participating through the mail. Over the summer,
Stephanie began college at University of Central Florida and Sarah started at
Florida State University. Morgan will attend Barry College and Kelby will attend
Northwestern University in the fall. The girls chose these colleges for the quality
they say has been the troop’s hallmark - its diversity. “Different races, religions
and high schools,” troop leader Helen Shacter mused,
“This is the only place they would have come together.” Each is certainly
thankful to Girls Scout of USA and the Center synagogue for just that.
This article was written for all the girls by Kelby Siddons.
We want to especially thank the parents for all the years of support
for their daughters and Girl Scouting!
— Helen Shacter & Shirley Jernigan

A Message from Rabbi Weiner
I am most passionate about Judaism when it approaches tradition in a
progressive way. My deep commitment to prayer, study and love of people form
the way I approach our Jewish heritage and teach our sacred texts. Fostering
mutual respect, lifelong learning and open discussion of serious issues yields a
more supportive and welcoming Jewish community.
Although Torah was given once, it is received every day, and everyone who
receives Torah hears it differently.
While studying at the Jewish Theological Seminary in New York,
I enjoyed approaching our tradition from a variety of perspectives, working
together with my colleagues and teachers to understand the details as well as
the bigger picture. I became particularly interested in the interaction between
law and narrative in rabbinic literature. Recognizing that book knowledge
affords an incomplete picture of the world, I also served as a hospital chaplain,
and I stand in awe of my patients’ physical and spiritual strength in the face of
overwhelming suffering. My passion for education and justice led me to the
Tikvah Program for teenagers with disabilities at Camp Ramah in Wisconsin,
where I worked as counselor, teacher and finally coordinator
of the program over six summers.
Before coming to the Jacksonville Jewish Center, I served for three years as the
visiting rabbi of a small congregation, Etz Chayim of Huntsville, Alabama.
Teaching and leading services for a diverse community, as well as counseling
members regarding Jewish approaches to the challenges they face, gave me an
appreciation of the myriad ways children, teens and adults connect with each
other and with God. Last summer at Temple Emanuel in Sydney, Australia, I had
a chance to experience the excitement of a vibrant, pluralistic, urban synagogue
and the thrill of working as a part of an accomplished professional team.
Rabbi Chanina taught: “From my teachers I have learned much; from my
colleagues I have learned more. But it is from my students that I have learned
the most.” (Babylonian Talmud, Taanit 7a) When I teach a class, my primary
concern is encouraging all learners in the room to explore their
relationship to the material at hand and the questions it raises.
And, like Rabbi Chanina, I include myself as a learner.
I find in the words of Leah Goldberg, an Israeli poet of the mid-twentieth
century, a particularly evocative prayer for this season of renewal:
Rabbi David Weiner
Teach me, my God, to bless and ponder
The secret of a wrinkled leaf, the glow of ripe fruit,
This freedom: to see, to feel, to breathe,
To know, to long, to stumble.
Teach my lips blessing and song of praise
As time renews itself each day and night,
Lest my day today repeat yesterdays,
Lest my days become routine.
Judith, Joseph and I are very excited to join
the Jacksonville Jewish Center family. We wish you a shana
tova umtuka , a year of sweetness, blessing and growth.
— Rabbi David Weiner

the latest technology.

A Message from the Cantor
5767 - A Year of Growth and Renewal
I am happy to report that this year brings a
new stage in my professional development. I was privileged to attend
the 59th annual convention of the Cantor’s Assembly in New York in May of
2006. At that time, I sat for and passed four of the six components of the
written examination for entry into the Cantors Assembly.
In addition, I was formally accepted as a Cantoral Intern of the Cantors
Assembly, and may now be called by the title, “Cantor.” This program was
set up to enable Ba’alay Tefillah to gain full membership in the Cantors
Assembly. Interns are paired with mentors who work with them during the
course of their studies. These studies are based on a curriculum of Jewish
liturgical and musical knowledge, which ensures the professional
qualification of Hazzanim in the Cantors Assembly. My own mentor is
Hazzan Laurie Rimland-Bonn in Orlando, with whom I have been
studying for some months now. In addition, Cantoral Interns are associate
members of the Cantors Assembly with access to most of its resources.
Of course, the most valuable resource of all is the opportunity to talk and
share music with other cantors.
Over the course of the coming year, I plan to continue the “Carlebach
Shabbat” services on selected Friday nights. Introduced over the summer,
these services feature modern liturgical tunes composed by
Shlomo Carlebach and others. In addition, a Middle School Youth Choir
will be formed that will participate in festival services as well as selected
Shabbat services during the year.
Finally, I would like to congratulate Blanche Slott for being honored by the
5767 Center Yearbook. From our first meeting, when I was a child and she
was Director of the Center’s Day Camp, through her term as
Executive Director of the congregation and up until today, Blanche has been
an integral part of my life, and everyone’s life here at the Center.
We all hope for many more active years for Blanche.
D’vorah, Ban and Eden join me in wishing a “Shanah Tovah uMetukah -
a good and sweet year” to the entire Center family.
B’shalom,
— Cantor Yitzhak Ben-Moshe

Marty Gottlieb B’rit Hinukh Covenant of Education Award
The Men’s Club Board of Directors established the Marty Gottlieb B’rit Hinukh
Covenant of Education Award to recognize individual educators within the
Jacksonville Jewish Center Schools for their lifelong commitment to intellectual,
professional and religious development and to the passionate motivation of their
students to learn about the language, laws, traditions, history and spiritual
strength of Conservative Judaism.
David Schmerer presented the B’rit Hinukh award to Judy Reppert at the PTA
Teacher Appreciation Dinner on May 22, 2006. For thirty years, Mrs. Reppert has
been a loved and respected teacher in both the Day School and Religious School.
Acclaimed for her creativity, Judy’s classes are the highlight of her students’ day.
She is currently part of the Middle School teaching team.
Yashar Kohahat to Judy on this well deserved honor.

Men’s Club Yad Shel Chai Award
In 1998, Center Men’s Club instituted this award-winning program to
encourage youngsters to continue reading Torah after their Bar or Bat Mitzvah.
Teens who read a minimum of 18 (chai) portions by age 18 are presented with
an inscribed Torah pointer (yad ) and a certificate. Over the years, many teens
have enrolled in this program, and to date 39 have received the award.

Jacksonville Mitzvah Clown Corps
The goal of the Jacksonville
Mitzvah Clowns is to create a self
perpetuating corps of local
Mitzvah Clowns available for
periodic visits and special
programs at local facilities
participation in local programs
which serve the Jewish community
and as well as the general
community. The Mitzvah Clown
program seeks to bring smiles,
companionship and cheer to sick
and aged people while at the same
time enhancing self-confidence
and creating a joy of doing mitzvot
among the Mitzvah Clown participants. The program also
seeks to instill in the participants a level of comfort when
performing the mitzvah of bikkur holim.
The Jacksonville Mitzvah Clowns have
participated in various community events during the past
year including:
• Chabad at the Beaches Hanukkah Celebration
• Center Preschool Kaleidoscope program
• Hadassah Birthday Celebration
• River Garden Grandparents Day
• Martin J. Gottlieb Middle School Mitzvah Program
• Mishloach Manot Deliveries for Purim 2006
• Purim and Anniversary Celebrations River Garden
• JCA Open House event
Religious School Etgar class Mitzvah Clowns
prepare to visit Ronald McDonald House.
The Jacksonville
Mitzvah Clowns are
especially proud to continue
to be recognized as one of the
most active Mitzvah Clown
troupes in the country. Many
thanks are owed to the many
volunteers who continue to give freely of themselves to
make this program so successful.
We were pleased to welcome a new group of clowns from
the Etgar Class of the Bernard & Alice Selevan Religious
School.
The group is always looking for new trainees and
hopes to sponsor additional training sessions for both adults
and teens in the sixth grade or older during the coming
year. Contact Vivo at lenjorge@comcast.net or Gigibell
at gigi- 18 @comcast.net .

This past year JFCS provided help and support
to over 2000 Jewish individuals and families?

the Jacksonville Jewish Federation and donations from

A Woman of Valor
A woman of valor who can find?
Her price is far above rubies.
The heart of her husband trusteth in her,
And he shall have no lack of gain.
She doeth him good and not evil,
All the days of her life.
She looketh well to the ways of her household,
And eateth not the bread if idleness.
She giveth food to her household,
And a portion for her maidens.
Strength and dignity are her clothing,
And she laugheth at the time to come*
She stretcheth out her hand to the poor,
Yea, she reacheth out her hands to the needy.
She openeth her mouth with wisdom,
And the law of lovingkindness is on her tongue.
Her children rise up and call her blessed,
Her husband also, and he praiseth her:
“Many daughters have done worthily,
But thou excellest them all.”
Grace is deceitful, and beauty is vain,
But a woman that revereth the Lord, she shall be praised.
Give her of the fruit of her hands,
And let her works praise her in the gates.
With All Our Love and Respect,
Jacksonville Jewish Center Sisterhood

Rabbi Golinkin
The year 5766 was filled with a variety of
programs and events to educate and inspire our
congregation. It began at Selihot in September, with
the annual Marilyn & Louis Safer Shabbaton Weekend.
Rabbi David Golinkin, president and professor of
Jewish Law at the Schechter Institute of Jewish Studies
in Jerusalem, was our dynamic scholar-in-residence.
He spoke on a variety of issues of Jewish law from the
perspective of the Conservative Movement, bringing a
modern twist to them all. Thank you to Sue & Mitch
Levine for hosting a Sunday morning reception for
donors.
In keeping with the theme “Shabbat for the Rest of your Life,”
Rabbi Lubliner taught a seven part series that explored the literature, laws and
lore of Shabbat. Many of our members enjoyed “Tiyul to Shul ,” which encouraged
worshippers to enjoy walking with friends to Shabbat services. Todah rabah to Ira
Grann, Helen Shacter and Etta Fialkow for coordinating this program.
Sunday seminars with Rabbi Lubliner, once again, offered the opportunity
to study several interesting topics in depth. Monday evening Talmud classes
continued the exploration of the Babylonian Talmud’s Tractate Megillah.
One Shabbat a month, Rabbi served up “Coffee Talk,”
a pre-services look at the Torah using the Etz Hayim Humash.
An assortment of other courses was taught throughout the year by our talented and
knowledgeable instructors. Marc Leventhal explored religious books in “What’s
in Your Library?” Janice Knack taught “Understanding Siddur Hebrew,” while
Wendy Brezin and Arlene Yegelwel offered a course in Hebrew literacy. Lorrain
Federman taught us to speak Israeli Hebrew, and Cantor Ben-Moshe showed us
how to daven. The popular “Firing Line” discussions continued to examine
contemporary Jewish issues using current periodicals.
We were fortunate to hear Steven M. Cohen, a sociologist of American Jewry,
speak on “Engaging the Next Generation in American Jewry,”
at the Setzer-Levit Lecture in March.
In May, thirty people began a new adventure in learning, “If You’ve Ever
Wondered . . . An Introduction To Judaism,” a 24-session course compiled by
Rabbi Lubliner and several of his colleagues from New Jersey. Thank you to
Karen Hamilton and Janice Knack for making classes so interesting that a
waiting list has started for a new class to begin in 2007.
The year closed with a Tikkun leyl Shavuot study session following the
Confirmation service and ending just before midnight. A variety of topics were
explored by Rabbi Lubliner, Cantor Ben-Moshe and Marc Leventhal, in one hour
formats, which allowed for participation in more than one discussion.

Committees and Organizations
Center Sisterhood
Jewish women have “Western brains.” Sisterhood
offers these same women ways to cultivate their
“Jewish hearts” at Sisterhood meetings, book
reviews, seminars, Women’s Minyan, Sisterhood
Shabbat and the Adult B’nai Mitzvah Class.
Sisterhood supports the Center’s children. At the
High Holidays, we provide them with snacks, and
at Purim, we serve Sisterhood-made hamantashen.
Sisterhood raises funds for youth scholarships to
Camp Ramah, USY-on -Wheels, and Israel
pilgrimage. We present sweets to our Consecration
children, candlesticks to B’nai Mitzvah, personalized Bibles to the Confirmation students
and Leagrams for many occasions. We do so because our children are the future of
Judaism.
The Center’s Building Fund benefits annually from our Donor Luncheon. Sisterhood
supports The Jewish Theological Seminary that trains future rabbis, cantors, educators,
and administrators, through the Women’s League Torah Fund Campaign. Benefactors can
be recognized by the beautiful theme pins that are given to each.
Located near the Center’s DuBow-Cohen Lobby,
the Sisterhood Judaica Shop is an ongoing source
of funding for many of our projects, and at the
same time offers a wide selection of religious
articles, Judaic books and lifecycle gifts.
Sisterhood is also there in times of need for
families who have just lost a loved one. Our
volunteers make sure that every mourning family
has at least a basic, first shiva meal upon returning
from the cemetery.
This year, we will be introducing some new programming that will be called Z’havah.
Z’havah is the feminine Hebrew word for “gold,” which is the acronym for Generating
Our Leadership Development. Through Z’havah, we will be reaching out to the younger,
unaffiliated women who may not be interested in the
traditional Sisterhood.
Left to right , top to bottom: Etta Fialkow ,
Phyllis Goldberg , Lara Werwa, Marsha Pollock , Barbara Safer .;
Lita Poehlman, Joanne Rosenberg and Maxine Romo
represented our Sisterhood at the Spring Branch Conference.
During the next two years, it is my desire that
Sisterhood will become “Team Sisterhood,” an
organizational arm of the Center where any woman who
chooses to “get involved” will have an opportunity to
better educate herself, make new friends, raise money
for our endeavors, and support our ongoing projects.
There is no limit to how many teammates we can have,
so I hope that every Center woman will find a way to
take part. Whether it’s baking hamantashen ,
attending a Sisterhood religious service, giving/
attending a Sisterhood book review, coming to our
programs, working on and supporting our fundraising
projects, or just paying dues, there’s something for
everyone. Sisterhood is also open to any Jewish woman
who might not yet be a member of the Jacksonville
Jewish Center.
— Etta Fialkow

The Jacksonville Jewish Center has continued

Shirley Bielski and Talie Zaifert, co-chairs

welcoming and friendly atmosphere at the Jacksonville

synagogue. All Center members are automatically part of
the Membership Committee and ambassadors of our
congregation. We encourage everyone to get involved in
the many activities we offer. It’s a wonderful way to make
new friends and truly become part of the Center family.
