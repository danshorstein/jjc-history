Lawrence Rackow, Director Jack Coleman, President
Founded in 1917, the JFCS has been assisting its clients solve
personal problems for the past 47 years. Last year some 300 different
families were served in one or more programs offered by the agency.
The monthly average came to 89.
For the sake of understanding, the services offered may be di¬
vided into 10 categories:
1. Personal Counseling
2. Adoption Program
3. Foster Home Program
4. Assistance to the Aged
5. Employment and Vocational
Services
6. New American Program
7. Short-term Financial
Assistance
8. Transient Program
9. Prison Program
10. Day Camp & College
Scholarship Program
Actually, problems frequently do not neatly fit themselves into
exclusive categories — even the broad ones suggested above. A work
problem or a parent-child problem might have ramifications that in¬
volve personal counseling, health, finances, family relations, etc. One
of the strengths of the agency is that it is so strongly client oriented,
With the help of an experienced sympathetic professionally trained
worker, the client is helped to get a clearer focus of his problem or
problems and to locate and devise means of overcoming them. Some¬
times “just talking” helps, or the resources can be found directly in
the clients or the agency. Other times the resources of the com¬
munity, region or even nation, public and private, may be required.
Here, because of the strictest respect for the rights of the clients and
the confidential nature of the relationship, the consent of the client
is always secured. The close cooperation of River Garden Hebrew
Home, the congregations, the 40 other UCS agencies, the schools,
the public and private health and welfare agencies and especially the
outstanding cooperation of the professional men — rabbis, doctors,
dentists, lawyers, etc. — makes possible the degree of success in so
many situations. Last year, in its search to constantly improve condi¬
tions and service, the agency became an agent for college loans pro¬
vided by the Children's Services of Atlanta, Ga.; worked out agree¬
ments with a number of agencies for care of unmarried mothers and
the children available for adoption, and established an independent
group of friendly visitors to the aged in their own homes.
The understanding and cooperation of the Jewish community is
vital to insure the best service. The response in terms of donations of
clothing has been magnificent but more work has to be done about
alerting the agency of available employment opportunities. A whole¬
hearted support of both the United Community Services and the
Jewish Welfare Fund drives makes this work possible!
